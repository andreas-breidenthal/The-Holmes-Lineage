MARCUS HOLCUS AURELIUS

~100 AD — Rome, Roman Empire

Bearing the name that would later be shared with the philosopher-emperor — whether by family design or coincidence, the record does not say — Marcus Holcus Aurelius was born in the age of the Five Good Emperors — the period that later historians would come to regard as the summit of Roman prosperity, which is perhaps an exaggeration but captures something true about the particular quality of light that falls on an empire at its zenith. He was a man formed by that light: educated in the logic of the Ionian sages, committed to the life of reason, convinced that the examined life was the only one worth living, and possessed of a detachment from his own comfort and advancement that his contemporaries found either admirable or impractical depending on their temperament.

He taught philosophy in Rome — not in the formal sense of a school or a chair, but in the old Socratic manner of the Athenian agora, engaging students in the examination of their assumptions through questions so precisely targeted that the student invariably ended by discovering, in the ruins of a position he had held comfortably for years, the truth that had been buried beneath it. His particular habit, noted even by those who did not admire him, was to refuse conclusions until every lesser explanation had been exhausted, a discipline that made his conversations slower than most and their conclusions more difficult to escape.

The account survives through the notes of Gaius Diocles himself, preserved among private papers that passed through the family and copied at a later date. Diocles was young, uncertain, and at times inclined to interpret events in moral rather than practical terms; the record bears those marks, though it preserves with unusual clarity the sequence by which Marcus Holcus Aurelius led him from uncertainty to action.

The Account of Gaius Diocles

Transcribed from His Private Papers

I have recorded here, as faithfully as I am able, the events of the fifth day before the Ides of October, in the consulship of Manius Acilius Glabrio and Bellicius Torquatus. I do so not because I believe the account flattering to myself — it is not — but because I have concluded that a true account of how Marcus Holcus Aurelius works is of greater value than a comfortable one. He would say, were I to show him these pages, that the distinction between those two things is the whole of philosophy. He might be right. He is, on the evidence of my experience with him, usually right, which is both the most useful and the most humbling quality a teacher can possess.

I have omitted certain names where their inclusion would place living persons in danger. Marcus himself suggested this. “Truth,” he said, “is not the same as disclosure. A physician who publishes the ailments of his patients has confused two virtues and produced a vice.”

I record it as he said it. I have tried throughout to record things as he said them.

The morning of Caius Primus’s disappearance began, as most mornings in the shadow of the Palatine begin, with noise.

Rome in October is not Rome in July. The heat that makes the city a kind of slow punishment has retreated, and in its place comes a particular energy — street vendors sharper, the clatter of cart-wheels on the stone of the Subura more assertive, the smell of the insulae in the cooler air not the thick rot of summer but something sharper and more complicated, the accumulated record of ten thousand daily lives compressed into the narrow streets. I mention this because Marcus has taught me to begin with the senses, to anchor the account in what was actually present rather than what I wished to be present, and what was present that morning was the smell of wet stone and lamp-smoke and the specific quality of autumn Roman light that turns everything the colour of old honey.

I arrived at the portico where Marcus conducts his examinations to find seven students already seated on the low benches arranged in the courtyard’s lower tier. Caius Primus was among them — I noticed him particularly because he had arrived before me, which was unusual. He was not a punctual young man by temperament, but that morning he sat with the posture of someone who has been awake for several hours and has spent them in uncomfortable thought. He was perhaps nineteen, the son of a grain merchant of middling but respectable prosperity, enrolled with Marcus for the kind of philosophical education that the better families in Rome had decided was appropriate preparation for a public career, though what a public career requires of natural philosophy I have never been entirely certain.

Marcus arrived last, as he always does — not from discourtesy but from what he calls the pedagogical advantage of observation. He stands at the entrance for a moment before anyone notices him, examining the group with the particular quality of attention that I have come to recognise as assessment rather than greeting. He sees, in that moment, things I have since learned to look for and still cannot reliably find.

He walked to his customary position and sat, not on a chair — there is no chair in his courtyard — but on a stone block that he maintains is sufficiently comfortable for a man who is not attending to his comfort.

“We were discussing,” he said, without preamble, “the conditions under which a man may be said to know a thing, as distinct from the conditions under which he believes it. Caius. You were preparing to offer an objection.”

Caius had not been preparing any such thing, so far as I could observe. But this is Marcus’s method — he assigns the student the position, then examines what the student makes of having been assigned it. It is more revealing, he says, than asking the student what he thinks, because what a student thinks he thinks and what he actually thinks are often different territories.

“I was going to say,” Caius said, slowly, “that the distinction seems to matter less than the consequences of acting on either.”

“Interesting.” Marcus appeared to consider this. “Expand.”

What followed was approximately forty minutes of examination that I have tried to reconstruct from my notes, though the notes are incomplete because Marcus moves quickly and I write slowly. The substance of it was this: Marcus drew Caius, through a sequence of questions each of which seemed unconnected to the last, toward a position on the nature of complicity. Specifically: at what point does a man who is aware of a wrongdoing and does not report it become responsible for that wrongdoing? Caius argued, initially, that awareness without action was morally neutral. Marcus dismantled this position efficiently and without apparent effort.

“If you are aware,” Marcus said, “that a man you know is engaged in an enterprise that causes harm to others, and you take no action, you are providing that enterprise with the protection of your silence. Is silence an action?”

“It is an absence of action.”

“Precisely. And is an absence always neutral?”

Caius thought about this for longer than the other students thought was necessary. I watched him. There was something in his posture that had changed — a careful stillness that was not the stillness of concentration but the stillness of a man who has suddenly recognised that the road he is walking leads somewhere he did not intend to go.

“No,” he said, finally. “An absence is sometimes a choice.”

“And a choice carries consequence.”

“Yes.”

“Then your position has collapsed. You now hold that awareness without action is not morally neutral, but morally weighted — that the man who knows and does not speak is complicit in the harm that follows.”

“I—” Caius stopped. “I suppose I do.”

I wrote it down. It did not seem, at the time, to be the most significant moment of the examination. It became, in retrospect, its most important.

The session ended at the second hour. The students dispersed in the manner of students released from intellectual effort — with the slightly stunned expression of men who have been told something they cannot quite arrange yet. I remained to discuss a question about Epictetus with Marcus that had occupied me since the previous week. We spoke for perhaps a quarter-hour.

When I left, Caius Primus was gone.

I noticed only because I had intended to walk part of the way with him and found his absence unremarkable. He had simply left. Students leave. I thought nothing of it.

I thought nothing of it for two days.

On the third morning after the examination, I arrived at the courtyard to find Marcus alone, seated with a small piece of carved bone in his hand — not examining it, precisely, but turning it in his fingers in the manner I have learned to associate with a mind that is working on a problem and using the hands to keep the body occupied during the operation.

“Caius has not been home,” he said, without looking up.

“How do you know?”

“His mother sent a message through the household of Quintus Flaminius, whose cook knows our neighbour’s wife. The message arrived last evening.” He set down the carved bone. “She asks whether I saw him leave. She asks whether he seemed well. The careful phrasing of the message suggests she is afraid of the answer to the second question.”

“Perhaps he went to visit someone. Young men—”

“Young men who intend to visit someone tell their mothers, or failing that, tell someone who tells their mothers. The telling may be delayed by a day. Three days is not delay. Three days is absence.”

He stood, with the sudden energy that appears in him when a problem has moved from the abstract to the operational — a transformation I have witnessed enough times now to recognise its specific quality. The languor falls away. What remains is something that moves quickly and with purpose.

“Where are you going?” I asked.

“To observe the things I did not observe sufficiently at the time. Come, if you like. You will be useful, and more importantly, the walk will do you good.”

Marcus’s method of observation is not what I expected, when I first witnessed it. I had imagined something systematic and deliberate — a careful examination of physical spaces, a methodical questioning of witnesses. What it actually resembles is a man following a thought. He moves. He stops. He looks at something that appears unremarkable and stands with it for a time, and then he turns and says something that reorganises everything that preceded it.

We began at the portico. He examined the space where Caius had sat.

“He arrived early,” I said.

“You noticed that.”

“He was already seated when I arrived. He is not usually early.”

“He was not early,” Marcus said. “He had not left.” He crouched and examined the stone beneath the bench. I could not see what he was looking at. “The lamp at the far end of the portico was lit when I arrived. I noted it as unusual for the hour. When a lamp is lit in daylight in an exposed space, it is because someone was present in the dark hours before and did not extinguish it before the light made it unnecessary. He was here before dawn.”

“Why?”

“Because he could not sleep. Or because he did not go home.” He straightened. “Both possibilities lead to the same question: what happened the evening before our session that made sleep or home impossible?”

I thought about it. “The day before the session — I saw him near the old citadel wall in the late afternoon.”

Marcus turned to look at me with the particular attention he gives to information that fits a space he has already identified.

“With whom?”

“Alone. He was — I thought he was simply walking. But he stopped at the entrance to the money-changers’ colonnade and looked in for a long time. I assumed he was looking for someone.”

“Or making certain someone had not seen him.” He began walking. “Come.”

The money-changers’ colonnade at the Basilica Julia is a place I visit infrequently and without pleasure. It smells of the specific combination of metal and human proximity that characterises places where money changes hands in quantity and not always by honest means. The light inside it is the yellow of old tallow lamps even in daylight, the small flames burning against the dimness of the interior arcade.

Marcus walked the length of it without stopping, looking at the faces of the men behind the tables in the way he looks at most things — as a collection of information to be processed rather than people to be greeted. At the far end he stopped at a pillar and stood for a moment.

“The third table from the left,” he said. “The man with the grey wool mantle and the broken clasp on his left shoulder. He saw us enter and he has not looked at us since. A man who has nothing to conceal from a philosopher and his student looks at a philosopher and his student — if only to confirm they are unimportant. A man who actively avoids looking has a reason.”

“That could be any number of reasons.”

“It could. But notice his table.” He did not point. He described. “The abacus is positioned to face himself rather than his clients. He is not working. He is waiting for something. And on the edge of the table, the small carved mark — do you see it? At the lower right corner.”

I looked. I saw a mark carved into the wood — a small symbol, like a stylised wheel with five spokes.

“A guild mark?”

“Not a guild that is licensed,” Marcus said. “I have seen that mark twice before. Once on a doorframe in the Subura, once on a wine amphora that arrived at a house I was asked to visit professionally.” He paused. “It is a mark of organisation. Of the kind that does not wish to be identified as an organisation.”

“A criminal consortium.”

“That is the dramatic formulation. The precise formulation is: an arrangement among men with shared interests that they wish to pursue without official scrutiny.” He turned away from the colonnade. “Caius came here the evening before the examination. He stood at the entrance and looked in for a long time. He then spent the night unable to return home. And the next morning, in the examination, he argued with notable discomfort that silence in the face of wrongdoing is morally neutral — until I demonstrated, to his own apparent surprise, that he did not believe this.”

He stopped walking. We were in the Forum now, in the pale October light, the Capitoline behind us and the Palatine ahead, the sound of Rome in the middle of its day surrounding us like water.

“He saw something,” I said.

“He saw something that he had been paid, or pressured, or simply frightened into not seeing. And then he sat in an examination of the moral weight of precisely that silence and found, to his horror, that he could not defend the position he needed to hold.” Marcus’s voice was entirely without drama. He was reporting an equation. “He left immediately after the session. He did not go home. He went — somewhere. The question is where a young man goes when he has been forced to understand that he is in a position he cannot sustain.”

“Somewhere safe.”

“Or somewhere that can tell him whether he is in danger.” He looked up at the sky, briefly. “A man who knows something about an organisation of the kind I have described — who has perhaps seen something he was not meant to see, or heard something inadvertently — is a variable that the organisation must account for. If the organisation becomes aware that this young man has now demonstrated, in front of seven witnesses, that his conscience is inconveniently active—”

He left the sentence unfinished.

I felt, for the first time, something that had not quite been fear before that moment become fear properly.

“We need to find him,” I said.

“Yes,” Marcus said. “We do.”

Marcus did not go to the Vigiles. I asked why.

“The Vigiles will open a record,” he said. “They will ask the obvious questions. They will speak to the obvious witnesses. The obvious witnesses will say nothing, because they are alive and prefer to remain so. The record will conclude, in the manner of all such records, that a young man left of his own accord. This is not speculation. It is the procedure.” He paused. “The procedure is not designed for the truth. It is designed for the management of the truth. These are related but distinct functions.”

Instead, he spent two hours in conversation with three people whose connection I could not identify — a freedman who sold herbs near the Temple of Saturn, an elderly woman who sat every day on a specific step of the Basilica Aemilia and appeared to be doing nothing, and a boy of perhaps twelve who ran messages in the neighbourhood of the Forum and whose rates for information were, I gathered from Marcus’s subsequent expression, quite reasonable.

At the end of this process, Marcus sat on the steps of the temple with his eyes closed and his hands folded and the expression he wears when the thinking is happening. I waited. The sun moved.

“Caius was seen,” he said, without opening his eyes, “on the evening of the second day, in the company of a man known to the herb-seller — not by name but by description — as one who visits the colonnade regularly and who has the reputation of being a person one does not offend. He was not being dragged. He was walking alongside this man in the manner of a person who has been told to walk alongside and has understood the wisdom of complying.”

“He was taken.”

“He was escorted. The distinction is significant — it suggests the organisation prefers to manage him rather than remove him. Removal is final and creates inquiries. Management is reversible and creates leverage.” He opened his eyes. “Which means he is somewhere close. The management of a variable requires proximity.”

“Where?”

“The herb-seller mentioned something about a warehouse in the Velabrum that has changed hands three times in two years — a rate of change that is inconsistent with legitimate commerce in a stable market. Each transaction involved different men, no one of whom appears to have operated the warehouse, and the interior of which has never been seen by anyone in the neighbourhood.” He stood. “Persistent obscurity in a commercial space. The mark of it being useful for purposes that commercial spaces are not licensed to serve.”

He set off at the rapid walk that means the thinking has completed and the operation has begun.

“Marcus,” I said, following. “We are two philosophers. If this is what you believe it is—”

“Then we will need someone with the authority to enter it without negotiation.” He glanced at me sideways. “Do you know the Decurion Quintilius Varus?”

“He examined my uncle’s property dispute three years ago.”

“He owes me a professional courtesy.” The ghost of something that was not quite a smile crossed his face. “I have occasionally been useful to the Vigiles in the kind of case that does not appear in their records. This is one of the currencies of such usefulness — that one may draw upon it in moments of necessity.”

I will compress the events of the next hour, partly because they occurred quickly and partly because my notes from that period are disordered by circumstances that I prefer not to dwell upon.

Quintilius Varus was a compact man with the posture of someone who has been a soldier and the expression of someone who has been a bureaucrat and is not certain which he resents more. He listened to Marcus for perhaps four minutes, asked two questions, received two answers, and stood.

“I have no jurisdiction over a private warehouse without—”

“A complaint from a neighbouring property owner regarding the storage of goods contrary to the customs of the street, which I have obtained from the woman who dries fish three doors to the east and who has been attempting to file such a complaint for eight months without success.” Marcus produced a small tablet. “The complaint provides the entry. The entry provides the justification. The justification permits you to state in your record that the visit was conducted in accordance with procedure.”

Varus looked at the tablet. He looked at Marcus. He picked up his cloak.

“I dislike,” he said, “working with people who think faster than I do.”

“A reasonable position,” Marcus said. “I frequently dislike it myself.”

The warehouse was in the lower Velabrum, between a fuller’s shop and a street that smelled of the cattle market even at a distance. The door was locked with a padlock of the expensive type — the kind that signals not security, as cheap padlocks serve security equally well, but the signalling of security, which is a different statement.

Varus knocked in the official manner. No answer. He applied the official authority. The door opened inward.

The interior was dark — the lamps unlit, the small high windows admitting the particular shade of late afternoon light that is more useful for orienting oneself than for seeing anything clearly. Crates were stacked along the far wall. The smell was oil, dust, and the slightly sweet undercurrent of things stored in an enclosed space.

And, from somewhere to the left, breathing.

Marcus moved toward it without hesitation, which I found at the time either brave or unwise and have since concluded was neither — it was simply the product of having already solved the problem and therefore knowing what the answer was.

Behind a stack of crates, on a sleeping mat that had been provided with the minimum of consideration for human comfort, was Caius Primus.

He was unharmed. He was, in fact, eating a piece of bread with the slightly mechanical air of someone who has been told to eat and has complied without appetite. When he saw Marcus, something in his face changed — relief, and beneath the relief, shame, and beneath the shame, something that might have been, if he were older and knew the word for it, philosophy.

He had been brought here, he told us. Not harmed. Not threatened beyond the threat of presence. Simply — managed. A man had come to him after the examination. He had explained, in pleasant and specific terms, what Caius had been observed knowing, what the demonstration of his conscience in a public examination had confirmed, and what the consequences of that demonstration proceeding further might be.

Caius had been placed here to consider his options.

He had been considering them for three days.

We were in the street outside — Varus, Marcus, and I — with Caius sitting on a barrel in the fuller’s doorway, wrapped in a cloak someone had found for him. Varus had the expression of a man assembling his report.

“I can file a charge of unlawful detention,” he said. “Against—”

“Against whom?” Marcus asked.

Varus paused. The man who had escorted Caius to the warehouse — described but not named. The warehouse, owned by a succession of fictions. The arrangement, invisible in the specific way that such arrangements are designed to be invisible.

“You will file a charge,” Marcus said, “against a building. The charge will be investigated. The investigation will find the building empty. The building will, within a week, be empty. The charge will be closed. The men involved will be inconvenienced. And Caius—”

He looked at Caius.

“Caius will have given testimony against people whose names he does not know but whose interest in his silence is currently the only thing protecting him.”

Varus’s jaw worked.

“That,” he said finally, “is a problem.”

“It is a problem,” Marcus agreed, “that the procedure is not designed to solve.” He looked at Varus steadily. “File the record as abandoned property, discovered during inspection following a neighbour’s complaint. Record that the warehouse was empty. Record that the complaint was resolved.”

“That is—”

“True. The warehouse was empty of anything the record needs to contain.” He turned. “The matter of Caius Primus’s whereabouts, which was never formally reported to the Vigiles, requires no resolution in any official document, because it never officially existed.”

Varus was quiet for a long moment. He was, I think, a more complicated man than his posture suggested.

“And what,” he said, “about what he saw?”

“What he saw,” Marcus said, “is a philosophical problem. Caius.” He walked to where the young man sat. “What did you see, and what will you do with it?”

I will not record what Caius told us. I will say that it was significant. I will say that Marcus listened to all of it with the attention he gives to problems that are not yet solved, and that when Caius had finished, Marcus sat beside him on the barrel — there was not quite room for two men, but Marcus is not a large person — and spoke to him for perhaps ten minutes, quietly, in the manner not of a teacher but of something closer to a father.

The substance of what he said was this: that knowledge is not the same as obligation. That a young man who has seen something he was not meant to see is not, by virtue of the seeing, required to destroy himself in the service of a justice the apparatus of justice is not equipped to deliver. That the examined life does not demand the heroic gesture; it demands only that one be honest about what one is choosing and why.

“You are not required to be a martyr,” Marcus said. “You are required to be a man who has thought carefully about what he is doing and accepted the responsibility for the choice.”

“Then what should I choose?”

Marcus stood. “That,” he said, “is not a question I can answer. If it were answerable by someone else, philosophy would be unnecessary and we could all become merchants.”

He walked back to where I stood.

“He will choose correctly,” he said, quietly, so only I could hear. “He has already demonstrated that his conscience is inconveniently active. Men with inconveniently active consciences usually end by listening to them.”

We walked back through the Velabrum as the sun descended behind the Janiculum and the particular smell of Rome in the evening — charcoal and bread and the thousand lamps beginning their work — rose around us. Varus had returned to his station. Caius had been returned, under quiet escort arranged by Marcus through means I did not fully understand, to his mother’s house.

“How did you know,” I asked, “that they would keep him alive?”

“I reasoned about their interests.” Marcus walked with the easy pace of a man whose problem is resolved. “The organisation you encountered is not violent in the way that popular imagination requires organisations of its kind to be violent. Violence is expensive — it attracts attention, requires disposal, creates gaps in the record that must be filled. Silence is cheaper. The management of variables is cheaper still. They preferred Caius frightened and compliant to Caius dead and investigated.”

“That is a cold way to account for someone’s survival.”

“Yes.” He did not sound troubled by this. “It is accurate, which is more useful than warm.”

We walked in silence for a time.

“And the man at the money-changer’s table,” I said. “The organisation.”

“Remains,” Marcus said. “I have disrupted a small part of one arrangement for one morning. The arrangement predates me and will outlast me. I am not deceived about the scope of what I have accomplished.” He glanced at me. “I have found one student, restored him to his mother, and provided him with the philosophical framework to make a responsible decision about what he knows. This is what I am capable of doing. It is enough for today.”

He said it without bitterness. That was the thing about Marcus Holcus Aurelius — he had made peace with the limits of the possible in a way that did not diminish his commitment to the necessary. He did not require the universe to be solvable in order to bother solving the problem in front of him.

“And tomorrow?” I asked.

“Tomorrow,” he said, “we have an examination. There is a student — you would not know him, he enrolled last month — who holds a rather comfortable position on the relationship between wealth and virtue that I have been looking forward to examining.” The ghost-smile appeared again. “I expect he will be significantly less comfortable with it by the time we have finished.”

He turned left at the Arch of Janus, toward the hills, and I followed, as I always follow, trying to write quickly enough in my head to preserve what I was witnessing for a record that Marcus himself would call unnecessary but that I cannot help but feel compelled to keep.

For what it is worth: I believe he is right that the procedure cannot solve what it is not designed to solve. I believe he is right that the choice belongs to the man who must make it. I believe he is right that the limits of the possible are not an argument against the possible.

I am less certain that he is right that the account is unnecessary.

He would say that my uncertainty is the beginning of an argument. He would be pleased by it.

I remain, in this as in most things, his student.

— G.D.
