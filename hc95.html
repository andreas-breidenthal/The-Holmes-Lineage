RAGNARR HOLMR Ⅱ

~900 AD — Yorkshire, England

He grew up in the years when the Danelaw was at its most confident and most vulnerable simultaneously — a Norse dominion that had sunk deep roots into the English north while remaining perpetually aware that the kingdoms of the south had not accepted its existence and would, when strong enough, contest it. Ragnarr Holmr Ⅱ understood this dynamic with the clarity of someone who had been reading it since childhood, watching the growing power of the southern English kingdoms under Edward the Elder and his son Æthelstan with the attention of a man who knows that the outcome of a distant conflict will eventually determine the terms of his own life.

He managed the family’s land with his father’s methodical precision and added to it a political alertness — a habit of maintaining connections across the boundary between the Danish and English communities, cultivating relationships on both sides of a line that he suspected, correctly, would not always be where it currently stood. He was neither warrior nor priest but something the age sometimes needed more urgently than either: a man who kept accounts, remembered testimony, and could be relied upon to hold a confidence without using it. When the English kings began extending their authority northward in the early tenth century, the Holmr family was positioned to accommodate the change without catastrophe.

Among those who kept his records was a clerk named Eadric, a man trained in the writing of charters and accustomed to the disciplined quiet of ink, parchment, and oath. Eadric did not claim to understand Ragnarr, but he understood that what he observed had weight, and so he preserved what he could. What follows is taken from a portion of his surviving notes, which came into my hands through a chain of custody I need not here recount in full, though it is enough to say the manuscript was folded into a trade ledger in Whitby, where it might easily have been overlooked had its margins not borne marks of careful correction.

A portion of the notes of Eadric

Set down in the time of Ragnarr Holmr, called the Younger, in the years when the Danelaw yet held the north, though not, as some were beginning to understand, for very much longer.

The fog that comes off the moors in autumn is not the fog of other places. I have written charters in York, and I have sat in the scriptorium at Whitby and watched sea-mist move through the harbour like something with purpose, but neither of those prepared me for what rises from the Danelaw flatlands in the weeks before the great cold sets in. It does not drift. It settles, the way debts settle — gradually, without announcement, until you look up from your work and find that the world has contracted to the length of your arm. The smell of it is particular: peat and standing water and something older, something the land exhales from deeper than any plough has ever reached.

It was in such a fog, in the fourth year of King Edmund's reign, that a man named Ulfketill Brandsson was found dead beside the boundary stone that marked the eastern edge of his own land.

I heard the news as Ragnarr Holmr heard most news of consequence — at the morning meal, from a man who had ridden through the night and arrived with mud on his boots and an expression that suggested he would not soon recover from what he had seen. The messenger was Ulfketill’s own steward, a nervous Englishman named Godwin who served the Danish household with the particular anxious diligence of a man who has made himself useful across a divide he does not fully trust. He stood in the doorway of Ragnarr’s hall while the fire-smoke tried to find its way out past him, and he said that his master was dead, and that the shire-reeve was already at the estate, and that he did not know what would happen to any of them.

Ragnarr Holmr set down his bread.

He was at this time perhaps thirty-five years of age, lean in the way of men who forget to eat rather than men who cannot afford to, with that quality his face sometimes wore in the early morning of utter blankness — as though he had not yet decided what expression the day required of him. His eyes, which were the colour of water over grey stone, moved to me briefly, then returned to Godwin.

“Which stone,” he said.

“My lord?”

“There are four boundary stones on the Brandsson estate. Which one?”

Godwin blinked. “The eastern one, my lord. The one that stands between Ulfketill’s land and the holding of Wulfric Alderman.”

Something changed in Ragnarr’s expression — not alarm, not interest exactly, but the particular quality of attention that I had come to recognise over my years in his service as the sign that information had arrived which connected to other information already stored. He was quiet for a moment. Then he rose from the table.

“Come, Eadric,” he said. “Bring your materials.”

The ride took us the better part of two hours, through flat land punctuated by stands of oak and the occasional village, the fog thinning as we moved east but never quite burning off. I rode behind Ragnarr and Godwin, who proved incapable of silence and spent the journey telling us everything he knew about the discovery of the body, the probable culprit, the mood of the household, and his own fears for his position. Ragnarr listened to this without appearing to listen to it, which I recognised as one of his more useful qualities.

“The son was seen near the boundary yesterday evening,” Godwin offered, as we came in sight of the estate’s outer fence. “Brandr Ulfketillsson. Several of the farmhands saw him cross the east field after supper. He denies it now, but—”

“He denies it,” Ragnarr said, and his tone was not a question.

“Loudly, my lord.”

“Innocent men are sometimes quiet,” Ragnarr said. “Guilty men are often loud. But loud men are not always guilty. Remember that, Godwin. It saves considerable injustice.”

The shire-reeve was a man named Æthelmund, and he was, in the way of shire-reeves throughout all the kingdoms of men, professionally certain of things he had not yet demonstrated. He was built like a man who had once been a soldier and had since become accustomed to being obeyed, and he stood beside the boundary stone with his arms crossed and his deputies ranged behind him and his conclusion already visible in his posture.

“Holmr,” he said, when Ragnarr dismounted. He said it in the tone of a man greeting someone he needs but would prefer not to need.

“Æthelmund.” Ragnarr did not look at him. He was already looking at the boundary stone, and at the ground around it, and at the body, which had not yet been moved. “Tell me what you have.”

“Ulfketill Brandsson, landowner, found this morning by a farmhand going to check the eastern drainage ditch. Dead since last night, most likely. Struck from behind — you can see the wound. The son was seen in this field after supper. The son benefits from the father’s death.”

“What benefit, specifically?”

Æthelmund looked at him with the expression of a man who has just been asked to explain why the sun rises. “The land. He inherits.”

“Does he.”

Something in the way Ragnarr said it made Æthelmund go still. “He is the son and heir.”

“He is the son,” Ragnarr agreed, and walked toward the body.

I will not describe in detail what Ragnarr did over the next hour, partly because some of it I did not understand at the time, and partly because the act of watching him work was one of those experiences that defies clean transcription. He moved around the body and the stone with the methodical unhurriedness of a man reading a document he has been given plenty of time to study. He examined the wound. He examined the ground. He examined, with particular attention, the base of the boundary stone itself.

He asked questions. He asked about charters, disputes, and court rolls. He asked about the York clerk. Then he turned toward me, and I understood I was to go to York.

Four days later, he gathered the charter, the court rolls, and the York instrument at the hearth, and read them in silence until the pattern resolved itself.

“You have understood it,” I said.

“Sit down, Eadric,” he said. “I shall explain it to you, and you shall tell me where I have made an error.”

He explained everything. The inheritance. The transfer. The timing. The motive. The unreliability of witnesses in fog. The structure of the land agreement between families. And finally the conclusion: Brandr had killed his father, but the legal and social consequences required shaping rather than simple accusation.

“Justice,” he said, “is a word that covers several different activities.”

On the fourth day he brought all parties into his hall.

He stated the charter would be honoured. The land would pass. The accusation would not be formally tried. Instead, Brandr would be sent north into service under a lord who did not require explanations of past deeds.

It was not presented as punishment. It was presented as outcome.

Brandr accepted it.

Afterward, Æthelmund rode with us for a time.

“You are certain,” he said.

“Completely,” Ragnarr said.

“And you believe this is just.”

“I believe it is the best available outcome,” Ragnarr said. “Justice is one part of what courts do. Not the only part.”

I asked him later why he had taken four days.

“Because suspicion is not knowledge,” he said. “And because a man should be confronted with the full shape of what is known.”

He added that documentation mattered. Not only for the present, but for the future — for when memory would no longer be enough.

I wrote everything.

And I kept writing.
