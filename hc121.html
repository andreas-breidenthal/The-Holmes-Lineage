SIR SELWYN HOLMES

~1715 — Yorkshire / London, England

Sir Selwyn Holmes inherited from his father Sir Richmond a name of distinction, a house at Hutton le Moors in a state of considerable disrepair, and debts that would have crushed a more prudent man entirely. He was not a prudent man. Reputed to frequent the company of Sir Francis Dashwood, he pursued pleasure with the same systematic energy that his ancestors had brought to the law, to commerce, and to the close observation of the world — the gift, in his case, bent entirely toward the identification of the next entertainment and the next creditor to be avoided.

He was not unintelligent; those who knew him noted that he could read a man’s weaknesses at a glance, and exploited that faculty without compunction in the card rooms of St James’s. He could identify, within minutes of sitting down at the hazard table, which of his companions was playing above his means, which was concealing a run of luck behind false dejection, and which was simply drunk. He used these assessments to extract money from all three, feeling that if providence had granted him the ability to read men it would be ingratitude not to profit from it.

There were, however, limits to his indifference. Carrow’s later annotations suggest that Selwyn was not without a certain internal resistance when confronted with a pattern that did not serve him. He would often dismiss such things, but dismissal was not the same as absence. When something persisted, he noticed it — and when he noticed it, he sometimes acted, though rarely with enthusiasm and never with any expectation of reward beyond the immediate correction of an inconvenience.

The account that follows is drawn from a portion of the same archive preserved by Samuel Carrow, whose hand becomes more uneven in these later pages. He writes with less confidence than in earlier generations, as though the subject itself resists easy ordering. Where the record falters, that faltering has been left visible, for it belongs to the case.

The Affair at Medmenham

Reconstructed from the Papers of Samuel Carrow, Gentleman's Secretary, Anno Domini 1751

A note in Carrow's hand, preceding the account:

I set this down with some reluctance, as Sir Selwyn has expressed his complete indifference as to whether the matter is recorded or forgotten. He said, when I raised the subject, that he could not imagine who would wish to read it. I told him I could not either, but that the alternative was to leave the evening unaccounted for in any form, and that such an evening, in my view, deserved an account. He considered this for a moment, then returned to his claret and said I might do as I liked. I have therefore done as I liked.

The torches along the river path at West Wycombe had been burning since dusk, and by the time our boat reached the bank below the Abbey at Medmenham the hour was late enough that the frogs had resumed their conversation and the torchlights were reflected in the Thames as broken columns of amber, shivering with the current. The smell of the place was particular to it: river mud and old stone and the specific quality of cheap tallow that Sir Francis's people used in the outer approaches, as distinct from the beeswax that burned within — a domestic economy that Sir Selwyn had observed and remarked upon during our first visit, though he had found it unremarkable in any moral sense.

"The servants use tallow," he had said then, with the same tone one might use to note that the weather was variable. "It tells you a great deal about the Host's priorities. He wishes the impression of expense, not its substance. The impression is for the guests; the substance is for himself."

On the evening in question, the company was perhaps thirty in number, and had been drinking since mid-afternoon by my calculation, which placed a portion of them in that particular region of inebriation that lies between the merely voluble and the genuinely dangerous. Cards had been established in the inner room — not the ceremonial chambers, which Sir Francis reserved for his more theatrical entertainments, but a panelled anteroom off the main hall, lit by a half-dozen candlesticks arranged on a central table and smelling of tobacco smoke and the stale sweetness of spilled wine on an uncleaned floor.

Sir Selwyn had positioned himself, as was his custom, not at the table but beside the fireplace. He had expressed, when I had enquired, no interest in playing that evening.

"The company is not sufficiently interesting," he said, examining the stem of his wine glass with the attention a physician might give a specimen. "Three of them are too drunk to be competently dishonest. One of them is too sober to be entertaining. And young Alleyne will lose everything he has brought before midnight, and some of what he has not, and will look bewildered throughout, which is the expression of a man who believes cards to be, at some fundamental level, a fair transaction."

Young Mr. Alleyne was perhaps twenty-two, fair-haired, with the complexion of a man who had spent the previous month in the country and had not yet adapted to London air. He was playing hazard with an intensity that was entirely sincere — the intensity of a man who believes that this throw will be the one that corrects the arithmetic of the preceding throws, an arithmetic that does not work in the manner he believes it does. Sir Selwyn watched him with the detached attention of a naturalist observing a familiar genus.

The cry came at half past ten.

It came from Viscount Harfield — a broad, red-faced man of fifty whose coat had cost more than Carrow's annual wage and whose capacity for drink was matched only by his capacity for grievance — and it came in the form of a single word shouted with enough force to extinguish the nearest candle.

"Cheat."

The room froze in the manner that rooms do when the word is spoken in earnest: a sudden stillness, like water that has been struck, before the surface reforms and the noise begins.

Harfield was on his feet, his chair flung back behind him, one finger extended across the table at young Alleyne. Alleyne himself sat with the expression of a man who has been struck without understanding the direction of the blow — colour rising, mouth slightly open, cards still held in his hand with the loose grip of a person who has not yet processed the necessity of putting them down.

"This man," said Harfield, with the formal precision of someone who has rehearsed the sentence while building toward it, "has been taking money from this table by means I will not tolerate."

The subsequent noise was considerable. I will not reproduce it in full, as it was largely repetitive and added nothing to the facts of the matter.

Sir Selwyn had not moved from the fireplace.

He stood with one hand resting on the mantel, his wine glass still in the other, and wore the expression he wore when something that had been mildly engaging became definitively tedious. He watched Alleyne attempt to produce a third objection, watched it dissolve like the first two, and then set his glass down on the mantel with a precision that was, in retrospect, the only warning he gave.

He crossed the room in perhaps four steps.

"Harfield," he said.

"Harfield," he repeated, now that he had sufficient quiet for the purpose, "you are accusing this young man of cheating."

"I am," said Harfield.

"On what grounds?"

"He has taken forty pounds from me in the last hour."

"That is a consequence," said Sir Selwyn, "not a ground."

He examined the table, the candlesticks, the glasses, the arrangement of the cards, and the position of the chairs, and then looked, lastly, at young Alleyne.

"Mr. Alleyne," said Sir Selwyn, "what is the device by which you are alleged to have introduced these cards?"

"I don't know what they mean."

"No," said Sir Selwyn. "You do not. Because you have not done it."

He turned back to the room.

"The cards on this table suggest that someone has been consistently holding combinations that are improbable under honest dealing and consistent under systematic management."

He oriented himself toward Mr. D—.

"The advantage of accusing another man immediately is that it establishes you as an aggrieved party rather than a suspected one."

Mr. D— said nothing.

Sir Selwyn retrieved his wine glass.

Twenty minutes later he stood by the river, the Thames moving darkly below.

"Will Harfield pursue it?" I asked.

"Against D—? No."

"And Alleyne?"

"Will lose his money by perfectly honest means within the year."

"You intervened," I said.

"I intervened because it was wrong," he said. "Specifically, demonstrably, geometrically wrong."

"Besides," he added, "D— was not very good."

I remained by the river as the sound of the house resumed behind me.

The official account of the evening records only that a dispute arose at cards and was settled to the satisfaction of the parties. This version is substantially incomplete.

— S. Carrow, Secretary, recorded at Hutton le Moors, September 1751
