SIR SELWYN HOLMES

~1715 — London, England

Sir Selwyn Holmes inherited from his father Sir Richmond a name of distinction, a house at Hutton le Moors in a state of considerable disrepair, and debts that would have crushed a more prudent man entirely. He was not a prudent man. Reputed to frequent the company of Sir Francis Dashwood, he pursued pleasure with the same systematic energy that his ancestors had brought to the law, to commerce, and to the close observation of the world — the gift, in his case, bent entirely toward the identification of the next entertainment and the next creditor to be avoided.

He was not unintelligent; those who knew him noted that he could read a man’s weaknesses at a glance, and exploited that faculty without compunction in the card rooms of St James’s. He could identify, within minutes of sitting down at the hazard table, which of his companions was playing above his means, which was concealing a run of luck behind false dejection, and which was simply drunk. He used these assessments to extract money from all three, feeling that if providence had granted him the ability to read men it would be ingratitude not to profit from it.

There were, however, limits to his indifference. Carrow’s later annotations suggest that Selwyn was not without a certain internal resistance when confronted with a pattern that did not serve him. He would often dismiss such things, but dismissal was not the same as absence. When something persisted, he noticed it — and when he noticed it, he sometimes acted, though rarely with enthusiasm and never with any expectation of reward beyond the immediate correction of an inconvenience.

The account that follows is drawn from a portion of the same archive preserved by Samuel Carrow, whose hand becomes more uneven in these later pages. He writes with less confidence than in earlier generations, as though the subject itself resists easy ordering. Where the record falters, that faltering has been left visible, for it belongs to the case.

The Affair at Medmenham

Reconstructed from the Papers of Samuel Carrow, Gentleman's Secretary, Anno Domini 1751

[A note in Carrow's hand, preceding the account:] I set this down with some reluctance, as Sir Selwyn has expressed his complete indifference as to whether the matter is recorded or forgotten. He said, when I raised the subject, that he could not imagine who would wish to read it. I told him I could not either, but that the alternative was to leave the evening unaccounted for in any form, and that such an evening, in my view, deserved an account. He considered this for a moment, then returned to his claret and said I might do as I liked. I have therefore done as I liked. What follows covers not one evening but a fortnight, of which the evening at Medmenham was the last and least explicable part.

The matter began on the twelfth of September, when a letter arrived at our lodgings in Pall Mall that Sir Selwyn read once, set aside without comment, and then retrieved twenty minutes later and read again — a thing I had not previously seen him do.

I did not ask what it contained. In three years as his secretary I had learned that questions addressed to Sir Selwyn before he had reached his own conclusions on a matter were answered with either silence or misdirection, and I could not always distinguish between the two.

He told me of his own accord, that evening, when he had finished with it.

"A mutual acquaintance has written to warn me," he said, "that a young gentleman of his circle is being systematically destroyed."

"Destroyed how?"

"Financially. Socially. By degrees, and with patience." He held the letter toward the candle, not to read it but to examine the paper against the light. "The acquaintance does not know the mechanism. He knows only the trajectory — debts accumulating, notes called in at inconvenient moments, a promising connexion broken off last spring under circumstances that were never quite explained. He suspects design."

"And you?"

"I suspect that a man who suspects design is usually correct about the existence of design, and usually wrong about its nature." He set the letter down. "The young man's name is Alleyne. He will be at Medmenham on the twentieth. We have been invited."

I had understood us to have declined the invitation.

"I have amended that understanding," said Sir Selwyn.

I will not dwell at length on what Sir Selwyn established in the eight days between that conversation and the twentieth of September, partly because he did not explain his methods to me at the time, and partly because the account I eventually extracted from him was more alarming than the event itself. He made enquiries — through channels I could not identify and did not press him on — into the recent history of Mr. Edmund Alleyne: his estate in Shropshire, his debts (more numerous than they should have been for a young man of twenty-two, and distributed across three moneylenders of different counties in a pattern Sir Selwyn described as deliberate dispersal), and the particular circumstances of the broken engagement, which had been conducted with his mortgagee's knowledge in a manner that struck Sir Selwyn as not coincidental.

"Someone wants the Shropshire property," he said, on the night before we departed for West Wycombe. "The method is sufficiently patient to suggest a professional arrangement. There is a hired instrument somewhere in this. I do not yet know its shape."

He said nothing else on the matter until we were on the water.

The torches along the river path at West Wycombe had been burning since dusk, and by the time our boat reached the bank below the Abbey at Medmenham the hour was late enough that the frogs had resumed their conversation. The torchlights were reflected in the Thames as broken columns of amber, shivering with the current. The smell of the place was particular to it: river mud and old stone and the specific quality of cheap tallow that Sir Francis Dashwood's people used in the outer approaches, as distinct from the beeswax that burned within.

"The servants use tallow," Sir Selwyn had remarked, on our first visit, two years before. "It tells you a great deal about the Host's priorities. He wishes the impression of expense, not its substance. The impression is for the guests; the substance is for himself."

Tonight he said nothing as we came ashore. He was watching the guests assembling on the lawn, and his attention had the quality of a man completing a catalogue.

The company was perhaps thirty in number, and had been drinking since mid-afternoon by my calculation, which placed a portion of them in that particular region of inebriation that lies between the merely voluble and the genuinely dangerous. Sir Francis himself moved among them with the theatrical bonhomie he deployed at such occasions — that carefully maintained performance of excess which was, I had come to understand, the entire point of Medmenham. The Abbey was a machine for producing the appearance of transgression. What occurred inside it, on any given evening, was rather less interesting than it was rumoured to be.

It occurred to me, watching Sir Francis greet a lord of the admiralty with an obscene gesture that both men found enormously amusing, that this was perhaps why someone had chosen it for what Sir Selwyn suspected was happening tonight. In a house constructed around the principle that nothing is quite what it appears, one more masked reality attracts no notice whatsoever.

I mentioned this thought to Sir Selwyn later. He said I was slower than he had expected, but not as slow as he had feared.

Cards had been established in the inner room — not the ceremonial chambers, which Sir Francis reserved for his more theatrical entertainments, but a panelled anteroom off the main hall. It was lit by a half-dozen candlesticks arranged on a central table and smelled of heavy Virginia tobacco and the stale sweetness of spilled wine.

Sir Selwyn had positioned himself, as was his custom, not at the table but beside the fireplace. He had expressed, when I enquired, no interest in playing that evening.

"The company is not sufficiently interesting," he said, examining the stem of his wine glass with the sharp attention a physician might give a curious specimen. "Three of them are too drunk to be competently dishonest. One of them is too sober to be entertaining."

He paused.

"And I need to watch."

Young Mr. Alleyne was perhaps twenty-two, fair-haired, with the open complexion of a man who had spent the previous month in the country. He was playing Macao with an intensity that was entirely sincere — the intensity of a man who believes that this hand will be the one that corrects the arithmetic of the preceding hands, an arithmetic that does not work in the manner he believes it does.

To his left sat Viscount Harfield, a broad, red-faced man of fifty whose coat had cost more than my annual wage.

Opposite them — and directly across the baize from young Alleyne, with the nearest candlestick burning at his left elbow — sat a quiet, precise gentleman in a plum-coloured waistcoat, known to me only as Mr. D—, who played with the silent regularity of a clockwork automaton. He had placed a silver snuff-box on the baize beside his right hand, as a man who likes his appointments arranged about him. It was a handsome piece, lid uppermost, catching the candlelight.

Once, in the second half-hour, I watched D— set down a hand that had seemed to me — in my limited understanding of Macao — a reasonable one. He folded it without expression and pushed back his stake. I took this for the discipline of an experienced player, conserving his capital against a run of Harfield's luck. I did not think about it again until much later.

For the first hour, Sir Selwyn said nothing. He watched with the detached attention of a naturalist observing a familiar, if slightly tedious, genus of insect.

I noticed, at some point around half past nine, that his attention had shifted — not to the table as a whole, but to Alleyne specifically. He watched the young man handle his cards, watched him hesitate before raising his stake on a hand that proved, when played, to be a strong one. Sir Selwyn's expression was one I had not seen before: not the cool amusement of a man observing folly, but something sharper. A provisional conclusion, not yet committed to.

I thought, watching him watch Alleyne: he thinks it is the boy.

I was wrong about what he thought. But I was, I learned later, not entirely wrong about the direction of his attention.

The cry came at half past ten.

It came from Viscount Harfield, and it came in the form of a single word shouted with enough force to extinguish the nearest candle.

"Cheat!"

The room froze in the manner that rooms do when the word is spoken in earnest: a sudden stillness, like water that has been struck, before the surface reforms and the noise begins.

Harfield was on his feet, his chair flung back behind him, one thick, ringed finger extended across the baize at young Alleyne. Alleyne himself sat with the expression of a man who has been struck without understanding the direction of the blow — colour rising, mouth slightly open, his cards still held in his hand with the loose grip of a person who has not yet processed the necessity of putting them down.

"This man," said Harfield, with the formal precision of someone who has rehearsed the sentence while building toward it, "has been taking money from this table by means I will not tolerate. I have watched his stakes!"

The subsequent noise was considerable. I will not reproduce it in full, as it was largely repetitive and added nothing to the facts of the matter. Alleyne stammered a furious denial; Harfield reached for the hilt of a sword he was not, thankfully, wearing.

Sir Selwyn had not moved from the fireplace.

He stood with one hand resting on the mantel, his wine glass still in the other. He watched Alleyne attempt to produce a third objection, watched it dissolve like the first two, and then — and this is the moment I have most clearly fixed in my memory — he watched Mr. D— for approximately four seconds with an expression of sudden, complete illumination. As a man who has been working a cipher in his head, and has just found the key.

He set his glass down on the mantel with a sharp clack that was, in retrospect, the only warning he gave.

He crossed the room in four long strides.

"Harfield," he said.

"Harfield," he repeated, his voice cutting through the din with a cool, resonant authority. "You are accusing this young man of cheating."

"I am!" barked the Viscount, turning his flushed face toward Sir Selwyn.

"On what grounds?"

"He has taken forty pounds from me in the last hour, and he raised his stake significantly before the last card was even dealt!"

"That is a consequence, Harfield, and a suspicion," said Sir Selwyn mildly. "It is not a ground."

Sir Selwyn approached the table. He did not look at the faces of the men. Instead, his grey eyes swept over the baize, taking in the arrangement of the cards, the spilled ash, the position of the candlesticks, and the array of coins. He was, I understood only later, confirming what he had already worked out while standing at the fireplace. At the time I assumed his attention was fixed on Alleyne's hands, which were visible and agitated. It did not occur to me that Alleyne, from where Sir Selwyn stood, was simply the foreground — and that D—, sitting directly beyond him, was the object of the view.

"To cheat at Macao," he addressed the room at large, slipping his hands into his pockets, "requires either an accomplice, a mechanical apparatus, or a marked deck. Let us examine the accused. Mr. Alleyne's coat is tailored too tightly at the cuffs to conceal a sleeve-hold. His hands are soft, lacking the callouses necessary for the bottom-deal. Furthermore, his gaze has been fixed entirely upon his own cards, or upon the ceiling in prayer, every time the shoe is passed."

"He knew my cards!" Harfield roared.

"Someone knew your cards, yes," Sir Selwyn corrected, his eyes locking suddenly onto the quiet man in the plum-coloured waistcoat. "Mr. D—, I wonder if you might oblige me by moving your left hand?"

Mr. D— looked up. Whatever he felt, his face showed nothing beyond a mild and patient surprise — the expression of a man interrupted in a perfectly reasonable activity by someone with a grievance he does not quite follow.

"Sir Selwyn." His voice was measured, even warm. "I have sat here quietly for three hours, lost eleven pounds to Harfield's luck, and made no complaint. If there is an accusation to be levelled at this table, I confess I find it difficult to identify myself as its natural recipient." He spread his right hand, palm upward, in a gesture of easy openness, and looked around the room with something close to sympathy. "I suspect the evening has been long, and the claret generous. Perhaps we might allow Harfield and young Alleyne to resolve this between themselves."

Two men at the edge of the room murmured assent. Harfield himself, I noticed, had lost some of his certainty — the outrage cooling now into the slightly embarrassed recalculation of a man who has committed publicly to a position and is beginning to wonder whether it will hold.

Sir Selwyn did not move.

He stood with his hands in his pockets, looking at D— with the still, unhurried attention of a man reading a document he has already understood but is confirming once more for form's sake. The silence extended long enough to become its own kind of statement.

"That," he said at last, "was very smoothly done."

D— held the pleasant expression. "I do not follow you."

"The open palm. The reference to your own losses. The appeal to the room's fatigue rather than to the facts of the matter." Sir Selwyn's tone remained conversational. "You have been composing that response since the moment Harfield shouted, which suggests you anticipated the accusation, which suggests you had a reason to anticipate it. An innocent man, Mr. D—, composes his denial. A guilty one composes his audience." He took his hands from his pockets. "The advantage of accusing another man, or allowing him to be accused, is that it establishes you as an aggrieved party rather than a suspected one. You allowed Harfield's outburst to develop for nearly a minute before making any move to calm it — not because you lacked the influence to do so, but because you required the room's attention fixed on Alleyne. You have made, however, one capital mistake."

Before D— could recalibrate, Sir Selwyn's hand shot out and gripped the man's left wrist, lifting it from the table.

Concealed beneath the shadow of Mr. D—'s lace cuff, resting upon the green baize, was a heavy silver snuff-box.

"A beautiful piece of craftsmanship," Sir Selwyn murmured, turning the box with his free hand. "Notice, Harfield, the polished silver lid. Notice, too, its exact placement. It sits at a precise forty-five-degree angle to the dealing shoe."

The room was utterly silent. Young Alleyne leaned forward, blinking.

"The laws of optics are absolute," Sir Selwyn continued, his voice ringing with sudden energy. "The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. A man sitting where Alleyne sits could see nothing in this polished lid but the reflection of your rather pronounced chin, Harfield. But a man sitting where Mr. D— sits commands a perfect, mirror-clear view of the under-surface of every card drawn from the pack."

Mr. D— went very pale. He wrenched his arm from Sir Selwyn's grip.

"You will find," Sir Selwyn said, turning his back on the man with complete disdain, "that Mr. D— has been folding his own hands whenever Harfield held a natural nine, yet raising his stakes to push the betting whenever he saw that Alleyne was about to draw a winning card. He used Alleyne's ignorant luck as a shield — folding his own strong hands whenever Harfield held a natural, raising his stakes only when he could see that Alleyne was about to win, so that the boy's good fortune drew the table's suspicion rather than his own manoeuvring. This evening's losses are Harfield's and Alleyne's together: the first taken directly, the second to be collected when the young man's remaining capital has been sufficiently reduced to require settlement.

Alleyne said: "I do not follow you, sir."

"No," said Sir Selwyn. "You would not. You were not meant to."

What followed I am not in a position to record in detail, as events moved quickly and I was not, in all honesty, looking in the right direction at the decisive moment. D— was removed from the Abbey by Sir Francis's people. Harfield recovered some portion of what he had lost. Alleyne sat at the empty table for some time after, looking at his cards, as a man who has been saved from drowning and is still uncertain from what direction the water came.

Twenty minutes later, Sir Selwyn stood by the river, his coat wrapped tightly against the chill, the Thames moving darkly below. He had lit a cheroot and was watching the Abbey's lights reflected in the water with the expression of a man whose thoughts are running considerably further ahead than the scene in front of him.

"Will Harfield pursue it?" I asked. My heart was still unsettled from the confrontation.

"Against D—? No," Sir Selwyn replied. "Harfield has his money back. The aristocracy abhor a scandal that requires them to testify in court."

"And Alleyne?"

"Alleyne is safe from this particular instrument." He drew on the cheroot. "D— was not working alone."

I waited.

"There is a principal," he said. "Someone who identified Alleyne's estate as a target, established his debts across multiple lenders in a pattern designed to prevent any one of them from seeing the whole picture, arranged the broken engagement — which cost him the one connexion that might have stabilised his finances — and finally sent D— to bleed him dry at cards, because a young man who loses his remaining capital to honest bad luck is merely unfortunate, whereas one who loses it at Medmenham to a card-sharp is additionally ruined." He paused. "The property in Shropshire is worth roughly six thousand pounds. This has been a six-thousand-pound operation."

"And D— will not identify the principal?"

"D— will be out of the country within a fortnight. He is a professional. He knows how these arrangements work." Sir Selwyn looked at the river. "The principal remains unknown to me."

"Then Alleyne is not safe."

"Alleyne is safer than he was. The immediate mechanism has been disrupted. The design will require adaptation." He tossed the stub of his cheroot into the black water of the Thames, where it extinguished with a sharp hiss. "I have until it does."

I turned back toward the lights of the Abbey, from which the sound of renewed celebration had resumed — Sir Francis's gatherings being constitutionally resistant to interruption.

"You said earlier," I ventured, "that I was slower than expected. About the choice of location."

"I said not as slow as feared. There is a distinction."

"Even so. You had reached it before I had."

"I reached it on the boat coming in," he said. "This house is a monument to the proposition that surfaces conceal their opposites. Dashwood has built, at considerable expense, a machine for making the respectable appear transgressive and the transgressive appear like a jest. D— chose it for the same reason a thief chooses a crowded market. In a room full of performances, one more performance is invisible." He was quiet for a moment. "D— is a competent technician. The snuff-box is a competent device. The man who employed him is neither a technician nor interested in competence. He is interested in outcomes."

He said nothing else. I remained beside him at the water for some time, the noise of the house at our backs, the Thames dark and cold and entirely indifferent to the evening's proceedings.

The official account of the evening, kept by the monks of Medmenham, records only that a dispute arose at cards and was settled to the satisfaction of the parties.

I could not, on reflection, argue with the record. For D—, and for Harfield, and in the immediate sense for Alleyne, the matter was settled.

It was not settled for Sir Selwyn. He was still working.
