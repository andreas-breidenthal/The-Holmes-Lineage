SIGURÐR HOLMR II

~930 AD — Yorkshire, England

Sigurðr Holmr II lived through the gradual erosion of the Danelaw — the slow reassertion of English royal authority that Edgar’s consolidation of a unified kingdom would complete in his lifetime, the Norse culture of the north receding year by year before the administrative and ecclesiastical machinery of a unified English kingdom. He adapted, as his family had always adapted, by making himself useful to whoever was consolidating power at any given moment.

His particular contribution was a facility with the documentary record — the land charters, the court rolls, the ecclesiastical records that the English administration used to govern in ways the Norse oral tradition had not required. He had taught himself to read Latin in middle age, at a point when most men of his background considered such learning either unnecessary or vaguely suspicious, because he had identified it as the key to the kind of influence his family had always sought — the influence of the person who understands the document on which everything else depends. He served in the household of an English thegn as a steward and local assessor, reading documents on the thegn’s behalf with a thoroughness that the thegn, who could not read, found entirely satisfactory.

The one who preserved Sigurðr’s work was the same clerk who had once written for his father, Eadric, now older, his hand less certain but his eye no less attentive. Eadric’s records are fragmentary, his notes occasionally compressed to the point where context must be inferred, yet they retain the marks of a man who understood that what was recorded would outlive the men who spoke it. What follows is drawn from his surviving folios, where he marked not only what Sigurðr said, but the pauses between what was said and what was concluded.

The Surviving Folios of Eadric

Set down in the thirty-first year of his service, in the household of the thegn Wulfric of Eskelby, in that part of Yorkshire which men of the old tongue yet called Þórr’s Dale.

The autumn had turned the dale the colour of rust and old blood. I write this not as a man given to the poetries of weather, for I am a clerk and my business is the accurate word, but because the colour of that season felt, in the accounting of it afterward, as though the land itself had been trying to tell us something about what moved beneath its surface. The fogs came early that year, rising off the river before the evening meal was finished, and by the time the household lamps were lit the world beyond the hall’s timbers had ceased to exist. One heard the river. One heard the gate-hinge, which had wanted oil since midsummer and had not received it. One heard, occasionally, the particular silence of a man who is thinking.

That man was Sigurðr.

He had been in one of his barren seasons for the better part of a fortnight — seated by the fire after the hall had emptied, occasionally turning a piece of carved antler in his fingers without looking at it, occasionally not doing even that. I had learned, in the years of recording him, that these silences were not emptiness. They were, rather, what happened when the engine of the man had no sufficient problem to consume. He was not resting. He was being slowly eaten, and he found it disagreeable, and he would not say so, and the only remedy was work of sufficient difficulty.

The boundary dispute arrived on a Wednesday, in the form of Osric the steward and a rolled document.

Wulfric was not a man who enjoyed documents. He was a thegn of the old practical sort — he could assess the quality of a plough team at a glance and tell you within three whether a flock would survive a hard winter — but the written instruments of English administration struck him as a form of sorcery he had never sought to be initiated into. This is why he kept Sigurðr. This is, in the larger sense, why Sigurðr’s family had always survived the turning of one power to another: they made themselves the indispensable interpreter standing between the man of action and the document on which everything depended.

Osric laid the charter on the table with the careful reverence men reserve for things they wish you to believe are unquestionable. He was perhaps fifty, with the particular solidity of a man who has administered other men’s property for long enough that he has come to feel a kind of proprietary interest in it himself. He had served Wulfric’s father before Wulfric, which he mentioned with some frequency. He had, he explained, been entrusted with the safekeeping of certain documents relating to the eastern boundary of the estate, and had recently come into possession of evidence — a charter, properly witnessed, properly sealed — demonstrating that the boundary stone which Wulfric’s tenant Gunnar had been farming past for three seasons was not, in fact, on Wulfric’s land, but on land belonging to a man named Ceolmund, whose grandfather, the charter demonstrated, had been granted it by proper royal instrument in the time of the previous king.

Ceolmund was standing at the hall’s far end with the expression of a man who has been told to appear confident and is attempting it with incomplete success.

Wulfric looked at the charter. Then he looked at Sigurðr.

“Well,” he said. “Read it, then.”

Sigurðr had been still throughout Osric’s presentation in the manner of a man who appears to be listening to something other than the words being spoken. He rose now with the sudden energy that always startled those who had only seen him in his dormant state — the transition was too abrupt, as though a different man had stepped into the same body — and took the charter without ceremony, carrying it to where the lamp gave the best light.

I moved closer. This is, I think, the thing Sigurðr valued in me most: I moved closer without being asked, because I had learned that the observations he made in the first minutes of examination were the ones that mattered, and that he made them aloud when there was a reliable ear to receive them.

“Vellum is sound,” he said. “The ink has aged properly. The hand is competent — a trained church hand, not a private scriptorium. The seal is Ealdorman Æthelferth’s device, which is consistent with the period claimed.”

He fell silent for a moment.

“Read me the witness list,” he said to me.

I read. The witnesses were five in number: a priest of Jorvik named Aldhelm, a reeve named Godwine of the eastern wapentake, a landowner named Beornwulf, and two men identified by their offices rather than their names.

“Godwine of the eastern wapentake,” he said very quietly. “And Beornwulf. And Aldhelm the priest.”

He set the charter down with a gentleness that was not genuine gentleness, but the control of a man managing evidence.

“Osric,” he said. “How did this document come into your keeping?”

Osric’s answer was prepared. It spoke of safekeeping, of family trust, of long custodianship.

Sigurðr listened without interruption.

Then he asked how many years past the document had been received.

Six or seven, Osric said. Before or after Gunnar began farming past the stone, he could not recall precisely.

Sigurðr nodded as though this were sufficient.

He said nothing further until evening, when the hall had emptied and the fire had settled.

“You know what it is,” I said.

“I have known since the witness list,” he said. “I wanted certainty, because accusation without certainty destroys the accuser.”

He named the impossibility of the witnesses: men dead, absent, or elsewhere. A document constructed from presence that could not have existed.

“Osric presented it,” I said.

“Osric acquired it,” he replied. “The direction is not the essential question.”

“What is?”

“What Wulfric wishes to do about it.”

In the morning, the household was convened. Sigurðr laid out the impossibility of the charter without haste or drama. The ink, the vellum, the seal — all plausible. Then the witnesses. Then the dead and absent.

Ceolmund collapsed under the weight of it.

Osric attempted explanation. He spoke of errors, of memory, of administrative complexity. He did so professionally, as a final service to his position.

Then Sigurðr asked how much had been paid.

Silence answered.

Wulfric resolved the matter privately. Ceolmund was spared. Osric was dismissed, though not without provision.

The boundary stone remained.

Later I asked why the matter had not been taken to court.

Sigurðr said the court would have exposed method, would have complicated the record, and would have revealed the fragility of the documentary system itself.

“The document is destroyed,” he said. “Three men know what it contained. Two will forget.”

“That is not justice,” he added. “It is the closest approximation available under the conditions.”
