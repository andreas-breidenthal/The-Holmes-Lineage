QUINTUS HOLCUS

~260 BC — Rome, Roman Republic

The Matter of the Carthaginian's Design

Recorded by Lucius Aemilius, in the Year of the Consulship of Paullus and Varro

I

The day I understood that Rome could be destroyed not by swords but by a thought, Senator Fabianus came through the door of the lower archive at a speed I had never seen a senator move — toga disordered, colour wrong, one hand pressed flat against the doorframe as though the building required steadying.

"Is he here?" he said.

I had been in the service of Quintus Holcus for four months by then, long enough to know that he required no further specification. There was only one he in the lower archive near the Tiber — in the room that smelled of old papyrus and lamp-oil gone slightly rancid, pressed from olives that had already seen better seasons and knew it.

"He is at his wall," I said.

Fabianus crossed the threshold without another word.

II

I should set down, briefly, who we were — for the record, and for whoever reads it after us.

Quintus Holcus was lean, perhaps fifty, with a face that appeared carved rather than grown — prominent nose, jaw set slightly forward, eyes that conducted a census of everything you had ever done wrong. He was what the archive men called the Accountant, meaning not ledgers of money, but ledgers of everything else: troop dispositions, march-speeds, the precise interval between a commander's probing raids and his chosen engagements. He had the energy of a man half his age, arriving in sudden excess and spent all at once — there were days he did not leave the archive, and other days he walked to the Capitoline and back twice before the city had finished its breakfast, for reasons no one could name. What no one had warned me about, when I was assigned to his service, was that the most precise mind I had ever encountered was also the loneliest, and that it wore its loneliness the way certain soldiers wear old wounds — not displayed, but present in every movement.

I am Lucius Aemilius, a man of letters without a man of letters' usual vanity about it. I was assigned to Holcus by Fabianus himself on the grounds that someone ought to keep a proper record of the analyses submitted to the senatorial council, and that someone ought to be junior enough to have no political opinions of his own. I had served in that capacity for four months — long enough to have stopped being surprised by the clay discs and the strings, and to have started being surprised by the man himself. Which is, I think, the correct order in which to approach Quintus Holcus.

But that is enough context. The account begins with Fabianus at the door.

III

Holcus was at his wall when we entered — the wall of wooden pins hammered into the plaster, strung with cord, hung with small clay discs each marked with a number representing a day. Wax tablets were stacked by region. Strings connected them in a system that looked, to a newcomer, like the evidence of a very organised collapse. To me, after four months, it looked like a mind made visible.

He had his back to the door, which was his habit. He did not turn when Fabianus entered.

"You have come about the dispatch," he said.

Fabianus stopped. "How did you—"

"You are moving at a speed inconsistent with ordinary senatorial business, and you have not slept. The dispatch from the forward scouts in Apulia arrived two nights ago. Its contents contradicted my submitted analysis in a specific and, to the council, very convenient way. I expected you before this." He turned then, and his eyes went to Fabianus with the particular quality of attention that always made me feel as though I were watching a physician complete a diagnosis before the patient had finished describing his symptoms. "Your son is a military tribune with Varro's force."

The senator's face did something complicated. "You knew that."

"I know the troop dispositions. Sit down, Fabianus. Both of you, sit."

We sat. Holcus remained standing, which was also his habit — he thought better on his feet, and the rest of us thinking from chairs suited him.

"The dispatch," Fabianus said. "It places Hannibal's cavalry moving northwest, toward Campania. Not south toward the Aufidus. The council is reading it as evidence that your analysis is wrong — that he is not moving toward Cannae."

"Yes," Holcus said. "They would read it that way."

"But you do not."

"I do not read it as evidence at all. I read it as a document." He crossed to the central table, where a papyrus roll lay open, weighted at its corners by small stones. "Aemilius. Come here."

I came.

"You have handled documents for four months. Look at this dispatch. Tell me what you observe — not its content. Its physical character."

I looked. A military dispatch, written in the compressed hand common to field reports, bearing the seal of the forward intelligence network in Apulia. I looked more carefully. The ink at the top of the roll, where the seal met the written text, was darker than the ink lower on the page — not dramatically so, but perceptibly. The papyrus at the upper margin showed less wear at its crease points than the lower third, which had been handled many times.

"The upper portion was written later than the lower," I said. "Or written on different days. The ink has not aged uniformly. And the crease pattern suggests the bottom of this document was read repeatedly before the top portion existed."

Holcus looked at me with the expression of a man who has just confirmed a calculation.

"You see," he said to Fabianus. "The scribe earns his position." To me: "What does that tell you about the document's history?"

"That it was assembled. That someone added the upper section — the section describing the cavalry movement toward Campania — to a document whose lower section already existed."

"A fabrication?" Fabianus said.

"A construction," Holcus corrected. "The lower section may be entirely genuine — observations by genuine scouts. The upper section, describing the supposed northwestern movement, was added afterward. By whom and at what level of the intelligence chain, I cannot yet say. What I can say is that this document was designed to be found. It reached the council with unusual speed, through channels that ordinarily take five days. It arrived in two." He moved back to his wall of strings and discs. "Hannibal has been reading Roman intelligence networks for two years. He knows what a document describing his cavalry moving away from a position will do to a Roman council that is already uncomfortable with restraint. He has handed us a door marked exit, and the council has walked through it with relief."

"Then he is going to Cannae," Fabianus said.

"He was always going to Cannae. The dispatch is the most elegant evidence of this, because it is evidence that he knows we know — and that he is not concerned. A man who plants false intelligence in your network is a man who believes the false intelligence will work." He paused. "This is not generalship, Fabianus. It is the reading of minds."

Fabianus was quiet for a moment — a careful man who had just understood that the structure he built to protect himself was part of the problem.

"My son marches with Varro in three days," he said.

"Yes."

"What do I do?"

"You go back to the council. You present the document analysis. You present my analysis of the terrain at Cannae. You make the argument one more time." Holcus's voice was precise and without sentiment. "Go tonight. Do not wait for morning."

After Fabianus had gone, Holcus remained at his wall for a long time, touching one of the clay discs — the one marked for Cannae — without moving it. Just touching it, the way you touch a closed door whose other side you already know.

IV

The following morning, Holcus put on his outdoor sandals.

In four months of service, I had not once seen him leave the archive for any operational purpose. The archive men spoke of his Capitoline walks as legend — events from an earlier era of his career, before whatever had settled him into this room with his strings and discs. He rolled a small tablet into his satchel, then paused at the door and looked back at me.

"You are coming," he said. It was not a question.

"Where are we going?"

"First, to the records office at the Porta Capena. Then to the Caelian Hill."

The records office at the Porta Capena handled the transit registry for all official military dispatches entering Rome from the southern roads. It was a low-ceilinged room staffed by three clerks who wore the hunted expression of men perpetually behind on their own work, and it smelled of size paste and damp stone. Holcus moved through it with the particular authority of a man who knows exactly what he is looking for and finds the room's occupants temporarily irrelevant.

"The Apulian dispatch of two nights ago," he said to the nearest clerk. "I require the transit record."

"Dispatches of that classification—"

"Are registered in the Porta Capena log when they enter the city. I know this because I wrote the registry protocol six years ago." He set a stylus on the counter and waited.

The clerk produced the log. Holcus turned to the correct entry and stood very still for a moment.

"Aemilius."

I came and looked.

The entry for the Apulian dispatch showed the standard notation — date, hour, courier name, point of origin. But the courier name had been written over a previous entry, imperfectly erased: the ghost of another name still showed in the pressed papyrus fibres beneath the fresh ink. And the point of origin listed was the standard Apulian forwarding station — but the courier name, now that I looked at it with the attention Holcus had taught me to bring to documents, belonged to a man whose route assignment covered the Latin Way, not the Apulian road at all.

"The courier who delivered this dispatch," I said, "does not carry the Apulian route."

"No," Holcus said quietly. "He carries the route from the Alban Hills. Which connects, by the hill road, to the staging post at Velitrae." He traced the name with one finger. "Gaius Turpio. I want to know where Gaius Turpio is at this moment."

The clerk, who had been listening with the expression of a man watching a storm develop on a clear horizon, went pale. "I wouldn't know, sir. Couriers return to the relay stations—"

"The relay station for the Latin Way route is the stable-yard behind the Temple of Castor," Holcus said. "Come, Aemilius."

We were in the street before the clerk had recovered himself. At the Temple of Castor's stable-yard, we found the relay station — a long stone building that smelled of horse and sawdust, where couriers rested between runs and grooms moved between the stalls with the focused indifference of men whose world is measured in twelve-mile stages.

The stablemaster, a compact Samnite named Rufio who had the leathery patience of a man who had managed men and animals with equal scepticism for thirty years, confirmed that Gaius Turpio had returned two nights ago and had since been given three days' leave — an unusual privilege, granted on written instruction from the logistics office.

"The logistics office," Holcus said. "Which officer signed the instruction?"

Rufio produced the tablet. The signature was a legate's mark.

Holcus looked at it for the length of a breath. Then he said, very quietly, "Thank you, Rufio. You have been entirely helpful." And he walked back into the street with the long stride that meant something had clicked into position in the mechanism he was assembling.

"The legate," I said, keeping pace. "This is how the dispatch was routed — through the legate's office. Through someone who controls courier assignments and can give a man three days' leave to ensure he is not interviewed."

"Yes," Holcus said. "And now we have something more than ink discrepancy. We have a chain: the construction of the document, the substitution of a Latin Way courier for an Apulian one, and the authority required to arrange both without detection. That chain has a very small number of possible origins." He did not elaborate. He was already moving toward the Caelian Hill.

V

The man on the Caelian Hill was a military tribune named Gaius Servilius — not the consul's son, a different man entirely — who had survived Lake Trasimene with a wound to his right arm that had not healed cleanly. He was convalescing in a house belonging to a cousin, and he received us in a garden where the late spring light lay across the stones with the flat precision of early afternoon.

He was perhaps thirty, with the look of a man who has recently revised his understanding of what his body can be made to do. The wound had left his right hand partially closed, and he held it in his lap with the careful stillness of a man who has stopped being angry about it and not yet started being resigned.

Holcus sat across from him without preamble.

"You were with Flaminius at the ridge approach."

"I was."

"You saw the mist."

"Everyone saw the mist. It was the point."

"Tell me what you saw before the mist. The previous evening."

Servilius looked at him with the measuring expression of a man deciding how much another man deserves to know. Then something in Holcus's attention — its quality, its complete absence of the usual social texture — seemed to settle him.

"The fires," Servilius said. "The Carthaginian camp-fires were burning normally. Full strength. The scouts reported cavalry at the northern edge of their position, moving slowly — not advancing, not retreating, just... present. Flaminius read it as a resting army."

"The cavalry. Describe them. How many, what formation?"

"Forty, perhaps fifty horses. No order. Spread across the approach, moving in no particular direction. It looked like an army's perimeter cavalry. Like men who had been told to be seen, not to engage."

"Like men who had been told to be seen," Holcus repeated, and I noticed him touch the tablet in his satchel — not writing, only touching. "And in the morning, when Flaminius advanced—"

"The mist was already on the lake. By the time we were committed to the valley, the cavalry on the ridge-line had become infantry. How they moved that many men in silence across that ground—" Servilius stopped. He looked at his closed hand. "I have thought about it often. The timing was not luck. The ground was chosen for what the mist does to sound. You cannot hear an army crossing chalk ridge in morning fog. You only hear them when they are above you."

"The cavalry you saw the previous evening. Were any of them Numidian?"

Servilius blinked. "How did you—yes. The light horses, the fast ones. Eight or ten, at the periphery."

"And at the Trebia," Holcus said, more to himself than to either of us, "the morning began with Numidian cavalry across the river. Loud. Purposeful. Sent to be noticed, not to fight." He rose, thanked Servilius in the abrupt manner of a man who has received exactly what he came for, and walked back through the garden toward the street.

I followed. On the Caelian Hill's descent, with the city below us in the particular midday haze that made Rome look as though it were dreaming itself, Holcus spoke without stopping or turning to face me.

"Numidian cavalry are his signature instrument. At Trebia: across the river to draw Sempronius's cavalry out in cold. At Trasimene: on the ridge-line the previous evening, to be seen and reported as a resting perimeter. At Cannae — and I am as certain of this as I am of my own name — there will be Numidian cavalry on the open ground north of the Aufidus, visible to the Roman advance, performing a withdrawal. Varro will see cavalry in retreat and read it as an army in retreat. It is what Roman doctrine teaches him to read."

"And the real force will be waiting."

"The real force is already in position. Has been for days." He stopped walking. Below us, the Forum showed its usual midday movement — the particular organised chaos of a city that does not yet know what is being prepared two hundred miles to its south. "The document, the cavalry, the timing of Hannibal's grain raids — they are not separate pieces of intelligence. They are a single communication. He has written a document in the language of Roman institutional decision-making, and it says: advance. And the council will advance." He resumed walking. "But now we know the letter was carried by the wrong courier. We know the courier was silenced with three days' leave signed by a legate's authority. And I know which legate's name I expect to find when I look."

He said the name quietly. I will not record it here, in this section. It will be apparent shortly enough.

VI

We were back in the archive before the ninth hour, and Holcus had barely set down his satchel when the door opened without a knock.

The man who filled the frame was large — not fat, but large in the way of men who have spent thirty years being the largest person in most rooms they entered. He wore the legate's narrow stripe with the ease of a garment so habitual it had become skin. Numerius Oppius, legate to the consul Varro, sent to Rome on logistics business. And, on the basis of some report about Holcus's morning activities, evidently on this business too.

"You are the pattern-reader," he said to Holcus, without greeting.

"I am." Holcus did not look up from the tablet he was writing on. I noticed, from my position at the secondary table, that the assembled dispatch — the forged document — was no longer in its usual place at the center of the table. It had been moved, while I was arranging the new testimony notes, to a position beneath two other tablets. I had not moved it. Holcus had.

"I have been told you are submitting analyses to the council arguing against the advance."

"I have submitted analyses describing what I believe will occur if the advance proceeds. This is my function."

"Your function," Oppius said, crossing the room, "is to support the military effort of this Republic. Not to undermine the confidence of the Senate in its appointed commanders."

"My function is to provide accurate analysis. These are not in conflict, unless the accurate analysis is unwelcome." Holcus set down his stylus and rose. He moved to the wall of strings and discs, presenting his back to Oppius in a way that I recognised — not carelessness, but deliberate provocation. An invitation for a man with a temper to make the next move without thinking. "I am curious about something, Legate. Purely a procedural matter."

Oppius had been scanning the table. His eyes had found the tablet nearest the forged document's former position and stopped there, assessing. "What procedural matter."

"The Apulian dispatch. Standard transit time from the Apulian forwarding station is five days. This document arrived in two. I have been attempting to determine how it was expedited." Holcus turned then, slowly, with the expression of a man asking a perfectly innocent question. "You oversee courier logistics for Varro's operation. You would have the authority to re-route a dispatch through the Latin Way relay if speed was a priority."

The room changed. Not loudly. The way a blade drawn in a quiet room changes it.

"Couriers are re-routed regularly," Oppius said.

"Of course. And Gaius Turpio — the Latin Way courier who delivered this particular dispatch — would have been given three days' leave afterward as a matter of routine. The leave authorisation, I understand, came from the logistics office."

Oppius was still. He was a man who had spent thirty years being the largest and most dangerous presence in most rooms he entered, and that habit of self-assurance was working against him now, because it had not prepared him for a conversation he had not controlled from its first word. "What are you implying."

"I am implying nothing. I am observing that the chain of custody for this document runs through your office, and that the courier who carried it has been made unavailable for questioning through a mechanism that required your signature." Holcus's voice was entirely without aggression — it was something worse, something more precise: the voice of a man who has already built the room and is merely watching his subject walk into it. "I would be grateful if you could help me understand the logistics. For the record."

"There is no record," Oppius said. "There is no investigation. You are a civilian analyst who has submitted a dissenting opinion. That is all this is."

"Then you will not object to the full provenance chain being submitted to the council alongside the document analysis. The courier re-routing, the leave authorisation, the ink discrepancy in the dispatch itself — and the testimony of the Porta Capena registry clerk, who noted the courier substitution in the transit log." Holcus tilted his head very slightly. "If the chain of custody is entirely routine, the documentation will confirm it. The council can assess the full picture and reach whatever conclusion the evidence supports."

Oppius crossed the room in four strides. He was wearing a short sword — unusual for a man conducting civilian business — and he put his hand on it. Not drawing it. Simply letting it be known that his hand was there.

"You will not submit that chain," he said.

I had not moved from my position at the secondary table. I am setting this down honestly: I was frightened. Oppius was a man of violence, and the hand on the sword was not a gesture of rhetoric. But I had the transit log notation open in front of me, and I had been transcribing it since we returned from the Porta Capena, and I understood in that moment what Holcus had understood before we left the records office — that the record, once made by more than one hand, was no longer a single document that could be taken and pocketed.

"The courier substitution is already transcribed," I said. My voice came out steadier than I expected. "In my hand. In the archive record. The Porta Capena clerk's notation is already copied. If you take every document in this room, the record still exists — in triplicate, in three separate archive sections, as of this morning." I met Oppius's eyes. "I am a scribe, Legate. Making copies is what I do."

This was not entirely true. I had made one copy. But Oppius did not know what was and was not in the archive, and the uncertainty was enough.

Holcus had not moved. He was watching Oppius with the stillness of a man at a pivot point — the stillness before the mechanism completes.

"The council will decide," Oppius said at last. His voice had shifted — still dangerous, but recalibrated. "Whatever documents you have assembled, whatever chain you believe you have constructed, it will be received by the council in the same way every previous dissenting analysis has been received. The army advances. That is already decided." He took the assembled dispatch from beneath the tablets where it had been hidden — found it, rather, because Holcus had concealed it imperfectly, which I now understood had been the point — and placed it in his own satchel. "I am a legate of Rome. The document is with Rome."

"Yes," Holcus said.

"And your analysis is noted."

"Yes."

Oppius looked at me once more. The look contained several things, none of them comfortable, all of them clearly communicated. Then he left.

The door closed.

Holcus turned back to his wall. He stood there for a moment, very still, and then he made a sound that it took me a moment to identify as quiet laughter.

"He took the bait," he said.

"The document was placed where he would find it."

"It was placed where a man who came to remove evidence would look. Yes." He removed the small tablet from his satchel — the one he had carried to Servilius's garden, covered in his private notation, the sketch of the river bend with its concealment marks. "The original document analysis is in the formal council submission, filed yesterday morning. Oppius has taken a copy — a copy I prepared this morning while you were transcribing the transit log. He does not know that. He believes he has suppressed the physical evidence." He set the tablet on the table. "What he has done, by taking it, is demonstrate consciousness of guilt in front of a witness." He looked at me. "You."

"I am a junior scribe," I said. "My testimony—"

"Your testimony, combined with the transit log notation now in three archive sections — you were very quick, Aemilius, in telling him it was in three sections." There was something in his voice that I think, in another man, would have been warmth. "Is it?"

"One section," I said. "I was working on the second when he arrived."

"Then finish the second and begin the third. Tonight." He was already reaching for a new tablet. "We have forced him to act. A man who came to observe and intimidate has been provoked into removing evidence — evidence whose removal can itself be documented. The council will receive the forgery analysis, the provenance chain, and a record of the evidence's suppression. They will receive everything. Whether they act upon it is not within my power to determine." He looked up then. "But we will have given them no excuse for ignorance."

VII

We submitted the full analysis to the council that evening — the forgery, the courier chain, the Servilius testimony, the record of Oppius's removal of the copy. Fabianus presented it in person, in the Senate, standing very straight for a man whose son was three days from a battlefield.

The council received it carefully.

They advanced anyway.

VIII

It was the second day of the month of Sextilis when the reports began to reach Rome.

I was in the archive when the first messenger arrived. Holcus was at his table. He had not been sleeping well; I had learned to read the particular stillness that overtook him when his mind had been working without rest — a stillness like a stone dropped into water that has not yet finished spreading its rings.

He read the first report without expression. He set it aside and read the second. Then he rose and walked to his wall of strings and discs and stood there, looking at them, for a considerable time.

I did not speak.

Finally he said: "The Aufidus."

"Yes."

"The river bend."

"Yes."

"The Numidian cavalry screen withdrawing across the plain." His voice was entirely without inflection. "And Varro advancing."

"The reports say the Roman formation was eight men deep. That the advance was rapid. That the Carthaginian center fell back and the Roman column pressed forward — and when the flanks closed—"

"Do not," he said quietly, "read me the details."

I stopped.

He remained at the wall for a long time. Then he reached out and removed the clay disc he had marked for Cannae from its string. He held it in his closed hand, the way you hold something you knew you were going to have to put down and have not yet decided where.

"How many?" he said.

"The first reports say perhaps fifty thousand. Some say more. The messengers are not in a condition to be precise."

Holcus opened his hand and looked at the disc.

"I described this room to them," he said. "I described this room in every particular — the walls, the floor, the ceiling, the dimensions — and they walked into it in the dark and were surprised to find themselves inside it." He set the disc down on the table without replacing it on the string. "This is not a failure of analysis. I want that written clearly, Aemilius. Not from vanity. From accuracy. The analysis was correct. The document I was given showed me that Hannibal understood it was correct. The failure was elsewhere, and it was a failure of courage in the receiving, not in the sending."

I thought of Fabianus. I had not seen him since the evening of the Senate submission. I did not yet know whether his son was among the fifty thousand.

"I know," I said.

"Do you?" He looked at me then — not the census-taking inventory look, but something beneath it, something the inventory look was usually positioned to conceal. "You are young. You found the courier substitution in the transit log before I directed you to look for it — I had seen only the name, but you found the route discrepancy independently. That is not instruction. That is instinct, and it is the only instrument in this work that cannot be trained into a man." His voice was very flat. "You will do this work for the rest of your life, and you will encounter this problem again. A pattern correctly read, correctly presented, with the physical evidence on the table and the inferential chain closed — and it will not be enough. The pattern unfolds anyway. File that fact somewhere you can find it."

I recorded it then. I record it now.

"There is one more thing," he said. "Hannibal Barca will not advance on Rome."

I stared at him.

"After this—"

"After this, he cannot. He has no siege equipment. He never intended to take the city — that was never the shape of his operation. He is trying to dissolve Rome's alliances. To demonstrate to Rome's clients that Rome cannot protect them. To make continued loyalty a calculation rather than a certainty. This battle is not the end of the war. It is the opening of a different kind of war." He was already reaching for a new tablet. "And that different kind of war I intend to read."

He wrote in silence. The lamp-oil smell was very strong. Outside, the sounds of the city were beginning to change — the particular quality of movement that happens when news of catastrophic size is spreading street by street, not yet official, not yet spoken aloud by any authority, but moving through the body of the city the way cold moves through water.

"Aemilius."

"Yes."

"Write this as the heading for a new section of your record." He did not look up. "On the occasion when the pattern was correctly read, the evidence correctly assembled, the forgery correctly identified, the testimony correctly gathered, the provenance correctly traced, the logical chain correctly closed — and the reading was set aside. Beneath it, write this, as my words: The analyst persists. The work continues. The pattern remains readable even when the reading is refused."

I wrote them.

I write them now.

IX

The official record will name the consuls. It will record the battle. It will note, in the careful language of institutional record-keeping, that the army advanced and was engaged and sustained losses, and it will proceed to the emergency measures and the Senate's emergency session.

This record will note something else.

It will note that a man in a room that smelled of rancid lamp-oil, surrounded by wax tablets and clay discs on strings, reconstructed the tactical mind of a Carthaginian general across two hundred miles of Italian terrain. That he found the forgery in the dispatch, identified the ink, traced the provenance through the Porta Capena transit log to a courier who carried the wrong road — and then sat across from a wounded tribune on the Caelian Hill and heard the testimony that closed the inferential chain. That he returned to the archive and laid a trap for a legate with a short sword and a guilty conscience, and forced that legate to demonstrate his guilt in front of a witness. That he submitted the full analysis — forgery, provenance, testimony, and the documented suppression of evidence — through the proper channels, with physical evidence and sworn record and logical demonstration, and watched, from a distance measured in weeks, while the pattern he had described unfolded exactly as he had described it.

He did not say: I told them. He said: The work continues.

I do not know, as I write this, whether Senator Fabianus's son is alive. I have been sitting with that not-knowing for two days, and I have no analysis to offer it. This is one place where Holcus's method runs out — at the edge of what is merely human, where the pattern gives no comfort because the pattern was always going to produce this outcome and the people inside it were not patterns. They were men.

What Holcus did was not enough to prevent the battle. But it was not nothing, either. He forced a guilty man to act in front of a witness. He ensured that the evidence of the forgery — and the evidence of its suppression — exists in the archive. That Oppius knew, and acted, and that this too was recorded. That when Rome turns, as Rome will turn, to understand what happened at the Aufidus, the chain will be there to read. Every link of it.

The pattern remains readable. Even now.

I set this down. I seal it. I give it to the family archive, where it will wait for a reader who understands what kind of record it is.

Quintus Holcus was not wrong. He was not passive. He was not merely correct.

He was simply not enough — and he went on working anyway.

Written in the hand of Lucius Aemilius, scribe, in the consulship of Paullus and Varro, in the weeks following the engagement at the river Aufidus, near the village of Cannae, in the year that Rome chose to learn by experience what it had been offered in writing. This account is not for the Senate archive. It is for the family of Holcus, and for whoever reads it after us, and for the instruction of those who will do this work in the years to come — when perhaps someone will be in a position to listen.
