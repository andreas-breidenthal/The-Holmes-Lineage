RIB-ADDI

~1570 BC — Tyre, Canaan

THE MATTER OF THE MISSING CARGO OF COPPER

As recorded by Hanno son of Ikarbaal, Port Clerk of Tyre, in the reign of the Great House of Egypt, in the year when the Nile ran low and the cedar-price rose three parts in ten. The tablet was copied twice at the direction of Rib-Addi himself, one copy for the harbor archive, one for his own house. The reader should know that I have written what occurred as faithfully as my hand permits, and that where I have failed to understand the reasoning of Rib-Addi, I have noted this, so that the gap in my understanding is visible rather than hidden by false confidence.

I.

The air in the harbor of Tyre in summer is heavy with three things: the cedar resin that sweats from every moored hull in the heat, the brine of the shrimp-pools at the southern quay, and the particular salt-and-metal smell of copper ingots, which are stored in the open air under reed matting when the warehouses are full, and which heat in the afternoon sun and breathe out their own strange exhalation — something between blood and clean stone.

I note this because Rib-Addi, when I came to him that morning with the summons from the harbor-master, was already standing at the window of his room above the Street of Rope-Sellers, and he had the window-facing look he sometimes wore when he had been up before dawn thinking about something that would not resolve itself. His morning oil-lamp was still burning, though the sun was already above the eastern hills of the mainland, making the lamp useless. He had not snuffed it. This was the sign, which I had learned to read, that he had not yet eaten or drunk water, that the problem — whatever problem had arrived in the night or been present when he woke — had consumed him entirely.

He was not a young man, Rib-Addi. He had come to the factor's work by an inheritance of two kinds: his father's eye for the false document, trained in the scribal precision of the Babylonian and Egyptian traditions that ran through the male line of his family, and the commercial intelligence of the Tyrian merchant world in which he had grown up, its double streams always visible to him — the official channel of the records, and the unofficial one that ran alongside it, skimming from it, sustaining itself on the difference between what the ledgers showed and what actually moved. He had applied both to the assessment of cargoes, hulls, and freight rates for twenty years at this port, with the comprehensive thoroughness of a man who has been given an interesting problem and who does not stop working at it merely because it has become inconvenient. I had been his scribe for three of those years. I maintained his duplicate copies for the harbor archive and kept his notes in order, and I had learned, in three years, to read his lamps.

He turned when I entered and said, without greeting: "The harbor-master has sent for me, Hanno. What does Zimri-Baal want with me at this hour?"

"He did not say the subject. He said only — urgent. He said that the captain of Adad's Breath has been taken up."

Rib-Addi was silent for a moment. Then he said: "Abdi-Ashirta." This was not a question, but a name — the captain's name — spoken in a tone I could not easily describe. Not pity, precisely. Something adjacent to it.

"You know the man?" I said.

"I know three things about him that Zimri-Baal almost certainly does not." He took his cloak from the peg by the door. "Come. Bring the stylus-case. We will need to take notes at the scene before anyone disturbs the ledgers further than they have already been disturbed."

He did not eat.

II.

The harbor-master's rooms occupied the second floor of the Customs House, which sat at the landward end of the southern quay between a rope-walk and the offices of the ship-brokers. Zimri-Baal was a large man, broad through the chest in the way of men who have worked the quays physically in their youth and who retain the architecture of that labor long after the labor itself has passed. He wore the seal of the harbor authority on a cord around his neck and had the habit of closing his fist around it when he was troubled. When we entered, both his hands were working at nothing, which meant he was very troubled indeed.

With him stood Shuppiluliuma, the merchant.

I had seen him in the harbor quarter before but never spoken with him, and I will say now — because the full account requires honesty about what I perceived and what I failed to perceive — that I did not, on that first morning, understand what I was looking at. He was a small man, dry as papyrus left in the sun, dressed in linen of a quality that announced money without announcing which kind of money. He was weeping. The weeping was composed and regular, the kind that comes not from sudden grief but from sustained pressure — or, as I would later understand, from careful performance. His harbor agent, a man named Baalmilk, stood slightly behind him with the stance of a man making himself smaller than he was.

In the corner, on a stool, with his wrists bound before him with cord, sat Abdi-Ashirta the captain.

He was perhaps forty years, weathered the way all the sea-captains are weathered, the skin dark and creased at the eyes, the hands enormous and scarred. He was not weeping. He was staring at the floor with an expression that was not guilt and not innocence but something that looked, to me, like a man who has heard a thing so completely wrong that he cannot locate the words to contest it and has therefore stopped trying. Around his neck, on a leather cord, hung a token I recognized: the cedar-yard mark of a mainland employer — the pressed-clay disc that workers wore to show their guild affiliation, stamped with the branch-and-axe symbol of the Shuppiluliuma family's lumber operations on the mainland coast.

I noted the disc without understanding why I noted it. It was only later that I understood Rib-Addi had also noted it, and had understood it immediately.

"Rib-Addi." Zimri-Baal pressed his seal in his fist. "Good. You know the cargo of Adad's Breath?"

"Sixty ingots of Alashiyan copper, certified weight, destined for the bronze-casters of Sidon, on consignment from" — Rib-Addi did not hesitate — "the trading house of Shuppiluliuma. I completed the freight assessment myself, fourteen days ago."

"Sixty ingots," Zimri-Baal said. "Certified. And now gone."

"Tell me how they are gone."

The harbor-master gestured at Shuppiluliuma, who collected himself sufficiently to speak. The story he told was this: Adad's Breath had departed Tyre twelve days ago with the copper and a mixed cargo of cedar oil and purple-cloth. She had struck weather south of the Carmel headland — not unusual, the season being what it was — and had taken water in her hold. The captain had, in his account, made the decision to jettison cargo to save the ship. The copper, being heaviest, had gone first. The ship had arrived in Sidon with the cedar oil and the purple-cloth intact, the copper gone, and Abdi-Ashirta had filed his jettison report with the Sidonian harbor authority. Shuppiluliuma had filed a cargo-loss claim with the Tyrian port authority — with Zimri-Baal — this very morning.

Sixty ingots of Alashiyan copper. I made a note of the value without being asked, because I am a port clerk and the valuation is reflexive: three talents of copper, very nearly. Enough to purchase a small house in the upper city. Enough to provision a ship for a full trading season.

"And the captain denies nothing," Zimri-Baal said. "He merely says the claim is false. He says —" and here he glanced at Abdi-Ashirta with something that was not unkindness but was not sympathy, merely the look of a man who does not understand a position well enough to argue for it — "that he did not jettison the cargo."

"I did not," Abdi-Ashirta said, to the floor. It was the first time he had spoken. His voice was even and flat, the voice of a man who has said the same thing many times and has ceased to inflect it because inflection has not helped.

"But the Sidonian report —"

"Is false."

"Signed under your seal."

A silence. Abdi-Ashirta raised his eyes from the floor and looked at no one in particular and said nothing more. His hand moved, briefly and without apparent intention, to the cedar-yard token at his neck. He did not touch it. He stopped the hand before it arrived, as a man stops himself from returning to a wound that he has resolved not to examine. Then the hand fell back to his knee.

Rib-Addi had been standing very still through all of this, in the particular stillness that I had come to recognize as concentrated observation — not the stillness of inattention but the opposite, the stillness of a man in whom every faculty is engaged and who has therefore no surplus energy for movement. He had been looking at Abdi-Ashirta's hands, and at the token, and then at Shuppiluliuma, and the sequence of his looking had a direction I could feel without being able to name.

"May I see the hull assessment for Adad's Breath," he said at last, "and the freight log — not the claim documents, the originals — and the departure record from fourteen days ago. And —" he paused, so briefly that I almost missed it — "the mainland employment ledgers for the Shuppiluliuma family's cedar operations. The labor rolls for the past two seasons."

Baalmilk's expression moved, very slightly, in a way that was not alarm and not quite not alarm. He said nothing.

"This is a matter for the harbor authority," Shuppiluliuma said. His tone was still even, but something beneath it had shifted — a new quality, measured and deliberate, like a man testing the weight of a thing before he uses it. "The loss is documented. The claim is submitted. I would remind Rib-Addi that the assessment work he completed fourteen days ago — which he himself just cited — represents the harbor authority's official certification of this cargo. If that certification is now to be questioned, the question falls upon the assessor as much as upon the claim." He paused. "I would not wish any uncertainty to attach to the reputation of a valued factor."

The room was quiet. Zimri-Baal looked at Shuppiluliuma, then at Rib-Addi.

Rib-Addi said, pleasantly: "The harbor authority has asked me to look. The records, please."

He spent the morning with the ledgers. I sat beside him and copied what he asked me to copy, which was considerable and seemed to me, in many cases, to bear no obvious relation to the matter at hand. He asked for the hull-assessment record of Adad's Breath from her last three voyages, not merely the current one. He asked for the freight logs of all three vessels belonging to Shuppiluliuma's trading house for the past trading season. He asked for the departure and arrival records of every ship that had put in at the small harbor of Dor — which lay south of the Carmel headland, between Tyre and Sidon — in the ten days surrounding the voyage of Adad's Breath. And he asked for the cedar-yard labor rolls, which arrived mid-morning and which he set aside in a particular place within his arrangement of documents, as a man sets aside a piece whose position in the pattern he already knows.

This last request required a letter to be sent to the Dor harbor authority, which Rib-Addi dictated to me in a form that disguised its urgency in administrative language. While we waited for the reply, which came by fast boat in the afternoon, he sat in the corner of the archive room and looked at what I had copied.

The archive room smelled of old clay and something alkaline — the chalk-wash that the clerks used to preserve the older tablets against the damp. It was a close smell, low and mineral. Rib-Addi seemed to find it congenial. He sat with the copies arranged before him on the floor, his knees drawn up, his chin resting on one hand, and he moved between them with the small rapid gestures of a man conducting an argument with inanimate objects.

I will admit that I could make nothing of it. I tried. I looked at the same freight logs and found in them nothing except the ordinary mathematics of the cedar-oil trade — quantities, dates, rates, the small deductions for spoilage and the small additions for late delivery. I looked at the hull-assessment records and saw that Adad's Breath was an old ship, adequately maintained, her strakes sound and her keel recently recaulked. She was, if anything, more seaworthy than many vessels I had seen certified. This struck me as slightly relevant — if the ship was sound, the claim that she had taken water in her hold in ordinary heavy weather was more questionable than if she had been a leaking wreck — but I held the observation, because whenever I had offered one to Rib-Addi prematurely he had received it politely and dismantled it with equal politeness, and I had not yet learned exactly when my observations were useful.

I looked at the labor rolls and saw a list of workers' names, stamped with their guild marks, and among them — in the second season's register, third column, eleven lines from the top — a name I recognized: Abdi-Ashirta, son of. The given name that followed was a common one, but the guild mark beside it was the same branch-and-axe symbol I had seen on the captain's token. His son. In the employ of Shuppiluliuma's brother-in-law. The contract notation beside the entry read: renewable at employer's discretion, each season.

I looked at Rib-Addi. He was watching me read it, with the expression he sometimes wore when he had been waiting for a student to arrive at a conclusion and was giving no assistance whatsoever.

"The captain's son," I said.

"Works in Shuppiluliuma's cedar-yards on the mainland," Rib-Addi said. "His position renewed or revoked each season at the employer's pleasure." He let that sit for a moment. "Abdi-Ashirta did not volunteer to sign that jettison report. He was presented with an alternative to signing it."

I thought about Abdi-Ashirta's hand moving toward the token and stopping. I thought about a man who has heard a thing so completely wrong that he cannot locate the words to contest it.

"He is protecting his son," I said.

"He was protecting his son. The protection is now contingent on our not noticing what we have noticed." He rose from the floor in the quick clean movement that always surprised me, because he was not a young man. "Come. There is one more thing I need to see before the light goes."

III.

The reply from Dor had arrived while we were in the labor rolls. Rib-Addi read it, set it down, and placed it face-up among my copying materials before he stood. He did not tell me to read it. He simply placed it where I would inevitably read it.

I read it.

The Dor harbor authority's record was brief and bureaucratic, as such records always are. It confirmed that a delivery of copper ingots had been received eight days ago at the Dor harbor warehouse — sixty in number, certified Alashiyan weight, by the tallying-marks — logged under a cargo name of unworked metal, mixed lot, and signed for by Baalmilk, for the house of S.

I read the initial twice. I looked at Rib-Addi's back as he lifted his cloak from the peg.

"The 'house of S.,'" I said.

"Shuppiluliuma," he said. "The copper did not go into the sea. It went into a warehouse at Dor. The ship made an unrecorded stop — small harbor, right payment to the right men, far enough from Tyre that the news does not travel quickly. The ingots were offloaded. The ship continued to Sidon with her legitimate cargo. The captain filed his jettison report. The ship-owner filed his loss claim." He paused at the door. "And the loss claim is payable from the Merchants' Common Pool, Hanno. Every trading house in this port compensating a theft committed against them."

The particular ugliness of that settled in me fully.

"Come," he said. "The ship."

IV.

Adad's Breath was moored at the northern quay, under guard — Zimri-Baal had set two of his quay-men to ensure she was not moved while the investigation proceeded. She was a round-ship, broad in the beam, of the Byblos-pattern that was common among the Levantine traders: a single mast, a large square sail currently furled and lashed, a low freeboard that made loading easy and which relied on the cargo's weight for stability. She was old, as I had noted from the assessment, but her lines were still true.

Rib-Addi went aboard without asking permission of anyone and I followed him, feeling as I always did in these moments that I was trespassing through his confidence rather than my own. He went directly to the hold.

The hold of Adad's Breath smelled of copper.

Not the faint residual smell I had noted in the harbor air — this was concentrated, specific, recent. The reed-matting that lined the hold floor was marked with the characteristic rust-red stain of copper ingots lying for a period of time in the damp. The marks were the shape and spacing of ingots. Sixty of them.

"They were here," I said. "Recently. The stain would be fainter if —"

"The ingots were in this hold until no more than eight days ago." He was examining the matting closely, moving his fingers along it. "And now observe." He pointed to the lashing-points at the hold's edge — wooden cleats fixed to the ribs of the ship, used to secure cargo against the ship's movement. "Do you see how the rope-marks sit?"

I looked. The cleats were worn smooth in the way that all working ship-fittings are worn smooth by use, and the ropes that had last been tied to them had left their marks — the compression of the fibers, the direction of the tension. "The tension runs —" I tried to read the direction. "Laterally. Across the ship."

"Precisely. Cargo secured for sea passage is lashed against fore-and-aft movement — the surge and the pitch. Cargo secured for offloading at a quay is lashed against lateral movement — to hold the ingots steady while they are lifted from the side." He stood. "These ingots were not dropped into the sea south of Carmel. They were lifted over the side at a quay. The lashing-marks tell you the direction of the last tension, and the last tension was the tension of men lifting, not the tension of a sea running."

He looked at me with the expression he sometimes wore when he had been waiting for a student to arrive at a conclusion and was giving no assistance whatsoever.

I wrote it down.

V.

That evening, Rib-Addi did not go to Zimri-Baal. He went instead to his room above the Street of Rope-Sellers and dictated two letters.

The first was addressed to the Dor harbor authority, bearing Zimri-Baal's seal, which the harbor-master had given him for the purposes of the investigation. It notified the Dor authority that all copper ingots held in the warehouse of the house of Shuppiluliuma were hereby placed under port-authority lien pending audit, and that the receiving agent Baalmilk was to be informed that a full inventory would be conducted beginning the morning of the following day. This was true: a full inventory would indeed be conducted. What the letter did not mention was when it would arrive at Dor, or by what route.

The second letter, which I wrote and which bore no official seal at all, was addressed to Baalmilk. It informed him, in the voice of a concerned subordinate who had overheard something alarming in the Customs House corridors, that the Dor harbor authority was planning to conduct an independent inspection of the copper warehouse that very night — tonight — before the Tyrian port authority could intervene, and that if the goods were still in place when that inspection occurred, the chain of evidence would lead directly to the house of Shuppiluliuma and thence to every person who had managed the delivery. The letter suggested, in the delicate language of a man who wishes to appear helpful, that there might be a narrow window in which a prudent agent could arrange to have the copper moved to a less visible location.

Rib-Addi read the second letter back to me twice, making two small corrections to the phrasing, and then said: "You look troubled."

"We are writing a false letter," I said.

"We are writing a letter containing one true statement — that the investigation will lead to everyone involved — surrounded by false urgency. Baalmilk will not know which harbor authority will inspect first. He will only know that the copper sitting in that warehouse is proof against him, and that moving it requires him to reveal himself as the man who controls it." He folded the letter. "A guilty man given a choice between sitting still and running will always run. He merely needs to be convinced that sitting still is the more dangerous option."

"And if he does not run?"

"Then we have the Dor record, the labor rolls, the lashing-marks, and the freight logs, and we assemble them before Zimri-Baal in the morning." He sealed the letter. "But he will run."

I delivered both letters myself, as Rib-Addi's scribe, which gave me the right to be seen in the port quarter after dark without occasion for question. The official letter went by the harbor authority's courier to Dor. The second letter went by my own hand through the door of Baalmilk's lodgings on the Street of Dyers, slipped beneath the threshold in the manner of the anonymous correspondence that was common in the merchant quarter and that everyone pretended not to recognize.

Then I went back to Rib-Addi's room and told him it was done, and he told me to sleep for three hours, and I did not sleep at all.

VI.

We were at the Dor road's southern junction before midnight — Rib-Addi, myself, and four of Zimri-Baal's quay-men, the harbor-master having agreed to the plan with the quiet efficiency of a man who has been told exactly what evidence exists and who understands what the trap requires. The night was warm and windless, the harbor sounds carrying from the quay in the stillness with unusual clarity: rope-creak, water-slap, the occasional cry of a night-bird working the shrimp-pools.

We waited.

The quay-men were patient, as harbor workers are patient — theirs is a trade that involves a great deal of standing in darkness waiting for something to happen. Rib-Addi stood apart from them in the manner I recognized: still, facing a direction that was not quite toward anything visible, his attention entirely inward. I stood between them and him and felt the weight of the night and tried to breathe evenly.

I had not, until that evening, fully appreciated what Shuppiluliuma had said in the harbor-master's rooms — the remark about the certification, about uncertainty attaching to the reputation of a valued factor. I appreciated it now. If the trap failed, if Baalmilk did not move, if Shuppiluliuma's lawyers were faster than the evidence — then the morning would begin with a merchant of three ships and considerable wealth leveling a formal complaint against the factor whose own certification appeared on the missing cargo's assessment documents. It was not, I thought, a comfortable position to have constructed for oneself.

I said as much to Rib-Addi, in a low voice, during the second hour.

He was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "The question Shuppiluliuma raised this morning is the correct question. My certification is on those documents. If I were wrong — if the cargo was legitimately jettisoned and I have accused a substantial merchant on the basis of ledger-reading and rope-fiber analysis — then the doubt he planted is entirely warranted." A pause. "I am not wrong. But the distinction between I am not wrong and I can prove I am not wrong is very precisely the distance between now and whatever arrives on that road."

I looked at the road.

At the third hour, torchlight appeared to the south. Two men, moving quickly and without the rhythm of travelers: the quick broken stride of men carrying something heavy between them, stopping, adjusting, moving again. Behind them, the flicker of a second torch.

Rib-Addi's stillness changed. He did not move — but the quality of his stillness changed, the way a drawn bow is different from a resting one.

"Now," he said.

The quay-men moved with the practiced efficiency of men who perform physical work for their living and who have been given clear instructions. Baalmilk was taken at the roadside, along with his associate — a dock-laborer hired for the night, who gave his name, his employer's name, and a detailed account of the evening's instructions within the first several minutes of questioning, being both frightened and transparently uninvested in anyone's protection but his own.

Baalmilk was not a brave man. He was also not, I think, a man who had planned for the possibility that his anonymous letter had been composed by his accuser. He looked at Rib-Addi in the torchlight with an expression I found difficult to read — not rage, not calculation, but something more like the bewildered exhaustion of a man who has run very hard in the wrong direction and has arrived at the wrong place at precisely the wrong moment. He said nothing for a long time. Then he began to talk.

He talked for quite some time. I wrote down everything.

VII.

They did not take Shuppiluliuma until morning — there was paperwork, there always is, and the harbor authority's machinery required specific forms, specific seals, the correct sequence of documents presented to the correct officials in the correct order. Zimri-Baal managed this with calm efficiency. Rib-Addi was not present for any of it. He had, by the time the first administration of the morning began, returned to his room, eaten bread and oil and dried fish, and gone to sleep.

I handled the morning's paperwork myself.

What followed was in some respects ordinary: Baalmilk's full confession before the harbor authority, Zimri-Baal's seizure of the copper at Dor, its eventual delivery to the bronze-casters of Sidon fourteen days after its scheduled arrival. The law was clear on cargo-fraud — confiscation, heavy fine, and in the case of Baalmilk, who had a prior mark against him in the port records for a matter of falsified weights, the cutting of the right hand's writing fingers, which ended his career as a clerk. Shuppiluliuma's two remaining ships were impounded pending full accounting. The name of Shuppiluliuma does not appear again in the surviving Tyrian commercial records.

Abdi-Ashirta was released. His cord was cut by Zimri-Baal himself, which I thought was a gesture of some grace.

The official record of the case, as entered in the harbor authority's archive, credited the resolution to the harbor authority's investigation, with a note that the evidence from Dor had been obtained through the harbor-master's correspondence and a night operation conducted at the harbor-master's direction. Zimri-Baal did not acknowledge Rib-Addi in the official text. He did, afterward, in private, say something brief and plainly meant, which Rib-Addi received without any particular expression and then appeared to forget.

I have written down what actually occurred.

VIII.

There is one more thing, which I debated whether to record, because I am not certain it reflects well on all parties, including myself.

On the evening after the resolution, Rib-Addi came to where I was making my fair copies and sat across from me and said: "You are going to ask me about the captain."

"I was," I said. "He signed the report. Under pressure, yes — but he signed it. He lied to the Sidonian authority. He let himself be —"

"He let himself be a man with a son," Rib-Addi said. "This is a common condition. The law sees the signed document. The law is correct to see it. But the law does not have children, and therefore does not know what the signed document cost."

"Will he be charged?"

"Zimri-Baal has the evidence before him and will make the decision that his office requires him to make." A pause. "I have not, in my copies for the harbor archive, included the specific language of Baalmilk's statement about the initial conversation with the captain. The language of the confession is — general. On the question of Abdi-Ashirta's precise degree of foreknowledge, the copies I have submitted are, I think, genuinely ambiguous."

I looked at him.

"I am a factor," he said. "I assess cargo. The harbor-master makes the legal determinations. If my copies leave him with ambiguity, he must navigate that ambiguity as his judgment allows."

"You have shaped what he knows."

"I have copied what was relevant," he said. "A copy is not the original. Something is always lost in transcription." He looked at me with an expression that was not quite a smile and was not quite not one. "You are the scribe, Hanno. What do you put in, and what do you leave out? That is always the question, is it not."

He went to bed. For the first time in three days, he ate something beforehand — the bread and the oil and the dried fish that his housekeeper left, and which had sat untouched through the working of the case.

I finished my copies.

I wrote everything down.

Here the first tablet ends. The second tablet, more damaged, contains what appears to be a continuation of Hanno's account in a later hand, possibly a copyist's recension, and several lines that cannot be restored. The copper, per the Tyrian harbor records, was delivered to the bronze-casters of Sidon fourteen days after its original scheduled arrival date. The freight rate is recorded as having been renegotiated.

The name of Shuppiluliuma does not appear again in the surviving Tyrian commercial records.

The name Abdi-Ashirta appears once more, seven years later, as master of a vessel called Rider of Adad, making a routine cedar-oil run to the Delta. No further notation is made.
