RIB-ADDI

~1570 BC — Tyre, Canaan

Addu is great — the storm-god of the northern Semitic world, whose name this ancestor shared with the later king of Byblos whose frantic letters to the pharaoh would survive in the Amarna archive, testaments to a man watching his city fall and finding, in the correspondence of diplomacy, no adequate response to the reality of military force. This Rib-Addi was not a king but a factor — a ship-owner’s agent at Tyre, responsible for the management of cargoes, the assessment of hulls, the negotiation of freight rates, and the constant, grinding detective work of determining whether a given merchant, a given ship, and a given cargo were what they claimed to be.

He grew up in his father’s house with the family’s double inheritance — the scribal precision of the Babylonian and Egyptian traditions running through the male line, and the commercial intelligence of the Tyrian merchant world surrounding him in the city where he was born. He had his father’s eye for the false document and his grandfather’s understanding of the parallel commercial stream — the unofficial channel that ran alongside the official one, skimming from it and sustaining itself on the difference between what the records showed and what actually moved. He applied both to the port with the comprehensive thoroughness of a man who has been given an interesting problem and who does not stop working at it merely because it has become inconvenient.

The surviving record of his work is preserved in fragments of correspondence copied by his scribe, Hanno son of Ikarbaal, a port clerk whose duties included maintaining duplicate copies of official logs for audit and storage. Hanno was not trained in the higher schools of temple scribes, but he had a steady hand and a habit of writing exactly what he was told, even when he did not understand it. His notes are preserved on two clay tablets, both broken, both restored in part by later archivists in Tyre, and both bearing the marks of repeated handling and careful reconstruction.

The Matter of the Ship Adad's Breath and Her Missing Cargo of Copper

As recorded by Hanno son of Ikarbaal, Port Clerk, in the reign of the Great House of Egypt, in the year when the Nile ran low and the cedar-price rose three parts in ten. The tablet was copied twice at the direction of Rib-Addi himself, one copy for the harbor archive, one for his own house. The reader should know that I have written what occurred as faithfully as my hand permits, and that where I have failed to understand the reasoning of Rib-Addi, I have noted this, so that the gap in my understanding is visible rather than hidden by false confidence.

The air in the harbor of Tyre in summer is heavy with three things: the cedar resin that sweats from every moored hull in the heat, the brine of the shrimp-pools at the southern quay, and the particular salt-and-metal smell of copper ingots, which are stored in the open air under reed matting when the warehouses are full, and which heat in the afternoon sun and breathe out their own strange exhalation — something between blood and clean stone.

I note this because Rib-Addi, when I came to him that morning with the summons from the harbor-master, was already standing at the window of his room above the Street of Rope-Sellers, and he had the window-facing look he sometimes wore when he had been up before dawn thinking about something that would not resolve itself. His morning oil-lamp was still burning, though the sun was already above the eastern hills of the mainland, making the lamp useless. He had not snuffed it. This was the sign, which I had learned to read, that he had not yet eaten or drunk water, that the problem — whatever problem had arrived in the night or been present when he woke — had consumed him entirely.

He turned when I entered and said, without greeting: “The harbor-master has sent for me, Hanno. What does Zimri-Baal want with me at this hour?”

“He did not say the subject. He said only — urgent. He said that the captain of Adad's Breath has been taken up.”

Rib-Addi was silent for a moment. Then he said: “Abdi-Ashirta.” This was not a question, but a name — the captain's name — spoken in a tone I could not easily describe. Not pity, precisely. Something adjacent to it.

“You know the man?” I said.

“I know three things about him that Zimri-Baal almost certainly does not.” He took his cloak from the peg by the door. “Come. Bring the stylus-case. We will need to take notes at the scene before anyone disturbs the ledgers further than they have already been disturbed.”

He did not eat.

The harbor-master's rooms occupied the second floor of the Customs House, which sat at the landward end of the southern quay between a rope-walk and the offices of the ship-brokers. Zimri-Baal was a large man, broad through the chest in the way of men who have worked the quays physically in their youth and who retain the architecture of that labor long after the labor itself has passed. He wore the seal of the harbor authority on a cord around his neck and had the habit of closing his fist around it when he was troubled. When we entered, both his hands were working at nothing, which meant he was very troubled indeed.

With him stood Shuppiluliuma, the merchant — ship-owner, three vessels, the largest trading house operating from the northern quay. He was a small man by contrast, dry as papyrus left in the sun, dressed in linen of a quality that announced money without announcing which kind of money. He was weeping. The weeping was composed and regular, the kind that comes not from sudden grief but from sustained pressure. He had been weeping, I thought, for some time.

In the corner, on a stool, with his wrists bound before him with cord, sat Abdi-Ashirta the captain.

He was perhaps forty years, weathered the way all the sea-captains are weathered, the skin dark and creased at the eyes, the hands enormous and scarred. He was not weeping. He was staring at the floor with an expression that was not guilt and not innocence but something that looked, to me, like a man who has heard a thing so completely wrong that he cannot locate the words to contest it and has therefore stopped trying.

“Rib-Addi.” Zimri-Baal pressed his seal in his fist. “Good. You know the cargo of Adad's Breath?”

“Sixty ingots of Alashiyan copper, certified weight, destined for the bronze-casters of Sidon, on consignment from” — Rib-Addi did not hesitate — “the trading house of Shuppiluliuma. I completed the freight assessment myself, fourteen days ago.”

“Sixty ingots,” Zimri-Baal said. “Certified. And now gone.”

“Tell me how they are gone.”

The harbor-master gestured at Shuppiluliuma, who collected himself sufficiently to speak. The story he told was this: Adad's Breath had departed Tyre twelve days ago with the copper and a mixed cargo of cedar oil and purple-cloth. She had struck weather south of the Carmel headland — not unusual, the season being what it was — and had taken water in her hold. The captain had, in his account, made the decision to jettison cargo to save the ship. The copper, being heaviest, had gone first. The ship had arrived in Sidon with the cedar oil and the purple-cloth intact, the copper gone, and Abdi-Ashirta had filed his jettison report with the Sidonian harbor authority. Shuppiluliuma had filed a cargo-loss claim with the Tyrian port authority — with Zimri-Baal — this very morning.

Sixty ingots of Alashiyan copper. I made a note of the value without being asked, because I am a port clerk and the valuation is reflexive: three talents of copper, very nearly. Enough to purchase a small house in the upper city. Enough to provision a ship for a full trading season. Enough.

“And the captain denies nothing,” Zimri-Baal said. “He merely says the claim is false. He says —” and here he glanced at Abdi-Ashirta with something that was not unkindness but was not sympathy, merely the look of a man who does not understand a position well enough to argue for it — “that he did not jettison the cargo.”

“I did not,” Abdi-Ashirta said, to the floor. It was the first time he had spoken. His voice was even and flat, the voice of a man who has said the same thing many times and has ceased to inflect it because inflection has not helped.

“But the Sidonian report —”

“Is false.”

“Signed under your seal.”

A silence. Abdi-Ashirta raised his eyes from the floor and looked at no one in particular and said nothing more.

Rib-Addi had been standing very still through all of this, in the particular stillness that I had come to recognize as concentrated observation — not the stillness of inattention but the opposite, the stillness of a man in whom every faculty is engaged and who has therefore no surplus energy for movement. He was looking at Abdi-Ashirta's hands.

“May I see the hull assessment for Adad's Breath,” he said at last, “and the freight log — not the claim documents, the originals — and the departure record from fourteen days ago.”

“This is a matter for the harbor authority,” Shuppiluliuma said. He was not weeping now. “The loss is documented. The claim is submitted. It is a matter of procedure, not —”

“The harbor authority,” Zimri-Baal said, with a steadiness I credited to him, “has asked Rib-Addi to look.”

He spent the morning with the ledgers. I sat beside him and copied what he asked me to copy, which was considerable and seemed to me, in many cases, to bear no obvious relation to the matter at hand. He asked for the hull-assessment record of Adad's Breath from her last three voyages, not merely the current one. He asked for the freight logs of all three vessels belonging to Shuppiluliuma's trading house for the past trading season. He asked for the departure and arrival records of every ship that had put in at the small harbor of Dor — which lay south of the Carmel headland, between Tyre and Sidon — in the ten days surrounding the voyage of Adad's Breath.

This last request required a letter to be sent to the Dor harbor authority, which Rib-Addi dictated to me in a form that disguised its urgency in administrative language. While we waited for the reply, which came by fast boat in the afternoon, he sat in the corner of the archive room and looked at what I had copied.

The archive room smelled of old clay and something alkaline — the chalk-wash that the clerks used to preserve the older tablets against the damp. It was a close smell, low and mineral. Rib-Addi seemed to find it congenial. He sat with the copies arranged before him on the floor, his knees drawn up, his chin resting on one hand, and he moved between them with the small rapid gestures of a man conducting an argument with inanimate objects.

I will admit that I could make nothing of it. I tried. I looked at the same freight logs and found in them nothing except the ordinary mathematics of the cedar-oil trade — quantities, dates, rates, the small deductions for spoilage and the small additions for late delivery. Numbers. I looked at the hull-assessment records and saw that Adad's Breath was an old ship, adequately maintained, her strakes sound and her keel recently recaulked. She was, if anything, more seaworthy than many vessels I had seen certified.

This last point struck me as slightly relevant — if the ship was sound, the claim that she had taken water in her hold in ordinary heavy weather was more questionable than if she had been a leaking wreck — but I said nothing, because whenever I had offered an observation to Rib-Addi he had received it politely and dismantled it with equal politeness, and I had not yet learned exactly when my observations were useful.

The reply from Dor arrived in the late afternoon. Rib-Addi read it once, set it down carefully, and did not move for a long moment.

Then he said: “Hanno. What would you do, if you wished to steal a cargo of copper and to ensure that someone else was blamed for its absence?”

I considered this. “I would — claim it was lost. At sea. Jettisoned.”

“Yes. And to make the claim credible?”

“I would need the captain's report. His seal on the jettison document.”

“And if the captain refused to provide his seal?”

I saw, then, where this was going, and a chill moved through me that was not the archive room's cool air. “I would — find a way to compel him. Or to obtain his seal without his knowledge.”

“Or,” Rib-Addi said, “with his knowledge but under a form of pressure that cannot be spoken aloud. Yes.” He rose from the floor in the quick clean movement that always surprised me, because he was not a young man. “Come. There is one more thing I need to see before the light goes.”

He went to the ship.

Adad's Breath was moored at the northern quay, under guard — Zimri-Baal had set two of his quay-men to ensure she was not moved while the investigation proceeded. She was a round-ship, broad in the beam, of the Byblos-pattern that was common among the Levantine traders: a single mast, a large square sail currently furled and lashed, a low freeboard that made loading easy and which relied on the cargo's weight for stability. She was old, as I had noted from the assessment, but her lines were still true.

Rib-Addi went aboard without asking permission of anyone and I followed him, feeling as I always did in these moments that I was trespassing through his confidence rather than my own. He went directly to the hold.

The hold of Adad's Breath smelled of copper.

Not the faint residual smell I had noted in the harbor air — this was concentrated, specific, recent. The reed-matting that lined the hold floor was marked with the characteristic rust-red stain of copper ingots lying for a period of time in the damp. The marks were the shape and spacing of ingots. Sixty of them.

“They were here,” I said. “Recently. Very recently, or the stain would be — fainter?”

“The stain would be fainter, yes. The ingots were in this hold until no more than eight days ago.” He was examining the matting closely, moving his fingers along it. “And now observe.” He pointed to the lashing-points at the hold's edge — wooden cleats fixed to the ribs of the ship, used to secure cargo against the ship's movement. “Do you see how the rope-marks sit?”

I looked. The cleats were worn smooth in the way that all working ship-fittings are worn smooth by use, and the ropes that had last been tied to them had left their marks — the compression of the fibers, the direction of the tension. “The tension runs —” I tried to read the direction. “Forward? No — laterally. Across the ship.”

“Precisely. Cargo secured for sea passage is lashed against fore-and-aft movement — the surge and the pitch. Cargo secured for offloading at a quay is lashed against lateral movement — to hold the ingots steady while they are lifted from the side.” He stood. “These ingots were not dropped into the sea south of Carmel. They were lifted over the side at a quay. The lashing-marks tell you the direction of the last tension, and the last tension was the tension of men lifting, not the tension of a sea running.”

He looked at me with the expression he sometimes wore when he had said something that seemed to him obvious and was watching to see if it had been understood.

I wrote it down.

The following morning, Rib-Addi went to Zimri-Baal and requested that Shuppiluliuma be summoned again, along with his harbor agent — a man named Baalmilk, who managed the ship-owner's dealings with the port authority and who had, I now knew from the freight logs, submitted the cargo-loss claim on behalf of his employer.

Abdi-Ashirta was brought also, still bound.

They assembled in the harbor-master's rooms as before. The morning light came through the sea-facing window and lay across the floor in a pale stripe. Shuppiluliuma was not weeping today. He stood with the composed stillness of a man who has already won and is waiting for the administration of his victory.

Rib-Addi stood at the window, looking out at the harbor for a long moment. Then he turned.

“I will tell you,” he said, “what happened to the copper of Adad's Breath.”

The room was quiet except for the harbor sounds below — the creak of mooring-ropes, the strike of a hammer on a hull somewhere on the quay, the cry of the sea-birds.

“The copper did not go into the sea,” he said. “It went into a warehouse at Dor. The harbor records of Dor show that the trading house of Shuppiluliuma — through its agent at that port — received a delivery of copper ingots eight days ago, sixty in number, certified Alashiyan weight, by the tallying-marks.” He held up the copy of the Dor harbor record, which I had prepared. “The delivery was logged under a fictive cargo name — ‘unworked metal, mixed lot’ — and the receiving agent is listed as ‘Baalmilk for the house of S.’” He looked at the harbor agent, who had gone the color of old plaster. “You will note the initial.”

Shuppiluliuma said nothing. He had stopped being composed. His hands were working at his linen robe.

“The mechanism is straightforward and has been used before, though not often with this degree of care. The ship departs with its cargo certified and witnessed — I certified it myself, which I note with some professional irritation. The ship makes an unrecorded stop at a secondary port — Dor, in this case, which is small enough that an irregular stop may be arranged with the right payment to the right men, and far enough from Tyre that news of it does not travel quickly. The cargo is offloaded. The ship continues to its destination with its legitimate cargo intact. At the destination, the captain files a jettison report. The ship-owner files a loss claim. The captain takes a share of the recovered copper's value. The ship-owner takes the rest, plus the insurance value of the claimed loss, which —” he looked at the freight log copy I had placed before him— “in this case would be payable from the Merchants' Common Pool, which means that every trading house in this port contributes to the compensation of a theft that was committed against them.”

A long silence.

“The captain's seal on the Sidonian jettison report,” Zimri-Baal said slowly. “He agreed to this?”

Rib-Addi looked at Abdi-Ashirta. Something passed between them — not words, not precisely, but an acknowledgment. “The captain's eldest son,” he said, “works in the cedar-yards of Shuppiluliuma's brother-in-law on the mainland. He has worked there for two years. His contract is” — I held up the relevant copy — “renewable at the employer's discretion each season.” He paused. “Abdi-Ashirta did not agree to this arrangement. He was presented with it. There is a difference, which the law does not always make clearly enough, between a man who schemes and a man who is cornered.”

The captain, on his stool, had raised his head. His expression was still difficult to read, but something in it had changed. Something had opened that had been closed for however many days he had been sitting on that stool staring at the floor.

Baalmilk broke first. He was not a brave man — the harbor agents rarely are; they are chosen for their administrative competence, not their resolution — and he began talking in the rapid, tripping way of a man who has been holding something behind his teeth and cannot hold it any longer. He talked for quite some time. I wrote down everything.

What followed was in some respects ordinary: Baalmilk's confession, Zimri-Baal's arrest of Shuppiluliuma's household agent at Dor by means of a letter to the Dor harbor authority, the recovery of the copper from the warehouse. The law was clear on cargo-fraud — confiscation, a heavy fine, and in the case of Baalmilk, who had a prior mark against him in the port records for a matter of falsified weights, the cutting of the right hand's writing fingers, which ended his career as a clerk. Shuppiluliuma's two remaining ships were impounded pending the full accounting.

Abdi-Ashirta was released. His cord was cut by Zimri-Baal himself, which I thought was a gesture of some grace.

The official record of the case, as entered in the harbor authority's archive, credited the resolution to the harbor authority's investigation, with a note that the evidence from Dor had been obtained through the harbor-master's correspondence. Zimri-Baal did not acknowledge Rib-Addi in the official text. He did, afterward, in private, say something brief and plainly meant, which Rib-Addi received without any particular expression and then appeared to forget.

I have written down what actually occurred.

There is one more thing, which I debated whether to record, because I am not certain it reflects well on all parties, including myself.

On the evening after the resolution, Rib-Addi came to where I was making my fair copies and sat across from me and said: “You are going to ask me about the captain.”

“I was,” I said. “He signed the report. Under pressure, yes — but he signed it. He lied to the Sidonian authority. He let himself be —”

“He let himself be a man with a son,” Rib-Addi said. “This is a common condition. The law sees the signed document. The law is correct to see it. But the law does not have children, and therefore does not know what the signed document cost.”

“Will he be charged?”

“Zimri-Baal has the evidence before him and will make the decision that his office requires him to make.” A pause. “I have not, in my copies for the harbor archive, included the specific language of Baalmilk's statement about the initial conversation with the captain. The language of the confession is — general. On the question of Abdi-Ashirta's precise degree of foreknowledge, the copies I have submitted are, I think, genuinely ambiguous.”

I looked at him.

“I am a factor,” he said. “I assess cargo. The harbor-master makes the legal determinations. If my copies leave him with ambiguity, he must navigate that ambiguity as his judgment allows.”

“You have shaped what he knows.”

“I have copied what was relevant,” he said. “A copy is not the original. Something is always lost in transcription.” He looked at me with an expression that was not quite a smile and was not quite not one. “You are the scribe, Hanno. What do you put in, and what do you leave out? That is always the question, is it not.”

He went to bed. For the first time in three days, he ate something beforehand — the bread and the oil and the dried fish that his housekeeper left, and which had sat untouched through the working of the case.

I finished my copies.

I wrote everything down.

Here the first tablet ends.
The second tablet, more damaged, contains what appears to be a continuation of Hanno's account in a later hand, possibly a copyist's recension, and several lines that cannot be restored. The copper, per the Tyrian harbor records, was delivered to the bronze-casters of Sidon fourteen days after its original scheduled arrival date. The freight rate is recorded as having been renegotiated.
The name of Shuppiluliuma does not appear again in the surviving Tyrian commercial records.
The name Abdi-Ashirta appears once more, seven years later, as master of a vessel called Rider of Adad, making a routine cedar-oil run to the Delta.
No further notation is made.
