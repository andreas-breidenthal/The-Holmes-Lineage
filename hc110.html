RICHARD DE HOLMES

~1330 — Yorkshire, England

Richard de Holmes was eighteen years old when the Black Death reached the North Riding in the autumn of 1348, and he observed its arrival not as a matter of doctrine or fear but as a disruption in systems he already understood. Labour, tenancy, and estate continuity were, to him, interlocking structures whose failure would propagate unless corrected at speed.

He responded with adjustments that ran ahead of custom: rents recalibrated before petitions were made, labour hired at revised rates without resistance, and demesne structures reorganised to reflect a new equilibrium in which labour had become scarce and land abundant. Where others saw moral collapse or divine judgment, he saw altered conditions requiring immediate rebalancing.

The estate survived the first wave of mortality with fewer losses than its neighbours. Those who observed him during this period noted that his attention consistently moved first to measurable facts, and only afterwards to interpretation. This habit, formed early, would later define his method in matters beyond estate management.

Certain Deaths in the Villages of Aysgarth

Recovered Among the Estate Papers at Michaelmas, 1352

Of the many deaths recorded in the mortality year of 1348, the one that stayed longest with Richard de Holmes was that of Geoffrey Marsh of Colby — and I confess this troubled me at the time, not because the death was remarkable, but because Richard's interest in it seemed so plainly a symptom of exhaustion. We had been recording deaths since August. A man's judgment, under that kind of sustained pressure, begins to find patterns where Providence has put none. I thought this was what had happened to Richard. I was trying, in my way, to be charitable.

I set down what follows because he asked me to, in the spring of 1351, when it had become clear that no court would ever hear the matter and that the record itself was all that would remain of it.

The autumn had brought with it, alongside the mortality itself, a secondary wave of alarm about the means by which the pestilence travelled. A Franciscan friar who passed through Aysgarth in late September — one of several mendicants moving between the stricken districts that season, carrying news as much as prayer — brought with him a written account from a physician in Beverley, which he read aloud at the church and which Richard afterwards asked to examine. The physician, one Master Aldred, had treated three cases in the summer in which the pattern of illness did not accord with plague, and had concluded, after correspondence with a colleague in Paris, that certain plant-derived substances were capable of passing from a treated surface into the body through the skin, producing fever and haemorrhage without the characteristic swellings. He named white hellebore specifically, and stated that he had confirmed the mechanism by experiment on animals, and that a preparation rubbed into cloth or applied to a handled object could deliver sufficient quantity to kill within days. The quantity absorbed, he noted with the precision of a man reporting experiment rather than opinion, was proportional to the duration of contact. Brief handling produced mild symptoms or none. Sustained handling over hours and days produced the full progression.

Master Aldred's conclusion was that this explained a category of deaths being recorded as plague variant which were in fact poisonings, and that physicians should be alert to the distinction.

I recorded the account in the estate journal as a matter of local intelligence. Richard read it twice, folded it carefully, and placed it in the estate correspondence box rather than the general file.

I thought he was being thorough. He was filing evidence. I did not know the difference yet.

Walter Crey arrived in Aysgarth on the eighth of October, travelling the York road alone. This was not remarkable. Many men travelled alone that autumn. He took lodging at the house of Robert Ash the miller and stated his business as the collection of outstanding debts from the quarter's trade — a common enough errand in those months. The mortality had disrupted payment schedules across the entire riding, and creditors were moving through the districts in numbers, attempting to recover what they could before their debtors resolved the question by dying.

I met Crey on the morning of the ninth, when Richard and I attended Ash's house to discuss a boundary dispute on the east field. Crey was eating at the miller's table — his own bread, his own flask. Ash's wife had offered the common loaf and he had declined it, pleasantly, with a small gesture toward his flask by way of explanation. It struck me as the behaviour of a sensible man. The plague moved through contact and breath, or so we then understood, and a merchant whose business required him to pass through infected districts had every reason to keep to his own provisions. I remember thinking that his caution was admirable — that here was a man who had calculated his risks carefully and arranged himself accordingly.

He was a compact man of perhaps fifty years, careful in his dress, with a ring on the first finger of his left hand — a merchant's seal ring, the face worn smooth with use, set in dark metal. His horse, which I had seen in Ash's yard, was a grey with a distinctive white blaze running crooked from poll to nostril, the kind of marking that lodges in the memory without effort.

Richard greeted him and asked, in the easy manner he used when he wished to seem merely sociable, whether the roads from York had been much troubled.

Crey said they had not been especially so. He spoke carefully — each answer complete and no more than complete. A precise man, I thought. The kind of precision that comes from long practice in transactions where loose words cost money.

Richard asked whether his collections had gone well, given the disruption of the season.

Crey said several accounts had required renegotiation. He said it without apparent feeling, which I took as professional composure. In that season, sentiment was a luxury most men had already spent.

Richard thanked him and returned to the boundary question. As we walked back to the estate house afterward, Richard said: "He did not shake hands."

I said that many men were avoiding contact.

Richard said: "He did not touch the table. He rested his hands in his lap throughout."

I said I had not noticed. And truly I had not — or rather, I had noticed it in the way one notices weather, as simple background, without attaching to it any significance beyond the general anxiety of the season. Master Aldred's account had made half the district cautious about touching things. Crey seemed to me simply another man who had read the signs of the season and acted accordingly.

Richard said nothing further. I thought the subject closed.

Geoffrey Marsh died on the fourteenth of October.

I had known him slightly — a tenant farmer, punctual in his obligations, unremarkable in his habits. He left a wife, Margaret, and two surviving children. Father Edmund at Colby recorded the death and noted its character: rapid onset, severe fever, bleeding from the mouth and nose in the final hours, death within four days of the first signs of illness.

I entered it in the mortality register as plague, consistent with all surrounding entries. It was consistent. The disease that autumn was killing men in every manner imaginable — the buboes, the lung sickness, the blood corruption that moved so fast a man was dead before his household understood he was ill. Marsh's presentation fit the faster forms well enough, and I had long since stopped expecting uniformity from a pestilence that seemed to take a particular pleasure in variety.

Richard read the entry twice.

"No swellings," he said.

"Father Edmund would have noted them if they were present," I agreed. "The plague presents in many forms. We have both read enough accounts to know that."

"We have," he said. He looked at the entries on either side of Marsh's. Then he said: "Crey arrived on the eighth. When did Marsh last attend court?"

I checked. The twenty-ninth of September.

He was quiet for a moment. Then: "Pull the spring court rolls. I want to know whether Marsh's name appears alongside any York mercantile entry from the first quarter."

I pulled them. It took some time, the rolls being in some disorder from the mortality's disruption of the administrative calendar. I found what he was looking for on the third page of the February sitting: a debt instrument recorded in Marsh's name against a York merchant of Goodramgate, for a sum arising from a wool transaction the previous autumn that had been disputed and referred to the riding court for resolution. The merchant's name was given as Walter Crey.

I read it back to Richard.

He nodded once, as a man nods when an expected thing arrives on schedule, and said: "Now I know why Marsh was first."

I did not yet understand what he meant by first. I wrote the entry in my notes as a point of mercantile history requiring follow-up for estate accounting purposes, and moved on.

He sent me to Colby eight days later to make general inquiries about the state of the parish. I went without particular expectation.

I spoke first to Father Edmund, who was managing three parishes alone and had the drawn, mechanical quality of a man operating well past his natural limit. He confirmed what his register recorded. Marsh had been healthy at the September court. The illness had come on suddenly, moved fast, and had a character he had not seen before in the district. He had concluded it was one of the rarer manifestations of the pestilence. I thought this entirely convincing.

Margaret Marsh received me in the yard. She said that Geoffrey had received a visitor on the evening of the tenth — a stranger, older, well-dressed, who had come to the yard and spoken with Geoffrey for perhaps a quarter of an hour before leaving. She had not known him. She described him as a compact man, not tall, careful in his dress, with a ring on his left hand that she had noticed because it caught what remained of the evening light. His horse, which she had seen tied at the field gate, was grey with a white mark down the face, crooked, she said, like a river on a bad map.

I noted this carefully, as a description useful for identifying the creditor involved should the debt instrument require further administration. It did not occur to me to place it alongside anything I had already seen and recorded.

She said Geoffrey had come inside afterward and set coins on the table — three, newer than the ones they usually handled. He had seemed settled. As a man might seem when a weight is lifted.

She said he had stood at the table for some time, turning the coins over in his hands, holding them to the lamp. He had been in good spirits — he had called the children to show them, and each child had held the coins briefly before Geoffrey took them back. Then he had put the coins in the box and washed his hands in the basin, not because they were dirty, she said, but as a habit he had developed that autumn, as many people had. Afterward he had sat by the fire and seemed, for the first time in months, at ease. He had returned to the box twice more that evening, she thought, to recount the coins — as men do, she said, when they have been worried about money and are suddenly not.

He had been ill by the morning of the twelfth.

I reported all of this to Richard. I noted the description of the visitor — the ring, the grey horse with the crooked blaze — as useful for administrative identification. It did not occur to me to connect it to anyone I had met.

Richard was very still when I finished.

He said: "The children."

I said: "They seem well, though the eldest girl was rubbing her palms together as we spoke, complaining of a faint numbness as if she had slept upon her hands. I saw them playing in the yard afterward; they seemed vigorous enough."

Richard’s eyes sharpened. "Numbness? Not a fever?"

"No," I said. "Only a tingling of the skin, which she attributed to the autumn chill."

He nodded, his face grim. "They handled the coins briefly. Marsh handled them at length — at the table, then again to put them away, then twice more that evening by the fire, then however many times in the days following, as a man checks and rechecks the evidence of his own relief." He paused. "Master Aldred was precise about duration. Go back and read what he wrote."

I went back and read it. Brief handling produced mild symptoms or none. Sustained handling over hours and days produced the full progression.

I sat with that for a long moment.

Then I thought about the ring on the first finger of the left hand. The grey horse with the crooked blaze. The debt instrument in the February court rolls, Marsh's name against Crey of Goodramgate. The man at the miller's table who had not touched the common loaf, had not touched the table, had kept his hands in his lap throughout, and whom I had found so admirably cautious.

I went to find Richard.

He rode to Colby himself the following morning, and I went with him. He spoke with Margaret Marsh again, briefly, then asked to see the box.

She brought it from the shelf behind the bed. Richard crouched and examined the contents without touching them, then lifted the three newer coins into a square of doubled cloth and folded it closed.

I asked whether it was necessary to handle them that way.

He said: "After what you read last night, do you need to ask?"

I did not.

As we rode back he confirmed what I had already reasoned to, imperfectly, in the hour before I went to find him. White hellebore, prepared and applied to the coin faces and worked into the milled edges where it would gather and transfer most readily. Crey had handled them wearing cloth or covered hands — hence the untouched table, the unshaken hands, the refusal of anything his host had touched. Marsh had handled them repeatedly over two days, as a man does with settlement coin, with the physical evidence of a burden lifted: counted them, showed them to his children, returned to check them, turned them over by firelight.

He had absorbed enough across those two days to be beyond help by the morning of the twelfth.

"The children recovered," Richard said, "because a child's curiosity lasts a moment and a father's relief lasts as long as the coins remain in the box."

I thought of Master Aldred's letter. Of Richard filing it in the correspondence box rather than the general file. Of the difference between a man who reads a physician's account as local intelligence and a man who reads it as a key, and keeps it where keys are kept.

We were three miles from Aysgarth when Richard said: "He will be at Thornton."

I asked how he knew.

"He told Ash he had three more accounts in the riding before returning to York. Thornton is the next holding of any size on the York road, and there is a Crey listed in the Thornton court rolls from the spring — a disputed wool transaction. If he follows his stated purpose, he will be there now or within the day."

I said: "And if his stated purpose conceals another?"

Richard said: "Then there is someone in Thornton who held a debt against him. In which case we have very little time."

He said it quietly, without drama, which made it considerably worse. The debt instrument Marsh had held against Crey had not been surrendered. It still existed, enforceable, held now by Marsh's estate — by Margaret Marsh and her children, who had each briefly held the coins that killed their husband and father, and who did not know that the man who had sent those coins might consider them unfinished business.

I said as much to Richard.

He said: "Yes. I have sent a rider ahead."

I recalled then the moment before we had mounted our horses at Aysgarth, when Richard had paused to hand a sealed scrap of parchment to a stable-boy. He had instructed the lad to ride for Colby with all haste and tell the widow Marsh that she must surrender no papers—not a single leaf of the court record—to any man until we arrived. I had thought it at the time a mere excess of administrative caution, the habit of a man obsessed with the integrity of the rolls. I saw now it was the securing of a flank.

We did not speak again for the remainder of the ride.

We reached Thornton as the light was failing. The village was quiet in the way of places the mortality had visited heavily — not empty, but reduced, the remaining inhabitants moving with the careful economy of people who have learned not to expend more than they can recover.

Richard made his inquiries at the inn. Crey had been there. He had conducted his business with a tenant named Fowler that afternoon and had taken supper alone and retired early. He was expected to leave for York in the morning.

Richard asked which room.

The innkeeper said the eastern chamber, at the top of the stair.

Richard sat down at the common table and ordered supper. I sat opposite him and ate without appetite. The fire was low. Outside, the wind had come up off the moor.

After perhaps an hour, Richard said quietly: "He came downstairs twenty minutes ago. He has been standing in the passage behind you for the last five, watching us."

I did not turn around.

Richard raised his voice to a conversational level and said, apparently to me: "The February court rolls show a debt instrument held by Marsh against Crey of Goodramgate, arising from a wool dispute the previous autumn. The instrument was not surrendered — Marsh received payment and was dead before he could produce the document. It is held now by Marsh's estate. My rider reached the Marsh holding this afternoon. The document is in the estate house, and Margaret Marsh knows its value."

There was silence in the passage behind me.

Then Crey came around the table and sat down across from Richard with the composure of a man who has decided that composure is his best remaining option. He was as I remembered him from the miller's table — compact, careful, well-dressed. The seal ring was on the first finger of his left hand. His hands were folded on the table in front of him, not touching anything else.

He said: "You are from the de Holmes estate."

Richard said: "I am."

Crey said: "I have conducted no business with your estate."

Richard said: "No. Your business in this district was of a more personal character."

What followed was not a confession. Crey was too precise a man for confession. But he did not deny, either. He sat and listened as Richard set out the sequence — the arrival, the physician's account that had given him both his model and his cover, the visit to Colby, the coins prepared and carried for a single purpose, the extended handling that had killed Marsh while the brief handling of his children had not, the departure the morning after, the grey horse with the crooked blaze that Margaret Marsh had described to a clerk who had not yet understood what he was being told — and when Richard had finished, Crey was quiet for a long moment.

He said: "You have the coins."

Richard said: "I have the coins."

Crey said: "They will show nothing that a physician could testify to with certainty."

Richard said: "Master Aldred in Beverley might disagree. But no. I do not expect a court. I expect you to understand precisely what I know and precisely what I cannot prove, and to conduct yourself accordingly."

Crey looked at the table between them. Then he said, with the tone of a man closing a ledger: "Then we are at an impasse."

Richard said: "We are. I want you to understand that clearly. I have the structure of what you did, and the evidence of it, and no means of bringing either before a court that would act on them. You understand this."

Crey said he understood it.

Richard said: "Good. Then you also understand that I know your routes, and your methods, and the name of every man in the riding who might have held a debt against you. And that I maintain careful records. And that I have correspondents in York."

He said it as a man might state a professional habit. Crey looked at him for a long moment, then stood, settled his coat, and went back upstairs.

We heard him leave before dawn.

Richard presented the case to Sir Edmund Vasey in February of 1349. He set out the sequence without drama and named the mechanism and the motive. Sir Edmund listened carefully. He was not a stupid man, and he followed the argument without difficulty.

He said that without a physician's examination of the body — long buried — and without Crey in custody, the available evidence would not support a prosecution. He acknowledged Margaret Marsh's description of the visitor, and said that a widow's account of a ring and a horse, offered without independent corroboration against a York merchant of established standing, would not survive a challenge before any court in the riding. He said the mortality had produced a great number of unusual deaths and that reopening cases on inference, however carefully constructed, would produce chaos the administration could not absorb.

He was not unkind. He was honest about the limits of what institutions can hold when they are already failing under their own weight.

Richard thanked him and left.

He spent two years attempting to establish Crey's movements and the full history of the debt. He found fragments — the disputed sum in the York merchant rolls, the reference to Crey of Goodramgate in the wool contract, the record in Marsh's own hand from the February sitting. Nothing that could be assembled into proof before a court. The witnesses died. The records thinned.

He did not abandon the conclusion.

When I asked him, in the spring of 1351, whether it troubled him to leave the matter as it stood, he considered it seriously before answering.

"The structure of what occurred is not altered by the absence of a court to confirm it," he said. "Marsh was killed deliberately, by a man who understood that a general mortality provides both the method and the explanation. The arithmetic is sound. That no institution will act on it is a fact about institutions."

I asked whether he intended to continue.

He said he did not. The merchant was beyond reach, the record beyond repair, and continued pursuit would consume what remained of his attention without producing anything that could be used.

He added, after a moment, that he had retained a particular interest in travelling merchants. Their routes. Their recent transactions. The names of those they had lately visited and the nature of the business between them.

He said it as a man might mention a long-standing habit. Those who knew him understood it was something else entirely.

The parish register at Colby records Geoffrey Marsh as deceased of plague, consistent with all surrounding entries.

The three coins remain wrapped in cloth in the estate strongbox, where Richard has asked they be kept.

I asked him once why he wished to preserve evidence that could now prove nothing.

He said: "It proves everything. That is why it should be kept."

I had thought, in October of 1348, that his interest in Marsh's death was the pattern-finding of an exhausted mind. I had thought Crey's caution at the miller's table was prudence. I had thought Master Aldred's letter was medicine dressed as rumour. I had heard a widow describe a ring and a grey horse with a crooked blaze, and written it down for accounting purposes. I had found the debt instrument in the February rolls and considered it a matter of mercantile history.

The arithmetic was sound. I know that now.

Thomas Lacey
Clerk of the North Riding
Michaelmas, 1352
