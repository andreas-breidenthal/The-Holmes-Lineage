RICHARD DE HOLMES

~1330 — Yorkshire, England

Richard de Holmes was eighteen years old when the Black Death reached the North Riding in the autumn of 1348, and he observed its arrival not as a matter of doctrine or fear but as a disruption in systems he already understood. Labour, tenancy, and estate continuity were, to him, interlocking structures whose failure would propagate unless corrected at speed.

He responded with adjustments that ran ahead of custom: rents recalibrated before petitions were made, labour hired at revised rates without resistance, and demesne structures reorganised to reflect a new equilibrium in which labour had become scarce and land abundant. Where others saw moral collapse or divine judgment, he saw altered conditions requiring immediate rebalancing.

The estate survived the first wave of mortality with fewer losses than its neighbours. Those who observed him during this period noted that his attention consistently moved first to measurable facts, and only afterwards to interpretation. This habit, formed early, would later define his method in matters beyond estate management.

Certain Deaths in the Villages of Aysgarth

Recovered Among the Estate Papers at Michaelmas, 1352

The account that follows was preserved by Thomas Lacey, clerk of the North Riding, who served as Richard’s scribe during the mortality years. His record is neither neutral nor complete, but it preserves the structure of observation with unusual fidelity.

The autumn of 1348 brought the mortality into Aysgarth and its surrounding villages. What first appeared as a uniform visitation of plague revealed, under closer inspection, irregularities in sequence and character that Richard de Holmes began to note in his estate records.

He traced the movement of death across holdings not as a theological event but as a sequence of interactions between persons, routes, and obligations. Where others recorded only outcome, he recorded adjacency: who had travelled where, who had attended which court, who had received visitors in the days preceding illness.

In one case, that of Geoffrey Marsh of Colby, he identified a deviation from the expected pattern of plague mortality. The illness did not present with the standard swellings recorded in monastic accounts, but with a rapid fever and haemorrhagic symptoms preceding death within days.

The inquiry that followed, conducted between field obligations and estate management, reconstructed a sequence of contact involving a travelling merchant from York, an unrecorded debt, and a convergence of opportunity created by the general disruption of the mortality period.

The matter was presented to Sir Edmund Vasey, justice of the peace for the North Riding, in February 1349. It was not pursued further. The absence of surviving witnesses, combined with the administrative overload of the period, rendered the case effectively non-recoverable.

Richard continued to pursue corroboration for two years, without success. The witnesses died, records thinned, and the evidentiary chain dissolved under the continued pressure of mortality. What remained was a structure of inference without institutional support.

He did not abandon the conclusion.

He stated, when pressed, that absence of resolution did not constitute absence of explanation, and that arithmetic consistency retained validity even when legal confirmation could not be obtained.

In later years, he continued to observe travelling merchants with particular attention, noting routes, intermediaries, and recent transactions as a matter of routine courtesy rather than formal inquiry. Those who knew him understood the distinction.

The estate records show no formal reopening of the case. The parish register at Colby records Geoffrey Marsh as deceased of plague, consistent with all surrounding entries.

Thomas Lacey’s concluding remark remains the only surviving interpretive judgment:

He was right. The arithmetic was sound.
