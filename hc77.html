HOLKOS THE BYZANTINE

~290 AD — Byzantium

Holkos the Byzantine reached the height of his career in the city that was still being built around him — Constantinople in the 350s and 360s, its great churches and colonnaded streets not yet finished, the smell of new stone everywhere, the sense of a world being deliberately constructed rather than gradually accumulated. He was among the first generation for whom the city was simply home rather than a transformation, and he brought to it the analytical habits of a family that had been reading the world’s changes since before Greece existed.

He taught at one of the schools attached to the new ecclesiastical foundations of the city, where the curriculum combined the classical philosophical tradition with the Christian intellectual culture that was now the empire’s official framework — a synthesis that was still being worked out in his lifetime and that produced, in those willing to engage with both traditions honestly, a habit of rigorous inquiry that owed as much to Plato and Aristotle as to the Gospels. He was known principally as a teacher — the kind remembered not for what he said but for the questions he asked, the ones that left students unable to rest until they had found the answer. He did not tell his students what to think. He showed them how, which was the harder gift, and the more durable one.

The record of his most significant intervention in public affairs survives in the account of his student Anastasios, preserved in a set of teaching notes later copied into the archive of a church school in Constantinople. The document bears signs of repeated copying, yet the essential exchange is preserved with sufficient clarity to reconstruct both the method and the reasoning that followed.

The account of Holkos of Constantinople

Set down by his student Anastasios, from notes preserved among the teaching records of the Scholē tou Hagiou Paulou, copied in the reign of Theodosius. What follows is reconstructed from those notes and from my own memory of the events, which I have endeavoured to set down with fidelity to the truth, though I confess that some exchanges I have rendered from impression rather than verbatim recollection, the hour being late and my stylus slow.

Constantinople smelled, in those years, of two things simultaneously: the lime-dust of construction and the cedar-oil of the lamp-shops that lined the Mese. You could not walk from the Milion to the Forum of Constantine without inhaling both — the empire’s future and its administrative present — and Holkos claimed that a man who could not distinguish those two smells by origin and proportion had no business forming opinions about the city’s character.

He made this observation to me one morning in the early spring of the year in which this account begins, standing at the window of the upper room we shared in a building near the scholē, watching the mule-carts bringing dressed stone toward the unfinished precinct of the great church. He had been in what I had learned, over three years as his student, to recognise as his difficult season — the interval between significant problems, when his mind, finding nothing sufficient to engage it, turned upon itself with a kind of slow corrosive patience. He had spent the previous week producing a complete grammatical analysis of the Septuagint’s rendering of Job, not because he had any theological interest in the question, but because he required the mental labour. He had eaten perhaps once each day. He had spoken to me only when I brought him food, and then only to note, without apparent interest, whether I had brought the right thing.

The morning I describe, he had woken early and was already at the window when I rose, wrapped in his dark teaching robe, his long fingers working a small smooth stone around and around in his palm — a habit I had come to associate with the preliminary stages of genuine engagement. His eyes were remarkable. People remarked upon them constantly and he found this tiresome. They were the eyes of a man who was always, in some portion of himself, reading something that was not present in the room.

“Anastasios,” he said, without turning from the window, “you have been standing there for some time. You want to tell me something but have decided, incorrectly, that the timing is wrong.”

I had, in fact, been trying to determine whether he was in a state to receive news. He was not always. “There is a man waiting below,” I said. “He has been waiting since before dawn. He is a notary of the imperial chancery — I could tell by the ink on his fingers and the particular cut of his—”

“I know the cut,” Holkos said. “What does he want?”

“He would not say to me. He said the matter was one for the teacher himself.”

Holkos turned from the window. The corrosive patience in his face had changed to something else — a sharpening, like a stylus being brought to a better point. He set down the smooth stone on the windowsill with a careful precision that I recognised as suppressed excitement.

“Send him up,” he said. “And bring the lamp with the cedar oil — the small one, not the fish-oil lamp. If this man is a notary of the chancery, we should give him the professional courtesy of a familiar smell.”

The notary’s name was Philippos. He was perhaps fifty years old, built with the compact solidity of a man who had spent decades carrying the weight of institutional secrets, and he sat across from Holkos with the careful posture of someone managing, through long practice, an emotion he considered beneath his dignity to display. Fear, I thought. Or something adjacent to it — the particular anxiety of a man who has understood that something is wrong in the machinery he tends, and cannot determine precisely where.

He had brought a document case. He set it on the table between them without opening it.

“Teacher,” he said — the forms were important to him; I had noted that he had used the correct form of address even when speaking to me on the stairs — “I am told you are a man who reads accurately.”

“I am a man who teaches reading accurately,” Holkos said. “The distinction matters. What do you read in that case?”

Philippos looked at the case. “Documents from the imperial chancery. Seven of them. Land deeds, principally — two registrations of inheritance, one transfer of estate between living parties, and four confirmations of earlier grants. All authenticated in the standard manner. All bearing the correct seals and subscriptions.”

“And all incorrect,” Holkos said.

Philippos’s hands were very still. “You cannot know that. I haven’t shown them to you.”

“No. But you have shown me yourself.” Holkos leaned back in his chair with the ease of a man who has already completed the calculation and is merely waiting for the other party to catch up. “You are a senior notary of the chancery — a man, I think, responsible for registration rather than drafting, which means you spend your days verifying that the documents produced by others conform to the standards of your office. You are very good at this work. The care with which you have handled that case, the way you set it down — not placed, but set — tells me that the contents are both valuable and troubling to you personally. If they were merely incorrect in a routine administrative sense, you would have referred the matter through normal channels; a senior notary does not consult a scholē teacher about a simple scribal error. Therefore the incorrectness is of a kind that the normal channels cannot address. Either because the forger has anticipated those channels, or because — and I suspect this is the more disturbing of the two possibilities — the forger is working within them.”

The silence that followed was of the particular quality produced when a man hears something he has feared articulated clearly for the first time. Philippos looked at Holkos the way students sometimes looked at him mid-lecture: with the combined expression of relief and unease that accompanies the recognition that someone else has already been where you are afraid to go.

“Open the case,” Holkos said, not unkindly.

I will not reproduce here a complete account of the documents themselves, both because the technical details of their production are difficult to convey to a reader unfamiliar with the specific conventions of chancery notation, and because Holkos himself cautioned me, when I asked to copy his examination notes, that some specificity was better omitted from any document likely to be widely read. He said: a detailed account of how a lock was picked is less a history than an instruction. I have honoured his caution.

What I will say is this: the documents were, as Philippos had said, correctly formed in every particular that a normal examination would check. The seals were proper. The subscriptions were in the correct hands — or appeared to be. The formulaic language was exactly as it should be, down to the abbreviated forms that varied by period and office. A thorough inspector, checking against the standard criteria, would have found them satisfactory.

Holkos did not check against the standard criteria. He examined them the way he had once, in a lecture I will never forget, described the proper method of reading any text: not asking what it says, but asking why it says it in precisely this way rather than another. Every choice is a choice. Every choice has a reason. Find the reason and you find the man.

He was at the table for perhaps two hours. He did not speak. He had the lamp I brought — the cedar-oil lamp — positioned to his left, and he held each document at varying angles to the light, sometimes turning them and examining the verso, sometimes laying two documents side by side and moving his finger between them with the slow methodical movement of a man comparing musical notations. He asked me, at one point, to bring him a bowl of water. He held a corner of one document above the steam and watched what happened to the surface of the ink. He noted this in his own hand on a wax tablet and then said nothing further for another hour.

Philippos sat and watched. I sat and watched Philippos watch. The lamp burned and the smell of cedar filled the small room and somewhere below in the street a gate-hinge, badly maintained and never repaired despite repeated notice to the building’s owner, gave its habitual irregular complaint as the morning traffic increased. Holkos appeared not to hear any of this. His eyes moved between the documents with the dispassionate attention of a man examining a theorem.

Finally he straightened, set the last document back in the case with the same careful precision with which Philippos had removed them, and folded his hands.

“Seven documents,” he said. “Produced, in my estimation, by one hand working over a period of at least two years, possibly three. Not a copyist’s hand — not someone skilled in the mechanical production of text, though the mechanical production is flawless. A thinker’s hand. Someone who understood the system he was producing documents for before he began producing them. He knew what would be checked. He accommodated every check. But in accommodating every check, he revealed himself, because the act of accommodation left traces that a normal document, produced without awareness of being examined, does not leave.”

“What traces?” Philippos asked.

“The care is the trace. A genuine document produced by a working notary has the qualities of a mind moving through familiar territory — small efficiencies, habitual abbreviations that vary slightly depending on the hour of the day, minor irregularities of ink pressure that correspond to the natural variation of a hand working quickly. These documents are too consistent. Not in the obvious sense — he was clever enough to introduce superficial variations. But the consistency of intent underlies everything. Every decision is optimised. A real document has the texture of ordinary human labour. These have the texture of argument.”

He paused. Outside, the gate-hinge sounded again. The cedared air was very still.

“Whoever made these,” Holkos said, “is not a forger in the common sense of the word. He is not a craftsman who has learned to copy. He is a man who studied the system, understood its logic completely, and then constructed documents that satisfy the system’s logic more purely than genuine documents do. He does not mimic. He perfects.”

Philippos was quiet for a moment. “Can the forgeries be proved?”

“To my satisfaction, yes. To the satisfaction of a legal proceeding, working from the established methods of documentary examination — I am less certain. The established methods check for the things he has already addressed. To demonstrate his method, you would need to explain the absence of imperfection, which is a more delicate argument than the presence of error.”

What followed occupied, in its initial phase, some weeks. I will compress it.

Philippos returned three times to the upper room with additional documents — not further forgeries, but genuine examples drawn from the same period and office, to allow Holkos to extend his comparative study. Each time he came, Holkos examined what was brought and then sent him away with questions to which Philippos returned, on his next visit, with answers. The questions concerned the administrative processes: at what stage each type of document moved through the office, which hands were required to touch it, what the intervals were between drafting and authentication, how disputes about registered deeds were normally resolved.

I understood, from watching this, that Holkos was not merely examining the documents but mapping the system. He was constructing, in his mind, a model of the chancery’s operation — how work moved through it, where the pressures and delays fell, what opportunities existed for a document to be substituted or altered between one stage and another. He said to me, during one of these intervals, while he was annotating his wax tablets by lamplight: “The forger did not attack the documents directly. He attacked the system’s assumptions. To understand his method, I must first understand what he understood. I must think as he thought.”

I asked him whether that was not a dangerous way to approach the problem — to think as the criminal thought.

He looked at me with the expression he used when a student had asked a question whose premise required examination before the question itself could be addressed. “Every genuine act of understanding requires temporarily inhabiting the mind you are trying to understand,” he said. “The danger is not in the temporary residence. The danger is in failing to come back out. I have not yet found that difficult. Though I will admit,” and here he returned to his tablets, “that this particular mind is more comfortable to inhabit than most. It is orderly in there. Pleasingly orderly.”

The question of the forger’s identity did not become clear to Holkos through any single dramatic recognition. It came through accumulation — the gradual convergence of small observations toward a point that had, in retrospect, been inevitable from the beginning. I will record the moment of convergence as I witnessed it, which was in the upper room, on a grey afternoon, when Holkos set down his stylus and sat very still for perhaps ten minutes without speaking.

Then: “Anastasios. The seventh document — the one confirming the old estate transfer from the Bithynian register. What do you notice about the confirmation clause?”

I examined it. I had, by this point, spent enough time in proximity to Holkos’s method to know that noticing required more than reading. I looked at the clause carefully. “The formula is correct,” I said. “Standard confirmation, the right subscript abbreviation—”

“The abbreviation,” Holkos said. “Yes. Which abbreviation?”

I looked again. “The contracted form. Conf. With the standard suspension mark.”

“Contracted form.” He was very still. “Now look at the fifth document. The registration of the Thracian inheritance.”

I looked. “The same contraction.”

“And the second document. The estate transfer between living parties.”

“The same.”

“Good. Now. These three documents were, according to Philippos’s account of the chancery’s process, produced in different offices by different hands, some years apart. The convention for that particular abbreviation had, over that span of years, varied. The office that produced the Thracian inheritance document should have used the older, uncontracted form. The office that produced the Bithynian confirmation should have used the newer contracted form but with a different suspension mark — the diagonal rather than the horizontal. Our forger knew both conventions. He chose, consistently, the contracted horizontal. It is his preferred form. The form his own hand returns to when the task of imitation is nearly complete and some small portion of attention has been released.”

I said, slowly: “You know whose preferred form that is.”

“I have been corresponding,” Holkos said, “with three chancery teachers and two retired senior notaries, in the course of establishing what conventions applied to which offices and periods. One of them — a man named Dorotheos, who teaches documentary form at one of the other scholai — has been notably more helpful than the others. He has answered every question I have asked with a precision and completeness that initially I attributed to particular diligence. I now attribute it to something else. He has been, in effect, monitoring my progress. Checking, through my questions, what I have understood and what I have not yet reached. He has been reading my investigation the way I have been reading his documents.”

The cedar lamp had burned low. I trimmed it without thinking, an automatic domestic action. The sound of the street below had diminished with the afternoon.

“He is the forger,” I said.

“He has the right kind of mind,” Holkos said. “He understands the system completely — he teaches it. He has access to the necessary materials and knowledge. He has a preference for the contracted horizontal form. And he has, for the past several weeks, been attempting, through the precision of his answers, to establish exactly the boundaries of my knowledge — what I can prove and what I cannot. He has been doing so with considerable skill.” He paused. “I have enjoyed the correspondence.”

I argued against it. I argued for some time, and with, I believed, considerable force. Holkos listened to all of my arguments with the attentive courtesy he always displayed when he had already decided something and was allowing the other party to exhaust themselves before explaining why.

“You want to meet him,” I said, having reached the end of my arguments. “Knowing that he is dangerous — knowing that men who operate through criminal arrangement, of whatever form, do not receive gracefully the attention of someone who can identify them—”

“I don’t want to meet a forger,” Holkos said. “I want to meet a mind. The forger is incidental.” He considered this. “Or rather — the forger is the mind, in this case. That is unusual. Usually the crime is external to the criminal. A man commits a fraud for money, for advantage, for revenge. The crime is a means. In this case, I believe the crime is the thing itself. He is not forging documents in order to achieve something. He has constructed an elaborate, invisible argument with the system of documentation itself. He wants to know whether the system can be beaten. He has beaten it. He wants to know if anyone noticed.”

“And you noticed.”

“And I noticed.” Holkos stood from the table and moved to the window, resuming the posture in which I had found him on the morning Philippos first came. “He has been waiting for me, Anastasios. The correspondence makes this clear. He did not know who I was when I began making enquiries, but he identified me — identified the quality of the enquiries — almost immediately. He has been, since then, conducting his own examination. Of me. We have been examining each other through the medium of polite letters about archival convention.” He paused. “It would be discourteous not to meet.”

The meeting was arranged, through a further exchange of correspondence whose surface concerned a question about a pre-Constantinian documentary form, in the library of Dorotheos’s scholē — a room that was, I thought when we arrived, precisely the kind of environment its occupant would choose. It had the quality of extreme order imposed on large quantities of material: codices and document rolls arranged with a regularity that was not merely practical but declarative, as if the room itself were an argument about the proper relationship between knowledge and its organisation. The smell was of old papyrus and of the particular preservative oil used in well-maintained archives — a smell I had encountered in only a few places in Constantinople, all of them institutions with very long memories.

Dorotheos was, by appearance, unremarkable. That is the first and most significant thing I can record about him. He was perhaps sixty, with the kind of face that resists description — not ugly, not handsome, simply a face that fulfilled its function without declaring anything beyond it. His hands were a scholar’s hands, ink-marked and careful. He rose from his desk when we entered, and his expression was that of a man welcoming colleagues with whom he has had a productive correspondence: pleasant, interested, entirely composed.

“Teacher Holkos,” he said. “Your student. Please, sit.”

There was a document on the desk between them. A deed — a land registration, I could see, though I could not read the text from where I stood. Dorotheos had placed it there deliberately, as a host places on the table the dish he intends to serve.

“I thought it might be useful,” Dorotheos said, settling back into his chair with the ease of a man on his own ground, “to have a concrete object for our examination. Rather than conducting it at one remove, through correspondence.”

“Very practical,” Holkos said. He looked at the document without moving to examine it. “Is it one of yours?”

“It is genuine,” Dorotheos said. “I thought that was the correct choice. To begin with something genuine.”

There was a quality to their exchange that I will attempt to describe though I am uncertain I have the vocabulary for it. It was not hostile. It was not even precisely tense. It was the quality of an encounter between two very similar instruments that have been precisely calibrated against different standards, and are now, for the first time, placed in direct proximity to allow comparison. Each man was measuring the other. Each man knew he was being measured. Neither appeared troubled by this.

“You chose the contracted horizontal form,” Holkos said, still not touching the document. “Consistently. It is your natural form, I think — the one your hand returns to when attention is distributed elsewhere.”

“A reasonable inference.” Dorotheos’s tone was exactly the tone of a scholar discussing a textual variant — professionally interested, not personally invested. “You are correct that it is my natural form. I am aware that it became so. I attempted, in the earlier documents, to vary it. I concluded, eventually, that variation introduced its own pattern. Consistent use of a natural form is, paradoxically, less identifying than inconsistent suppression of it.”

“You concluded correctly.”

“And yet you identified it.”

“I identified it because I was not using the standard criteria. I was looking for what was not wrong, which is a different task from looking for what is wrong. Your documents are correct in every way that the standard examination checks. They are too correct, which is invisible to the standard check but apparent to a different kind of attention.”

Dorotheos considered this with the undisguised pleasure of a man hearing a good argument. “You are saying that perfection is itself a defect.”

“I am saying that perfection, in a context that normally produces imperfection, is an anachronism. It stands out precisely because it does not belong.”

The afternoon light came through the library’s high window at a long angle, crossing the desk and illuminating the deed between them. Dorotheos looked at it for a moment, then at Holkos.

“What can you prove?” he asked. The question was entirely direct — the directness, I thought, of a man who respects precision too much to approach it obliquely.

“To the satisfaction of a rigorous philosophical argument: everything I have described. To the satisfaction of a legal proceeding, in the current state of documentary jurisprudence: very little. The argument from the absence of imperfection is not a form of proof that the existing examination procedures recognise. A court would require a positive demonstration of falsification — a specific error, a specific anachronism, a forged seal or a subscribed hand proved to be wrong. I have found no such error. You made none.”

“No,” Dorotheos agreed. “I made none.”

“The seven documents represent, in aggregate, a significant transfer of land rights to parties who were, I infer, associated with interests you were serving. The scale of the fraud is considerable.”

“The scale of the problem was considerable,” Dorotheos said. “The solution required to be commensurate.”

There was a pause. The light moved on the desk. Somewhere in the scholē below, I could hear the sound of students reciting — the rhythmic communal voice of a class working through a text together.

“Who are they?” Holkos asked. “The interests you served.”

“You know I won’t answer that.”

“I know. I asked because a complete examination requires the question to be posed, even when the answer is not available.”

Dorotheos almost smiled. “You are very thorough.”

“You are thorough. I am thorough in response.” Holkos was quiet for a moment. “You knew someone would eventually notice. That was part of it.”

It was not a question. Dorotheos treated it as the accurate observation it was. “I have been producing documents for eleven years,” he said. “They have passed every examination conducted upon them. They will continue to do so, in all probability, because the examination procedures do not ask the question you asked. You asked it because you think differently from the procedures. There are very few people who think as you think.”

“And yet one found you.”

“And yet one found you,” Dorotheos agreed, gently transposing the observation in a way that made its full meaning available.

Holkos looked at him. Then he looked at the deed on the desk, which remained between them, undisturbed, the afternoon light lying across its surface like a judgment.

“You should know,” Holkos said, “that I have conveyed my analysis, in general terms, to Philippos. He is aware that the standard examination procedures are insufficient for the kind of work you have done. He is, I believe, in the process of advocating for the development of new examination criteria — criteria of the kind that would, eventually, produce the result you see before you now.”

“I see before me,” Dorotheos said carefully, “a teacher and his student, having a conversation about documentary history.”

“Yes.”

“And the analysis you have conveyed to Philippos — it is, as you say, general in nature.”

“It addresses the systemic vulnerability. It does not, at this stage, identify a specific individual.”

Dorotheos was very still. He had the stillness of a man who has understood the precise scope of what has just been placed before him, and is completing the calculation that determines what it means.

“You have a student with you,” he said.

“I do.”

“He will record this.”

“He records everything. It is his function, and his gift.”

Dorotheos looked at me for the first time in the conversation — directly, with the same assessing quality he had brought to Holkos. I held the look because I thought it was required of me, though I will admit that the look was uncomfortable. It was the look of a man reading a text and deciding how seriously to take what he reads.

“What will he record that you permit him to record?” Dorotheos asked.

“That is, ultimately, his decision,” Holkos said. “Though he generally takes my advice in matters of editorial judgment.”

The light had moved further. The voices of the students below had changed timbre — the recitation had ended, replaced by the quieter sound of individual work.

“You have not told me what you intend,” Dorotheos said.

“No.”

“You intend for me to know that you exist. That a person exists who asks the question you asked, and will in future ask it more systematically. That the system is being developed that will, eventually, see what you have seen.”

“I intend for you to know what is true,” Holkos said. “What you do with true information is your own concern.”

He rose from the chair. He did not extend his hand — the gesture would have been, I think, wrong in some way that neither of them would have been comfortable articulating. He looked at Dorotheos for a moment with the evaluating attention he brought to any problem of genuine interest, and Dorotheos returned the look with the same quality.

“Your correspondence has been,” Holkos said, “the most instructive I have received in some years.”

“And yours,” said Dorotheos, “has been the only correspondence in eleven years that I was uncertain how to answer.”

We walked back along the Mese as the afternoon light failed, the smell of construction lime fading with the heat and the cedar-oil lamps beginning to show in the shop fronts on either side. I waited until we were clear of the scholē’s precinct before I spoke.

“You let him go,” I said.

“I reached the limits of what I can prove,” Holkos said. “Those are not the same thing.”

“You know who he is. You know what he did. Philippos has the authority to bring a formal accusation—”

“Philippos has the authority to bring a formal accusation that would fail,” Holkos said, with a patience that was not unkind. “Because the proof I have does not satisfy the existing criteria for proof. And a failed accusation against a teacher of the chancery, without evidence of the type the proceedings require, would accomplish several things: it would warn the interests he has served, who would then have time to make their arrangements; it would discredit Philippos for bringing an unsubstantiated case; it would discredit me; and it would teach Dorotheos exactly which aspects of his method I was unable to break, which would allow him to make those aspects stronger.” He paused. “A prosecution I cannot win is not justice. It is a very expensive way of making the problem worse.”

“And instead?”

“Instead, he knows that I exist. He knows the kind of attention that will now be applied to the system he has been exploiting. Philippos knows that the system requires reform and will advocate for it. New examination criteria will be developed, of the kind that would identify this type of work. In five years, perhaps ten, the vulnerability he exploited will be substantially closed.” He walked for a moment in silence. “And he knows all of this. I made certain he understood it.”

“You warned him.”

“I informed him of the state of the world.” He glanced at me. “It is not the same thing, Anastasios.”

I considered this for some time as we walked. The gate-hinge of our building, when we reached it, made its habitual complaint as Holkos pushed through, and he paused for a moment — which he had not done before — and looked at the hinge with the attention he brought to things that had, suddenly, become data.

“Someone should repair that,” he said. “I have been hearing it for three years and have always meant to notice it properly.” He looked at it a moment longer. “In the morning, remind me to speak to the owner.”

He went up the stairs. I followed, with my notes and my uncertainties and the memory of two men in a library, each completely mapped by the other, neither having yielded anything the other could use. I sat at my own small table by the window and wrote, by the light of the cedar lamp, what I have set down here — which is, as Holkos would say, not everything, but everything that is true.

The rest is editorial judgment. And that, he tells me, is the writer’s proper burden.

Here ends the account of Anastasios, student of the scholē. The copyist notes that several passages in the original were damaged by water and have been restored by inference from context, with care taken to preserve the sense where the letter was lost. Whether this is what the author would have wished, the copyist cannot say. He proceeds on the assumption that a teacher who instructed his students to read carefully would have wanted the same care applied to his own words.
