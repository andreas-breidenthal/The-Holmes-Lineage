RAGNARR HOLMR

~795 AD — Vestfold, Norway

The Viking Age began, by the reckoning of those who lived through it, in the summer of 793, when the longships appeared off the coast of Lindisfarne and the monks of that holy island learned what the men of the north had become. Ragnarr was born into the world the raids had made — the generation for whom raiding was not a new and shocking thing but simply the condition of their age, the background noise of an expanding Norse world. The expeditions departing from Vestfold were the stories of his childhood, and he grew up building from those accounts a detailed understanding of what the raids were actually for, what they found, and what they left undone.

He did not go raiding himself — he was not that kind of man, and he understood his own value clearly enough to know that it was not enhanced by a sword. He stayed in Vestfold, managing the networks of intelligence and supply that the raiding expeditions depended on more than they admitted, and ensuring that his son grew up understanding both the world that was passing and the one that was arriving. He gave Sigurðr the Norse world at its height — and the analytical tools to survive whatever came next.

The account that follows is taken from the preserved writings of Þórir of Tønsberg, a clerk attached to a small but increasingly formalised house of records maintained under the jarl’s oversight. Þórir wrote in a mixed fashion — part memory, part immediate notation — and was not trained in the same habits of restraint as Þorvald in the preceding generation. Where he understood, he explains more than necessary; where he did not, he records faithfully what he was told. His account survives in a fragmentary state, and what is presented here preserves his sequence while smoothing only those gaps that obscure rather than illuminate the chain of events.

A record set down by Þórir of Tønsberg

The fog came off the fjord that morning as it always does in the last weeks of summer — not cleanly, not quickly, but in slow grey masses that clung to the boathouses and turned the familiar outlines of the harbour into suggestions of themselves. I had been at my work since before first light, copying a tally of the season’s provisions into the jarl’s record, when the factor Ketill Ormsson arrived at the hall door with his clothes still salt-stiff and his face arranged in the expression of a man who has survived something terrible and has not yet decided exactly what it was.

I noticed the expression because I notice faces — it is, I suppose, the clerk’s equivalent of what Ragnarr does with everything else. But I could not have told you what was wrong with it then. I only knew there was something.

Ketill’s report, delivered first to the jarl’s steward and then, at the steward’s insistence, to the assembled household, was this: he had departed Tønsberg twelve days prior with a cargo of Frankish silver — payment for furs already delivered south the previous season, and now returning north in coin — along with three bales of prime winter fox taken in trade at a settlement along the way. The ship had been his own small knarr, crewed by four men he had sailed with before. On the ninth day out, some thirty miles south of the Hvaler islands, they had been set upon by two longships moving fast out of a morning haze. The attack had been swift and merciless. His men were dead. The silver and the furs were gone. He alone had survived by going into the water and clinging to floating wreckage until a fishing vessel out of Sarpsborg had pulled him from the sea half-dead of cold.

The steward received this with the gravity appropriate to a significant loss — the silver was not a trivial sum, and the jarl had expectations attached to it — and announced that enquiries would be made among the coastal settlements for any account of two longships moving in the area. This was the correct and expected response. It was also, as I would shortly learn, entirely useless.

I should explain how Ragnarr came to be involved, since his involvement was not automatic. He held no official position in the jarl’s household — held no position of any formal kind, in truth, though the jarl had long since ceased pretending that this meant he was not consulted on matters where official positions had proven insufficient. He maintained a long-house at the edge of the settlement, slightly apart from the main cluster of buildings, and he lived there with his son Sigurðr, who was then perhaps twelve years old and already possessed of his father’s unnerving habit of watching people’s faces while they spoke. Ragnarr was called upon when the steward had exhausted the possibilities that the steward’s methods could reach. He was not always pleased to be called upon.

He was sitting, when I arrived at his door, in the particular attitude of vacancy that I had learned to distinguish from actual inattention — his eyes fixed on the middle distance, his long fingers interlaced across his chest, his breathing so shallow and even that Sigurðr, who was seated nearby with a piece of carved antler he was supposed to be shaping into a gaming piece, glanced at me with the slight grimace of a boy who has learned to recognise his father’s moods and gauge their depth.

“A factor,” Ragnarr said, without moving or looking at me. “Ketill Ormsson. He has reported a pirate attack.”

I had not yet spoken. I said so.

“You were walking faster than usual,” he said. “You walk quickly when someone has given you something to carry that you cannot assess. And the steward sent you rather than one of the household men, which means this involves something the steward considers to require — ” he paused, and a faint something crossed his face that I might, if I were being generous, call a smile “ — a particular quality of attention.”

He came. He always came. Whatever he told himself about the consumption of his energies, the problem was already working on him, and he knew it.

Ketill Ormsson was being kept comfortable in a side room of the hall — dry clothes, hot broth, the general atmosphere of a man who has suffered and is being recognized as having suffered. He looked up when Ragnarr entered. I watched him look. Something shifted in his expression, so briefly that I could not have named it, and then it was gone and he was simply a tired and shaken man regarding a stranger.

Ragnarr sat down across from him. He did not introduce himself. He looked at Ketill for a long moment with the expression I can only describe as mathematical — as though Ketill were a set of quantities to be evaluated rather than a person to be spoken to — and then he said, “Tell me about the wreckage.”

Ketill told him about the wreckage. He was fluent and detailed. The longships had come from the east. His men had fought but been overwhelmed in moments. He had gone over the side and swum clear of the killing. The wreckage he had clung to was from his own ship’s prow, which had been broken in the attack.

“The prow,” Ragnarr said.

“Yes.”

“A knarr’s prow.”

“Yes.”

“Made of — ?”

Ketill described the timber. Ragnarr listened. Then he asked about the fishing vessel that had rescued him — its name, the name of the man who captained it, the harbour it had come from. Ketill answered each question without difficulty. Then Ragnarr stood, thanked him for his time with a courtesy that seemed entirely genuine, and walked out.

In the corridor, he stood still for a moment. Sigurðr, who had followed us uninvited and apparently invisibly, materialized at his father’s elbow.

“He was not cold,” Ragnarr said.

“No,” said Sigurðr.

“His hands.” Ragnarr glanced at me. “A man pulled from the sea in the last weeks of summer, after sufficient time in the water to constitute a survival ordeal, will show it in his hands above all — the skin, the colour, the way the joints move when he gestures. His hands were those of a man who had been inconvenienced by salt water, not half-killed by it. And his feet.”

“His feet,” I said.

“He walked into this hall, Þórir. He walked without the stiffness of a man whose lower extremities have been seriously chilled. I watched him cross the threshold. The gait of a man feigning recovery is not the same as the gait of a man achieving it.” He turned and walked toward the door. “I need to speak to three people. Come if you like. Stay if you prefer. Either way, have writing materials.”

I came.

What followed seemed, at the time, like a series of disconnected enquiries. Only later did it resolve into a single line of reasoning: the dates recorded at Dorestad, the log at Skagen, the mismatch between departure and arrival. The silver, as he would show, had arrived before it was stolen. Which meant it had never been stolen.

We went, the next morning, to the steward. Ragnarr laid out the account — the dates, the cargo, the impossibility of Ketill’s story. The steward listened, and being a competent man, followed the argument to its conclusion.

Ketill Ormsson was brought again into the hall. He looked at Ragnarr, and the expression I had not been able to name the day before remained on his face now, with nowhere to go. It was recognition.

He did not confess immediately. He attempted denial, protest, the invocation of his suffering. Ragnarr let him complete it. Then he placed the written account on the table between them and said nothing.

Ketill looked at it. Then he sat down. Then he told us where the silver was.

I asked Ragnarr afterward whether the crew would be pursued. He considered it.

“They participated,” he said. “But they did not know what I know. Send the enquiry if it must be sent. I would not expect it to find them.”

He walked back toward his long-house. Sigurðr fell into step beside him, and I saw the boy nod at something his father said — as though the answer had confirmed something he had already suspected.

I have recorded what I witnessed and what I was told. The methods I understand only partially. The conclusion I have verified. Ketill Ormsson made restitution, and the matter was entered in the jarl’s records as a case of fraud detected and resolved, the means of detection attributed to the diligence of the steward’s enquiries.

The steward, to his credit, looked slightly uncomfortable when this was read aloud.

I looked at what I was writing.

Some things belong in the official account. Others belong here.

Here ends the account of the factor and the silver. Þórir of Tønsberg set this down from memory and from his own notations within a fortnight of the events described, and attests to the accuracy of what is recorded, within the limits of his understanding of the same.
