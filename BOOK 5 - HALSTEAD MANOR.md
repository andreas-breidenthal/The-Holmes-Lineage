# BOOK V: HALSTEAD MANOR
## *~1090 AD – 1461*
### Yorkshire, England

---

*Sources: Nick Rennison (The Unauthorised Biography, 2005)*

*Entries marked ✦ are canonical or pastiche-documented. Entries marked ◇ are gap-fills — original composition consistent with the historical record. All children are listed under their parent's entry. Only the direct line receives a full entry.*

*This book bridges the Norse-Danelaw line of Book IV with the first named Holmes in any canonical source — Urkell de Holmes, recorded in the York Assizes of 1219. The three opening entries complete that bridge; the family seat at Halstead Manor is established, "Holmr" becomes "Holmes," and the Norman-French form "de Holmes" comes into use. From Urkell onward, the record is drawn from Rennison.*

---

### ◇ WILLIAM HOLMR
**~1120 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Robert Holmr. A generation after the Conquest, the family is consolidating under the new Norman order — holding its land, adapting its name. "Holmr" begins its drift toward the anglicised form.

---

### ◇ ROBERT HOLMR
**~1150 — Halstead, Yorkshire**

Son of William. The family seat at Halstead takes shape in this generation — land held, a house established, a name attaching itself to a place.

---

### ◇ GEOFFREY HOLMES
**~1180 — Halstead Manor, Yorkshire**

Son of Robert. "Holmes" fully anglicised from "Holmr." The family seat at Halstead Manor established. Rennison's *Unauthorised Biography* records that the family traces Norman-era ancestors — the line running forward through Sir Ralph Holmes, Sir Symonds Holmes, and Sir Richmond Holmes to Sherlock himself. Geoffrey Holmes is the man who gave that line its name and its place.

### ✦ URKELL DE HOLMES
**1219 — Yorkshire, England** · [REN]

The earliest named Holmes in any canonical or pastiche source. Mentioned in the records of the York Assizes in 1219 — a legal record, not a biography. His name is Norman-French in form: *de Holmes*, "of Holmes," indicating a family already settled on land bearing their name in Yorkshire. Nothing further is recorded of him. He is a name in a court roll and nothing more — but he is the first.

*Children:*
- **Thomas de Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

### ◇ THOMAS DE HOLMES
**~1240 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Urkell de Holmes. He held his land through the Baron's Wars of 1264–67 — the conflict between Henry III and Simon de Montfort — choosing, as Yorkshire gentry tended to, the safer course of local administration over baronial adventure. The gift here: recognising which quarrels were not his to join.

*Children:*
- **John de Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

---

### ◇ JOHN DE HOLMES
**~1270 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Thomas. The reign of Edward I — the Hammer of the Scots, builder of castles, collector of debts. John de Holmes served in the local court system as a minor judicial officer, a role that suited the family's analytical temperament. He helped administer the Statute of Winchester (1285), which reorganised local law enforcement. A man of the law rather than the sword, in an age that increasingly needed both.

*Children:*
- **William de Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

---

### ◇ WILLIAM DE HOLMES
**~1300 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of John. Edward I's Scottish wars were draining Yorkshire of men and money. William de Holmes was a wool man — the North Riding's great product — trading through the markets at York. He kept the estate solvent through the Scottish raids that followed Bannockburn (1314), when Bruce's armies burned deep into Yorkshire. A careful, methodical man who recorded everything.

*Children:*
- **Richard de Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

---

### ◇ RICHARD DE HOLMES
**~1330 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of William. The Black Death arrived in England in 1348. Richard de Holmes survived. He lost labourers to the plague and to the new mobility it created, but he read the changed economy quickly — adjusted rents, restructured the estate, hired where others wouldn't. The gift applied to crisis management. The land survived.

*Children:*
- **Robert de Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

---

### ◇ ROBERT DE HOLMES
**~1360 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Richard. Born in the shadow of the Black Death. Robert de Holmes consolidated and acquired, making the estate larger than his grandfather had left it. The Peasants' Revolt of 1381 barely touched Yorkshire; he watched it from a careful distance and made no enemies among his tenants.

*Children:*
- **Henry Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

---

### ◇ HENRY HOLMES
**~1390 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Robert. The name drops the Norman "de" — fully English now. The Lollard controversy was burning through the Church; Henry Holmes kept the family out of religious controversy entirely — a habit of analytical detachment that would express itself very differently in his descendants.

*Children:*
- **Edward Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

---

### ◇ EDWARD HOLMES
**~1420 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Henry. The reign of Henry V — Agincourt (1415), the conquest of Normandy. By the 1430s the English position in France was collapsing. He came home to a kingdom beginning to come apart, and raised a son canny enough to fight on the right side when the reckoning came.

*Children:*
- **Walter Holmes of Kirkbymoorside** — direct line; see Book VI.
