ABDI-MILKI

~1630 BC — Canaan, the Levant

I am servant of Milki — the divine royal title, the declaration of subordination to the celestial king that the Canaanite naming tradition expressed as devotion and the political tradition expressed as something more complicated. Abdi-Milki was born in Ashkelon to a father who had already become more Canaanite than Egyptian, and grew up in the full possession of the Levantine world — its multiplicity of languages and loyalties, its commercial sophistication and its embedded acceptance of the need to navigate between competing powers. The Late Bronze Age was, in the Levant, a period of extraordinary interconnection: Egyptian, Hittite, Babylonian, Aegean, and indigenous Canaanite interests overlapped in every significant city, every significant transaction, and every significant political arrangement.

He worked the routes between Egypt and the northern cities as a merchant-factor — not a great merchant himself, not a political power, but the person who made the transaction between them possible, the indispensable intermediary whose knowledge of both sides was the service he sold. He was attentive to surface detail in a way that had been cultivated through years of handling goods whose value depended on their condition — lapis, cedar, oil, and grain — and he learned to trust what could be physically verified over what was merely declared. He understood that trust in commerce is maintained not by certainty, but by the alignment of repeated verification, and that a man who depended on that alignment must learn where it could be disrupted. He was, by the evidence available, both commercially successful and something rarer: trusted. The merchant who is trusted by parties with competing interests is the merchant who survives the longest in a world where the competing interests periodically resolve themselves through violence, because trust is the only capital that retains its value when the political order collapses.

Among those who worked alongside him in Megiddo was a junior tally-scribe named Kiri, responsible for recording weights, seals, and transactions at the garrison receiving point. Kiri’s record is precise in what it records, but limited in what it questions; his attention follows the documents closely, yet hesitates when the implications of those documents extend beyond his immediate responsibility. His account preserves the sequence of events as they were observed and the questions that were asked, but also the points at which he did not yet understand why those questions mattered.

The Account of Kiri

I am a keeper of records. I say this not as a boast but as a limitation. What I record, I record precisely. What I do not understand, I record also, and leave the understanding to those who come after. This account concerns events that I witnessed in part, and was told in part, by a man whose method of understanding I cannot fully explain, only faithfully transcribe. I have hesitated for some months before committing these words to clay. I hesitate still. But the merchant Abdi-Milki told me once that a record not made is a truth already half-dead, and I have decided, in my small way, to agree with him.

Megiddo in the season of early rains smells of wet limestone and old cedar and something beneath both — something the garrison cooks call the breath of the city, which is the accumulated exhalation of ten generations living inside the same walls. The sky in those weeks is the colour of unpolished tin, and the light that comes through it is flat and directionless, the kind of light that makes shadows disappear and makes the faces of men difficult to read. I mention the light because Abdi-Milki mentioned it to me first, standing at the threshold of the receiving point on the morning this account begins, looking out at the lower city with an expression I had not yet learned to interpret.

“When a man cannot see his shadow,” he said, “he becomes careless about what he steps on.”

I wrote that down. I did not know why. I have since come to understand that it was his way of telling me something had already begun.

He had arrived in Megiddo six days earlier with a consignment of cedar oil from the northern coast and a smaller parcel of unworked lapis that he was handling on commission for a merchant house in Byblos. I had received the consignment myself: four sealed jars, the seals intact, the weights matching the manifest to within the margin that the administration allowed for evaporation in transit. He was not a difficult merchant to process. His documents were always in order, his weights were always honest, and he never attempted the small adjustments that the garrison clerks were accustomed to negotiating with less scrupulous factors. He was, for these reasons, regarded by my superior Harkhuf as a useful nuisance — useful because his honest consignments provided a standard against which other consignments could be measured, and a nuisance because his very honesty tended to make the other merchants’ adjustments more visible.

I had worked alongside him on three previous transit seasons and had come to understand certain things about him. He was perhaps forty years of age, possibly older — it was difficult to say, because his face had been worked on by sun and wind and the particular kind of sustained attention that gives some men an ageless quality, as though they have been worn to their essential form and have nothing left to lose to time. He was lean in the manner of a man who eats when the work is done rather than when the hour suggests it. His eyes were extraordinarily dark and possessed the unsettling quality of appearing to be focused on something approximately three handspans behind whatever they were looking at — as though the surface of a thing was merely the beginning of his examination of it.

He spoke Canaanite as a native, Egyptian with a facility that Harkhuf found mildly offensive, and enough Akkadian for administrative correspondence. He knew the weights of Ugarit, of Byblos, of the Delta, and of the Hittite trading posts, and could convert between them in his head with a speed that made the garrison clerks uncomfortable. He was, as I have said, trusted. This is rarer than it sounds.

On the morning I am describing, he was not examining a consignment. He was watching the lower city in the flat tin light, and when I came to stand beside him — partly because I was curious and partly because the alternative was to return to a disputed copper manifest that I had been avoiding for three days — he did not turn to acknowledge me for a long moment.

“Kiri,” he said finally, still looking outward. “What do you know of the elder Zakar-El?”

I knew several things about Zakar-El. He was one of the senior council members of Megiddo’s indigenous administration — not the governor, who was an Egyptian appointee, but one of the men who had managed the relationship between the city’s Canaanite population and its Egyptian administration for the past twenty years. He was wealthy in the manner of men who had been wealthy long enough that the wealth had become invisible, simply the texture of their existence. He had a house in the upper city with a gate whose bronze hinge I had noticed for its distinctive sound — a low, flat note, more groan than squeak, the sound of very old metal under very old weight. He was perhaps sixty years of age, and he had the face of a man who had made many decisions and was still, in some part of himself, living inside several of them.

I said some of this to Abdi-Milki.

He nodded. “And what do you know of a man called Abdi-Yapa? Not the king of Byblos — a smaller man than that. A trader in dried fish and olive oil who has been wintering in Megiddo’s lower market for the past four months.”

I knew less about Abdi-Yapa, but I knew the name. He had registered a small storage room in the lower market at the beginning of the winter season. His goods were unremarkable. He had not presented any consignments at the garrison receiving point, which meant his trade was purely local — or purely something else.

“I know the registration,” I said.

“Of course you do.” Abdi-Milki turned from the threshold at last. “You are a man who knows the registrations. This is why I am speaking to you rather than to someone else.”

He asked me to pull the storage registration for Abdi-Yapa, and then, without pausing, the transaction records for Zakar-El’s household steward for the past three months. I did not ask why he wanted them together. I had already begun to understand that his questions arrived in a particular sequence that only made sense once the sequence was complete, and that asking him to explain himself mid-sequence was like asking a man to describe the shape of a knot while he was still tying it.

The records took me the better part of the morning to locate. Megiddo’s administrative archive was organised according to a system that had been modified by three successive garrison commanders and two Egyptian-appointed scribal supervisors, each of whom had believed that the previous system was inadequate and each of whom had been transferred before completing their revision. The result was a cross-hatched accumulation of tablet formats, seal conventions, and filing categories that required the particular kind of institutional memory that junior tally-scribes develop when they stay in one place long enough.

When I spread the records before him on the counting table, Abdi-Milki examined them in silence for a time that felt longer than it probably was. He did not make notes. He did not speak. His fingers moved occasionally across the surface of a tablet without quite touching it, as though he were measuring something invisible above the clay.

“Here,” he said at last, pointing to a notation in the steward’s household accounts. “What is this entry?”

It was a monthly payment to an unnamed recipient — designated only by a symbol I recognised as a generic marker for an external obligation, the kind of notation a steward used when the nature of the payment was known to the household but not required to be explained to the administration. The amount was modest in absolute terms but regular with a precision that was itself unusual. Most irregular payments varied slightly. This one did not vary at all. It was the same weight of silver, rendered on the same day of each month, with the same symbol, for eleven consecutive months.

“A regular external payment,” I said. “The symbol is—”

“The symbol is deliberate.” Abdi-Milki straightened. “A steward who knows what a payment is for writes what it is for, even obliquely, because the oblique notation is itself a record of the category. A steward who uses a completely generic marker is a steward who has been instructed to record the fact of payment without any trace of its nature. This is not administrative discretion. This is administrative silence. Somebody is receiving this silver who cannot be named.”

I looked at the entry again. He was right that the marker was more generic than convention required.

“Abdi-Yapa’s storage registration,” he continued, “lists no outgoing trade goods for the entire four months of his residence. He arrived with dried fish and olive oil. The fish would have been consumed or spoiled by now. The oil records no sales. Yet he is still here, still paying his storage fee, still eating.” He paused. “A man who is not trading is either waiting for something or receiving something. Abdi-Yapa is receiving something. The question is whether the weight of what he receives matches the weight of what leaves Zakar-El’s household each month.”

I thought about this. “You think the payments in the steward’s account—”

“Are going to Abdi-Yapa. Yes. I thought so before I looked at the records. The records have confirmed it.” He said this without apparent satisfaction, as though the records had done nothing more than perform a function he had already assumed they would perform.

I asked him how he had thought so before examining the records.

He looked at me for a moment with the expression I had come to recognise as the one he wore when he was deciding whether an explanation was worth the time it would take.

“Three days ago,” he said, “I was in the lower market before the third hour. I observed Zakar-El’s steward entering Abdi-Yapa’s storage room. The steward is a man who wears the seal of Zakar-El’s household openly — he has no reason to conceal his errands, and on every other occasion I have seen him in the market, he moves without any particular attention to who might be watching. On this occasion, he looked at the street behind him before he entered. Once. Quickly. But once is enough, when it is a man who does not otherwise look.” Abdi-Milki picked up his cloak from the chair where he had placed it. “A man looks behind him when he is carrying something he would prefer not to be seen carrying.”

He told me, before he left, that he intended to speak with Abdi-Yapa. He told me this in the tone of a man informing a colleague of a plan, not in the tone of a man requesting permission or company. I understood that I was not invited. I also understood, from the particular quality of his voice when he said it, that this was not because he was dismissing me but because whatever was about to happen between him and Abdi-Yapa was a conversation that would be better preserved in its effects than in its details.

“If I do not return by the evening meal,” he said, pulling on his cloak, “tell Harkhuf that I have gone to the lower market and that he should not be concerned.”

“Should he be concerned?” I asked.

“No,” he said. “But telling him not to be will ensure that he is, which will ensure that someone checks. This is useful to me.”

I did not fully understand this at the time. I have since come to understand that he had, in that casual instruction, ensured himself a form of protection while maintaining the appearance of requiring none.

He returned before the evening meal. He sat down across from me at the counting table, accepted the cup of barley beer that I had left within his reach — he would not have asked for it, but he drank it without comment, which told me something about the afternoon — and then he looked at the cedar-beam ceiling of the receiving point for a time before he spoke.

What I set down here is the account he gave me. I cannot verify every detail of it. I record it as he told it, and I record also my own responses, because they are part of the truth of what was transmitted.

He had found Abdi-Yapa without difficulty. The man was in his storage room, doing very little with the oil jars. He was a man of perhaps fifty years, running somewhat to softness, with the look of someone who had once been a competent small trader and had discovered a more reliable income. He had not been pleased to see Abdi-Milki. He had been, Abdi-Milki said, immediately and visibly frightened — not the ordinary discomfort of a man who has something to hide, but the specific fear of a man who has built his safety on the assumption that no one is looking directly at him, and who has just discovered that someone is.

Abdi-Milki had been brief with him. He had explained, without apparent emotion, that he understood the nature of the monthly payments, that he understood approximately how long the arrangement had been in place, and that he was not there on behalf of the garrison administration, the Egyptian governor, or any other authority.

“Then who are you here on behalf of?” Abdi-Yapa had asked.

“Myself,” Abdi-Milki told him. “And the truth, which is the same thing.”

He had asked Abdi-Yapa what he held over Zakar-El.

Abdi-Yapa, he told me, had refused to say. He had become loud, then quiet, then loud again. He had threatened, obliquely, consequences that he did not name. He had finally, when Abdi-Milki showed no reaction to any of this, become very still.

“You already know,” Abdi-Yapa said.

“I know enough,” Abdi-Milki told him.

He did not, at that point, know anything specific. He knew the shape of it — a man paid to be silent is a man who knows something that another man would pay to keep silent — but he had not yet determined the content. He told Abdi-Yapa this directly, which I found, when he described it, both surprising and characteristic. Most men in his position would have pretended to more knowledge than they possessed. Abdi-Milki had told Abdi-Yapa precisely what he knew and precisely what he did not know, because, he explained to me, a man who feels that his interrogator already possesses the essential information has no reason to guard the details, whereas a man who suspects the interrogator is bluffing may guard everything.

What Abdi-Yapa told him:

Twenty-three years ago, in the period of significant political instability that had preceded the current Egyptian administrative arrangements, there had been a dispute in Megiddo between two families over water rights to a cistern on the boundary of their properties. The dispute was not unusual; such disputes were common in that period and most of them resolved themselves through negotiation or arbitration. This one had not. A young man from one family — the younger son, not the heir — had been found dead in the cistern. The official finding, recorded in whatever administrative record existed in that period, was drowning by misadventure.

Abdi-Yapa had been, twenty-three years ago, a water-carrier who worked the upper quarter. He had seen something. He had seen Zakar-El — then a young man of the rising merchant class, not yet an elder, not yet connected to the council — speaking with the dead man at the cistern’s edge, and then he had seen the dead man in the cistern, and he had seen Zakar-El walking away.

He had said nothing at the time. He was a water-carrier. The dead man’s family was less powerful than Zakar-El’s family. The administrative apparatus of that period was not one in which the testimony of a water-carrier outweighed the standing of an established merchant household. He had calculated, and his calculation had seemed to him correct, that the risk of speaking outweighed the benefit, and so he had not spoken.

He had left Megiddo. He had lived in other cities — Lachish, Gezer, briefly Ashkelon. He had moved in the way that men with inconvenient knowledge sometimes move, putting distance between themselves and the thing they know. And then, four months ago, he had returned to Megiddo, with his dried fish and his oil jars, and he had gone to Zakar-El’s house and asked for a private meeting, and the silver had begun.

When Abdi-Milki finished telling me this, he was quiet for a moment.

“What did you do?” I asked.

“I thanked him for his account,” he said, “and I left.”

I asked whether he had told Abdi-Yapa what he intended to do with what he had learned.

“No.”

“And what did you intend—”

But he had already raised his hand slightly — not a dismissive gesture, more the gesture of a man indicating that a question should wait — and I waited.

He asked me the following morning to accompany him to Zakar-El’s house. He did not explain why he wanted my presence; I believe now that he wanted a witness of a particular kind — not an official, not a person of consequence, but a person whose function was the faithful recording of weights and transactions, and who would therefore record what he was about to do with the same fidelity that he applied to copper manifests.

Zakar-El’s house was in the upper city, as I have said. It was a good house — large by the standards of Megiddo’s indigenous inhabitants, with walls of well-dressed stone and a courtyard that I could partially see through the gate. The gate was bronze-hinged and old, and when the servant opened it at Abdi-Milki’s knock, it produced the sound that I had noticed before: that low, flat groan, the voice of ancient metal under the weight of old decisions.

We were admitted without question. Abdi-Milki had sent word the previous evening, through the household steward, that the merchant Abdi-Milki of Ashkelon requested a private consultation on a matter of commercial documentation. This was the kind of request that an elder of Zakar-El’s position received routinely and dismissed routinely through a subordinate. The fact that Zakar-El had agreed to see him personally, Abdi-Milki had noted to me on the walk up from the receiving point, was itself information.

“He could have sent the steward,” I said.

“He could have. He didn’t.” A pause. “He knows who I am. Not me specifically — what I am. A factor who moves between cities, who knows the routes, who talks to many people. He is trying to determine the radius of his exposure. Meeting me in person is faster than waiting.”

Zakar-El received us in a small room off the main courtyard — a room for private transactions, I thought, with a low table and cushioned benches and the smell of cedar oil from a lamp that was slightly too large for the space, which gave the room a warmth that felt deliberate. He was exactly as I have described him: sixty years of age, the face of a man who had been living inside several decisions for a long time. He was composed in the manner of men who have governed themselves carefully for many years and have developed composure as both habit and tool.

He looked at me when we entered, and the look was a question.

“My scribe,” Abdi-Milki said. “He records transactions. Today he will record this one.”

Zakar-El looked at me a moment longer, then returned his attention to Abdi-Milki. “Sit,” he said.

We sat.

What followed I will set down as directly as I can, though the words are reconstructed from my memory of them and may not be exact in every particular.

Abdi-Milki said: “I have spoken with Abdi-Yapa.”

Zakar-El was still. Not the stillness of surprise — the stillness of a man receiving information he had feared was coming and had already, in some part of himself, prepared for.

“I see,” he said.

“I want you to understand,” Abdi-Milki said, “that I am not here on behalf of the garrison. I am not here on behalf of any authority. I am here because I find incomplete accounts troubling, and because I have spent many years learning to read what is missing from a record as fluently as I read what is present.”

A long silence. The lamp produced its warmth. Outside, distantly, I could hear the sounds of the city’s morning — a vendor’s call, a cart-wheel on stone, water being drawn from somewhere nearby.

Then Zakar-El said, in a voice that was very quiet and very level: “What do you wish to know?”

“Nothing,” Abdi-Milki said. “I wish to give you the opportunity to tell me whatever you choose to tell me.”

And Zakar-El told him.

I will not set down the whole of it in this account, because the whole of it belongs to a man and his private grief, and I do not think it is mine to transcribe in full. I will say only what I believe is necessary to make the record honest.

Twenty-three years ago, there had been an argument at the cistern’s edge. The argument had been about the water rights, yes, but also about something older — a debt of honour between the two families that the water rights dispute had surfaced, a thing that had been kept underwater for a generation and had, in the pressure of that afternoon, forced its way up. The young man — his name was Shalem-bel, Zakar-El said, and he said the name with the specific quality of a man who has said it to himself many times in private — had come to the cistern already enraged, already resolved on some confrontation. The argument had been brief and violent in its feeling if not in its actions. There had been a shove. Zakar-El, who was then twenty-four years old, had shoved back.

Shalem-bel had fallen.

Zakar-El had stood at the cistern’s edge and looked down for a long time. Shalem-bel did not surface. The cistern was deep.

He had walked away.

He had told himself, during the years that followed, various things about that moment. That it was an accident. That it was the consequence of the other man’s rage, not his own. That he had been young. That the world he had made in the years since — the family he had protected, the commerce he had sustained, the city he had helped to manage through several periods in which its management was genuinely difficult — was evidence of a character that the moment at the cistern did not fully represent.

He told Abdi-Milki all of this. He told him in the tone of a man who has been waiting, for a very long time, for someone to ask.

When he was finished, the room was quiet again. The lamp continued to produce its warmth.

Abdi-Milki said nothing for a long moment. Then he said: “Shalem-bel had a family.”

“The family is gone,” Zakar-El said. “They left Megiddo within two years of his death. I do not know where they went.” He looked at his hands on the table. “I have asked myself whether to find them. I have never been able to determine what I would say.”

Another silence.

“I have one question,” Abdi-Milki said. “Not about what happened. About Abdi-Yapa.”

Zakar-El looked up.

“How long did you intend to pay him?”

Zakar-El was quiet for a long moment. “I had asked myself that,” he said. “I did not have an answer that I could make myself believe.”

Abdi-Milki stood. I stood. Zakar-El remained seated.

“I will not take his place,” Abdi-Milki said. “I am telling you this directly so that you do not spend the next months constructing that fear. I am a merchant. My capital is my reputation for accurate accounting. I have made an accurate account of this transaction, and I will file it in the place where I file all such accounts — which is inside my own head, where it will remain unless something requires its retrieval.”

He moved toward the door. He paused.

“Abdi-Yapa is a small man with small ambitions,” he said. “He will continue to require payments until the requirement becomes more dangerous to you than the alternative. When that calculation changes, you will know what to do.”

We left. The gate-hinge groaned behind us with that low, flat voice, the sound of old metal and old weight.

I walked beside him back down toward the lower city in the flat tin light, and I did not speak for some time because I was trying to understand what I had just witnessed.

“You let him keep his secret,” I said finally.

“I established that the secret is known,” he said. “That is a different thing.”

“And Abdi-Yapa? You said you wouldn’t take his place, but—”

“Abdi-Yapa’s account is a different matter.” He was quiet for a moment. “A man who has carried a secret for twenty-three years and confessed it freely, unprompted, to a stranger who gave him the opportunity — that man is not the same problem as a man who identified a weakness and built an income from it. Zakar-El’s difficulty with Abdi-Yapa will resolve itself. These arrangements always do. They carry their own termination within them.”

I thought about this. “You knew he would confess,” I said. “Before we arrived. You didn’t know what he would confess, but you knew he would.”

He glanced at me sideways. It was, I think, a glance of something that was not quite surprise and not quite approval — the glance of a man who has been observed by someone he had partially underestimated.

“A man who meets his potential blackmailer in person,” he said, “when he could have sent a subordinate — is not managing his exposure. He is looking for his confessor. The two things look similar from outside. They are not the same thing.”

I had no response to this.

“What will you do with what you recorded?” he asked. He meant, I understood, the record I was already composing in my mind — not the official record, not the garrison record, but this one.

“I don’t know,” I said. “File it, perhaps. In the way you described.”

He made a sound that was not quite a laugh. “Then we are both merchants of the same type,” he said. “We deal in things whose value lies in their not being spent.”

We parted at the lower market’s edge. I watched him move into the crowd with the particular quality of attention that distinguishes a man who is observing from a man who is merely walking, and then the crowd received him and he was gone.

I returned to the receiving point. The disputed copper manifest was still on my table, still waiting. I sat down and began, at last, to work through it.

This account was composed by Kiri, junior tally-scribe, and kept among his private records. He did not submit it to the garrison archive. He did not show it to his superior Harkhuf. He was uncertain, for many years, whether he had done right or wrong in making it at all.

He made it because Abdi-Milki told him once that a record not made is a truth already half-dead.

He has decided to agree.
