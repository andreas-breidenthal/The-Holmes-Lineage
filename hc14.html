NEFER-IB-RA

~1660 BC — Ashkelon, Canaan

The Death of Dagon-Malik

A note upon the circumstances of this record:
I am a man of accounts. I have kept the grain tallies of Ashkelon's central storehouses for eleven years, and in that time I have learned that a number written clearly is a number that cannot be argued with. What follows is not an account I wished to keep. It implicates the records I maintained, it required me to stand in a dark storehouse with a murderer, and it required me to sit at the elbow of a man who treated my careful notations as preliminary sketches rather than finished truths. I set it down now because it is true, and because I am old enough to know that the truth, like improperly stored grain, will force its way out eventually. Better to control the opening.

The body of Dagon-Malik was discovered on the morning of the fifteenth day of Shemu by a porter named Yabni-El, who had come to shift a consignment of emmer from the eastern bays toward the threshing floor. He did not find him immediately. He found the smell first.

I was at my writing table in the counting room when Yabni-El came to the door, and I will say this for the man: he did not scream. He stood in the doorway with both hands pressed flat against the frame, as though he required the cedar's assistance to remain upright, and said, in a voice of remarkable steadiness, that something in the eastern bay required a senior official's attention.

By the time I arrived, two other porters had gathered at the bay entrance and refused to go further. The morning light entered through the high clerestory apertures in pale columns, each one thick with the chaff dust that the eastern bay perpetually exhaled — the smell of it always sweet-rotten, the smell of a living process slowly consuming itself. But underneath that familiar ferment was something else. Something the chaff dust could not sweeten.

Dagon-Malik lay between the third and fourth grain-bins, on his back, his linen robe twisted beneath him as though he had turned in his sleep and simply not returned. The lamps in the eastern bay had been extinguished. The clay of the floor around him was unmarked by any dramatic disturbance. He might have lain down to rest, except that no man of sixty-three years rests with that particular stillness, and no resting man turns the colour he had turned.

I sent for the city's Overseer of Merchants, a Hyksos-appointed official named Shamash-Gamil — a conscientious man of the kind that administration produces in quantity, thorough within the walls of procedure, genuinely lost outside them. He arrived within the hour, examined the body from a distance of four paces, and declared that Dagon-Malik had died of a seizure of the heart, which was, Shamash-Gamil said, entirely consistent with the man's age, his weight, and his known habit of working alone in the storehouse after the other factors had gone home.

I wrote this down. It was a clean explanation. It required no further inquiry and disturbed no existing arrangements.

It was Nefer-ib-Ra who disturbed them.

He appeared at the entrance to the eastern bay while Shamash-Gamil was still composing his preliminary notation. I had worked with him on several transactions over the preceding three months — he had arrived in Ashkelon from the Delta as a trading factor, speaking Akkadian with the precision of formal scribal training, wearing Egyptian linen but Canaanite sandals — and I had found his attention to detail admirable in a manner that was occasionally inconvenient. The merchants who had introduced him called him a physician before they called him a factor, which I had initially taken as the loose flattery of trade correspondence. I revised this opinion that morning.

He was a lean man, not tall, with the particular economy of movement that belongs to those who have learned to conserve effort in hot climates — Egyptian in that, and in the stillness of his attention, which had the quality of a crocodile's patience rather than a man's. When he moved, it was with sudden precision. When he was still, he was entirely still, and it was sometimes difficult, watching him, to determine when one had given way to the other.

He was still now, in the bay entrance, looking upward at the clerestory apertures.

"The lamps were extinguished," he said.

"Dagon-Malik may have extinguished them himself before he was taken ill," I offered.

He did not respond to this. He stepped into the bay and crouched beside the unlit lamp nearest the entrance, lifting it with two fingers at the base, tilting it with the same unhurried attention a physician gives to a patient's urine. He replaced it with care.

"This lamp has oil," he said. "It was extinguished. Not burned dry." He rose. "Who holds the lamp oil accounts for this building?"

"I do," I said.

"I will want to see them."

Shamash-Gamil looked up from his clay tablet with the expression of a man being inconvenienced in a minor official capacity. "The matter is settled," he said. "A seizure of the heart requires no further—"

"Then my looking at the accounts will confirm it," Nefer-ib-Ra said pleasantly, "and cause no difficulty whatsoever."

He did not look at the accounts first. He looked at Dagon-Malik.

I had not, until that morning, watched a physician work at a death scene. Nefer-ib-Ra moved around the body with a focused attention that was neither solemn nor brief, and whose emotional character I found difficult to classify. He was not cold. He was simply elsewhere — occupying a different room from his body, one whose windows looked out onto a landscape I could not see. His father, I was told later by those who had known him in the Delta, had been a physician of some standing in the House of Life at Memphis, and the son had learned there that the body was a document more reliable than any papyrus. He moved like a man reading.

He examined the hands first, spending a time upon them that seemed excessive. Then the neck, pressing two fingers with extraordinary gentleness against the discoloured skin below the jaw. Then he examined the cylinder seal that hung at Dagon-Malik's belt — turning it in his fingers, testing its weight, rolling it once across his own palm to observe the impression it left — before replacing it precisely as he had found it.

Then he examined the floor in a radius of perhaps five cubits around the body, moving with small careful steps, his eyes tracking the clay surface with the patience he gave the grain tallies. Once he stopped and pressed two fingers gently into the clay at the edge of the third grain-bin, holding the posture for a long moment before continuing.

"Come here," he said, after some time.

I came. Shamash-Gamil came also, with the expression of a man who expects to be impressed against his will.

"Tell me what you see," Nefer-ib-Ra said.

"A man who has died," I said.

"You observe. You do not yet see." He crouched beside the body and indicated, without touching, the area of the throat. "The skin here — look at the colour. Not the colour of death alone. That darkening at the margins. And the eyes — do not flinch, Shubanu, you are a man of records, this is simply another record — the vessels have burst within them. What does that suggest?"

"He was strangled?"

"The body speaks more clearly than any witness. A man whose heart fails him does not burst the vessels of his eyes. A man whose air is prevented from reaching him does." He rose in a single unhurried motion and moved to the clay floor near the lamp stand. "Here — a heel-mark, pressed deeply. A man standing in one place for some time. Not walking. Waiting."

Shamash-Gamil cleared his throat. "This is suggestive, but the records—"

"The records," Nefer-ib-Ra said, in a tone of such patient precision that it felt almost like instruction, "are precisely what I have come to examine. The body establishes that a man was killed. The records will establish when, and by whose arrangement."

I brought the accounts to a table in the counting room: the daily oil ration logs, the grain movement tallies, the porter attendance records, and the factor access register — a clay tablet on which those who entered the storehouse after the regular hours were required to press their cylinder seal. It was this last document that Shamash-Gamil had consulted when Dagon-Malik had not appeared at his home on the evening of the fourteenth day. The register showed Dagon-Malik entering at the second watch of the night and no seal-press thereafter. The Overseer had concluded that Dagon-Malik had suffered his seizure during that same second watch, had lain undiscovered through the night, and had been found the following morning.

Nefer-ib-Ra read the access register. He set it down. He read the grain movement tallies. He set those down also. Then he asked me to take him into the eastern bay and show him the physical condition of the grain in bins three and four.

I confess I did not see why he wanted this.

He saw that I did not see. He seemed to find this neither irritating nor surprising — simply an opportunity.

"Grain is a living archive," he said, as we moved between the bins. "It does not lie the way documents can lie. A written tally tells me what a man recorded. The grain itself tells me what was actually done to it." He ran a hand along the surface of the grain in the third bin, pressing at several points, testing the resistance. "When was this bin last disturbed? According to the tallies?"

"The twelfth day," I said. "A measure was drawn out for the mill allocation."

"And the thirteenth and fourteenth days — nothing drawn from it?"

"Nothing."

He pressed his hand deeper into the grain at the far corner of the bin. He held it there. Then he looked at me with the expression of a man who has already written the final line of an account and is waiting to read the preliminary ones aloud.

"Then why is the grain in this corner at a different temperature from the grain at the centre?"

I stared at him.

"Grain that has not been disturbed equilibrates," he said. "It settles into the ambient temperature of the storehouse. Grain that has been disturbed — shifted, walked upon, used to conceal or position something — retains the warmth of that disturbance for some time, particularly in the sealed heat of a storehouse in Shemu. The centre of this bin is cool. It has not been touched since the twelfth day, as the tally states. But this corner was disturbed on the thirteenth day. Or the early hours of the fourteenth."

He straightened.

"Dagon-Malik did not die on the night of the fourteenth day when the register shows him entering this storehouse. He died on the night of the thirteenth, when he entered by a different means — one that left no seal-press, because someone else had arranged for him to be here already, or had admitted him through a route that bypassed the register entirely. His body was moved to its present position afterward, with considerable effort, because a dead man of his weight requires assistance at the corner of a grain-bin. The print of a second heel in the clay near the third bin — deeper than any print that walking would produce — is the print of a man who set down a weight and straightened up. The grain shifted beneath that weight and has retained the evidence of it."

He paused. His eyes went to the high clerestory apertures again, briefly, then back to me.

"The question is not what the register says. The question is whose seal does not appear in the register on the night of the thirteenth day, but who had reason to be in this storehouse regardless."

This is where I must speak with some care, because what follows implicated the accounts I had maintained for eleven years, and I cannot pretend my position was comfortable.

The oil ration logs showed a discrepancy.

I had not noticed it before Nefer-ib-Ra asked me to lay all the records from the twelfth through the fifteenth days side by side upon the counting table. When they were so arranged, a pattern emerged that a single document would never have shown: the lamp oil drawn from the central stores on the evening of the thirteenth day exceeded, by a measure of one half-vessel, the amount that the lit lamps of the building could have consumed in a single night. Someone had drawn lamp oil on the thirteenth day who had no recorded reason to be in the building.

Only three men held the authority to draw oil from the central stores without a written requisition counter-signed by myself: Dagon-Malik himself, his deputy factor Baal-Rimmon, and the storehouse's night-keeper, an Egyptian named Hori, who had served in that post for eight years and whose records were, I had always thought, impeccable.

Dagon-Malik was dead.

Baal-Rimmon's cylinder seal appeared in the access register on the evening of the fourteenth day — after, according to the body's evidence, Dagon-Malik had already been moved into position.

Hori's seal did not appear in the register at all, on either the thirteenth or the fourteenth day. His attendance record showed him present for his regular duties on the morning of the fifteenth. He had entered through the night-keeper's side entrance, which was his right, and which did not require a register seal.

It was also the entrance that would have admitted a man — and a body — without leaving any mark in the document that Shamash-Gamil had consulted.

I knew Hori. He was a solidly built man of perhaps fifty years, heavy through the shoulders, with the quiet deliberateness of someone who has spent decades moving between large objects in confined spaces. His Egyptian was accented in the manner of men who have spoken Canaanite dialects since boyhood — he had come to Ashkelon as a young man, he said, when the Delta had grown uncertain, which was a thing many Egyptians said in those years when the Hyksos consolidation had made permanence a matter of calculation rather than inheritance. He was not a man who appeared anxious. He was a man who appeared settled, in the way that a stone appears settled — as though the surface concealed a weight that would be difficult to shift and dangerous if it moved.

I had found him, until that morning, perfectly unremarkable.

Nefer-ib-Ra sent me to the central storeroom and asked me to examine the grain in the sealed container from which Hori controlled the night portions. I did not know what I was looking for. I found it nonetheless, because it was not subtle once you were searching for it: the grain was mixed, in the deep layers, with a portion of inferior stock — the kind of adulteration that reduces the actual measure while the recorded tallies remain correct. It was not a large amount. Over two years, across four storehouses, it would have been considerable.

When I returned to the counting room and reported this, Nefer-ib-Ra was sitting motionless at the table, looking at nothing in particular, with the stillness I had come to recognise as the outward form of an inward acceleration. He sat that way for some moments after I finished speaking.

Then he said: "I am going to need your assistance this evening. And I must tell you, before you agree, that there is some risk attached to it."

He told me only what was necessary. We were to enter the eastern bay at the second watch of the night, before Hori's customary duties began. Nefer-ib-Ra had sent word to Hori through a porter — not Yabni-El, a different man — that a discrepancy in the night-portion tallies required the night-keeper's immediate attention and that discretion was essential, as the matter reflected upon Hori's own records. He had signed the note with my seal, with my permission, and in my name.

"You are using me as bait," I said.

"I am using your administrative authority as bait," he said, with a precision that was perhaps not entirely comforting. "You will stand at the counting room entrance with a lamp. I will be in the eastern bay. You will tell him that the discrepancy is in the grain records and direct him through. Then you will remain at the entrance."

"And if he runs?"

"He will not run. A man who has spent two years building a careful crime does not run from a document discrepancy. He comes to manage it."

"And if he does not come to manage it? If he comes instead to ensure that the man who found the discrepancy cannot report it?"

Nefer-ib-Ra looked at me with something I had not previously seen in his expression — not coldness, but the acknowledgment of a true proposition. "Then I will be between you and him," he said. "And I ask that you trust that this is not a negligible fact."

I am a man of accounts. I calculated the risks as clearly as I could, found the arithmetic uncertain, and agreed.

The storehouse at the second watch of the night is a different world from the storehouse by day. The clerestory apertures admit no light; the darkness is absolute save for a single lamp. The sweet-rotten smell of the grain is thicker without the sea-wind to thin it, and the silence has a quality — not of emptiness, but of the kind of stillness that large quantities of accumulated matter produce, the weight of ten thousand measures of emmer pressing the air down around itself. I stood at the counting room entrance with a lamp that felt inadequate to the occasion, and I waited.

Hori came through the side entrance at the second watch, precisely as Nefer-ib-Ra had predicted. He moved with his customary deliberateness, carrying his own lamp, and when he saw me at the counting room entrance he nodded in the manner of a man attending to a professional obligation.

"The clerk," he said. "You found a discrepancy."

"In the night-portion tallies," I said. "It touches your records directly. I thought it better to raise it with you before taking it to Shamash-Gamil's office."

He studied me for a moment. In the lamplight his face was composed, but his eyes moved once — quickly, involuntarily — to the entrance of the eastern bay. "Show me," he said.

"The grain itself," I said. "The bin three corner. Nefer-ib-Ra identified a temperature irregularity this morning and I need you to account for it before the records can be corrected."

Something altered in his face. Not panic — that is not the right word. Something more careful than panic, and more dangerous. "The Egyptian factor," he said. Not a question.

"He is thorough," I said, which was true, and which seemed the safest honest thing to say.

Hori moved toward the eastern bay. I remained at the entrance, as I had been instructed. He passed through the doorway and into the darkness beyond my lamp's reach, and then I heard, rather than saw, what happened next.

A lamp came to life at the far end of the bay — Nefer-ib-Ra's lamp, which he had carried shielded and now uncovered. It illuminated the third grain-bin, and Nefer-ib-Ra standing before it, and the arrangements he had made on its surface: the daily records, the oil ration logs, the access register, and — laid carefully in the centre, in a position that made its presence unambiguous — a clay impression he had taken himself that morning of the heel-print at the bin's edge, pressed into fresh clay beside a recent impression from Hori's own sandal as the night-keeper had crossed the counting room threshold a minute earlier.

The two impressions were identical.

Hori went still. In the lamplight he looked, for a moment, like a different kind of stone — not settled, but arrested, a weight caught mid-movement.

"The grain temperature places you here on the night of the thirteenth," Nefer-ib-Ra said. His voice in the bay's acoustics was flat and carried everywhere. "The oil overdraw places you here with a lamp. The heel-print places you beside the bin where Dagon-Malik's body was positioned. The seal impression on the fourteenth-day register entry — pressed by a man holding another man's cylinder seal with the deliberateness of unfamiliarity rather than the loose habit of ownership — places the seal in your hand after Dagon-Malik was already dead. I tested his seal's weight myself this morning when I examined the body. I know what it produces when pressed by its owner's habitual grip. What the register shows is not that."

Hori said nothing. His lamp was still in his hand.

"The adulterated grain has continued for two years," Nefer-ib-Ra continued, in the same flat, carrying tone. "Dagon-Malik discovered it. I do not yet know all of what passed between you, but I know that he came to this storehouse on the night of the thirteenth day, through the side entrance, by arrangement. I know that he did not leave."

The lamp moved. Not quickly — Hori was not a man of quick movements — but with the deliberateness that I now understood was not composure but simply the way he did everything, including things that were not administrative in nature. He took three steps toward Nefer-ib-Ra and I saw, in that moment, what I had not seen before: the settled weight of him was not temperament. It was capability. He was a man who knew how to make himself very difficult to move.

Nefer-ib-Ra did not step back. He stood with the complete stillness I had seen in him all day, and in the lamplight his face showed nothing except attention.

What happened next happened quickly enough that I can only report what I saw from the bay entrance: Hori lunged; Nefer-ib-Ra stepped — not back but sideways and forward, inside the reach of the heavier man — and applied, with two hands and the precise leverage of what I can only describe as anatomical knowledge, a pressure to Hori's right arm and shoulder that brought the larger man to his knees in the clay as efficiently as a butcher's technique, the lamp going out as it struck the floor. In the sudden darkness I heard Nefer-ib-Ra's voice, entirely unaltered in tone, saying: "Shubanu. Your lamp, please, and then Shamash-Gamil's house."

I ran.

Shamash-Gamil brought Hori before the judge's hall. I was present. Nefer-ib-Ra was not, which had ceased to surprise me.

The evidence was arranged before the judge — a Hyksos-appointed official named Yantin, a man of administrative caution who was visibly uncomfortable with a case that called into question three days of official records. Hori, when confronted with the grain temperature, the heel-print comparison, the over-pressed seal, and the oil discrepancy, offered a denial that lasted the length of time it takes a methodical man to determine that his documents have been turned against him. He had built his crime on records, and a man who has built on records, when those records are disassembled before him, finds the ground beneath him is not what he imagined.

Yantin pronounced a verdict that I am told was communicated to the Hyksos court at Avaris as a demonstration of the city's effective administration.

Shamash-Gamil received the commendation of his superiors.

I recorded all of this correctly.

That evening I found Nefer-ib-Ra at his customary place near the southern gate, where he had taken to sitting in the late afternoon with a cup of palm-wine and a wooden writing board on which he worked, apparently for his own satisfaction, at mathematical problems I did not recognise — geometrical progressions, he told me, when I had first asked, that described the movement of water through different substrates. He pursued them with the focused distraction of a man who requires this particular muscle's exercise to prevent it from consuming him instead. I had wondered, when I first observed the habit, whether a physician trained in Memphis by a father of the House of Life would not find such calculations too familiar to require the quality of attention he brought to them. I understood now that it was precisely because they were familiar that they served — they required enough of him to keep the other part quiet.

He had a cloth wrapped around his right hand, tied with the neat economy of a man who has bandaged his own injuries before.

"Yantin has closed the case," I said.

"I know."

"He did not mention your name."

"I know that also." He did not look up from the writing board. "Was it a just outcome?"

I sat with this question for some time. "Hori killed a man," I said. "He stole systematically from the city's stores for two years. He tried, last night, to do to you what he did to Dagon-Malik."

"Also yes, to all of those."

"Then it is just."

Nefer-ib-Ra set down his stylus and looked at the gate, through which the evening traffic moved with the indifferent rhythm of a city that had absorbed the day's events and would not be altered by them. The torches had just been lit at the gate-posts; the oil in them — good oil, properly measured — sent up a thin black thread of smoke that the sea-wind immediately unravelled.

"I have been thinking," he said, "about Dagon-Malik's own role in what occurred. The adulteration had continued long enough that either Dagon-Malik was among the least attentive factors in the Levant, which the quality of his records does not suggest, or he had reason to permit it to continue. If he had permitted it for a share of the benefit, and then sought to increase that share, and Hori could not or would not meet the demand — that is a different moral geometry from a simple murder."

"It changes nothing about Hori's guilt," I said.

"No. It changes the shape of the truth, which is a different matter." He picked up his stylus again. "The judge does not know this. He does not need to. The outcome is the same. But you should record it, I think, in whatever account you keep for yourself, because a truth that is only partially told is a document with missing clay."

He returned to his progressions. After a while I left him to them. I do not think he noticed.

Nefer-ib-Ra remained in Ashkelon for another seven months before departing northward, toward the trading routes through Megiddo. He left as he had arrived: useful before he asked anything of the city, and gone before the city had finished deciding what it thought of him. His letters of introduction, when I finally had occasion to verify them through correspondence with the Delta ports, were genuine — he had been known there as a physician before the political situation in Memphis had made continued residence a matter of the calculation that the Hyksos consolidation had forced upon many Egyptians of his generation, those who had grown up watching the old administrative certainties dissolve from the north like grain-dust in a sea-wind.

In the matter of the death of Dagon-Malik, the evidence he assembled was available to any man who looked at it. The grain temperature, the heel-print, the oil ration discrepancy, the over-pressed seal — none of these required exceptional perception. They required only the refusal to accept a document as the thing itself rather than a representation of the thing.

"A written account is a shadow," he told me once. "It has the shape of what was done, but it is cast by a single light, from a single angle, and the man who cast it had his own reasons for standing where he stood. The physician learns early that the body overrules the case-notes. I have simply applied the same discipline to other kinds of bodies."

I have been a clerk of records for thirty years. I have accepted this instruction, and I am diminished and enlarged by it in equal measure.

The official account of the Ashkelon storehouse case credits the Overseer of Merchants Shamash-Gamil with the resolution of a fraud and a murder. This is what the administrative records of the city will show to any judge who inquires.

It is accurate, in the way that shadows are accurate.

Thus concludes the record of Shubanu, Clerk of the Storehouses of Ashkelon, set down in his own hand.
