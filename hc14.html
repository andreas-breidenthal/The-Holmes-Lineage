NEFER-IB-RA

~1660 BC — Memphis / Canaan

Beautiful is the heart of Ra — a name of exquisite theological optimism for a moment of considerable political deterioration. The Hyksos had been consolidating their control of Lower Egypt for a generation, their capital at Avaris in the Delta displacing Egyptian authority from the north and creating the situation of dual rule — Hyksos pharaohs in the north, Theban princes in the south, Memphis caught between them — that would define Egyptian politics for the next century and a half. Nefer-ib-Ra was born into that displacement and came of age in a Memphis increasingly uncertain of its own political identity, its old administrative certainties dissolving as the authority that had once issued from the north now issued from a foreign court whose relationship to Egyptian tradition was the subject of contested interpretation. He responded with the family’s characteristic pragmatism: he left.

He had been trained by his father as a physician, but his formation was wider than medicine in the strict sense — the House of Life’s curriculum encompassed medical texts, astronomical calculations, and the accumulated correspondence of temple administrations across Egypt. He was methodical to a fault, and slower than his father in committing to conclusions, requiring physical verification before he would accept a pattern as real. This caution shaped his work in Canaan, where he often found that assumptions were made more quickly than evidence could support. He carried with him his medical knowledge, his scribal formation, and his family’s accumulated facility with languages: Akkadian, the diplomatic lingua franca of the Levant, and enough of the coastal Canaanite dialects to function in the trading cities. He went north as a factor and settled as a man of the Levantine world, the Egyptian layer of his identity gradually thinning as the Canaanite one thickened.

Among those who worked with him in Ashkelon was a junior clerk named Shubanu, whose role was to maintain the grain and trade records for the city’s central storehouses. Shubanu recorded Nefer-ib-Ra’s inquiries with careful attention, but also with a certain reluctance, for his own position depended on the stability of the system that was now being questioned. His record preserves not only what was observed, but also the moments where questions were asked that seemed, at first, unrelated to the matter at hand.

An Account Of The Inquiry Into The Death Of Dagon-Malik

A note upon the circumstances of this record:

I am a man of accounts. I have kept the grain tallies of Ashkelon’s central storehouses for eleven years, and in that time I have learned that a number written clearly is a number that cannot be argued with. What follows is not an account I wished to keep. It implicates the very records I maintained, and it required me to sit at the elbow of a man who treated my careful notations as preliminary sketches rather than finished truths. I set it down now because it is true, and because I am old enough to know that the truth, like improperly stored grain, will force its way out eventually, in one form or another. Better to control the opening.

The body of Dagon-Malik was discovered on the morning of the fifteenth day of the month of Shemu by a porter named Yabni-El, who had come to shift a consignment of emmer from the eastern bays toward the threshing floor. He did not find him immediately. He found the smell first.

I was at my writing table in the counting room when Yabni-El came to the door, and I will say this for the man: he did not scream. He stood in the doorway with both hands pressed flat against the frame, as though he required the cedar’s assistance to remain upright, and said, in a voice of remarkable steadiness, that something in the eastern bay required a senior official’s attention.

By the time I arrived, two other porters had gathered at the bay entrance and refused to go further. The morning light entered through the high clerestory apertures in pale columns, each one thick with the chaff dust that the eastern bay perpetually exhaled — the smell of it always sweet-rotten, the smell of a living process slowly consuming itself. But underneath that familiar ferment was something else. Something that the chaff dust could not sweeten.

Dagon-Malik lay between the third and fourth grain-bins, on his back, his linen robe twisted beneath him as though he had turned in his sleep and simply not returned. The lamps in the eastern bay had been extinguished. The clay of the floor around him was unmarked by any dramatic disturbance. He might have lain down to rest, except that no man of sixty-three years rests with that particular stillness, and no resting man turns the colour he had turned.

I sent for the city’s Overseer of Merchants, a Hyksos-appointed official named Shamash-Gamil, a conscientious man of the kind that administration produces in quantity — thorough within the walls of procedure, genuinely lost outside them. He arrived within the hour, examined the body from a distance of four paces, and declared that Dagon-Malik had died of a seizure of the heart, which was, Shamash-Gamil said, entirely consistent with the man’s age, his weight, and his known habit of working alone in the storehouse after the other factors had gone home.

I wrote this down. It was a clean explanation. It required no further inquiry and disturbed no existing arrangements.

It was Nefer-ib-Ra who disturbed them.

He had arrived in Ashkelon three months earlier as a trading factor from the Delta, speaking Akkadian with a precision that suggested formal scribal training, wearing Egyptian linen but Canaanite sandals, carrying letters of introduction from grain merchants in the trading ports that I could not immediately verify but had no reason to distrust. He rented a room near the southern gate and conducted himself with the quiet industry of a man who has learned that the most effective way to understand a new city is to be useful to it before asking anything of it. I had worked with him on several transactions involving the southern storehouse’s emmer accounts, and I had found his attention to detail admirable in a manner that was occasionally inconvenient.

He appeared at the entrance to the eastern bay while Shamash-Gamil was still composing his preliminary notation.

“The Overseer has reached a conclusion,” I said, by way of explanation.

Nefer-ib-Ra did not respond to this. He was looking at the high clerestory apertures.

“The lamps were extinguished,” he said.

“Dagon-Malik may have extinguished them himself before he was taken ill.”

“He may have.” He stepped into the bay. “Who holds the lamp oil accounts for this building?”

This was, I thought, a peculiar first question. “I do,” I said.

“I will want to see them.”

Shamash-Gamil looked up from his clay tablet. He was not accustomed to factors requesting accounts at a death scene. “The matter is settled,” he said, in the tone of a man who is less certain than he sounds. “A seizure of the heart requires no further—”

“Then my looking at the accounts will confirm it,” Nefer-ib-Ra said pleasantly, “and cause no difficulty whatsoever.”

He did not look at the accounts first. He looked at Dagon-Malik.

I had not, until that morning, watched a physician work. I had imagined — to the extent I had imagined it at all — that the examination of a dead man would be solemn, brief, and conducted at some emotional remove. Nefer-ib-Ra moved around the body with a focused attention that was neither solemn nor brief, and whose emotional character I found difficult to classify. He was not cold. He was simply elsewhere — his mind occupying a different room from his body, one whose windows looked out onto a landscape I could not see.

He examined the hands first, spending a time upon them that seemed to me excessive. Then the neck. Then the floor in a radius of perhaps five cubits around the body, moving with small careful steps, his eyes tracking the clay surface with the same patience he gave the grain tallies. Once he stopped and pressed two fingers gently into the clay at the edge of the third grain-bin, holding the posture for a long moment before continuing.

“Come here,” he said, after some time.

I came. Shamash-Gamil came also, with the expression of a man who expects to be impressed against his will.

“Tell me what you see,” Nefer-ib-Ra said.

I looked at Dagon-Malik. “A man who has died,” I said.

“You observe. You do not see.” He crouched beside the body and indicated, without touching, the area of the throat. “The skin here — look at the colour. Not the colour of death alone. That faint darkening at the margins. And the eyes — do not flinch, Shubanu, you are a man of records, this is simply another record — the vessels have burst within them. What does that suggest to you?”

“He was — strangled?”

“The body speaks more clearly than any witness. A man whose heart fails him does not burst the vessels of his eyes. A man whose air is prevented from reaching him does.” He rose and moved to the lamp that stood unlit upon its stand near the bay entrance. He lifted it, tilted it, replaced it with care. “This lamp has oil. It was extinguished, not burned dry. And here—” He indicated the clay floor near the stand. “A heel-mark, pressed deeply. A man standing in one place for some time. Not walking. Waiting.”

Shamash-Gamil cleared his throat. “This is suggestive, but the records—”

“The records,” Nefer-ib-Ra said, in a tone of such patient precision that it felt almost like instruction, “are precisely what I have come to examine. The body establishes that a man was killed. The records will establish when.”

I brought the accounts to a table in the counting room: the daily oil ration logs, the grain movement tallies, the porter attendance records, and the factor access register — a clay tablet on which those who entered the storehouse after the regular hours were required to press their cylinder seal. It was this last document that Shamash-Gamil had consulted when Dagon-Malik had not appeared at his home on the evening of the fourteenth day. The register showed Dagon-Malik entering at the second watch of the night and no seal-press thereafter. The Overseer had concluded that Dagon-Malik had suffered his seizure during that same second watch, had lain undiscovered through the night, and had been found the following morning.

Nefer-ib-Ra read the access register. He set it down. He read the grain movement tallies. He set those down. Then he asked me to take him into the eastern bay and show him the physical condition of the grain in bins three and four.

I confess I did not see why he wanted this.

He saw that I did not see why. He seemed to find this neither irritating nor surprising — simply an opportunity.

“Grain is a living archive,” he said, as we moved between the bins. “It does not lie the way documents can lie. A written tally tells me what a man recorded. The grain itself tells me what was actually done to it.” He ran a hand along the surface of the grain in the third bin, pressing in at several points, testing the resistance. “When was this bin last disturbed? According to the tallies?”

“The twelfth day,” I said. “A measure was drawn out for the mill allocation.”

“And the thirteenth and fourteenth days, nothing was drawn from it?”

“Nothing.”

He pressed his hand deeper into the grain at the far corner of the bin. “Then why is the grain in this corner at a different temperature from the grain at the centre?”

I stared at him.

“Grain that has not been disturbed equilibrates,” he said. “It settles into the ambient temperature of the storehouse. Grain that has been disturbed — shifted, walked upon, used to conceal something or reveal something — retains the warmth of the disturbance for some time, particularly in the sealed heat of a storehouse in Shemu. The centre of this bin is cool. It has not been touched since the twelfth day, as the tally states. But this corner—” He pressed his palm flat against the grain surface. “—was disturbed on the thirteenth day. Or the early hours of the fourteenth.”

He turned to me with the expression of a man who has already written the final line of an account and is waiting to read the preliminary ones aloud.

“Dagon-Malik did not die on the night of the fourteenth day when the register shows him entering this storehouse. He died on the night of the thirteenth, when he entered by a different means — one that left no seal-press in the register, because someone else had arranged for him to be here already. His body was moved to its present position afterward, and it was placed here with some effort, because a dead man of his weight requires assistance with the corner of a grain-bin. The print of a second heel in the clay near the third bin — deeper than any print that walking would produce — is the print of a man who set down a weight and straightened up. The grain in the corner of this bin shifted beneath that weight and has retained the evidence of it.”

He paused.

“The question is not what the register says. The question is whose seal does not appear in the register on the night of the thirteenth day, but who had reason to be in this storehouse regardless.”

This is where I must speak with some care, because what follows implicated the accounts I had maintained for eleven years, and I cannot pretend that my position was a comfortable one.

The oil ration logs showed a discrepancy.

I had not noticed it before Nefer-ib-Ra asked me to lay all the records from the twelfth through the fifteenth days side by side upon the counting table. When they were so arranged, a pattern emerged that a single document would never have shown: the lamp oil drawn from the central stores on the evening of the thirteenth day exceeded, by a measure of one half-vessel, the amount that the lit lamps of the building could have consumed in a single night. Someone had drawn lamp oil on the thirteenth day who had no recorded reason to be in the building.

Only three men held the authority to draw oil from the central stores without a written requisition counter-signed by myself: Dagon-Malik himself, his deputy factor Baal-Rimmon, and the storehouse’s night-keeper, an Egyptian by the name of Hori, who had served in that post for eight years and whose records were, I had always thought, impeccable.

Dagon-Malik was dead.

Baal-Rimmon’s cylinder seal appeared in the access register on the evening of the fourteenth day — after, according to the body’s evidence, Dagon-Malik had already been moved into position.

Hori’s seal did not appear in the register at all, on either the thirteenth or the fourteenth day. His attendance record showed him present for his duties on the morning of the fifteenth. He had entered through the night-keeper’s side entrance, which was his right, and which did not require a register seal.

It was also the entrance that would have admitted a man — and a body — without leaving any mark in the document that Shamash-Gamil had consulted.

Nefer-ib-Ra sent me to the central storeroom and asked me to examine the grain in the sealed container from which Hori controlled the night portions. I did not know what I was looking for. I found it nonetheless, because it was not subtle once you were searching for it: the grain was mixed, in the deep layers, with a portion of inferior stock — the kind of adulteration that reduces the actual measure while the recorded tallies remain correct. It was not a large amount. Over two years, across four storehouses, it would have been considerable.

Dagon-Malik had discovered it. Possibly he had discovered it some time before, and had confronted Hori privately rather than reporting it to Shamash-Gamil’s office, either because he wished to give the man a chance to restore what he had taken, or because — and this is my own conjecture, unsupported by any document — he had been taking a share of the benefit himself and their arrangement had collapsed. I do not know. The body does not tell us everything, and Nefer-ib-Ra was careful to say that the evidence supported only what the evidence supported.

What it supported was this: Hori had met Dagon-Malik in the eastern bay on the night of the thirteenth day, by an arrangement made between them. Dagon-Malik had been killed — the burst vessels of the eyes, the bruising at the throat — and his body had been repositioned, and the register had been made to suggest a different night through the straightforward means of Hori pressing Dagon-Malik’s own seal into the clay of the fourteenth-day entry after the man was already dead.

The seal had been pressed slightly too hard. A living man holds a cylinder seal with the loose pressure of habit; a man pressing another man’s seal presses with the deliberateness of effort. Nefer-ib-Ra showed me the impression beside two others from earlier in the month. I saw the difference once he showed me. I had not seen it before.

“You could not have seen it,” he said, without condescension. “You were not looking for it. Your records were not lies, Shubanu. They were simply incomplete. A record is only as complete as the questions put to it.”

Shamash-Gamil brought Hori before the judge’s hall. I was present. Nefer-ib-Ra was not, which surprised me until I understood that his absence was deliberate.

The evidence was arranged before the judge — a Hyksos-appointed official named Yantin, a man of administrative caution who was visibly uncomfortable with a case that called into question three days of official records. Hori, when confronted with the grain temperature, the heel-print, the over-pressed seal, and the oil discrepancy, offered a denial of limited duration. He was a methodical man who had built his crime on documents, and a methodical man, when his documents are taken from him, has less to stand upon than he imagines.

Yantin pronounced a verdict that I am told he communicated to the Hyksos court at Avaris as a matter of the city’s effective administration. The records were amended. The case was formally closed.

Shamash-Gamil received the commendation of his superiors.

I recorded all of this correctly.

That evening I found Nefer-ib-Ra at his customary place near the southern gate, where he had taken to sitting in the late afternoon with a cup of palm-wine and a wax writing board on which he worked, apparently for his own satisfaction, at mathematical problems of a kind I did not recognise — geometrical progressions, he told me, when I asked, that described the movement of water through different substrates. He pursued them with the focused distraction of a man who requires the exercise of this particular muscle to prevent it from consuming him instead.

“Yantin has closed the case,” I said.

“I know.”

“He did not mention your name.”

“I know that also.” He did not look up from the wax board. “Was it a just outcome?”

I sat with this question for some time. “Hori killed a man,” I said.

“Yes.”

“And he was stealing from the storehouses. Systematically. For years.”

“Also yes.”

“Then it is just.”

Nefer-ib-Ra set down his stylus and looked at the gate, through which the evening traffic moved with the indifferent rhythm of a city that had absorbed the day’s events and would not be altered by them. The torches had just been lit at the gate-posts; the oil in them — good oil, properly measured — sent up a thin black thread of smoke that the sea-wind immediately unravelled.

“I have been thinking,” he said at last, “about Dagon-Malik’s own role in what occurred. The adulteration had continued for long enough that either Dagon-Malik was among the least attentive factors in the Levant, which I do not believe, or he had reason to permit it to continue. If he had permitted it for a share of the benefit, and then sought to increase his share, and Hori could not meet the demand — that is a different moral geometry from a simple murder.”

“It changes nothing about Hori’s guilt,” I said.

“No. It changes the shape of the truth, which is a different matter.” He picked up his stylus again. “The judge does not know this. He does not need to. The outcome is the same. But you should record it, I think, in whatever record you keep for yourself, because a truth that is only partially told is a document with missing clay.”

I am a man of accounts. I have followed his instruction.

Nefer-ib-Ra remained in Ashkelon for another seven months before departing northward, toward the trading routes through Megiddo. I do not know where he went after that. His letters of introduction, when I finally had occasion to verify them, were genuine — he had been known in the Delta ports as a physician of some repute before the political situation in Memphis had made continued residence impractical.

In the matter of the death of Dagon-Malik, it should be understood that the evidence he assembled was available to any man who looked at it. The grain temperature, the heel-print, the oil ration discrepancy, the over-pressed seal — none of these required exceptional perception. They required only the refusal to accept a document as the thing itself rather than a representation of the thing.

“A written account is a shadow,” he told me once. “It has the shape of what was done, but it is cast by a single light, from a single angle, and the man who cast it had his own reasons for standing where he stood. The physician learns early that the body overrules the case-notes. I have simply applied the same discipline to other kinds of bodies.”

I have been a clerk of records for thirty years. I have accepted this instruction, and I am diminished and instructed by it in equal measure.

The official account of the Ashkelon storehouse case credits the Overseer of Merchants Shamash-Gamil with the resolution of a fraud and a murder, and this is what the administrative records of the city will show to any judge who inquires.

It is accurate, in the way that shadows are accurate.

Thus concludes the record of Shubanu, Clerk of the Storehouses of Ashkelon, set down in his own hand.
