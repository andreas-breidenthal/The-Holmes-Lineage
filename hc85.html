HOLMAR OF THE RHINE

~550 AD — Rhine, Frankish territory

The second Holmar of the Rhine was not a migrant like the first but a settled man — a man of a particular valley and a particular estate, rooted in the rich agricultural land between Cologne and the sea that had been his family’s territory for three generations. The Frankish world in 550 was more stable than it had been in 400, more organised, more Christian, and considerably more violent in the specific, calculated way of a society that was building the structures of obligation and loyalty from which feudalism would eventually emerge.

He managed the family’s land holdings with the methodical precision that had characterised the line since the scribal schools of Nippur, recording every transaction, every tenancy, every obligation in documents that survived him by generations. He had a particular facility for detecting the falsified account — the altered measure, the understated yield, the inflated claim — that his estate workers quickly learned to respect, or rather to fear. Nothing was taken from him twice. He was a fair man, by the standards of his age, which is to say he was harder than mercy and kinder than the law required.

The surviving account of his administration is preserved in the records of Anselm, a steward in charge of the estate’s accounts, whose duty it was to reconcile the written records with the physical yields of the land. Anselm’s notes are uneven in places, reflecting both his diligence and his uncertainty, but they preserve the sequence of events as they unfolded under Holmar’s direction.

The Death of the Tenant Wulfrid

Recorded by Anselm, steward of accounts, in the year of Our Lord five hundred and seventy-three, set down here in faithful testament to what I witnessed, though I confess my hand trembles even now at the memory of those three days.

The fog came in from the Rhine that autumn as it always does — not the clean cold fog of the high hills but the low, vegetable fog of the river flats, smelling of rot and standing water and the dark silt that the Rhine deposits in its slow dominion over the valley. It settled on the estate like a judgement. The oxen would not move in it. The grain-stores sweated. The rushlight in the counting-house burned orange and thick, and the smoke from the hearth could not find the smoke-hole but pooled instead at the height of a man’s shoulders, so that all work was conducted in a permanent haze, the accounts laid out on the trestle-table appearing and disappearing as the smoke shifted.

It was in this fog, on the morning of the feast of Saint Remigius — which the older tenants still called by the older name they would not say before the priest — that the boy came running from the lower fields to say that Wulfrid the tenant was dead in his doorway.

I had seen Wulfrid the evening prior. He had come to submit his autumn yield-accounts at the hour appointed for such things, which was after the compline bell and before the watch was set — a peculiarity of our lord Holmar’s arrangement that I had always found excessive. Most estates received their tenants’ accounts at the morning hour. Holmar received them at night, by lamplight, because — as he once explained to me with the patient air of a man instructing a child — a man who falsifies figures does so in the morning, when he is fresh and his hope is high. A man who submits figures in the night, after a day’s labour, submits figures that are merely tired. The tiredness, he said, was legible.

Wulfrid had been tired. I remembered it now, standing over his body in the doorway of his holding — the low stone threshold, the body slumped half-in and half-out as though he had been attempting to leave or attempting to return and had not managed either. There was blood, black in the fog-light, at his temple. A blow. The fog moved over him without ceremony.

I sent the boy for Lord Holmar.

He came without haste, which was itself a kind of haste — the deliberate, measuring pace of a man who is already thinking and requires only the evidence to confirm what he has begun to suspect. He was a broad man, Holmar, dark-bearded in the Frankish fashion, with hands that had written more documents than they had held a sword and eyes that gave no indication of what they recorded. He wore no cloak against the fog. He stood over Wulfrid for some time without speaking.

I offered the obvious.

“A robbery, perhaps, my lord. He had his season’s reckoning with him last night. If someone knew—”

“His purse is on his belt,” Holmar said, without looking at the purse. He crouched beside the body. “The blow was delivered from the right side, and from above, which means his attacker was right-handed and taller than Wulfrid, who was not a short man. The strike was precise — one blow, no defensive marks on his hands, which means Wulfrid did not see it coming, which means the attacker was someone Wulfrid did not think to fear.” He looked up at me then. “What time did Wulfrid leave the counting-house last night?”

“After the watch was set. Past the second hour.”

“Who else was in the counting-house at that hour?”

“Myself,” I said. “And Brennus.”

Holmar stood. “Fetch me Wulfrid’s accounts,” he said. “The ones he submitted last night. And fetch me the accounts from the preceding three seasons.”

The counting-house smelled of tallow and old parchment and the particular mustiness of accumulated obligation — every hide of land represented, every basket of rye and measure of barley translated into figures that stood in for the real weight of things. Holmar sat at the trestle with Wulfrid’s accounts spread before him, and the season-accounts beside them, and his own master-ledger open at the relevant holdings, and he read.

After a long time he said: “Anselm. In your experience, how does a tenant farmer’s yield typically move across seasons?”

“It varies,” I said. “Good years and bad.”

He spread three documents side by side. “Look.”

I looked. And then I saw.

The measures were wrong — not in the figures, but in the conversion. A fraction altered, quietly, two years prior. Enough to bleed value without drawing notice.

“Someone,” I said, “altered the conversion rate.”

“And corrected the figures to match,” Holmar said. “Wulfrid was honest. His honesty was used.”

He closed the ledger. “He discovered it last night.”

“Brennus,” I said.

Holmar did not answer. He did not need to.

What followed I record briefly. Brennus confessed. Debt in Cologne. A plan to correct the accounts in time. A man caught between arithmetic and fear.

“I did not plan to kill him,” Brennus said.

“No,” Holmar said. “You planned to stop. And could not.”

Brennus was delivered to the Count’s justice.

On the evening before his removal, Holmar opened the ledger again.

“Wulfrid’s family,” he said.

“His wife and three children remain.”

Holmar placed a figure before me. “This was taken from their labour. It will be returned.”

“The Count’s household—”

“Will receive what the accounts show,” he said.

I corrected the accounts that night.
