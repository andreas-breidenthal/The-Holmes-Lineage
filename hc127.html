SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅲ

1914 — Sussex, England

He was born in 1914 in Sussex — the same county where his grandfather had retired the previous year to keep bees on the Downs, and where, accordingly, his earliest memories included a tall, spare old man who moved through the garden with the unhurried precision of someone accustomed to reading things at a distance. His first year of life coincided with the outbreak of a war that would kill a generation, and his childhood was shaped by the long aftermath of that killing: the silence in households where the men had not come back, the particular English habit of not speaking about things that had happened, the sense, pervasive in the twenties and thirties, that something fundamental had been broken and not yet repaired.

He grew up in Holborn, in his father’s household, a spare and watchful boy who read everything and spoke selectively. His father, Holmes II, had himself grown up without the Great Detective’s direct presence; he had come to know his own father late, and at a careful distance, and understood the value of that acquaintance precisely because it had been so hard to come by. He was not going to allow his son to be similarly deprived. The boy grew up knowing his grandfather — visiting the Sussex cottage, sitting with the old man on the Downs — and when the gift declared itself early, as it did, the explanation of what it was and where it came from came from its proper source, directly and without intermediary. The Great Detective, by then in his seventies and eighties, was entirely willing to provide it. He was not, as his grandson later observed, a man who found it easy to be retired.

He did not attend university. He was recruited instead, in the spring of 1936, by a department of the Foreign Office whose precise functions were not described in his letter of appointment, which was brief and which he was asked to destroy. He spent the next three years learning languages — German, principally, and enough French and Italian to be useful — and the related disciplines of close observation, documentary analysis, and the careful management of what other people believed about you. He was good at all of these. He was particularly good at the last.

In the years between his recruitment in 1936 and the outbreak of war, Holmes III undertook, under the direction of the Foreign Office, a piece of misdirection so audacious that only a man of the family’s particular temperament could have conceived it. He took the screen name Basil Rathbone and appeared, in two films produced in 1939 — The Hound of the Baskervilles and The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — as his own grandfather, rendering Watson’s Victorian record with a fidelity that drew on something rather more than research: the man himself was eighty-five years old and living in Sussex, and had been consulted, in his characteristically oblique fashion, on whether the enterprise was worth undertaking. He had indicated, with the economy of expression that his grandson had grown up reading, that it was.

When war came, the Universal Pictures series that followed updated the setting to the present conflict, and Holmes III continued in the role — now, in effect, playing himself, a consulting detective operating against German agents in wartime Britain, under a name the public associated with fiction and the Intelligence Inner Council knew to be rather more than that. It was, by any measure, the most sustained and elaborate piece of cover that any member of the line had yet constructed. The original, still alive on the Downs, is not recorded as having commented on it. That, too, was characteristic.

One further acknowledgement is warranted. Nigel Bruce played Dr. Watson alongside Holmes III in all fourteen films — a loyal, bumbling, good-hearted Watson who bore little resemblance to the precise and formidable man of the original record, but who brought to the role an affection for his subject that was entirely genuine. Bruce never knew that the man beside him on screen was the grandson of the very detective he was portraying — a detective who was, at the time of filming, alive and in his mid-eighties in Sussex — nor that the actual grandson of John H. Watson was working alongside Holmes III in the field when the cameras were not rolling. Had anyone told him, it is difficult to imagine he would have believed it. It is equally difficult to imagine a more perfect illustration of the family’s gift for operating in plain sight.

Sherlock III and the Spyder Woman

Being a narrative by James Watson

The following account occurred in the spring of 1944, at the height of the global conflagration, when the world was facing the existential threat from the merciless German war machine. Espionage infiltration was a risk in every walk of life, and Hollywood was not exempt.

I.

It is a peculiarity of the motion picture business that its apparent chaos conceals a machinery of extraordinary precision. A film set presents itself to the uninitiated as organised bedlam — cables snaking across concrete floors, men in period costume arguing beside men in shirtsleeves, the acrid smell of arc lights burning overhead and the sweeter, subtler smell of greasepaint applied and re-applied throughout the long day. I had grown, over the weeks of our engagement at the Universal lot in the spring of 1944, rather fond of it. This, I later came to understand, was precisely what Holmes had anticipated.

We were engaged ostensibly as technical consultants on the production. In practice this meant that Holmes appeared on screen four days a week as Basil Rathbone — which is to say as his own grandfather, which is to say as the Great Detective himself — a circumstance so audacious that it had become, through sheer repetition, entirely unremarkable to everyone on the lot. To the cast and crew he was an actor of considerable presence and somewhat exacting professional standards. To the Intelligence Inner Council he was Holmes III, and the name on his dressing room door was a cover so complete that its completeness was its least interesting feature.

My own function was less glamorous and, I think, more useful. I moved through the production with a clipboard and the expression of a man attending to period detail, and I watched. It is the Watson function, as my grandfather’s friend once observed to my grandfather, and it has not changed across the generations: to be present, to be unalarming, and to notice everything without appearing to notice anything at all.

There was one further party to our arrangement, and I must treat him with the care he deserves. Nigel Bruce played Watson in the pictures — a Watson rather unlike my grandfather, warmer and considerably more bewildered, but played with an affection so genuine that nobody who spent an hour on that set could mistake it for performance. He knew Holmes as Rathbone and knew me, when he knew me at all, as a quiet Englishman with a clipboard who occasionally had opinions about walking sticks. The truth of the matter — that the man beside him on screen was the grandson of the very detective he was portraying, that said detective was alive and in his mid-eighties in Sussex and had been consulted on the enterprise, and that the actual grandson of John H. Watson was standing fifteen feet away watching the whole arrangement with professional interest — would, I think, have defeated him entirely. There are some realities that resist absorption, and this was one of them. He was, in every sense that mattered, the most innocent man on the lot.

The production office occupied a long, low-ceilinged room on the eastern side of Stage Four. It smelled of carbon paper and cold coffee and the particular human warmth of people who have been working in an enclosed space since before the light. Along the eastern wall stood the documentary infrastructure of three productions proceeding in simultaneous and overlapping stages: distribution ledgers and consular invoices for The Scarlet Claw, whose May release was pressing down on every desk; daily camera logs and rushes schedules for The Pearl of Death, whose principal photography was running through April and into May on the Borgia Pearl sets while the Canadian village sets from the previous picture were still being struck and hauled from the far end of the lot; and the projected timing templates and location travel manifests for The House of Fear, which existed at that moment as little more than working titles and ambition. Three productions, one set of clerks, a single filing system: it was, in the argot of the lot, a clerical storm, and it had been building since April. Dense columnar documents known as timing sheets moved through it in daily cycles — records of projected frame-length against actual footage, cross-referenced against laboratory processing times and distribution schedules, including, for The Scarlet Claw, the consular invoices that would accompany the master reels to neutral markets. Lisbon among them.

I was in the production office on a Tuesday morning in late April, making notes on nothing of consequence, when a production assistant named Hobbs appeared from the records room carrying a sheaf of timing sheets and wearing the expression of a man executing a habitual and mildly tiresome duty.

“Same as last week,” he said, to the room in general. Then: “Always the same columns. Never wrong enough to matter — just wrong enough to fix.”

He dropped the sheets on the nearest available desk and returned the way he had come.

I noted this as the ambient friction of institutional life and thought no more of it.

Holmes was standing two feet to my left, apparently absorbed in a call sheet. I did not see his expression change. I did not, at that moment, think to look.

<3031">II.

The work of the production continued. Holmes appeared on screen in the inverness cape and the deerstalker, delivering the Victorian cadences of a man he had spent his boyhood watching, with a fidelity that drew on something rather more than research. Nigel Bruce reacted to him with the warm, slightly baffled admiration he brought to every scene, and the cameras turned, and the footage accumulated, and between setups Holmes moved through the lot with the unhurried precision of a man who is always doing two things at once.

The Pearl of Death sets provided their own particular texture to these weeks. The sequences involving Rondo Hatton required extraordinary quiet — the Mitchell BNC cameras had been specified precisely for their silence, the better to capture certain scenes without mechanical intrusion, and the effect on the lot between takes was of a held breath, a stillness enforced by technical necessity rather than discipline. As the quiet Englishman with the clipboard who had no immediate function during those intervals, I had nothing to do but watch. Holmes, I came to understand, had known this would be the case.

What I observed, in the weeks that followed, was that one of the two things Holmes was always doing increasingly involved the production office.

He did not draw attention to this. He had developed the habit of stopping at the distribution ledgers on his way through — running a finger along the spines, pulling out a volume, replacing it. Once I saw him hold a timing sheet at an angle to the overhead light, for perhaps three seconds, before setting it back on the pile with the mild, incurious manner of a man who has satisfied a passing interest. His expression during these intervals was entirely unremarkable, and I took it for unremarkable, because I had not yet learned — or rather, had not yet relearned, for this lesson had been available to me my entire life — to distinguish between Holmes browsing and Holmes working.

There was a clerk in the production office whose typewriter I had come to know by its sound.

She operated it with a speed and evenness that distinguished it from the general office percussion the way a first violin distinguishes itself from the rest of the orchestra — not louder, but more deliberate, each keystroke placed with a precision suggesting the typist’s relationship to the machine was not merely functional. Her name on the production roster was Vera Hartmann, and she held the position of distribution coordinator, which required her to consolidate the timing sheets and consular invoices before they went to the accountant’s review each Wednesday morning — and, in the final weeks before a release, to prepare the documentation that would accompany the master reels as they moved through neutral ports.

I knew her by sight and by her typewriter and by the fact that Hobbs always delivered his complaint to her desk. I had not, at that stage, thought very much about her at all.

It was on a Thursday afternoon, crossing the lot between setups, that I saw something which lodged in my mind without immediately explaining itself.

Miss Hartmann was at the window of the production office. She did not know she was observed — she was attending to the sheet in front of her, and I was at the wrong angle for her to see me through the glass. On the desk beside her typewriter lay a small circular object, the size of a shilling, which I recognised after a moment as a typewriter eraser — the hard rubber disc, brush attached, that was the standard corrective instrument of the professional typist. She was using it on a timing sheet with the concentrated, unhurried care of a watchmaker attending to a mainspring — working at a single point on the page, brushing the surface clean with the small stiff brush, then rolling the sheet back into the typewriter carriage and retyping.

It was not, in itself, remarkable. Typists corrected errors. The eraser was the standard tool.

What remained in my mind was the care. The extraordinary, almost surgical care of a woman correcting a figure that, by Hobbs’s own account, was never wrong enough to matter.

<5657">III.

Holmes came off the stage on a Friday morning in the inverness cape and walked, without pausing at the coffee stand, directly across the lot. I fell into step beside him.

“The records annexe,” he said, without preamble. “The small room behind the filing cabinets. I’ve spoken with the production manager — we have the use of it this morning.”

“For what purpose?”

“Miss Hartmann has been asked to assist us with a query regarding the distribution records. She is expecting us at ten.”

I looked at him. “She is expecting us.”

“She has been told it is a routine matter.” He glanced sideways at me. “It will not be routine. But she will not know that until we are in the room with the door closed, and by that point it will not matter.”

I said nothing. I had learned, over the years, that there are questions one can usefully ask Holmes before an operation and questions one cannot, and that the difference between them is almost never apparent in advance.

The records annexe was a small, windowless room behind the main filing wall — shelves of archive boxes on three sides, a single overhead bulb, a table and three chairs that had clearly been arranged there for the morning’s purpose. There was no other entrance. Holmes set his leather case on the table and did not open it, and we waited.

Miss Hartmann knocked and entered at two minutes past ten.

She was dressed as I had always seen her — neat, professional, nothing remarkable. She looked at the room, at the closed door, at the two chairs on one side of the table and the single chair on the other, and I saw something pass through her expression — not fear, not quite, but the rapid, controlled assessment of a person who has entered a space and is calculating its dimensions.

“Gentlemen,” she said.

“Miss Hartmann.” Holmes indicated the single chair with the easy courtesy of a host. “Thank you for coming. Please sit down.”

She sat. Holmes sat opposite. I took the chair to his left, which placed me between her and the door without appearing to do so intentionally. I do not know whether this was Holmes’s arrangement or my own instinct. It may have been both.

“I’ll come directly to the matter,” said Holmes. “It concerns the timing sheets.”

“Of course.” Her voice was perfectly level. “Hobbs mentioned there had been some confusion about the correction process. I’m happy to explain —”

“The corrections,” said Holmes, “are in the same columns. Week after week, since the beginning of principal photography. Always the third column, occasionally the sixth.”

“The floor runners’ estimates tend toward the same errors. It’s a function of how they’re trained to —”

“Miss Hartmann.” Holmes’s voice did not change in temperature or pace. He opened his leather case and removed a timing sheet, which he placed on the table between them. “The third column. Rows four, seven, eleven, and nineteen. From the sheet dated the eleventh of April.”

He reached into the case again and produced a second document — a single column of figures, typed on plain paper, unheaded, without provenance — and laid it beside the first.

I looked at the two documents.

The timing sheet figures read: forty-three, forty-three, forty-three, forty-three.

The plain paper figures read: forty-three, forty-three, forty-three, forty-three.

I looked at them again, more carefully, because what I was seeing did not, for a moment, fully assemble itself into meaning. Four scenes — an interior dialogue, a chase sequence, an establishing shot, a confrontation of nearly four minutes. Their projected frame-lengths bore no rational relationship to one another. They were independent quantities, produced by independent calculations at different times by different people. There was no production logic under which they should share a third-column figure.

They shared one exactly.

Vera Hartmann looked at the two documents side by side. The composure she had been maintaining with such deliberate skill underwent, for a fraction of a second, a structural adjustment — not collapse, nothing so legible, but the faint, unmistakeable shift of a person who has been carrying a weight in careful balance and has felt the ground move beneath them.

She said nothing.

Holmes produced a second pair of documents. Third-column figures: sixty-one, seventeen, thirty-eight, sixty-one. Plain paper beside them: sixty-one, seventeen, thirty-eight, sixty-one.

Then a third pair. Twenty-two, forty-four, nine, twenty-two. The same.

The overhead bulb hummed. Somewhere beyond the wall a camera was being repositioned — I could hear the mechanical grind of the dolly track.

“A coincidence,” she said.

Her voice was steady. I will give her that.

“No,” said Holmes. “These figures match, precisely and without variation, a series of decoded intercepts received through the Lisbon routing by British Intelligence between March and April of this year.” He folded his hands on the table. “A clerk in Lisbon reads a frame-count of forty-two as a ship-tonnage of forty-two thousand. The consular invoices for The Scarlet Claw are the vehicle. They are not coincidences, Miss Hartmann. They are transmissions. And you sent them.”

“I process hundreds of figures a week,” she said. “The Scarlet Claw release alone —”

“The paper tells a clearer story than the figures,” said Holmes. He picked up the April timing sheet and held it at an angle toward the overhead bulb — tilting the surface so that the light skimmed across it at an oblique angle rather than falling flat upon it. He turned it toward her. “There.”

I leaned forward. In the fibres of the bond, in the third column, at each of the four corrected positions, there was a faint but unmistakeable roughening — a slight disruption in the surface of the paper, visible only at that particular angle, the ghost of an erasure performed with skill and care sufficient to leave no mark under flat light, but insufficient, under oblique examination, to entirely conceal that the space had been occupied before.

“You erased the original figure,” said Holmes, setting the sheet back down, “and retyped a new one in its place. Not a correction — a replacement. The original number is gone. Only the substitution remains.” He paused. “It is excellent technique. On studio-grade bond stock, a hard eraser leaves no visible trace under standard lighting conditions. I would not have found it without knowing precisely where to look. And I would not have known where to look without the intercepts.”

Vera Hartmann looked at the sheet. She looked at it for a long time.

“I was approached,” she said at last. “Before I took this position. I was told it was a minor thing. Production schedules. Nothing that would —”

“When did you understand what it was?”

A pause. “After the third week.”

“Seven months ago. And you continued.”

“By then —” She stopped. Her jaw set slightly. “By then I had no choice.”

“The people who arranged your position are not accommodating of resignations,” said Holmes. “Yes. I understand the logic of it.” He regarded her with an expression that contained, I thought, something close to genuine sympathy, constrained by the habit of not allowing sympathy to interfere with facts. “It would require me, however, to also believe that a woman of your abilities spent seven months encoding and transmitting material whose content was unknown to her. And I find that I cannot quite believe it.”

The silence that followed was of the particular quality that fills a room when a door has been closed that cannot be reopened.

Vera Hartmann looked at Holmes across the table. The assessment in her eyes was entirely open now — the calculation of a professional encountering another professional, measuring the distance between her position and his with the precision of a surveyor.

“What do you want?” she said.

<152153">IV.

What followed occupied the better part of an hour.

Holmes wanted, first, the encoding schedule — the key that mapped the column figures against the Lisbon routing. She produced it from the inside of her jacket: a single folded card, dense with figures, which she had been carrying on her person. I noted this and understood that she had been carrying it for some time, against the possibility of exactly this conversation — that she had known, at some level, that the conversation was approaching, and had prepared for it in the only way available to her.

Holmes examined it, folded it, and placed it in his case.

He wanted, second, the name of the financial intermediary who had arranged the Lisbon routing before principal photography began. This took longer. She offered resistance — not the resistance of ignorance but the resistance of a person who understands that the giving of a name is an irreversible act, and who is not yet certain that the alternative is worse.

Holmes waited. He was, in my experience, the most patient man in any room where patience was required.

She gave the name.

He wrote it in his notebook without visible reaction and did not tell me what it was. I did not ask.

“Now,” said Holmes, closing the notebook, “the matter of what happens next.”

She looked at him steadily. “You will take me in.”

“I will not.”

Whatever she had expected, it was not that. The steadiness faltered, very slightly. “Then what?”

“The channel you have spent seven months constructing represents a significant investment of time, skill, and operational infrastructure — not yours alone, but your principals’. Closing it accomplishes one arrest and alerts the receiving station that the line has been compromised, at which point they revise their methods and we lose both the channel and the intelligence picture it has provided.” Holmes picked up his coffee cup, which he had set on the corner of the table when he sat down, and turned it once in his hands. “Running it, with altered content prepared by people with a considered interest in what the German military high command believes about Allied operations, accomplishes rather more.”

“You want me to continue.”

“I want the channel to continue. Your continuation is the mechanism.” He set the cup down. “You will return to your desk this morning as you have returned to it every morning for seven months. Your work will be indistinguishable from your ordinary work, because it will be your ordinary work — the encoding, the correction, the filing, the consular invoices, the review. The single difference is that the figures you encode on Friday mornings will be provided to you by a colleague, and you will treat them as your own.”

“And if I refuse?”

“Then we have a rather different conversation, and you have a rather less comfortable summer.” He said this without emphasis, as one states a logistical fact. “But I do not think you will refuse, Miss Hartmann. I think you are a practical woman in a position that has run out of good options, and I think you can see as clearly as I can that this is the least bad one available.”

She was quiet for a long moment.

“You are asking me,” she said, “to betray them.”

“I am asking you to continue doing precisely what you have been doing. The content changes. Nothing else does.” He paused. “The distinction is yours to make of what you will.”

She looked at the table. At the timing sheets, the intercepts, the ghost of the erasure still faintly visible under the oblique light of the single overhead bulb.

“You said a colleague,” she said. “Who?”

“That,” said Holmes, “is a conversation for this evening.”

<200201">V.

We left the annexe at a quarter past eleven — Holmes first, then Miss Hartmann, then myself, with perhaps two minutes between each departure, enough to ensure that anyone who observed the procession would see three people who had been engaged in separate business in the same part of the building and happened to be leaving at roughly the same time. Holmes had thought of this. He had thought, I was coming to understand, of most things.

The day proceeded normally. Holmes returned to the set for the afternoon’s shooting. I attended to my usual function. Miss Hartmann was at her desk when I passed the production office at three o’clock, typing with her customary precision, and she did not look up as I went by, and I did not look in, and the timing sheets moved through their ordinary cycle of production and review and filing without incident.

At half past six Holmes appeared at the door of my temporary office in the costume he wore off-screen — a good dark suit, the inverness cape abandoned for the evening. He said nothing, and I asked nothing, and we walked together to the car that was waiting outside the lot.

Miss Hartmann was already in it.

She sat in the rear seat with the composed stillness of a woman who has decided that composure is the only resource remaining to her and intends to conserve it. She looked at Holmes as he got in, and at me, and then at the city moving past the windows as the car pulled away from the kerb.

“Where are we going?” she asked.

“Dinner,” said Holmes.

<218219">VI.

The restaurant was on Wilshire — quiet, wood-panelled, the kind of establishment that understood the value of tables set sufficiently far apart for private conversation. A city with as much to hide as Los Angeles in wartime develops a reliable supply of such places.

The man already seated at our table when we arrived was perhaps sixty, with the build of someone who had been large and strong in his youth and had settled, without particular regret, into a more considered solidity. He wore a dark suit of English cut and had the particular quality of stillness that I have come to associate with men who have spent long careers in rooms where a great deal depended on their not drawing attention to themselves. He rose when we approached and shook Holmes’s hand with the brief, undemonstrative warmth of an old professional acquaintance.

“Watson,” said Holmes, “this is Colonel Baxter. He represents the department that has been receiving Miss Hartmann’s transmissions for the past three weeks, and which has been receiving rather less useful ones for the seven months before that.”

Baxter looked at Miss Hartmann with the expression of a man examining a mechanism that has been running against him and is now, potentially, available to run for him. It was not an unkind expression. It was a thorough one.

“Miss Hartmann,” he said. “Please sit down.”

We sat. A waiter appeared, menus were distributed, wine was ordered — a Burgundy that Baxter selected with the brief authority of a man who has more important things to attend to but sees no reason to drink badly while he attends to them. When the waiter had gone he folded his hands on the table and looked at Miss Hartmann.

“Mr. Holmes has outlined the arrangement he proposes,” he said. “I want to be precise about what that arrangement means in practice, so that there is no ambiguity between us.”

She returned his gaze without flinching. “Please.”

“You will remain in your current position for the remainder of the production, and thereafter in whatever position we mutually agree upon. You will continue your work exactly as before — the encoding, the correction, the distribution, the consular invoices. Nobody in that office will know you as anything other than what you have always appeared to be.” He paused. “Each Friday morning you will receive, through a channel I will describe to you, the figures for that week’s transmission. You will encode them using your existing system and transmit them in your existing manner — including the Scarlet Claw consular documentation moving on the Serpa Pinto run to Lisbon. The content will have been prepared by my department. It will be accurate enough to maintain credibility and inaccurate enough to be useful.”

“And the people who recruited me?” she said.

“Will receive what we send them. For as long as it serves us to send it.” Baxter picked up his wine glass, examined it briefly, set it down without drinking. “If at any point they become suspicious of the material — if they contact you to query a transmission, or to alter the encoding system, or to make any change to the established arrangement — you will inform your handler immediately. You will not attempt to manage that situation yourself.”

“My handler,” she repeated.

“You will meet once a week. The location will vary. The contact will be made through a mechanism we will establish tonight.” Baxter glanced at Holmes. “Mr. Holmes will not be your handler. He is, as you have no doubt gathered, not the sort of man who keeps regular appointments.”

Holmes appeared to find this characterisation entirely acceptable.

“What do I receive in return?” she said.

“Your liberty,” said Baxter, without inflection. “Your continued employment. And the not inconsiderable advantage of being on the side that is going to win this war.” He permitted a brief pause. “Those are the terms. They are not negotiable, but they are, I think, considerably more agreeable than the alternative. The question is whether you understand them.”

“I understand them,” she said.

“And do you accept them?”

The wine arrived. The waiter poured with the elaborate ceremony of his profession and withdrew. Outside on Wilshire the wartime city moved through its careful paces — reduced, purposeful, managing itself with the stoic efficiency of a place that has accepted disruption as a permanent condition and has adapted accordingly.

Vera Hartmann looked at the wine in her glass. She looked at Holmes, who was examining the menu with the complete tranquillity of a man whose part in the evening’s business is finished. She looked at Baxter, who was watching her with the patient, professional attention of a man who is very good at waiting.

“Yes,” she said. “I accept them.”

Baxter nodded, once, with the finality of a man closing a ledger.

“Then let us have dinner,” he said, “and discuss the particulars.”

<268269">VII.

We were at the table until ten o’clock.

The particulars, as Baxter called them, occupied the better part of two hours — the mechanics of the handoff, the signal for the weekly meeting, the protocol for emergency contact, the precise procedure by which the Friday figures would reach her without passing through any hand that should not touch them. Holmes contributed to this discussion sparingly and with precision, offering details when asked and otherwise attending to his food with the appreciation of a man who has been eating studio commissary meals for two months and finds the improvement considerable.

I watched Miss Hartmann across the table.

She was, I had come to understand over the course of that long day, a more complex figure than the role she had been playing allowed her to appear. The composure was real, but it was not the composure of indifference — it was the composure of a woman carrying a weight that had become, over seven months, almost insupportable, and who had found, in the events of the morning, something she had not expected to find: a door that opened onto something other than ruin. She asked precise questions. She made no unnecessary concessions and sought none. When Baxter outlined a procedure she found impractical she said so, and proposed an alternative, and the alternative was adopted. I thought of Holmes’s observation in the annexe — that she was a practical woman, and that practical women in impossible positions are among the most useful people in any intelligence operation, because they understand very clearly what is at stake and they do not allow sentiment to interfere with the arithmetic of survival.

At five minutes to ten Baxter folded his napkin, shook hands with Holmes and with me, and looked at Miss Hartmann.

“Friday morning,” he said. “The mechanism we discussed.”

“Yes,” she said.

He left. The three of us remained for a moment at the table — Holmes, myself, and the woman who had, twelve hours earlier, been a German intelligence asset and was now something rather more complicated than that.

“The car will take you home,” said Holmes. He said it with the same ease with which he had said everything else that day, as though the events of the past twelve hours were a perfectly ordinary sequence of professional transactions, which I suppose, from his perspective, they very nearly were. “Tomorrow morning you will be at your desk at your usual hour. Nothing will have changed.”

She looked at him. “Everything has changed.”

“Outwardly,” said Holmes, “nothing. That is, in the end, the only thing that matters.” He rose. “You are better at this than the people who recruited you knew, Miss Hartmann. It would be a waste to leave it in their service.”

She considered this for a moment, and something in her expression shifted — not quite to warmth, but in that direction.

“Good evening, Mr. Holmes,” she said.

“Good evening,” said Holmes, and picked up his coat.

<300301">VIII.

On the last day of shooting for The House of Fear, the unit wrapped at half past five and the lot began its familiar process of dissolution — grips coiling cable, the Scottish village set beginning its slow dismemberment under the direction of the construction foreman, Nigel Bruce departing with his usual warmth and considerable noise. Holmes did not change out of the costume.

We were on the roof of the production building — the flat tar-papered expanse above the accounts department, accessible by a fire stair that nobody used and therefore useful for precisely that reason. The June evening was warm and clear, the last of the light going gold across the lot below us, the raw timber skeleton of the Scottish village visible at the far edge of Stage Three. Holmes had a telegram.

“You will explain it to me now,” I said.

“From the beginning?”

“From the beginning.”

He turned to face the lot.

“Hobbs,” he said. “The complaint. Always the same columns, never wrong enough to matter. That is where it began. Genuine errors are random — they fall where carelessness puts them, in different positions, at varying frequencies. Errors that occupy the same columns, week after week, without variation, are not errors. They are a system expressing itself as error because error is the only form a system of that kind can safely take in plain sight.”

“The erasure,” I said.

“The erasure was the proof of intent. A clerk who strikes through and amends is correcting a record — she wants the original visible beside the correction, for the auditor’s satisfaction. A clerk who erases and retypes is replacing a record — she wants the original gone, because the original, compared to the replacement, would reveal exactly what the replacement is. The motive for that level of care is not professional pride.” He paused. “Bond stock of studio grade holds a clean erasure for some weeks, if the technique is good. Under flat light it is invisible. Under oblique light — held at an angle toward a direct source — it shows as a slight roughening in the fibre. Barely perceptible. Entirely unmistakeable, once one knows what one is looking for.”

“She knew,” I said. “When you walked in in the costume.”

“She suspected something was wrong. An actor in full period dress crossing a working lot to visit the production office between setups is an anomaly, and she was a woman trained to notice anomalies.” The ghost of something moved in his expression. “I had not thought sufficiently about the costume. Or perhaps I had thought about it precisely enough. The anomaly is what put her on the back foot before I had said a word. It gave the opening.”

“And the coercion account?”

“Contained enough truth to serve as a negotiating position. She almost certainly was recruited before she took the position, and the routing mechanism was almost certainly built around her rather than by her. She had the intelligence to construct an account that was partly true and the inexperience to believe it would hold under pressure. When it didn’t, she moved to the practical question immediately. That,” said Holmes, “was when I knew she would be useful.”

He produced the telegram from his pocket and handed it to me.

It was brief, in a hand I recognised from two visits to a Sussex cottage in my childhood — compressed, precise, the handwriting of a man who had spent sixty years annotating things that needed to be kept in clean copy and had long since internalised the discipline completely.

Your instinct regarding the paper was correct. Bond stock of studio grade will hold a clean erasure to approximately twelve passes of a hard eraser before the fibre disrupts under oblique light. She would have known this and worked well within the tolerance. The angle of examination is everything. As it generally is. — H.

I read it twice.

“He solved it from a letter,” I said.

“In four sentences. He had encountered the technique once before — a different application, a different purpose, but the same principle. The replacement rather than the correction. He said it is always chosen for the same reason: the reader must see only what is intended, and nothing of what came before.” Holmes took the telegram back and folded it carefully. “He is eighty-nine years old, Watson. He has been retired to Sussex for thirty years. And he solved it in four sentences.”

“He is not,” I said, “particularly retired.”

“No,” said Holmes. “It is, I think, a family trait.”

Below us the lot completed its end-of-day rituals — the stage lights going out one by one, the last of the crew making for the gate, the Scottish village continuing its quiet dismemberment in the long California dusk. Holmes stood at the parapet in the inverness cape and the deerstalker with his hands in his pockets, looking out across it all, and I stood beside him, and neither of us said anything further.

There was nothing, by that point, that needed saying.

— J.W., Hollywood, June 1944
