AKAROS OF ATHENS

~1000 BC — Athens, Greece

The Tablets Of Nikanor

I.

The boy found us at the counting house before the harbour mist had lifted from the water, and he was running. That detail matters. In fifteen years of knowing Philagros, I had never seen a man in his employ run.

I should say something of Akaros, for those who will read these tablets at a remove from the events they record. He had come to Athens from Sparta with his father's directness and his family's long habit of commerce — a habit that had always carried with it a secondary quality, less common and more unsettling: the refusal to accept the surface of a thing as its substance. He had built his position in the Aegean trade not through fortune or favour but through a patience most men found uncomfortable. I was his account-keeper, son of a minor Attic scribe, and I had been with him long enough to know that the pauses mattered more than the words. What follows is what I set down as faithfully as wax allows.

The boy delivered his message in the breathless manner of a child who has been told something terrible and told it importantly: Sostias was dead. The silver was gone. The lock had not been touched.

Akaros set down his stylus.

He did not ask questions. He put on his cloak and was at the door before I had closed the account scroll, and I had the impression — as I sometimes did — that he had been waiting for the boy, or for something like the boy, the way a man waits at a game board for an opponent to finally make the move that will end it.

II.

I should speak of Sostias, because I did not speak of him enough at the time and I have regretted this in the years since.

He was a small, precise man, sixty or near it, with the particular dignity of someone who has performed a modest function very well for a very long time and understood that modest functions performed excellently are the architecture of a working world. He had watched Dromon's warehouse for thirty years, first for Dromon's father and then for Dromon himself, and in that time he had never lost a jar of oil to theft, never permitted a weight discrepancy to go unrecorded, never failed to be at his post when the sun went down. His family had held this trust since before Dromon's father received it, and the trust had been repaid in kind: small gifts at festivals, a word put in at the deme when Sostias's son had a dispute about a boundary wall, the general and unspoken understanding between men of good faith that the relationship was mutual.

He had known Lykios for twelve years.

I knew Sostias well enough to share a cup with him when I came to the warehouse on Akaros's business, and well enough that when I saw him lying across the threshold of the cedarwood door, I felt in my chest something that was not professional observation. I note this because Akaros noticed it too, and — though he said nothing — adjusted accordingly. He is not without feeling. He is without the luxury of permitting feeling to arrive before the work is done.

III.

Philagros met us at the top of the slope above the warehouse district. He was not a man given to visible agitation; his professional composure had survived thirty years of commercial dispute and the ordinary disasters of the harbour trade. That composure had not survived the morning. When he clasped his hands before him, they were not entirely steady.

"Sostias is dead," he said. "Found at the door of the inner room before dawn. The silver is gone — every bar of Akaros's consignment — and the lock on the cedarwood door has not been forced. The outer warehouse door was locked when I arrived. I do not understand what I am looking at."

The harbour smell reached us on the slope — salt brine and old rope and beneath it the sharper thing, fish oil rendered in the sun too long, stored in amphorae imperfectly sealed. I had grown used to the port, but that morning the smell sat differently on the air. Thicker. Close, as though the fog off the water had given it a body.

Akaros was not looking at Philagros. He was looking down the slope toward the western arm of the harbour, head tilted slightly to the left — the posture I had come to read as attentiveness, not the attentiveness of a man scenting danger but of a man filing a detail he does not yet understand. Then, without responding to Philagros in any way a casual observer would have recognised as a response, he said: "You have eaten nothing this morning."

Philagros blinked. "I — no. I came directly."

"Yes. And you slept badly before that, which means the boy came to you in the night rather than at dawn, and you have been standing at the edge of this thing for several hours, deciding what to do. You sent the boy to us last, not first. Why?"

Below us, a rope struck the hull of a moored vessel. Gulls cried somewhere in the fog.

"Because," Philagros said, with the deliberate care of a man selecting words from a reduced supply, "I did not know what I was sending you to. I wanted to be certain Sostias was dead before I said so."

"He is certainly dead," Akaros said. Then, not unkindly: "The Phocaean ship. What time does it sail?"

Philagros looked up sharply. "The tide turns at midday. Telesphoros has the manifest. If the silver is not loaded by—"

"Then we have until midday." Akaros had already started down the slope. "Come."

I will say this: when Akaros begins to move toward a problem, something changes in the air around him. He does not hurry — his pace is always the same — and yet those around him feel the urgency as though it were a physical pressure. Philagros and I exchanged a look and followed.

IV.

The warehouse of Dromon lay at the far end of the harbour's western arm, pressed between two larger storehouses belonging to Athenian families of longer tenure and greater wealth. Dromon had built his business on Ionian trade — Miletos, Phocaea, Samos — and Akaros had consigned to his care a considerable weight of silver in small formed bars, intended for the purchase of dyed wool and certain spices that moved better through Ionian hands than Athenian ones. The silver had been held in the innermost room, behind a cedarwood door with a lock of Cyprian bronze. Three men had keys: Dromon himself, his chief assistant Lykios, and Sostias, whose family had held this trust for two generations.

Akaros crouched at the threshold of the outer door. He did not look at the lock. He looked at the door's lower edge, where the wood met the packed earth of the floor, and ran the tip of one finger along the grain from the hinge side toward the latch, examining whatever had collected on his skin. He stood, rubbed his fingers together once, and stepped inside.

I followed, watching him rather than the room. He does this. In any space that holds a question, he occupies himself with the surfaces before he permits himself to look at the whole — a wall, a shelf support, the join between two planks — as though the room must be introduced to him in pieces before he will consent to understand it together. Others find this maddening. I have come to suspect it is the thing that makes him what he is.

The outer room held the ordinary material of a working warehouse: amphorae racked along the eastern wall, folded sacking in piles, a small brazier cold near the door, a low table with a sealed pot and the remains of what had been Sostias's last meal. Akaros paused at the brazier and looked into it without touching it. Then he came to the table.

He examined it with the slow care of a man reading an inscription in poor light — the position of the cup, the crumbs from a barley cake, the single oil lamp. He turned his head to change the angle of the light. He lifted the lamp and looked at its rim. He set it down, and something in his stillness sharpened in a way I had learned to recognise: the quality of a man who has just found the first thread and is deciding whether to pull it now or wait.

He waited. He looked at the ring marks on the table's surface — the wet circles left by a cup moved from one position to another — and he straightened.

"When Dromon is away," he said to Philagros, "who manages the counting house?"

"Lykios. He has access to all of Dromon's correspondence and his spare keys."

"Yes," said Akaros. He looked at the lamp rim once more, then away. "The inner room."

V.

Sostias lay on his back across the threshold of the cedarwood door, which stood open. The lock plate was undisturbed; the bolt had been properly retracted — withdrawn in the ordinary way, by a key, not forced. He had died from a blow to the back of the head delivered when he was already on the ground. The wound was not large but it was deep, made by something edged and heavy.

I looked at him and felt again what I had felt when Philagros said his name. Thirty years at this post. His family's trust repaid across generations. He had known Lykios for twelve years, and Lykios had sat across from him at that table and eaten his food, and then followed him to this door and killed him.

I filed this away in the place where a man files things he intends to examine when there is time, and I paid attention to Akaros.

The shelves inside were empty. Not ransacked — empty. Whatever had held the silver bars had been removed in good order, the cloth wrappings folded and left in a pile in the corner, the bars themselves gone.

I said what seemed to me the reasonable case: "Sostias opened the door himself, perhaps to take the silver, perhaps to allow someone else inside. He was struck from behind when his purpose was accomplished. The killer did not wish to share."

Akaros was moving his palm slowly across the empty shelf from one end to the other. He did not look up.

"What do you smell?"

I smelled the harbour, as I had all morning. And under it, in the confined space of the inner room, lamp oil. "Oil. From the lamp."

"Which lamp?"

There was no lamp in the inner room. There was a lamp hook on the wall — a simple iron peg — and below it, on the floor, a small dark stain, recently dried. The outer room had a lamp on the table near where Sostias had eaten. That was all.

"Someone brought a lamp in here," I said, "and left a lamp's worth of oil on the floor when they departed."

He looked up from the shelf then, with the expression he wears when a student has recited without understanding — not unkind, but not satisfied. "Someone brought a lamp in here and dropped it," he said, "when the work was almost complete, before the oil was entirely consumed. They were startled. They retrieved it quickly, not carefully — here, you can see where the rim dragged against the floor when it was righted — and they left. The stain is too small and too contained for a lamp that had been allowed to empty itself. And the silver was moved in the dark."

"In the dark?"

"Come here." He stepped back so I could stand at the shelving. "Look at the wood."

Old cedar, good quality. Smooth where hands had moved across it over many years. At the leading edge of each shelf, the faintest lines — fine, parallel, nearly invisible — scored in groups.

"Tally marks. Dromon's men counting the bars."

"Yes. But look at the marks at the far right of the middle shelf. Three sets of counts, and then a fourth — fainter, in the wrong direction, scoring across the grain rather than with it. Made by someone unfamiliar with the conventions of the house. Someone who lost their count and began again. And these scratches — not tally marks — where a bar was dragged across the wood at an angle, knocked rather than lifted. A man who can see what he is doing does not drag a bar at an angle. He picks it up."

I was following him now, as a man follows a guide along a path that keeps revealing itself. "The silver was moved in darkness," I said. "By someone who did not know the counting conventions. Who knocked a bar in the handling. Who brought a lamp but dropped it. And Sostias?"

"Sostias was already dead when the inner room was opened."

That brought me to a stop. "The blow to his head—"

"Was struck first. Before the door was unlocked. Look at where he lies." He gestured to the body in the threshold. "He is on his back. His heels are toward the inner room, his head toward the outer room. A man struck from behind while facing a locked door falls forward — into the door, or onto the floor between himself and it. He does not fall backward. Sostias was walking away from the door when he was struck. He had already unlocked it, already opened it, and turned to go back to his table. He was struck as he walked away and fell back across the threshold."

The fog had come off the water and sat in the doorway behind us. I could hear the harbour sounds muffled through it — a rope striking a hull, the cry of birds. "So Sostias opened the door for someone," I said. "Someone who moved the silver in darkness. Sostias had worked here thirty years — he would not have needed a lamp, and he would not have lost his count."

"He would not."

"Then Sostias let someone else in. Willingly. A man he trusted enough to share his meal with. And who left through the outer door, which was locked when Philagros arrived."

"Dromon is in Miletos," Akaros said, "but his key is not. Lykios manages the counting house." He paused. "A man who knew the warehouse well enough to know what it held and where — but not well enough to count in the dark or carry a bar without knocking it." He walked back to the outer room and stood at Sostias's table. "He shared this meal. You can see the ring marks — the cup in two positions. He sat across from Sostias, ate with him, and when Sostias rose to unlock the door and came back — he was struck from behind. A trusted colleague. Twelve years of shared employment."

He looked at the lamp on the table. He looked at it for a long time.

"He came back through this room on his way out," Akaros said, very quietly. "He had two lamps in this warehouse: Sostias's, which he had to leave, and his own, which he could not. He set his own lamp on this table in the dark beside the one already here, and in his haste he struck the rims together." He touched the lamp's rim with one finger — the particular mark I had not known to look for. "His lamp went home with him. Bent."

He straightened. His voice changed — became the efficient, decisive voice he uses when the problem is solved and what remains is engineering.

"Nikanor. Go to the eastern pier and tell Telesphoros that his silver will arrive before the tide. Not possibly — before the tide. Make it a statement of fact." He turned to Philagros. "Send word to Lykios's house. Tell him there is new evidence at the warehouse that must be examined by the chief assistant before the elders of the deme are summoned. Tell him I am there now and require his expertise to establish the inventory of what has been taken. Tell him—" He paused, and the pause had an edge to it I had not heard before. "—that the evidence is delicate and I need someone who knows these shelves."

Philagros stared at him. "You want to bring him back here."

"I want him to bring himself back here," Akaros said. "There is a difference."

VI.

I spoke to Telesphoros, who looked at me with the placid scepticism of a man who has been promised things by merchants before, and I told him with a certainty I did not entirely feel that his silver would be there, and he looked at me a moment longer and went back to his ropes without committing to anything, which in the maritime trade is as close to trust as one usually receives.

When I returned to the warehouse, Akaros was standing in the inner room in the near-dark, having closed the shutters of the single high window. He was standing at the empty shelves with his arms at his sides, doing nothing that I could see, and when I came in he turned and regarded me as though checking that I was the right person.

"I need you to do something that will be difficult," he said.

"What?"

"When Lykios arrives, I need you to be genuinely uncertain. Not performing uncertainty — genuinely uncertain. So I am going to tell you only this much: when he comes, he will attempt to offer an explanation for everything I show him. He is not a stupid man. He will have constructed a plausible account during the walk here. You will hear things that sound reasonable. You must allow them to sound reasonable and let it show in your face. Do not look at me for guidance. Do not nod when I make a point. Be what you are — a scribe who is not sure what he is looking at."

"You want to read his responses against a man with no settled view."

"I want his eyes to find yours when he is uncertain, and I want yours to give him nothing. Neither agreement nor suspicion." He looked at me with the directness he reserves for moments of genuine importance. "Can you do this?"

I said that I could. I was not entirely certain I could.

"Good. One more thing." He reached inside his cloak. He had a short wooden baton — the kind a harbour porter uses to tally amphora loads, thick as two fingers together, weighted at one end. He set it on the table beside the brazier, close enough to be reached quickly. "If he moves for me or moves for the door with any urgency, you will put yourself at that door. Not to fight him. To be there. The delay may be enough."

I looked at the baton. I looked at Akaros. "He killed Sostias."

"Yes."

"And you mean to stand in a closed room with him."

"That is where the evidence is," Akaros said, with a simplicity that was either very courageous or very characteristic, and I have come to believe it was both.

VII.

Lykios came within the hour.

He came alone, which was either the action of a man with a clear conscience or the action of a guilty man who understood that a witness to this conversation would not serve him. He was perhaps forty years of age, broad through the shoulders, with the careful, composed face of a man who has been constructing his performance since before dawn. He wore a clean tunic. He had washed his hands. He greeted Philagros in the doorway with the warm, grave concern of a colleague who has heard terrible news and come immediately.

"Sostias," he said, and shook his head. "A dreadful business. I came as soon as your boy reached me. What is this evidence you've found?"

Akaros led him through the outer room and into the inner room without preamble. The shutters remained closed. In the dim light, the empty shelves had a particular quality — not ransacked, not disordered, simply gone, as though the silver had never been.

"The inventory," Akaros said, gesturing to the shelves. "I need you to tell me what was held where. Dromon's records are in Miletos with him, and I need to establish exactly what is missing before the elders convene."

Lykios looked at the shelves. He moved to them with the practiced ease of a man who has stood in this room before, and he began to describe the storage arrangement — which shelf held which consignment, how the bars had been racked, in what order the cloth wrappings had been applied. He spoke with the fluency of knowledge. He did not hesitate.

I watched him and I did what Akaros had asked: I let it register on my face that this was useful, that it sounded right. Because it did sound right. He was good at this.

"And the tally conventions," Akaros said. "Dromon's method. How were the bars counted in and out?"

Lykios explained. He described the marks on the shelf edges — the direction of the scoring, the groupings — with the same fluency. He was, I noted, describing exactly the correct method, the one that matched the three proper sets of marks on the wood and contradicted the fourth, the wrong one, the one made by someone who didn't know.

Akaros let him finish. Then he said: "Show me."

Lykios took a small stylus from the fold of his tunic — the working tool of an account-keeper, always carried — and he demonstrated on the air above the shelf the motion of the marks, left to right, with the grain.

"Yes," said Akaros. "And the fourth set? The ones going across the grain?"

A silence.

Not long. Lykios filled it quickly, with the ease of a man who has prepared for exactly this question. "Sostias's own marks," he said. "He had an old habit — Dromon corrected him many times. He never entirely lost it. If there are anomalous marks on the wood, they will be Sostias's, made years ago."

It was a good answer. I felt my face do something that was not quite belief and not quite doubt, and I let it stay there because it was genuine. It was a good answer.

Akaros nodded slowly. "And the lamp?"

"What lamp?"

Akaros crossed to the iron peg on the wall and pointed to the floor beneath it — the small dark stain, dried now but still distinct. "A lamp was dropped here. You can see the oil stain, and here the drag mark where the rim caught the floor as it was retrieved."

Lykios looked at it. He looked at it with the attentiveness of a man seeing it for the first time, and I will say this for him: he was convincing. "Sostias," he said. "He kept a second lamp for the inner room. He must have dropped it — perhaps when he heard whoever came for the silver. Perhaps in the struggle."

"He kept no lamp in the inner room," Akaros said, without heat. "There is one hook, and it is empty. The warehouse accounts show one lamp in the inventory — the one on the outer table. I checked this with Philagros before you arrived."

Another silence. Shorter this time. The account that Lykios had been building — carefully, steadily, one explanation fitted against the next — had sprung a joint. I watched his eyes move to me, seeking, and I gave him nothing. Neither agreement nor suspicion, as Akaros had instructed. I was a scribe who did not know what to think.

"Then it was the thief's lamp," Lykios said. "Dropped in the dark when Sostias discovered him."

"Sostias was already dead when the inner room was opened," Akaros said.

The air in the closed room changed.

It was not dramatic. It was not a visible thing. But something shifted in Lykios — not capitulation, not yet, but the quality of a man who has felt the ground move beneath a foot he had believed to be certain. He was still standing at the shelves. He was still composed. But the composure had become a different thing — no longer easy but maintained, the way a heavy load is maintained when the arms are beginning to fail.

"The body's position tells us that," Akaros continued, and his voice was level and clear, the voice of a man reading from a text. "He fell backward. A man struck from behind while facing the locked door falls forward. Sostias had already opened the door, already turned back toward the outer room, and was struck as he walked away. He fell across the threshold with his heels toward the inner room and his head toward the table where he had been sitting. Where he had been sitting with a guest."

Lykios said nothing.

"The cup," Akaros said. "Two ring marks on the table. One where a guest's cup rested during the meal. One where it was set down again, closer to the edge, when the guest stood up — stood up to follow Sostias toward the door."

Lykios moved.

He did not run — there was nowhere to run — but he turned from the shelves with a speed that was sudden and purposeful, and he put himself between Akaros and the door, and what had been the face of a composed account-keeper was something else now. He was larger than I had registered. The shoulders that I had noted as broad were not the shoulders of a man who kept accounts; they were the shoulders of a man who also loaded things, carried things, worked. His hands came up from his sides, and they were not empty. He had a knife — a short working blade, the kind a warehouse man carries for rope and bindings — and he had had it since he came through the door, and I understood then that the composed face and the clean tunic and the good answers had been one kind of calculation, and this was another, and he had arrived here prepared to use whichever calculation the situation required.

"You have a theory," he said. His voice was still controlled. "You have marks on wood and a stain on a floor. Take it to the elders of the deme and let them weigh it."

"I also have the lamp," Akaros said.

He said it the way a man lays down a tile in a game — without emphasis, without drama, setting it into its place in the pattern.

"The lamp on Sostias's table," he continued, "which you returned to the outer room on your way out, setting it beside the one already there in the dark and striking the rims together. Your lamp went home with him. Bent at the rim where the two struck. I saw the mark on Sostias's table lamp this morning, where the collision happened. And I know that a lamp with a bent rim consistent with that collision is in your possession, because you did not have time between the night's work and this morning to replace it. I know this because no lamp-maker in the harbour quarter opens before the third hour, and you came here when the second hour was not yet finished."

I had moved to the door. I do not entirely remember deciding to do this; my body made the decision before my mind ratified it. I was not a large man. I had the baton from the table and I was standing in the doorway and I was aware, with a very clear and cold clarity, that Lykios was a man who had killed Sostias and that Sostias had been sixty years old and small, and that I was neither sixty nor small, but that the knife was a significant complication and that the door was what mattered, not the fight.

Lykios looked at me. He looked at Akaros.

"Stand away from the door," he said.

"No," I said. I was surprised by how level my own voice was.

He took a step toward me and Akaros said, from directly behind him and very close: "The silver is already at the pier."

Lykios stopped.

"I sent word to Telesphoros before you arrived," Akaros said. "The silver is not in your mother's house. The floorboard in the back room has been lifted, the bars counted, and the cart is being loaded at this moment. The ship will sail with my consignment, and you will be here, and there will be no profit and no departure and no version of this morning in which the knife changes anything except the charge the elders of the deme consider."

This was, I learned later, not entirely true. The silver was still under Lykios's mother's floor. Akaros had sent Philagros's boy to establish where the cart was and whether there was a back room with a board that moved, and the boy had come back before Lykios arrived, and the information was accurate. The rest — the loading, the counting — was architecture. A structure built to hold a specific weight at a specific moment.

Lykios stood in the middle of the room with the knife in his hand and the door behind him blocked and the walls of what Akaros had built pressing in on all sides, and the thing that went out of him went quickly, as I recorded before: the way water goes out through a crack, not dramatically but entirely, leaving the vessel diminished and different in shape.

He put the knife on the shelf.

He did not speak for a long time. When he did, he said: "He saw the cart. He came to the door of the warehouse as I was loading — he had forgotten something, or heard something, I don't know which — and he saw it, and he looked at me, and he understood. He would have called out."

"Yes," Akaros said. "I know."

"He was my friend," Lykios said.

"Yes," Akaros said again. "I know that also."

VIII.

We loaded the silver ourselves. Lykios carried the bars under Akaros's eye and said nothing. His hands were certain with the weight in the daylight — practised, efficient — and I thought about that the whole way to the pier: how a man who had handled inventory his entire life, who could carry silver bars with ease in the morning sun, had lost his count and knocked a bar from the shelf in the dark of the inner room. Because knowledge of a thing and knowledge in a thing are separated, as Akaros would say, by the distance between the map and the ground. Lykios had known the warehouse. He had not known it the way Sostias had known it — in the body, in the hands, in the dark.

We reached Telesphoros with time enough. The captain looked at the silver, looked at us, looked at Lykios, and asked no questions. Maritime courtesy. You learn, in this trade, that the story behind the delivery is rarely one you want to have heard.

Philagros went to the elders of the deme. It was, as Akaros had said, the right thing.

IX.

That evening, I asked him about the lie.

We were walking back through streets that were fully awake now, the morning's events sitting in the recent past with the particular weight of things that have not yet been absorbed.

"The silver was not at the pier," I said. "You told him it was."

"I told him it would be," Akaros said. "Which was true. I intended it to be there, and it was. The sequence of events was compressed in the telling." He glanced at me sideways. "Does that trouble you?"

I considered it. "Not in the way it would trouble a philosopher," I said. "In the way it troubles a scribe who is supposed to be writing the accurate account."

"Write that I told him the silver was as good as at the pier," he said, "and that the pier was where it ended up, and leave the middle for a philosopher."

I wrote it as it happened. He will have to live with that.

I asked him also when he had known about the lamp. He told me what I have already set down — the bent rim seen in the outer room that morning, the realisation that the killer had returned through that room, had set his own lamp beside Sostias's on the table in the dark, had struck the rims together in his haste. The collision mark was on Sostias's lamp. The bent rim was on Lykios's. The two together described the whole geometry of the departure.

"You could have told me that in the warehouse," I said. "Instead of letting me walk into that room not knowing."

"Yes," he said. "But I needed your face to be the face of a man who was genuinely uncertain. Not performing uncertainty. A man in Lykios's position — who has prepared an account, who has rehearsed his explanations, who is watching every face in the room for the angle of danger — such a man sees through a performed face immediately. He reads the eyes. He reads the stillness. Your face gave him nothing because you had nothing, and that nothing was the space in which he chose to keep explaining, keep building, keep adding weight to an account that could not hold it."

I stopped walking. "You used me," I said. "As a component."

"I used your honesty," he said. "Which is a different thing, and a more respectful one." He tilted his head — the left-side tilt of his attentiveness, that private metronome. "Are you angry?"

I examined the question with the seriousness it perhaps did not entirely deserve, and with the thought of Sostias lying across a threshold with his heels toward the door he had opened for a man he trusted. "A little," I said. "Yes."

"Good," he said. "It would indicate a healthy self-regard."

He walked on. After a moment, I followed.

X.

Philagros thanked Akaros with the formal thoroughness of a man who has been frightened and is now safe and wishes to deposit the experience in the past as completely as possible. Akaros accepted this with his usual brevity. He is not a man who needs gratitude. He is a man who needs the problem to have been a good one.

When all of it was settled, I asked him what I had meant to ask at the warehouse: whether justice was well served in this case — not legally, but in the older sense, the weight of the thing.

"Sostias is dead," he said, without inflection. "That is not recoverable. The silver is recovered. The contract holds. Whether what will follow for Lykios is equal to what he did — that is a question for the gods, or for the elders of the deme, and I am neither." He looked at the water. "What I can say is that I was given a problem, and the problem was soluble, and I solved it. The satisfaction is in that. The rest is grief."

He went inside. I stayed a moment longer, looking at the harbour — the water doing what water always does, indifferent and continuous, keeping no record of the things that happen at its edges.

The honest difficulty is not finding the answer. It is living with how simple it always turns out to be.

He said that, in the inner room, looking at the empty shelves. I have put it here at the end because it did not belong there — at the moment of discovery, it would have been a flourish. It belongs here, at the close of the account, where it is the truth about a man I have followed for fifteen years into rooms that held questions.

He had sat across from Sostias. He had eaten his food. Thirty years of faithful service, and at the end of it a friend with a knife and a cart waiting in the lane and a locked door behind him.

I find — as Akaros would perhaps approve — that the facts are the thing, and the man is only incidental to them.

But I have been his account-keeper long enough to know that the man is not incidental at all. And I have known Sostias long enough to know that neither is the dead.

This account is copied from the original wax by my hand in the year of my own stewardship, as faithfully as the condition of the tablets allows. Some words are lost where the wax cracked in the later storage. What I have given is what I have.

— N., son of Epithetes.
