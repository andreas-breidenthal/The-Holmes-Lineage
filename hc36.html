AKAROS OF ATHENS

~1000 BC — Athens, Greece

Athens at the turn of the millennium was a town rather than a city — a community of perhaps a few thousand on the rock of the Acropolis and the slopes beneath it, with the memory of the Mycenaean palace faded to legend and the great age still two centuries away. Akaros arrived from Sparta with his father’s directness and his family’s long habit of commercial and intellectual engagement. He carried with him a habit of weighing things twice — once with the hand and once with the mind — and a preference for certainty over assertion that set him apart from other men of trade.

He was among those who strengthened Athens’ position in the widening web of exchange across the Aegean, establishing durable ties with Ionian ports and maintaining correspondence with Phoenician merchants whose routes reached beyond sight of any Greek horizon. He moved between counting houses and shipyards with equal ease, and he brought to both the same quality his family had always brought to the places where commerce and deception intersected: a patience with uncertainty that most men found uncomfortable, and a refusal to accept the surface of a thing as its substance.

Among his company was Nikanor, son of a minor Attic scribe, who kept accounts and notes of Akaros’ dealings. Nikanor recorded not only transactions but the manner of the man himself — his pauses, his questions, and the peculiar habit he had of touching a surface before speaking of it. What follows is taken from Nikanor’s surviving tablets, preserved in fragments and later recopied into family record.

The Tablets Of Nikanor

The harbour smell reached us before the harbour did. Salt brine and the particular rot of old rope and the sharper thing beneath it — fish oil rendered in the sun too long, stored in amphorae that had been imperfectly sealed. I had grown used to the port, but that morning the smell sat differently on the air. Thicker. Close, as though the fog off the water had given it a body.

Akaros noticed it too. He stopped at the top of the slope above the warehouse district, his head tilted slightly to the left in that posture I had come to read as attentiveness — not the attentiveness of a man scenting danger but of a man filing a detail he did not yet understand. Then he continued down without speaking.

The warehouse of Dromon lay at the far end of the harbour’s western arm, pressed between two larger storehouses belonging to Athenian families of longer tenure and greater wealth. Dromon had built his business on Ionian trade — Miletos, Phocaea, Samos — and Akaros had consigned to his care a considerable weight of silver in small formed bars, intended for the purchase of dyed wool and certain spices that moved better through Ionian hands than Athenian ones. The silver had been stored in the innermost room of Dromon’s warehouse, behind a cedarwood door with a lock of Cyprian bronze — a fine piece of work, brought north at some expense. Three men had keys: Dromon himself, his chief assistant Lykios, and the nightwatchman Sostias, whose family had served Dromon’s family in that same capacity since Dromon’s father’s time.

Sostias was dead. The overseer Philagros, a stout man whose professional composure was visibly at war with his personal agitation, had sent his boy to find us before the sun was fully above the hills.

“He was found at the door of the inner room,” Philagros said. “The silver is gone. Every bar. The lock has not been forced.”

I said what seemed obvious to say. “Then Sostias had a key, and Sostias opened the door. He was killed afterward, perhaps in a quarrel over the division.”

Akaros said nothing. He was looking at the outer door of the warehouse.

“Was this door locked when you arrived?” he asked Philagros.

“Yes. I have the only other key to the outer door, besides Dromon’s.”

“And Dromon?”

“Dromon is in Miletos. He has been gone twelve days.”

Akaros crouched at the threshold of the outer door. He did not look at the lock. He looked at the door’s lower edge, where the wood met the packed earth of the floor. He ran the tip of one finger along the grain of the wood, from the hinge side toward the latch, and brought it back to examine whatever had collected on his skin. He stood, rubbed his fingers together once, and stepped inside.

I followed, watching him rather than the room. He does this. In any space that holds a question, he occupies himself with the surfaces of things before he permits himself to look at the whole. A wall. A shelf support. The join between two planks. It is as though the room must be introduced to him in pieces before he will consent to understand it together. Others find this maddening. I have come to suspect it is the thing that makes him what he is.

The outer room held the ordinary material of a working warehouse: amphorae racked along the eastern wall, folded sacking in piles, a small brazier cold now near the door, a low table with a sealed pot and the remains of what had been Sostias’s meal. Akaros paused at the brazier. He looked into it without touching it. He looked at the low table, at the pot, at the arrangement of Sostias’s things, and his eyes moved quickly, back and forth, in the manner of a man reading.

Then we went through to the inner room.

Sostias lay on his back across the threshold of the cedarwood door, which stood open. The door itself showed no damage. The lock plate was undisturbed and the bolt had been properly retracted — withdrawn in the ordinary way, by a key, and not pulled. Sostias had died from a blow to the back of the head delivered when he was already on the ground; I could see that much. The wound was not large but it was deep, made by something edged and heavy.

The shelves inside were empty. Not ransacked — empty. Whatever had held the silver bars had been removed in good order, the cloth wrappings folded and left in a pile in the corner, the bars themselves taken.

I said, again, what seemed to me the reasonable case: “Sostias opened the door himself, perhaps to take the silver, perhaps to allow someone else inside. He was struck from behind when his purpose was accomplished. The killer did not wish to share.”

Akaros was touching the empty shelf with his right hand, moving his palm slowly across the wood from one end to the other, as though the silver might have left an impression. He did not look up.

“What do you smell?” he asked.

I smelled the harbour, as I had all morning. And under it, in the confined space of the inner room, lamp oil. “Oil,” I said. “From the lamp.”

“Which lamp?”

There was no lamp in the inner room. There was a lamp hook on the wall — a simple iron peg — and below it, on the floor, a small dark stain, recently dried. The outer room had a lamp on the table near where Sostias had eaten. That was all.

“Someone brought a lamp in here,” I said, “and left a lamp’s worth of oil on the floor when they departed.”

Akaros finally looked up from the shelf. He looked at me with the expression he sometimes wears when a student has recited without understanding — not unkind, but not satisfied. “Someone brought a lamp in here and dropped it,” he said, “when the work was almost complete, before the oil was entirely consumed. They were startled. They picked the lamp up quickly, not carefully — here, you can see where the rim dragged against the floor when it was righted — and they left. The stain is too small and too contained for a lamp that had been allowed to empty itself.” He looked back at the shelf. “And the silver was moved in the dark.”

“In the dark?”

“Come here.” He stepped back so I could stand before the shelving. “Look at the wood.”

I looked. Old cedar, good quality. Smooth where hands had moved across it over many years. And at the leading edge of each shelf, the faintest lines — fine, parallel, nearly invisible — scored in groups.

“Tally marks,” I said. “Dromon’s men counting the bars in and out.”

“Yes. But look at the marks at the far right of the middle shelf, where the most recent consignment was held. Three sets of counts, and then a fourth — see it, fainter, in the wrong direction, scoring across the grain rather than with it. Made by someone who was not familiar with the conventions of the house. Someone who lost their count and began again.” He ran his fingernail along one of the marks. “And these scratches — not tally marks — where a bar was dragged across the wood at an angle, knocked rather than lifted. A man who could see what he was doing does not drag a bar at an angle. He picks it up.”

I was following him now, though slowly, as a man follows a guide along a path that keeps revealing itself. “The silver was moved in darkness,” I said, “by someone who did not know the counting conventions, who knocked a bar in the handling, who brought a lamp but dropped it. And Sostias?”

“Sostias was already dead when the inner room was opened.”

That brought me to a stop. “The blow to his head—”

“Was struck first. Before the door was unlocked. Look at where he lies.” He gestured to the body in the threshold. “He is on his back. His heels are toward the inner room and his head is toward the outer room. A man struck from behind while facing the locked door falls forward — into the door, or onto the floor between himself and it. He does not fall backward. Sostias was walking away from the door when he was struck. He had already unlocked it, already opened it, and turned to go back to his table. The killer struck him from behind as he walked away, and he fell backward across the threshold.”

The fog had come off the water and sat in the doorway behind us. I could hear the harbour sounds muffled through it — a rope striking a hull somewhere, the cry of birds. I said, after a moment: “So Sostias unlocked the door, opened it, and then was killed. But he did not take the silver himself, because the silver was moved in darkness by someone uncertain of their surroundings. Sostias had worked in this warehouse for years. He knew where the bars were. He would not have needed a lamp, and he would not have lost his count or dragged a bar.”

“He would not,” Akaros agreed.

“Then Sostias let someone else in. He opened the door for them.”

“Yes.”

“Willingly?”

Akaros said nothing. He walked back to the outer room and stopped at Sostias’s table — the cold brazier, the sealed pot, the remains of the meal. He picked up the pot and looked at the seal, which was a simple clay stopper pressed with a thumb. He did not open it. He set it down and looked at the arrangement of the table as a whole: the position of the cup, the crumbs from a barley cake, the single oil lamp that had been burning.

“Someone shared this meal with him,” Akaros said. “The cup has been at two different places on the table — you can see the ring marks. Someone sat across from Sostias, ate with him, and was still here when the inner room was opened. Sostias rose from the table to unlock the door, opened it, turned back toward his guest — and was struck.”

“A man he trusted,” I said. “A friend. Someone he would eat with at his watch.”

“A man he trusted,” Akaros agreed, “who knew the warehouse well enough to know where the inner room was and what it held, and who came prepared to move silver in quantity — but who nevertheless dropped his lamp and lost his count in the dark, because knowing of a place is not the same as knowing in it.” He paused. “And who left through the outer door.”

“But you said the outer door was locked when Philagros arrived.”

“Yes.”

I thought about that. “Philagros has a key,” I said slowly. “Dromon has a key. And Dromon is in Miletos.”

“Dromon is in Miletos,” Akaros said. He looked at me in that patient way that I have written before and will not tire of writing: the look of a man who has already arrived and is waiting, without cruelty, for the rest of the world to find its way to the same point. “But his key is not.”

We found Lykios at the house of his mother, which was where he had always returned in difficulty, as men do. He had silver dust in the creases of his palms — the particular residue of handled bar silver, worked into the skin — and a lamp with a bent rim that he had not yet thought to dispose of. He had arrived at the warehouse before the watch change, had shared Sostias’s meal as he had sometimes done over the years of their joint employment, and had used the spare key from Dromon’s counting house — where he had access as chief assistant — to lock the outer door behind himself when the silver was carried out to the cart he had waiting.

He had struck Sostias from behind because Sostias had seen the cart.

Philagros, when told, wished to go to the prytaneis immediately. The Athenian habit of public administration was beginning even then to make itself felt. Akaros said nothing for a long moment, during which he looked at nothing in particular — the middle distance, as he does when the case has closed and the question has become something else.

“Go,” he said to Philagros. “Yes. It is the right thing.”

When Philagros had gone, I asked him whether justice was well served in this case, meaning the question in the way one means such questions — not legally but morally, the question of whether what would follow was equal to what had been done.

“Sostias is dead,” Akaros said, without inflection. “That is not recoverable. The silver is recoverable. Whether the weight of the one against the other is a matter for the gods or for the prytaneis, I cannot say. What I can say is that I was asked to find where the silver went, and I have found it.”

He put on his cloak and walked to the door. The fog was gone and the harbour lay bright and indifferent below us, the water doing what water always does. He paused at the threshold and looked back at the inner room — the empty shelves, the folded wrappings, the small dark stain from the dropped lamp — in the way a man looks at a problem that has been answered, and he said:

“The honest difficulty is not finding the answer. It is living with how simple it always turns out to be.”

Then he went down to the harbour to send word to the Ionian partners about a delay in their silver, and I returned to my tablets, where I find — as he would perhaps approve — that the facts are the thing, and the man is only incidental to them.

This account is copied from the original wax by my hand in the year of my own stewardship, as faithfully as the condition of the tablets allows. Some words are lost where the wax cracked in the later storage. What I have given is what I have. — N., son of Epithetes.
