BAAL-YATON

~1510 BC — Knossos, Crete

THE AFFAIR OF THE SANCTUARY OF THE DOUBLE AXE

Helian writes this. I set it down because Baal-yaton will not. He says records are for grain and oil and the movement of copper ingots; that the other kind of record — the kind that catches a man whole, preserved in his moment the way a body is preserved in natron — serves no commercial purpose and is therefore beneath his attention.

I am a palace-trained clerk, an Eteocretan assigned to manage the administrative affairs of foreign merchants. Baal-yaton is a merchant of Tyre. He came to Knossos thirty years ago on a trading voyage and did not return to the Semitic world. He stayed because the Minoan palace culture held him in the way the extraordinary holds any man of intelligence. He learned our undeciphered Linear A script, mastered the palace economy, and found this entirely alien world navigable once he had learned to read it. He reads everything.

He said this regarding records while lying on the floor of our shared workroom with a lump of black obsidian balanced on his chest, staring at the ceiling and refusing to eat for the second consecutive day, waiting, as he always waited between cases, for the world to become interesting again. I know my obligation to history. I write this because someone should.

The smell of Knossos in high summer is specific and unforgettable: cedar oil burned in the lamp niches against the humidity, beeswax worked into the frescoed plaster to keep the colours from bleeding, the faint copper sweetness of ritual blood from the sanctuary levels above, and beneath all of it, always, the old wet-stone smell of the palace itself, which has been building and rebuilding for six centuries. I had been breathing it for twenty years. I no longer noticed it.

Baal-yaton noticed it on his first morning and has noticed it every morning since. He says a man who stops smelling his environment has stopped thinking about it, which is fatal. He says this while simultaneously maintaining that he has no interest in anything that cannot be expressed as a ratio.

He had been in one of his black periods for eleven days when Iphimedeia came.

I heard the gate-hinge — that particular hinge on the third west corridor, which has a dry, ascending shriek that no amount of oil entirely cures — and I heard, beneath it, the sound of a woman running. Palace women of rank do not run. They move in a deliberate, unhurried way, because they do not expect to be stopped. I had learned to hear the difference.

Baal-yaton did not open his eyes.

“She has been here before,” he said to the ceiling. “She knows the corridor to avoid the service passage. She is ranked, but she is terrified. She is not sending a subordinate, which means the matter carries a terminal consequence. She is running from an executioner.”

I opened the door.

Iphimedeia, Second Keeper of the Sanctuary of the Double Axe, stood in the corridor with her hair disarranged and her eyes carrying the particular, hollow brightness of the condemned. She smelled of smoke and something older. Something like fear, but ceremonial.

“Come in,” said Baal-yaton, still on the floor. “And bar the door behind you, Helian. What I am about to be told is apparently not for the corridor.”

Iphimedeia was perhaps forty, a woman who had survived three changes of the palace hierarchy and the lethal internal politics the sanctuary conducted behind its painted screens. She sat on the stool I provided. Baal-yaton sat up from the floor, set the obsidian aside, and looked at her with the expression I have come to think of as his working face: not cold exactly, but depopulated, as though everything in him that is not an instrument of observation has temporarily gone elsewhere.

“The Axe of the Founding is gone,” she said. Her hands were shaking. “And Lord Klytarch knows.”

Baal-yaton waited.

“It is the original axe. The one from which all the sanctuary’s ritual authority derives. Kept in the inner shrine, seen by no one below the rank of First Keeper except at the Renewal Festival. This morning, Klytarch, the High Administrator of the East Wing, sealed the upper precincts with his spearmen. He has declared a Sundown Audit of Sanctity. If I cannot produce the Axe for him before the sun touches the western hills…”

“Sacrilege,” I said, the word cold in my mouth. “They will cast you into the deep vaults.”

“Yes,” she whispered. “I will be starved in the dark, and Klytarch will seize the sanctuary.”

“You came to me rather than fleeing,” Baal-yaton noted. “Why?”

“Because I do not believe it was stolen. I believe it was never there to be stolen.” She reached into a fold of her gown and produced a small fragment of a wax tablet. “My predecessor, the First Keeper, died four years ago. This was in her private effects. I did not understand it then.”

Baal-yaton took the fragment. It was old, the wax cracked. Four symbols were incised on it. I recognised them: *sanctuary, reconciliation, seal, renovation.*

“The Great Renovation,” he said, his voice suddenly alive with a predatory energy. “Three years ago, after the shaking. The sanctuary level was closed for two seasons for structural reinforcement.” He was on his feet in an instant, the way a fire is suddenly burning. “She feared this day. She left you a map, Iphimedeia. A map pointing to the moment the lie was last secured.”

He threw his linen travelling cloak over his shoulders. “We are going to the renovation archive in the West Magazine. We have three hours until sundown. Helian, bring the wax board.”

I brought it. I always bring it.

The palace of Knossos is not a building; it is an accretion of six centuries of architecture layered on itself. Baal-yaton moved through it without apparent uncertainty, reading the wear patterns on the floors and the oxidation rates on the bronze lamp brackets, navigating the palace as its records indicated it should be.

We descended into the lower levels of the West Magazine. The air grew cold. Ahead, blocking the entrance to the archival sequence, stood two of Klytarch’s palace guards, their bronze-tipped spears crossed before the doorway.

“Halt,” the larger guard commanded. “By order of the High Administrator, the archives are sealed until the Sundown Audit is complete.”

I stepped forward, my administrative cylinder seal held ready. “By the authority of the West Corridor section—”

“Your authority ends at my spear-point, clerk,” the man sneered.

Baal-yaton’s hand rested lightly on my arm, stopping me. He looked at the guards, not as men, but as a problem in mechanics.

“The grip on your shield is non-standard,” he said to the larger guard, his voice calm and conversational. “The thong is laced for a left-handed man, but your spear is braced for a right-handed thrust. An interesting accommodation. Does it affect your balance in a narrow corridor?”

The guard’s eyes flickered down to his own shield arm, a flicker of pure instinct and professional pride. It was a single moment of distraction, but it was enough.

“Now, Helian,” Baal-yaton said.

I lunged, not at the man, but at the space he had created. I drove my shoulder hard into the crossed spear-hafts, using my full weight as a lever. There was a grunt of surprise, a clatter of bronze on stone, and a path was clear for half a heartbeat. Baal-yaton moved like a shadow slipping off a wall, vanishing into the labyrinth of massive clay pithoi inside the archive.

The second guard recovered and swung the butt of his spear, catching me high on the ribs. The blow drove the air from my lungs and sent me stumbling against a pithos. The first guard, enraged at being tricked, lunged, and the point of his spear tore through my linen tunic, biting a hot line of pain into the flesh beneath my collarbone.

But it was too late. Baal-yaton emerged from the darkness, two clay tablets in his hands.

“I have what I require,” he said crisply. He glanced at my bleeding shoulder. “A necessary cost. Come.”

We did not wait for the guards to rally. We hurried up the stairs, leaving them to their post. “Read,” he commanded, thrusting the first tablet into my hands. It was a materials accounting from the renovation—items removed from the sanctuary for safekeeping.

The Double Axe of the Founding was not listed.

“And the second,” he said, as we reached the upper levels.

It was a return accounting. Again, the Axe was not listed.

We reached the upper precincts through a service stair. The sanctuary was dim and close. Iphimedeia was waiting, pacing before the painted wooden screen of the inner shrine. The light from the skylight was turning the deep gold of late afternoon. She unlocked the screen with a trembling hand.

Inside, the cavity in the stone ledge was empty.

Baal-yaton crouched and held his lamp close. I leaned in, ignoring the throbbing in my shoulder. The plaster was smooth. The fine dust that had settled on it was of a uniform density. As a clerk who has unsealed hundred-year-old document jars, I know the look of undisturbed time. This was it.

“The plaster mix is consistent with the renovation-era materials,” I murmured. “Sealed three years ago and never opened.”

“It was never stolen,” Iphimedeia breathed.

“It has not existed for generations,” Baal-yaton corrected. “The ritual became the object.”

Heavy, rhythmic footsteps echoed in the outer court. The ring of bronze on stone.

“The sun has touched the hills,” a voice boomed. Lord Klytarch stepped into the sanctuary, flanked by four spearmen. He was a massive man, draped in the heavy gold chains of his office. “The Audit is called, Iphimedeia. Step aside. Show me the Founding Axe.”

Iphimedeia froze, the blood draining from her face.

Baal-yaton stepped out of the inner shrine, holding the two clay tablets.

“The Keeper will not show you the Axe, Lord Klytarch,” he said, his voice slicing through the heavy air. “Because the inner shrine is sealed, and its sanctity is absolute.”

Klytarch laughed, a thick, brutal sound. “A Canaanite trader speaks for the gods? You have found nothing but old inventory lists. Tablets can be misfiled, or forged. Your word against a High Administrator of Knossos? I will look upon the empty shelf myself.”

He gestured to his men. “Arrest them.”

“You are right,” Baal-yaton said softly, holding up the second tablet. “Tablets can be forged. But a High Administrator’s personal seal is unique. Irrevocable.” He pointed to a small, circular impression in the clay at the bottom of the tablet. “Your seal, Lord Klytarch. Placed here three years ago, certifying that all sacred objects were accounted for and returned to the sanctuary.”

Silence fell over the sanctuary, so absolute I could hear the oil hissing in the lamps.

“Your seal is on a document that proves the shrine was empty,” Baal-yaton pressed, his voice a lethal whisper. “If you declare the Axe missing now, you declare your own sacrilege. You certified a fiction. You failed to protect the most sacred object in Knossos. The deep vaults are large enough for two, my lord.”

Klytarch stared at the seal on the tablet, a perfect impression of his own authority, now turned into the instrument of his ruin. The muscles in his jaw worked furiously. He looked at the empty inner shrine, then at the depopulated, unyielding face of the foreign merchant who had cornered him in his own ambition.

“The... the administrative records are clear,” Klytarch choked out, his face pale and shining with sweat. He took a step back. “My seal is absolute. The Axe is safe within its shrine. The Audit is concluded.”

He turned and marched out of the sanctuary, his spearmen trailing in confused silence.

Iphimedeia collapsed onto a stone bench, a long, shuddering sigh escaping her. She was safe, but the foundation of her world was gone. “What happens now?” she whispered. “It is all built on nothing.”

“It is built on tradition,” Baal-yaton corrected gently. “The fiction has been doing important work for two hundred years. I believe in whatever keeps this palace stable, because a stable Knossos is a reliable trading partner. A sanctioned Keeper has the power to commission a new ritual object, to consecrate it, and to install it as the legitimate continuation of the tradition.”

He looked at her steadily. “The Founding Axe exists as an idea. Ideas can be materially re-expressed. That is precisely how traditions survive.”

He turned and left. He does not say goodbye; he simply stops being in the room.

I walked back to the workroom with him. When we were inside, the door barred, he turned to me. “Let me see that.”

Before I could protest, he had pushed aside the torn fabric of my tunic. He examined the bloody line on my shoulder with an impersonal, focused intensity.

“A shallow cut,” he pronounced. “The spear-tip is triangular; it cuts cleanly. It will not fester if it is cleaned.” He took a flask of harsh Canaanite wine from his shelf and a strip of clean linen, and began to wash the wound with an efficiency that was entirely without comfort, yet was, in its own way, a form of care.

“You already knew,” I said, wincing as the wine stung the cut. “When you saw the wax fragment. You knew about the renovation archive. You had read those tablets before.”

“I knew there was an absence in the record, and that Klytarch’s seal was upon it,” he replied, his eyes on his work. “It was a notation in my memory, waiting for a context that would give it meaning. Iphimedeia provided the context. The rest was merely mechanics.” He tied the bandage with a neat, functional knot.

He went to the floor and retrieved the block of black obsidian, but he did not lie down. He stood with it in his hand, weighing it.

“You will write it down,” he said.

“I have already begun.”

“Good,” he said. “In thirty years, when you are dead and I am dead, someone may be glad that someone wrote it down. And if not—” He settled the obsidian more precisely on his sternum and finally lay down. “—the palace has survived six hundred years of records that no one will ever read. One more will make little difference.”

I finished the tablet. I sealed it under my own seal, not the palace seal, and placed it in the cedar box where I keep the records that are not for the palace sequence. This is that record.

— Helian, Second Clerk, West Corridor Administrative Section
In the year of the Third Harvest Following the Great Renovation At Knossos.
May the Keeper of the Sanctuary commission a beautiful axe.
May it serve as well as the one it replaces.
May no one look too closely at the dust.
