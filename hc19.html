BAAL-YATON

~1510 BC — Knossos, Crete

Baal has given — the great god of Tyre, patron of the city and of its sailors, whose name this man bore as a declaration of origins and whose gift was the western sea. From Cyprus the trade routes ran west and north into the Aegean, toward the great island whose fleet had dominated the eastern Mediterranean for centuries — Minoan Crete, the Keftiu of the Egyptian records, whose palace at Knossos was the most sophisticated administrative complex in the pre-classical world. Baal-yaton joined a trading voyage west and did not return.

He stayed on Crete because Crete held him in the way that the extraordinary always holds the man of intelligence who encounters it for the first time. The Minoan palace culture was unlike anything in the Semitic world from which he came — its art, its religious practices, its administrative system with its undeciphered Linear A script, its women in positions of priestly and possibly political authority, its absence of the defensive walls that every other palace culture considered essential. He settled at Knossos as a merchant and factor, learned enough of the Minoan tongue to function in the palace economy, and spent thirty years in the most culturally distant world any member of the family had yet inhabited. He found it entirely navigable, once he had learned to read it — which was, of course, the family’s particular competence, and which had never yet failed to find application in any world it encountered.

The surviving record of his work at Knossos is preserved in a set of tablets maintained by his scribe, Eteocretan Helian, a palace-trained clerk assigned to foreign merchants permitted to operate within the palace economy. Helian’s writing shows a tension between Minoan administrative convention and Canaanite commercial structure; he writes in the Minoan system, but the structure of his entries reflects the influence of Baal-yaton’s methods. What follows is reconstructed from those tablets, some of which were later copied into a secondary archive after the destruction of the palace, and whose ordering has been partially restored through comparison with other administrative sequences.

The Affair Of The Sanctuary Of The Double Axe

Helian writes this. I set it down because Baal-yaton will not. He says records are for grain and oil and the movement of copper ingots; that the other kind of record — the kind that catches a man whole, preserved in his moment the way a body is preserved in natron — serves no commercial purpose and is therefore beneath his attention. He said this while lying on the floor of our shared workroom with a lump of black obsidian balanced on his chest, staring at the ceiling and refusing to eat for the second consecutive day, waiting, as he always waited between cases, for the world to become interesting again.

I am a palace-trained clerk. I know my obligation. I write this because someone should.

The smell of Knossos in high summer is specific and unforgettable: cedar oil burned in the lamp niches against the humidity, beeswax worked into the frescoed plaster to keep the colours from bleeding, the faint copper sweetness of ritual blood from the sanctuary levels above, and beneath all of it, always, the old wet-stone smell of the palace itself, which has been building and rebuilding for six hundred years and carries every season of itself in its walls. I had been breathing it for twenty years. I no longer noticed it.

Baal-yaton noticed it on his first morning and has noticed it every morning since. He says a man who stops smelling his environment has stopped thinking about it, which is fatal. He says this while simultaneously maintaining that he has no interest in anything that cannot be expressed as a ratio.

He had been in one of his black periods for eleven days when Iphimedeia came.

I heard the gate-hinge — that particular hinge on the third west corridor, which has a dry, ascending shriek that no amount of oil entirely cures, because the bronze pin is slightly ovoid from a casting imperfection and catches at the top of every swing — and I heard, beneath it, the sound of a woman walking in the deliberate, unhurried way of someone who does not expect to be stopped. Palace women of rank move differently from palace women of function. I had learned to hear the difference.

Baal-yaton did not open his eyes.

“She has been here before,” he said to the ceiling. “She knows the corridor. She is not staff or she would have come through the service passage. She is ranked but she is not sending a subordinate, which means the matter is one she cannot commit to an intermediate without defining its edges. She is afraid of its edges.”

The obsidian rose and fell on his chest as he breathed.

“Also,” he added, “she has been running.”

I had not heard running. I opened the door.

Iphimedeia, Second Keeper of the Sanctuary of the Double Axe, stood in the corridor with her hair disarranged and her eyes carrying the particular brightness of someone who has not slept and is deciding, in this very moment, whether to trust a foreign merchant with the most dangerous secret she has ever held.

She smelled of smoke and something older. Something like fear, but ceremonial.

“Come in,” said Baal-yaton, still on the floor. “And close the door behind you, Helian. What I am about to be told is apparently not for the corridor.”

Iphimedeia was perhaps forty, which in a Keeper of the Sanctuary meant she had survived three changes of the palace hierarchy, two reconstructions of the ritual calendar, and whatever internal politics the sanctuary conducted behind its painted screens. She had the hands of someone who worked — lamp-trimming, offering-preparation, the physical maintenance of sacred space — and the eyes of someone who had learned, very early, that what she saw and what she was permitted to say about what she saw were two entirely different registers.

She sat on the stool I provided. Baal-yaton sat up from the floor, set the obsidian aside, and looked at her with the expression I have come to think of as his working face: not cold exactly, but depopulated, as though everything in him that is not an instrument of observation has temporarily gone elsewhere.

“The Axe of the Founding is gone,” she said.

Baal-yaton waited.

“It is the — it is the original axe. The first axe. The one from which all the sanctuary’s ritual authority derives. Kept in the inner shrine, wrapped in linen renewed at each new moon, seen by no one below the rank of First Keeper except at the Renewal Festival.” She stopped. Started again. “It was there at the new moon sealing. It is not there now.”

“When was it discovered missing?”

“This morning. By myself. I came to prepare the lamp oil for the dawn rite.”

“Who has been informed?”

Something moved across her face. “No one. I came here first.”

Baal-yaton turned to me. I was already writing. He has trained me to begin writing before he begins asking; he says the record of a conversation is more valuable than the conversation itself because the record cannot be revised by the experience of knowing how it ends. I write in Minoan administrative script, which is not designed for dialogue, which is Baal-yaton’s problem and not mine.

“Why here first?” he said.

“Because if I report it to the Administrator, the sanctuary will be searched. Everyone within it questioned. The suppliers, the junior keepers, the musicians, the olive-oil factor who delivered last week — everyone who has come near the outer precincts. And the Administrator’s investigators will find nothing, because —” She stopped.

“Because?” said Baal-yaton.

The lamp between them guttered in a draft from somewhere in the palace’s deep interior, and for a moment her face went half-dark, and I thought: she is about to tell us something she has never told anyone, and the act of telling it will change the shape of the world she lives in, and she knows this, and she is doing it anyway.

“Because I am not certain it was ever there,” she said.

Baal-yaton stood. When he moves from stillness to motion, there is no intermediate state; he is simply suddenly standing, the way a fire is suddenly burning. He went to the corner of the room where the cedar shelves hold what I have come to think of as his working library — not books, because we do not have books, but tablets, and wax records, and several dozen inscribed clay balls whose sealing system I have never entirely decoded, and three copper plates covered in his own notation system, which is not Linear A and is not the Canaanite alphabet and appears to be something he invented for purposes of pure compression.

He found what he wanted. A tablet. He held it at an angle to the lamp.

“The palace renovations,” he said. “Three years ago. The sanctuary level was closed for two seasons. Structural reinforcement after the shaking — the third shaking in a generation, which means the palace administrators were, reasonably, concerned about lateral load in the upper courses. The renovation archive would be in the West Magazine sequence.”

I looked at him. “How do you know about the renovation archive?”

“Because I had a dispute about an olive oil consignment eighteen months ago and the resolution required me to trace a delivery record back through three archival sequences. I ended up in the renovation records by accident and read them because I was there and they were in front of me and it seemed wasteful not to.” He set the tablet down. “The sanctuary was sealed for two seasons. The inner shrine was inaccessible. Who held the documentation of that inaccessibility?”

Iphimedeia’s face was very still. “The Administrator’s office. And the First Keeper at the time.”

“Who is now?”

“Dead. Four years ago. She had no deputy. I was appointed from outside the sanctuary hierarchy when she died. There were — there were very few who remembered the old protocols.” She pressed her palms together in her lap. “When I took the position, the documentation I received was — the Administrative Coordinator called it a complete inventory. I accepted it as one.”

“But you did not conduct an independent verification.”

“The inner shrine is not something you — you don’t open it and look in to check. The sanctity of the space—”

“Is the reason no one has looked,” Baal-yaton said, not unkindly. “Yes. I understand.”

He picked up his travelling cloak, which in Knossos means a linen wrap of Minoan cut, which he wears with the resigned air of a man who has accepted that the world is not organized for his comfort. “We are going to the renovation archive. Then we are going to the sanctuary. Then I am going to tell you something you already know, and we will decide together what to do about it.”

Iphimedeia stood. “You need a sanctuary pass for the upper levels. I can arrange—”

“I have one,” said Baal-yaton. “I have had one for six years. I received it in exchange for locating a discrepancy in the shrine-oil delivery records that no one else had been able to find. The Administrator believed it was theft. It was arithmetic.” He paused at the door. “Helian. Bring the wax board.”

I brought it. I always bring it.

Here I should explain something about the palace, because if you have not been in it, you cannot understand what it costs a mind to navigate it. The palace of Knossos is not a building. It is an accretion — six centuries of addition, reconstruction, and renovation layered on themselves like geological strata, so that to move through it is to move simultaneously through space and through time. A corridor that begins in the new western section ends, without warning, in a storage magazine that is older than living memory. Stairwells drop into levels that are not on any current plan. The administrative system that manages it — the Linear A records, the clay balls, the sealed tablets — reflects this palimpsest exactly: it is a system built to track an organism that is always changing faster than the record of it.

Baal-yaton moved through it as he moved through everything — without apparent uncertainty, as though the palace had agreed in advance to arrange itself for his convenience. I have watched people who do not know the palace try to navigate it. They stop. They retrace. They ask for guides. Baal-yaton, who has been here thirty years, simply reads it — reads the wear patterns on the floors, the oxidation rates on the bronze lamp brackets, the administrative stamps on the magazine seals — and navigates not the palace as it currently is but the palace as its records indicate it should be, which is usually close enough.

The renovation archive was in the West Magazine lower level, behind a sequence of pithoi whose contents were no longer current but whose displacement would require a formal request. Baal-yaton simply stepped around them with the ease of a man who has done it before, which, I realized, he had.

He found the sanctuary renovation tablets in perhaps twelve minutes. He read them in perhaps four. I watched his face, which was doing its depopulated thing, which means the part of him that processes is running and the part that presents results is offline.

Then he found something. I saw it in the slight change in his breathing before I saw anything else.

“Helian,” he said. “Read this entry.”

I read it. It was a materials accounting from the second season of the renovation — a record of items removed from the sanctuary level for safekeeping during structural work, listed by type and weight and storage location.

The Double Axe of the Founding was not listed.

“Now read this one,” he said, and handed me a second tablet from two entries later.

This one was a return accounting — items restored to the sanctuary on completion of the renovation, listed in the same format.

The Double Axe of the Founding was not listed.

An object that was not removed for safekeeping, and therefore did not need to be returned. An object that existed, in the administrative record, on neither side of a two-season interval during which the space it supposedly occupied was inaccessible to everyone with the authority to notice its absence.

“When was the First Keeper’s last independent inventory of the inner shrine?” Baal-yaton asked Iphimedeia.

She had gone very pale under her oil-smooth complexion. “I don’t — the records don’t show an independent inventory. The shrine is inventoried at the Renewal Festival, but the inventory is conducted by the First Keeper, and the First Keeper’s record is the inventory. There is no secondary verification.”

“There was not,” Baal-yaton agreed. “Because a secondary verification would require a second person with sanctuary access to the inner shrine, and such people are rare, and the creation of such a person is a political event with implications for the authority of the First Keeper.” He set the tablet down with the precise, contained movement of a man who is being very careful not to show how interested he is. “Let us go to the sanctuary.”

The sanctuary occupies the upper south wing, which catches the afternoon light but not the morning light, so that in the early hours it is dim and close, lit by oil lamps in wall niches and by the small shaft of skylight above the central court. The frescoes are the oldest in the current palace — the women in their flounced skirts leaping the bull, the processional figures with their offering vessels, the great double axes painted on the columns in red and black — and they have the slightly unfocused quality of images that have been restored so many times that the original is somewhere beneath all the restoration, preserved by being buried.

The inner shrine was behind a painted wooden screen. Iphimedeia opened it with a key worn on a cord at her wrist, and the smell that came out was: ancient linen, cedar, lamp-oil gone slightly rancid, and the mineral smell of old stone that has been enclosed.

There was a low offering table of polished limestone. On it: two smaller double axes in bronze, mounted on short poles, which were current ritual objects and unquestionably present. Lamp holders. A libation vessel. Against the back wall, a stone ledge, and on the ledge, a cavity in the stone approximately the width of a man’s forearm, lined in the kind of fine plaster that is used on the most sacred surfaces.

Empty. But not recently emptied.

Baal-yaton crouched before the cavity and held his lamp close. I came to look. The plaster was smooth and very clean. Too clean — not cleaned recently, but untouched. The fine dust that accumulates even in sealed spaces had settled on the plaster at a density consistent with years of undisturbed accumulation.

“What is the last documented record of the Founding Axe being seen?” he asked.

Iphimedeia stood very still in the doorway of the inner shrine, and I thought that whatever she had half-known before this morning, she was completing the knowing now, and it was not a comfortable process. “The Renewal Festival. But — the Renewal Festival documentation is a ceremonial account, not an administrative one. The First Keeper would describe the unwrapping, the inspection, the re-wrapping. There are no measurements. No weight records. No secondary witnesses.”

“Because the ceremony itself provides all the authentication required,” Baal-yaton said. “The First Keeper says the axe is there. The ceremony proceeds. The axe’s presence is confirmed by the fact of the ceremony’s completion.”

“Yes.”

“And the First Keeper who died four years ago conducted the last four Renewal Festivals.”

“Yes.”

He stood. “The dust in this cavity has not been disturbed in longer than four years. Possibly longer than eight. Possibly longer than twelve. I cannot date dust with the precision I would like, but I can say with certainty that nothing has been removed from this space within any timeframe that is administratively relevant to your current crisis.” He brushed the lamp-shadow across the plaster one more time, as though checking his work. “The Founding Axe was not stolen this morning. The Founding Axe was not stolen at any point before this morning. The Founding Axe was not here to be stolen.”

The lamp in the outer shrine flickered in the draft from the open screen.

“It never existed,” Iphimedeia said. She said it very quietly, in the tone of someone who has known this for some time and is only now permitted to say it aloud.

“Or it existed once and was destroyed or lost in circumstances sufficiently embarrassing or sufficiently threatening to sanctuary authority that the record of its existence was maintained while the object itself was not,” Baal-yaton said. “The distinction matters less than the mechanism. Someone — not you, not your predecessor, not anyone alive — decided that the authority of the sanctuary derived from the axe, and that the axe could be replaced with the record of the axe without any practical diminishment of that authority. They were correct. For however many generations, they were correct. The ritual is the authentication. The ceremony is the object.”

He stepped back from the inner shrine and waited for Iphimedeia to lock the screen, which she did with the same practiced key-turn she had used to open it.

“The renovation,” she said. “Someone in the Administrator’s office would have known. When the sanctuary was cleared—”

“They would have found nothing to clear. And they would have recorded nothing, because to record nothing would be to create the record of an absence, and someone in the Administrator’s office — someone who understood exactly what the sanctuary’s authority meant to the palace’s stability — would have understood that the record of an absence was more dangerous than the absence itself.” He paused. “The renovation records simply do not mention the Founding Axe. This is not an oversight. It is a professional courtesy extended across several generations to a fiction that was doing important work.”

We sat in Iphimedeia’s outer office, which smells of beeswax and the particular aromatic herb the Minoans use to scent the oil for lamp-trimming. Baal-yaton had taken a cup of water, which is the closest he comes to refreshment when he is working; I had taken a tablet and was rendering the morning’s discoveries into as neutral an administrative form as I could manage, which is what I do when I am deciding how I feel about something.

“I want to understand how you knew,” Iphimedeia said. She was composed now — the composure of someone who has been holding a weight for a long time and has finally set it down, and the absence of the weight feels strange and not entirely like relief.

Baal-yaton settled back. When he is about to explain, there is a particular quality to his stillness — not the ennui-stillness, but a focused, pleasurable stillness, the stillness of a man who has worked very hard to arrive at a place and is taking a moment to acknowledge the arrival.

“You came to me,” he said, “instead of to the Administrator. This told me you suspected the disappearance was more complex than a theft. A theft is the Administrator’s problem; a complexity is mine. You are not a woman who confuses the two.” He held up one finger in the Canaanite counting gesture, which he does when he’s listing, and which always looks slightly foreign in this palace. “You told me you were uncertain whether the axe had ever been there. This was extraordinary precision. You did not say you thought it might have been moved, or misfiled, or substituted. You used the word ‘ever.’ This suggests you had already, privately, conducted some portion of the reasoning I then conducted formally.”

Iphimedeia looked at her hands. “I found a reference, in the old Keeper’s personal records, to a ceremony conducted during the renovation period. A ‘reconciliation of the inventory.’ I did not know what it meant. I did not ask.”

“Because asking would have required defining the question. Yes.” He put up a second finger. “The dust in the cavity. The plaster of an inner shrine that is opened ceremonially at each Renewal Festival should show the disturbance pattern of those openings — the slight compression and re-settling that comes from air movement and the proximity of lamps. It did not. It showed the undisturbed accumulation of a sealed space. This alone was sufficient, but not necessary — the renovation records confirmed the matter independently.” A third finger. “The administrative design of the verification system itself. Any object whose existence is authenticated only by the ceremony that displays it, and whose display is witnessed only by the person responsible for its custody, is an object that exists as a category of record rather than as a physical fact. This is not unusual in administrative systems that are very old. Categories of record develop a persistence of their own, independent of whatever physical fact originally generated them. The palace knows this. I know this. Anyone who has spent thirty years reading Linear A tablets knows this.”

He put his hand down.

“The more interesting question,” he said, “is what happens now.”

I want to record this part carefully, because it is the part that I find most — I will say “instructive,” since I cannot find a word that means “both troubling and right.”

Baal-yaton stood and walked to the small window of Iphimedeia’s office that looks out over the west court, where in the early afternoon the bull-leapers practice on wooden frames and the palace children watch them from the pillared gallery. The light in that court is extraordinary — the particular gold of Cretan afternoon, filtered through the dust of the dry season, which makes everything in it look slightly consecrated.

“The sanctuary’s authority rests on the Founding Axe,” he said. “The Founding Axe does not exist. If this becomes publicly known, what happens to the sanctuary’s authority?”

“It falls,” Iphimedeia said. “Or at minimum it must be entirely reconstituted, which takes a generation and requires the palace administration’s active support, which is not — which is not certain.” She paused. “The sanctuary manages the ritual calendar. The agricultural ceremonies, the sea-offering before the trading voyages, the consecration of new palace spaces. If the sanctuary’s authority is questioned—”

“The calendar is disrupted,” Baal-yaton said. “The farmers plant at the wrong time, or don’t plant, or plant in the wrong spirit. The traders don’t make the sea-offering and the sailors are frightened, and frightened sailors don’t sail, or sail badly. The palace spends a generation rebuilding what it had.” He turned from the window. “The fiction was doing important work. It has been doing important work for perhaps two hundred years. The question is whether the truth serves more than the fiction.”

“You’re a Canaanite merchant,” Iphimedeia said. “You don’t believe in the Founding Axe.”

“I believe in the agricultural calendar,” he said. “I believe in sailors making their sea-offerings before they sail. I believe in whatever keeps this palace stable, because a stable Knossos is a reliable trading partner, and a reliable trading partner is more valuable to me than any quantity of abstract truth.” He said this with absolute directness, which is the Canaanite way, and which the Minoans always find slightly startling. “I also believe in the woman sitting in front of me, who has spent four years administering a sanctuary whose central object she had private reason to doubt, and who has conducted herself with sufficient integrity to come to me this morning rather than simply close the inner shrine and continue as before.”

Iphimedeia was quiet for a moment. Then: “What would you recommend?”

“Nothing was stolen,” he said. “Nothing was ever there to steal. There is no crime. There is a question of administrative documentation, which I will leave entirely to you, except to note that a sanctioned Keeper, properly documented and with full administrative authority, has the power to commission a new ritual object, to consecrate it according to the proper forms, and to install it as the legitimate continuation of whatever tradition it continues.” He picked up his cloak. “The Founding Axe exists as a tradition. Traditions can be materially re-expressed. This is, in fact, precisely what traditions do, across centuries, and how they survive.”

He looked at her steadily. “The Administrator will hear from me that I was consulted on a documentation question, found no irregularity requiring official action, and consider the matter concluded. Helian’s record of this morning will be maintained in my private archive, not in the palace sequence.” He nodded toward me. “He is, as I have told him many times, an exceptionally thorough scribe. I have also told him that thoroughness requires judgment about which archive receives which record.”

He left. He does not say goodbye; he simply stops being in the room.

I walked back to the workroom with him through the corridor with the bronze-pin hinge, which cried out on the upswing as always, and I set the wax board down, and I looked at the collection of copper plates and clay records that cover the walls of Baal-yaton’s half of the room, and I thought about what I had just watched him do.

He was already on the floor. The obsidian was back on his chest.

“You have a question,” he said.

“The renovation record,” I said. “You knew about the renovation archive from the olive-oil dispute. You had been in it before.”

“Yes.”

“But you had read the sanctuary tablets then. When you were in the archive for the oil dispute. You had read them because they were there and you read everything that is in front of you.”

He did not answer.

“You already knew,” I said.

The obsidian rose and fell.

“I knew there was an absence in the record,” he said, finally. “I did not know what the absence meant, because I did not then have sufficient information about the internal accounting of the sanctuary to interpret it. It was a notation in my memory — a gap in an otherwise complete administrative sequence — waiting for a context that would give it meaning.” He paused. “This morning provided the context.”

“You could have brought this to the Administrator’s attention eighteen months ago.”

“I could have. I did not, because I had no framework for it and because the palace is full of gaps in the administrative record and not all of them represent crises. Some of them represent decisions made by clever people in previous generations that deserve the respect of being left alone.” Another pause. “And because it was not, at that time, anyone’s problem.”

“But now Iphimedeia has made it her problem.”

“Iphimedeia,” he said, “made it her problem the day she accepted the position and did not look in the inner shrine. She has been living with the problem for four years. I have simply helped her find its true dimensions, which are rather smaller than she feared.”

The afternoon light moved across the plaster walls, which in the palace’s west section are painted with a running spiral pattern in blue and ochre, very old, in the style before the current palace. In the lamp-niche above his head, the cedar oil burned.

“You will write it down,” he said. It was not a question.

“I have already written it down.”

“Good,” he said, and closed his eyes. “In thirty years, when you are dead and I am dead and Iphimedeia is dead, someone may be glad that someone wrote it down. And if not—” He settled the obsidian more precisely on his sternum. “— the palace has survived six hundred years of records that no one will ever read. One more will make little difference.”

I finished the tablet. I sealed it under my own seal, not the palace seal, and placed it in the cedar box where I keep the records that are not for the palace sequence.

This is that record.

— Helian, Second Clerk, West Corridor Administrative Section
In the year of the Third Harvest Following the Great Renovation At Knossos, in the palace of the Minos, in the island called Keftiu by the Egyptians.
May the Keeper of the Sanctuary commission a beautiful axe.
May it serve as well as the one it replaces.
May no one look too closely at the dust.
