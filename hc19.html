BAAL-YATON

~1510 BC — Knossos, Crete

THE AFFAIR OF THE SANCTUARY OF THE DOUBLE AXE

Helian writes this. I set it down because Baal-yaton will not. He says records are for grain and oil and the movement of copper ingots — that the other kind of record, the kind that catches a man whole, preserved in his moment the way a body is preserved in natron, serves no commercial purpose and is beneath his attention. He said this while lying on the floor of our shared workroom with a lump of black obsidian balanced on his chest, staring at the ceiling and refusing to eat for the second consecutive day, waiting, as he always waited between cases, for the world to become interesting again.

I am a palace-trained clerk. I know my obligation. I write this because someone should.

The smell of Knossos in high summer is specific and unforgettable: cedar oil burned in the lamp niches against the humidity, beeswax worked into the frescoed plaster to keep the colours from bleeding, the faint copper sweetness of ritual blood from the sanctuary levels above, and beneath all of it, always, the old wet-stone smell of the palace itself — a building that has been building and rebuilding for six hundred years, that carries every season of itself in its walls. I had been breathing it for twenty years and no longer noticed it.

Baal-yaton noticed it on his first morning and has noticed it every morning since. He came to Knossos from Cyprus, from a trading voyage that was supposed to turn west and return, and stayed because the island held him in the way that the extraordinary always holds the man of intelligence who encounters it for the first time. He was a Canaanite — born in the shadow of Tyre, in a world of harbour walls and cedar ships and the great god who gives wealth to those who move across water — and the palace culture of Knossos was unlike anything in that world. Its art. Its women in positions of priestly authority. Its palace with no defensive walls, which every other palace culture considered indispensable. He settled here as a merchant and factor, learned the tongue well enough to function in the palace economy, and had spent thirty years learning to read the island as he read everything: completely, without apparent effort, and in a manner that made the knowledge available at whatever moment it became necessary. He says a man who stops noticing his environment has stopped thinking about it, which is fatal. He says this while simultaneously maintaining that he has no interest in anything that cannot be expressed as a ratio.

He had been in one of his black periods for eleven days when Iphimedeia came.

I heard the gate-hinge — that particular hinge on the third west corridor, which has a dry, ascending shriek that no amount of oil entirely cures, because the bronze pin is slightly ovoid from a casting imperfection and catches at the top of every swing — and I heard, beneath it, the sound of a woman walking in the deliberate, unhurried way of someone who does not expect to be stopped. Palace women of rank move differently from palace women of function. I had learned to hear the difference.

Baal-yaton did not open his eyes.

"She has been here before," he said to the ceiling. "She knows the corridor. She is not staff, or she would have come through the service passage. She is ranked, but she is not sending a subordinate — the matter is one she cannot commit to an intermediate without defining its edges. She is afraid of its edges."

The obsidian rose and fell on his chest.

"Also," he added, "she has been running."

I had not heard running. I opened the door.

Iphimedeia, Second Keeper of the Sanctuary of the Double Axe, stood in the corridor with her hair disarranged and her eyes carrying the particular brightness of someone who has not slept and is deciding, in this very moment, whether to trust a foreign merchant with the most dangerous secret she has ever held.

She smelled of smoke. And something older. Something like fear, but ceremonial.

"Come in," said Baal-yaton, still on the floor. "And close the door behind you, Helian. What I am about to be told is apparently not for the corridor."

Iphimedeia was perhaps forty — which in a Keeper of the Sanctuary meant she had survived three changes of the palace hierarchy, two reconstructions of the ritual calendar, and whatever internal politics the sanctuary conducted behind its painted screens. She had the hands of someone who worked — lamp-trimming, offering-preparation, the physical maintenance of sacred space — and the eyes of someone who had learned, very early, that what she saw and what she was permitted to say about what she saw were two entirely different registers.

She sat on the stool I provided. Baal-yaton sat up from the floor, set the obsidian aside, and looked at her with the expression I have come to think of as his working face: not cold exactly, but depopulated — as though everything in him that is not an instrument of observation has temporarily gone elsewhere.

"The Axe of the Founding is gone," she said.

Baal-yaton waited.

"It is the original axe. The first axe. The one from which all the sanctuary's ritual authority derives. Kept in the inner shrine, wrapped in linen renewed at each new moon, seen by no one below the rank of First Keeper except at the Renewal Festival." She stopped. Started again. "It was there at the new moon sealing. It is not there now."

"When was it discovered missing?"

"This morning. By myself. I came to prepare the lamp oil for the dawn rite."

"Who has been informed?"

Something moved across her face. "No one. I came here first."

Baal-yaton turned to me. I was already writing. He has trained me to begin writing before he begins asking; he says the record of a conversation is more valuable than the conversation itself, because the record cannot be revised by the experience of knowing how it ends. I write in the palace administrative script, which is not designed for dialogue, which is Baal-yaton's problem and not mine.

"Why here first?" he said.

"Because if I report it to the Administrator, the sanctuary will be searched. Everyone within it questioned. The suppliers, the junior keepers, the musicians, the olive-oil factor who delivered last week — everyone who has come near the outer precincts. And the Administrator's investigators will find nothing, because—" She stopped.

"Because?" said Baal-yaton.

The lamp between them guttered in a draft from somewhere in the palace's deep interior, and for a moment her face went half-dark, and I thought: she is about to tell us something she has never told anyone, and the act of telling it will change the shape of the world she lives in, and she knows this, and she is doing it anyway.

"Because I am not certain it was ever there," she said.

Baal-yaton stood. When he moves from stillness to motion there is no intermediate state; he is simply suddenly standing, the way a fire is suddenly burning. He went to the corner of the room where the cedar shelves hold his working library — tablets, and wax records, and several dozen inscribed clay balls whose sealing system I have never entirely decoded, and three copper plates covered in a notation that is not the palace script and is not the Canaanite alphabet and appears to be something he invented for purposes of pure compression.

He found what he wanted. A tablet. He held it at an angle to the lamp.

"The palace renovations," he said. "Three years ago. The sanctuary level was closed for two seasons — structural reinforcement after the earth-shaking, the third in a generation, which gave the Administrator reasonable cause for concern. The renovation archive would be in the West Magazine sequence."

I looked at him. "How do you know about the renovation archive?"

"Because I had a dispute about an olive oil consignment eighteen months ago, and the resolution required me to trace a delivery record back through three archival sequences. I ended up in the renovation records by accident and read them because I was there and they were in front of me, and it seemed wasteful not to." He set the tablet down. "The sanctuary was sealed for two seasons. The inner shrine was inaccessible. Who held the documentation of that inaccessibility?"

Iphimedeia's face was very still. "The Administrator's office. And the First Keeper at the time."

"Who is now?"

"Dead. Four years ago. She had no deputy. I was appointed from outside the sanctuary hierarchy when she died. There were very few who remembered the old protocols." She pressed her palms together in her lap. "When I took the position, the documentation I received was — the Administrative Coordinator called it a complete inventory. I accepted it as one."

"But you did not conduct an independent verification."

"The inner shrine is not something you open and look in to check. The sanctity of the space—"

"Is the reason no one has looked," Baal-yaton said, not unkindly. "Yes. I understand."

He reached for his travelling wrap — the linen of Minoan cut that he wears with the resigned air of a man who has accepted that the world is not organized for his comfort — and paused. "Before we go: the inventory document you received when you took the position. Do you have it still?"

"It is in my office."

"Bring it." He was already at the door. "Helian — the wax board."

I brought it. I always bring it.

I took the inventory tablet from Iphimedeia in the corridor. I am a trained scribe. I cannot help examining a tablet when I hold one, any more than Baal-yaton can help reading whatever is set before him. This one stopped me in the doorway of our workroom.

"What is it?" said Baal-yaton.

I turned the tablet in the light. The clay was wrong. Not in the obvious way — the text was correct palace script, the format impeccable, the seal impression clean. But the clay itself was finer than the sanctuary archive sequence. I have handled several thousand palace tablets in twenty years, and the sanctuary archive uses a grog-heavy fabric — local clay with coarse temper, slightly porous — that holds the impression of a reed stylus differently from the finer, denser clay used in the Administrator's west corridor offices. This tablet was Administrator's clay. It was Administrator's hand.

"This inventory was not written in the sanctuary," I said. "The clay is from the west offices. And the hand — the hand is the Administrative Coordinator's. I have his tax tablets in our own archive. The stroke on the vessel-determinative is his."

Baal-yaton looked at the tablet for three seconds. Then he looked at me with an expression I had not seen before — not the depopulated working face, but something that was almost, in another man, satisfaction.

"Good," he said. Then, to Iphimedeia: "We are going to the renovation archive. Then we are going to the sanctuary. Then I am going to tell you something you already know, and we will decide together what to do about it."

Here I should explain something about the palace, because if you have not been in it, you cannot understand what it costs a mind to navigate it. The palace of Knossos is not a building. It is an accretion — six centuries of addition, reconstruction, and renovation layered on themselves like geological strata, so that to move through it is to move simultaneously through space and through time. A corridor that begins in the new western section ends, without warning, in a storage magazine that is older than living memory. Stairwells drop into levels that are not on any current plan. The administrative system that manages it reflects this palimpsest exactly: it is a record built to track an organism that changes faster than any record of it can follow.

Baal-yaton moved through it as he moved through everything — without apparent uncertainty, as though the palace had agreed in advance to arrange itself for his convenience. He reads it the way he reads everything: the wear patterns on the floors, the oxidation on the bronze lamp brackets, the administrative stamps on the magazine seals. He navigates not the palace as it currently is but the palace as its records indicate it should be, which is generally close enough.

The renovation archive was in the West Magazine lower level, behind a sequence of great storage jars whose contents were no longer current. Baal-yaton stepped around them with the ease of a man who had done it before — which, I realised, he had.

He found the sanctuary renovation tablets in twelve minutes and read them in four. I watched his face do its depopulated thing, which means the part of him that processes is running and the part that presents results is offline.

Then he found something. I saw it in the slight change in his breathing before I saw anything else.

"Helian. Read this entry."

It was a materials accounting from the second season of the renovation — a record of items removed from the sanctuary level for safekeeping during structural work, listed by type and weight and storage location.

The Double Axe of the Founding was not listed.

"Now read this one." He handed me a tablet from two entries later.

A return accounting. Items restored to the sanctuary on completion of the renovation, in the same format.

The Double Axe of the Founding was not listed.

An object that was not removed for safekeeping, and therefore did not need to be returned. An object that existed, in the administrative record, on neither side of a two-season interval during which the space it supposedly occupied was inaccessible to everyone with the authority to notice its absence.

"When was the First Keeper's last independent inventory of the inner shrine?" Baal-yaton asked Iphimedeia.

She had gone very pale. "The records don't show an independent inventory. The shrine is inventoried at the Renewal Festival, but the inventory is conducted by the First Keeper, and the First Keeper's record is the inventory. There is no secondary verification."

"There was not," Baal-yaton agreed. "Because a secondary verification would require a second person with sanctuary access to the inner shrine, and such people are rare, and the creation of such a person is a political event with implications for the authority of the First Keeper." He set the tablet down with the precise, contained movement of a man being very careful not to show how interested he is. "Let us go to the sanctuary."

The sanctuary occupies the upper south wing, which catches the afternoon light but not the morning light, so that in the early hours it is dim and close, lit by oil lamps in wall niches and by the small shaft of skylight above the central court. The frescoes are the oldest in the current palace — the women in their flounced skirts leaping the bull, the processional figures with their offering vessels, the great double axes painted on the columns in red and black — and they have the slightly unfocused quality of images that have been restored so many times that the original is somewhere beneath all the restoration, preserved by being buried.

The inner shrine was behind a painted wooden screen. Iphimedeia opened it with a key worn on a cord at her wrist, and the smell that came out was: ancient linen, cedar, lamp-oil gone slightly rancid, and the mineral smell of old stone that has been enclosed for a long time.

There was a low offering table of polished limestone. On it: two smaller double axes in bronze, mounted on short poles — current ritual objects, unquestionably present. Lamp holders. A libation vessel. Against the back wall, a stone ledge, and on the ledge, a cavity in the stone approximately the width of a man's forearm, lined in fine plaster used for the most sacred surfaces.

Empty. But not recently emptied.

Baal-yaton crouched before the cavity and held his lamp close. I came to look. The plaster was smooth and very clean — too clean; not cleaned recently, but untouched. The fine dust that accumulates even in sealed spaces had settled on the plaster at a density consistent with years of undisturbed accumulation.

We had been there perhaps three minutes when I heard it.

Sandals on stone. More than one pair. Moving without haste — the particular unhurried pace of men who do not believe they need to hurry, because the authority is behind them rather than ahead.

Iphimedeia heard it at the same moment. Her hand tightened on the lamp cord.

"The Renewal Festival is tonight," she said, very quietly. "I was to confirm the sanctuary ready this morning. When I did not send word—"

Baal-yaton did not look up from the cavity. "Who would have sent them?"

"The Administrative Coordinator. Kratos. He manages the Festival arrangements. He would have noticed my absence from the morning rite." She paused. "He has been managing the Festival arrangements for eight years. Since before I was appointed."

"Since before you were appointed," Baal-yaton repeated, with a particular flatness that I have come to recognise. It is not agreement. It is the sound of a piece fitting into a frame he has already constructed. He stood, brushing dust from his knees, and turned to face the screen just as it opened.

Kratos was perhaps fifty, with the build of a man who had been strong when young and had since reorganised that strength into stillness. He wore the Administrative Coordinator's collar — lapis and gold, impressive at a distance, oppressive at close quarters — and he carried himself with the absolute composure of someone who is very rarely in a room he does not control. Behind him, two palace guards filled the doorway. They had not drawn their short blades, but their hands were at the right height.

"Second Keeper." He did not look at Baal-yaton initially, which told me a great deal. "You did not confirm the sanctuary. I came to ensure no irregularity—"

"None," said Baal-yaton.

Now Kratos looked at him. The composure held perfectly, which was itself information; a man whose presence here was innocent would have been faintly surprised to find a Canaanite merchant in an inner shrine. Kratos was not surprised at all.

"The Canaanite factor," he said. "You are a considerable distance from the West Magazine."

"I have a sanctuary pass," said Baal-yaton. "I have had one for six years, issued for services to the Administrator's office in the matter of the shrine-oil accounting. You will find it in the records — in the west corridor archive, which uses a finer clay than the sanctuary sequence and a more consistent hand. Your hand, as it happens, Administrative Coordinator. I have admired it often."

A silence. Outside, through the shaft of skylight, the morning was brightening toward the particular gold of the Cretan noon, and the dust in the air of the sanctuary was moving in the draft from the open screen.

"Helian," said Baal-yaton, without looking away from Kratos, "describe the inventory tablet."

I described it. The clay composition. The hand. The stroke on the vessel-determinative. I described it as a scribe describes evidence — precisely, in the sequence in which I had observed it, without conclusion, letting the observation make its own argument. This is what Baal-yaton has taught me, and it is the one lesson I do not need to be taught twice.

Kratos listened. His composure remained intact. But there was something behind it — not fear exactly, but the quality of a man performing composure rather than possessing it.

"A scribe's opinion on clay composition is not an administrative finding," he said.

"No," Baal-yaton agreed pleasantly. "But it is the beginning of one. And the renovation tablets in the West Magazine, which I have this morning read in the presence of the Second Keeper and my own clerk — whose record I will note is already complete and sealed under his private seal — those tablets contain a rather significant administrative omission. The Founding Axe is not listed in the removal accounting. Nor in the restoration accounting. An object of the sanctuary's central ritual significance, and the renovation record is silent on it. Which suggests one of three possibilities." He held up the Canaanite counting gesture, one finger. "The object was too sacred to list — but palace administrators do not omit objects for reasons of sanctity; they omit them for reasons of convenience." A second finger. "The object was lost before the renovation and the omission conceals a prior loss — possible, but this would require the Administrator's office to have known of the loss and chosen silence, which implicates the office." He paused. A third finger. "The object was never there. The renovation revealed nothing because there was nothing to reveal, and the office — specifically the officer managing the sanctuary arrangements, who has had that responsibility for eight years, who managed the renovation documentation, who this morning dispatched guards to an inner shrine he had reason to believe was being examined — that officer has spent eight years maintaining a record whose value lies precisely in no one looking too carefully at what the record describes."

The guards had not moved. But they were not watching Iphimedeia anymore. They were watching Kratos.

"The Renewal Festival is tonight," Baal-yaton said. He was very still now — the focused stillness, the pleasurable stillness of a man who has arrived somewhere. "The Second Keeper is present in the sanctuary. The sanctuary is undisturbed. The question before the Administrative Coordinator is whether he wishes to file a report of irregularity — which will require the renovation records to be formally examined, which will require the inventory tablet to be formally examined, which will require a scribe's full testimony on clay composition and scribal hand — or whether he wishes to confirm that his attendance this morning was entirely routine, that the sanctuary is prepared for the Festival, and that there is nothing here requiring official action."

Another silence. Longer this time.

Then Kratos stepped back from the screen. He inclined his head — the Administrative Coordinator's formal inclination, the one that means: this conversation has not occurred, which is the palace's way of saying: you have won this, and we both know it.

"The sanctuary appears to be in order," he said, in the tone of a man dictating to a scribe who is not present. "I will confirm the Festival preparations are proceeding." He turned. The guards turned with him. The sound of their sandals retreated — measured, unhurried, the pace of men performing dignity.

Iphimedeia locked the screen. Her hands were steady. Her face was very still, which in a woman who had held this position for four years in a palace like this one meant she was feeling a great deal and had decided, with considerable competence, to feel it privately.

We sat in Iphimedeia's outer office afterward, which smells of beeswax and the particular herb the Minoans use to scent the lamp oil. Baal-yaton had taken a cup of water — the closest he comes to refreshment when working. I had taken a tablet and was rendering the morning into as neutral an administrative form as I could manage, which is what I do when I am deciding how I feel about something.

"I want to understand how you knew," Iphimedeia said. She was composed — the composure of someone who has been holding a weight for a long time and has finally set it down, and the absence of the weight feels strange and not entirely like relief.

Baal-yaton settled back. When he is about to explain, there is a particular quality to his stillness — not the ennui-stillness, but a focused, pleasurable stillness; the stillness of a man who has worked hard to arrive at a place and is taking a moment to acknowledge the arrival.

"You came to me," he said, "instead of to the Administrator. This told me you suspected the disappearance was more complex than a theft. A theft is the Administrator's problem; a complexity is mine. You are not a woman who confuses the two." He held up one finger. "You told me you were uncertain whether the axe had ever been there. This was extraordinary precision — not that it might have been moved, or substituted, but that it might never have existed. This suggests you had already, privately, conducted some portion of the reasoning I then conducted formally."

Iphimedeia looked at her hands. "I found a reference, in the old Keeper's personal records, to a ceremony conducted during the renovation period. A 'reconciliation of the inventory.' I did not know what it meant. I did not ask."

"Because asking would have required defining the question. Yes." A second finger. "The dust in the cavity. The plaster of an inner shrine that is opened ceremonially at each Renewal Festival should show the disturbance pattern of those openings — the slight compression and re-settling from air movement and the proximity of lamps. It did not. It showed the undisturbed accumulation of a sealed space. This alone was sufficient, but not necessary — the renovation records confirmed the matter independently." A third finger. "The administrative design of the verification system. Any object whose existence is authenticated only by the ceremony that displays it, and whose display is witnessed only by the person responsible for its custody, is an object that exists as a category of record rather than as a physical fact. This is not unusual in systems that are very old. Categories of record develop a persistence of their own, independent of whatever physical fact originally generated them."

He put his hand down.

"Kratos," Iphimedeia said.

"Kratos has known for eight years," Baal-yaton said. "Possibly longer. He managed the renovation. He found nothing to clear from the inner shrine. He recorded nothing, because the record of an absence is more dangerous than the absence itself — it would have required him to name what was missing, and naming it would have created an official question that demanded an official answer. Instead, he filed a correct and impeccable silence, which is a very sophisticated administrative act. And he has spent eight years in a position of singular knowledge — the only living person, so far as he knew, who understood that the sanctuary's authority rested on nothing. This is not, as it happens, a comfortable position. It makes a man—" He paused, choosing the word with care. "—watchful."

"He wanted to use it," Iphimedeia said. Not a question.

"He wanted the option of using it. A man who controls a secret of that magnitude has leverage over the sanctuary's authority, and through the sanctuary's authority, over the ritual calendar, the agricultural cycle, the sea-offerings. The palace administrator manages the physical palace. The person who can destabilise the sanctuary's authority at will manages the palace's relationship to everything outside the palace. This is not a modest form of power." Baal-yaton turned his cup of water in his hands. "You were appointed from outside the sanctuary hierarchy, without the mentorship of the previous First Keeper. Kratos will have understood that you had no one to tell you what to look for — and no one to tell you what was missing. You were, from his perspective, a Keeper who could be managed."

"Until I came to you."

"Until you came to me. At which point the question became whether I would find what I found before he could establish that there was a theft to investigate — which would have given him a crisis of his manufacture, controlled by him, with you in the position of the administrator who lost the most sacred object in the palace." He set the cup down. "He sent the guards this morning because he had reports that you had left the sanctuary without reporting the discovery. He suspected you had come to me, and he wished to arrive at the shrine before I had time to read the dust."

"He was eight minutes too late," I said.

"He was," Baal-yaton agreed. He looked at Iphimedeia steadily. "The question now is what happens tonight."

"Nothing was stolen," she said slowly. She was thinking aloud — the way she had thought aloud since she arrived, which told me that what she valued was not instruction but a mind capable of keeping pace with hers. "Nothing was ever there to steal. There is no crime."

"There is a question of administrative documentation, which I leave entirely to you. Except to note—" He paused, and there was something in the pause that was, in him, the closest thing to delicacy. "—that a sanctioned Keeper, properly documented, has the power to commission a new ritual object. To consecrate it according to the proper forms. To install it as the legitimate continuation of whatever tradition it continues."

A silence.

"The Founding Axe exists as a tradition," he said. "Traditions can be materially re-expressed. This is what traditions do, across centuries — this is precisely how they survive. The Administrator will hear from me that I was consulted on a documentation matter, found no irregularity requiring official action, and consider it concluded. Helian's record will be maintained in my private archive." He nodded toward me. "He is an exceptionally thorough scribe. I have also told him, many times, that thoroughness requires judgment about which archive receives which record."

He left. He does not say goodbye; he simply stops being in the room.

I walked back to the workroom with him through the corridor with the bronze-pin hinge, which cried out on the upswing as always, and I set the wax board down, and I looked at the copper plates and clay records that cover the walls of Baal-yaton's half of the room, and I thought about what I had watched him do.

He was already on the floor. The obsidian was back on his chest.

"You have a question," he said.

"The renovation record. You had been in the archive before, for the oil dispute. You read the sanctuary tablets then."

"Yes."

"You already knew."

The obsidian rose and fell.

"I knew there was an absence in the record," he said, finally. "I did not know what the absence meant, because I did not then have sufficient information about the sanctuary's internal accounting to interpret it. It was a notation in memory — a gap in an otherwise complete administrative sequence — waiting for a context that would give it meaning." A pause. "This morning provided the context."

"You could have brought this to the Administrator's attention eighteen months ago."

"I could have. I did not, because the palace is full of gaps in the record and not all of them represent crises. Some represent decisions made by clever people in previous generations that deserve the respect of being left alone." Another pause, shorter. "And because it was not, at that time, anyone's problem."

"But Kratos had already been using it — waiting to use it — for eight years."

"Yes. That I did not know. I knew an absence. I did not know an ambition." He settled the obsidian more precisely on his sternum. "If you are asking whether I should have acted sooner — the answer is that I had no framework for the action. A merchant who brings unformed suspicions to a palace administrator's attention is not a merchant for very long."

I considered this. "And Helian's contribution," I said, which I will admit was not an entirely disinterested observation. "The clay."

"Was the fulcrum," he said, without hesitation. "I had the renovation record and the dust. I did not have proof of current interference — evidence that a living person had actively constructed the scenario rather than merely inherited it. The inventory tablet was that proof. I would not have seen it. I do not handle enough tablets to know one clay from another by touch." He paused. "You have been more useful to me today than in any other case. I want this noted."

"I am noting it now," I said.

"Good," he said, and closed his eyes.

The afternoon light moved across the plaster walls — the running spiral pattern in blue and ochre, very old, in the style before the current palace — and the cedar oil burned in the lamp-niche above his head, and I finished the tablet.

That evening, when the Festival lamps were lit along the processional route and the smell of cedar and ritual smoke rose through every level of the palace, I walked out to the west court colonnade and watched the procession from above. Iphimedeia moved at the head of it in her formal robes, her hair dressed and her bearing perfectly composed. She carried a lamp in each hand. Behind her, in the hands of the junior keeper, was a wooden chest that had not been in the sanctuary this morning and was in it now — I had watched it arrive, an hour after we left, under the escort of a bronzesmith I recognized as one of Baal-yaton's commercial contacts.

The Renewal Festival proceeded without difficulty. It always does. The ritual is complete in itself. The ceremony is the authentication.

I went back to the workroom. Baal-yaton was on the floor. The obsidian was on his chest. His eyes were open, watching the ceiling with the expression that means the world has become interesting again — not the black period's vacancy, but the lit interior of a mind that has found its object and is now simply running, the way a well-made ship runs before a good wind, with no apparent effort and considerable speed.

"You sealed the tablet," he said.

"An hour ago."

"Under your seal."

"Under mine. Not the palace seal."

"Good." He turned the obsidian once in his fingers — a gesture I have come to recognize as the equivalent of closing a book. "In thirty years, when you are dead and I am dead and Iphimedeia is dead, someone may be glad that someone wrote it down. And if not—" He set the obsidian back on his sternum with the precision of a man completing a ritual of his own. "— the palace has survived six hundred years of records no one will ever read. One more will make little difference."

I sealed it. I placed it in the cedar box where I keep records that are not for the palace sequence.

This is that record.

— Helian, Second Clerk, West Corridor Administrative Section In the year of the Third Harvest Following the Great Renovation at Knossos, in the palace of the Minos, in the island the Egyptians call Keftiu. May the Keeper of the Sanctuary commission a beautiful axe. May it serve as well as the one it replaces. May no one look too closely at the dust.
