ÞORFINNR HOLMR

~960 AD — Yorkshire, England

A note on pronunciation:
The letter Þ/þ (thorn) is an Old English letter representing the unvoiced “th” as in “thing”.
So Þorfinnr is pronounced as THOR-fin-ur

Þorfinnr Holmr bore a fully Norse name in a Yorkshire that was becoming, generation by generation, less Norse — the Danelaw’s cultural distinctiveness fading under the pressure of English kingship, English law, and the English Church, which had been working steadily northward since the conversion of the Danish settlers had given it a foothold in the region. He was a man caught between identities and comfortable in neither, which may explain why he invested so heavily in the one identity that transcended both: the family’s ancient role as the man who knew things.

He served in the household of a Yorkshire thegn as a steward and counsellor, managing the accounts and correspondence of an estate that sat precisely on the cultural boundary between the English and Danish communities. He heard cases in both languages, applied law from both traditions, and developed in the process a scepticism about the claims of any single system of rules to represent the truth of a matter that would have been familiar to his ancestors in the Merovingian courts of three centuries earlier. The truth, in his experience, was not in the law; it was in the facts, which preceded the law and frequently survived it.

The record of his work is preserved in a different hand — that of Gunnarr, a younger man taken into service under Þorfinnr, who had been trained not only to write but to observe the man he served. Gunnarr’s notes are uneven in places, his judgments occasionally colored by loyalty, but his descriptions carry a clarity of witness that allows the events to be reconstructed with care. Where Gunnarr hesitates, it is not from ignorance but from the difficulty of placing what he saw into words. What follows is drawn from his account, preserved in fragments of parchment that show signs of having been recopied more than once, each iteration preserving the essential record while slightly compressing its detail.

A record set down by Gunnarr

Preserved in the memory of what was not spoken aloud, and of what was allowed to remain unspoken.

There is a particular quality to the autumn fog that settles in the vale of the Tees, different from any fog I have known elsewhere. It does not arrive — it accumulates, as debt accumulates, thickening across three or four days until the boundary between river and field and sky becomes a matter of faith rather than sight. It carries the smell of the river, which is the smell of cold iron and rotting reed, and it muffles sound in a way that does not merely quieten the world but seems to remove it, so that a man speaking at twenty paces might be speaking from the bottom of a well. I mention this because the affair of Sigurðr Kálfsson began in such a fog, and I have sometimes thought that the fog was the first and most honest witness to the whole business — it saw nothing, heard nothing, confirmed nothing, and in doing so described the truth of the matter more accurately than any court could have managed.

Þorfinnr Holmr noticed the merchant first at Martinmas. I did not see him notice. That is the difficulty of writing about Þorfinnr — his noticing precedes any observable event that would prompt a man to notice. By the time he had told me that something was wrong, he had already finished the interesting work, and what remained was merely the arrangement of confirmation.

Sigurðr Kálfsson was a man of perhaps fifty years, broad through the shoulder in the way of men who have spent a lifetime managing loads on ships and in warehouses. He had a face that encouraged trust — open, weathered, familiar with smiling — and a manner that adjusted itself with remarkable fluency to whatever company he found himself in. With Ulfkell’s English thegns he was English. With the Danish farmers along the eastern edge of the estate he was Danish. He spoke both tongues without accent, or rather with both accents simultaneously, in a way that made each listener feel they had met a man of their own country.

He traded in furs, in salt fish, in certain spiced goods that came through the port at Jorvik, and in the small items of worked metal — clasps, pins, decorated sword-fittings — that passed easily between communities because neither the English Church nor the Danish custom had any particular objection to them. He had traded along this boundary for fifteen years, he said, and Ulfkell’s reeve had done business with him for twelve of those years, and there was no account against him.

This is what made Þorfinnr interested.

“A man with no account against him,” he said, on the evening of the third day after the merchant’s arrival, when we sat in the small room he used for estate correspondence — two tallow candles, a smell of wax and old parchment, the sound of the gate-hinge in the wind, which squealed on its iron pivot in a way that Þorfinnr had twice refused to have remedied on the grounds that it gave him useful information about the hours of the night — “is either an honest man or a careful one. The question is whether the carefulness is of the kind that produces honesty, or of the kind that conceals the opposite.”

I asked him what had drawn his attention.

He set down the stylus he had been turning in his fingers, which was a gesture I had learned to recognise as the prelude to something he wished me to understand and remember.

“He knew,” Þorfinnr said, “that the old mill at the ford north of Sowerby had been rebuilt with a second wheel. He mentioned it in passing, to the reeve, as evidence that the northern track was worth using in winter because the ford had been improved. But the second wheel was completed in August, and Sigurðr Kálfsson’s last recorded visit to this part of the vale was at Whitsuntide. The improvement was not discussed in the markets at Jorvik, because the work was done by Ulfkell’s own men and the mill serves only the immediate estate. A man who learned of it through legitimate trade would have been here between August and now. A man who learned of it through some other means might not have been.”

I said that perhaps he had simply passed through without stopping at the estate.

“Then he would not have mentioned it,” Þorfinnr said, “because a man who merely passes a ford notes that it has been improved but does not know that the improvement was a second wheel unless he stopped to look, and if he stopped to look he would have stopped at the estate, and if he stopped at the estate there would be a record. I have read every record of this estate for the past four years, Gunnarr. It is, I confess, not a task I would have chosen had I any alternative. There is no record.”

He picked up the stylus again. The gate-hinge cried in the wind.

“He has a source,” Þorfinnr said. “Someone who passes through the estate regularly and who carries information outward, about the estate and its surrounds, without it appearing to be anything other than ordinary movement. A servant, perhaps. A craftsman. Someone whose presence is unremarkable.”

I asked the question that seemed to me most important: to whom was the information being carried?

Þorfinnr looked at me with the expression he reserved for questions that were not wrong but were not yet the right question either.

“That,” he said, “is what I intend to establish before I tell Ulfkell anything at all.”

I should say something here about the position Þorfinnr occupied, because without understanding it the subsequent events are difficult to place correctly. He was the thegn’s steward and counsellor, which meant that he sat at the intersection of every current that ran through the estate — English law, Danish custom, Church interest, the concerns of the freemen, the complaints of the bondsmen, the ambitions of the minor lords on the boundary. He had no formal authority over any of these currents. What he had was the trust of Ulfkell, who was an intelligent man but not, in Þorfinnr’s private estimate, a man who trusted his own intelligence sufficiently to act on it without confirmation from someone he trusted more.

This gave Þorfinnr a particular kind of power that was real but could not be named. He could not arrest a man. He could not conduct a search. He could not compel testimony. What he could do was know, and arrange, and suggest, and wait — and occasionally, as in this case, create a situation in which a man’s choices became visible.

The reeve, a solid Englishman named Wulfric who had his own kind of competence and who regarded Þorfinnr with the wary respect of a man who knows he is dealing with something he does not fully understand, had a different approach entirely. When Þorfinnr asked him, with apparent casualness, what he made of the merchant, Wulfric said that the accounts were in order, the goods fairly priced, the man’s character well-known on the trading routes, and that there was no cause for concern that he could see.

Þorfinnr agreed with him entirely.

This, I have since realised, was the most alarming thing he could have done.

The estate accounts for the quarter ending at Martinmas were, in fact, in order. Everything balanced. This was itself, to Þorfinnr’s eye, a kind of information.

“An account that balances perfectly,” he said, spreading the tallied sheets across the correspondence table, “at every line, in every column, without any of the small errors and adjustments that real commerce produces, is not an account of commerce. It is a performance of an account of commerce. Real trade is ragged. Real trade has a consignment of salt fish that arrives short-weight, a fur that is disputed between two grades, a debt carried over from one quarter because the debtor was ill. This account has none of those things. It has been prepared to survive examination, which means that someone expected it to be examined, which means that someone knew it contained something that required concealing.”

I asked how one concealed something by making an account balance perfectly.

He tapped one column with his finger. The tallow candle nearest him guttered in some draught I could not locate, and the shadows of his hand moved across the parchment like something alive.

“By putting the irregular transaction into a category where irregularity is expected,” he said. “Miscellaneous goods. Sundry trading items. Things that are, by their nature, unspecified. The payment recorded here, against ‘sundry exchange at the October crossing,’ is slightly larger than the others in that category. Not enough to draw the eye if one is moving quickly. But this crossing — the October crossing at the ford — took place on a day when, according to the reeve’s own weather record, the ford was impassable after two days of rain. I checked the weather record this morning.”

He sat back. For a long moment he was entirely still, which was the other mode I had learned to recognise — not the restless energy of the noticing phase, but the absolute quiet of the mind that has finished arranging and is now simply waiting to speak.

“Sigurðr Kálfsson,” he said, “met someone at that ford who was not coming to trade. He paid them. The payment passed through the estate accounts — with Wulfric’s knowledge or without it, I do not yet know, and I will not accuse Wulfric until I know — disguised as sundry goods. What he received in return was not goods. It was information. And the information he received has been, over a period of months, assembled into an account of Ulfkell’s dispositions — the state of his men, the condition of his fortifications, the relationships between his estate and the Danish freeholders to the east — that would be of considerable interest to a Danish lord who is calculating whether the settled boundary can be moved.”

I sat with this for a moment. Then I asked which Danish lord.

Þorfinnr looked at the candle.

“There is a lord named Áskell, holding lands east of the Derwent, who has spent the last two years acquiring influence among the Danish freeholders in this vale through a combination of loans and marriages. He has done nothing that could be brought before any court. He has said nothing that could be reported to the king’s reeve. He has simply been accumulating, quietly, the kind of information and relationship that a man accumulates before he decides whether a move is worth making. If the move would succeed, he makes it. If not, he waits for better conditions.”

He paused.

“Sigurðr Kálfsson has been providing the data for that calculation.”
