LEIFR HOLMR

~750 AD — Vestfold, Norway

Leifr Holmr was his father’s student in the art of memory and his own man in the application of it — where Sigr had used the accumulated knowledge of precedent to resolve disputes, Leifr used it to anticipate them. He could trace an inheritance dispute three generations before it became one, identify the competing claims before either party had articulated them, and in several recorded instances approached the families concerned with a proposed settlement before either had thought to quarrel. This was not meddling; it was the recognition, arrived at early in his career, that a conflict prevented is worth considerably more than a conflict resolved.

He was a man of the assembly — the thing, the Norse gathering where matters of law and community were decided — and his standing there was formidable. He spoke rarely, which made his contributions conspicuous; and he was right with a frequency that, over years, became the basis of a reputation that outlasted him. When his son Ragnarr was born, the naming of the child was attended by half the prominent men of Vestfold, which tells you something about the position Leifr Holmr had built, and the expectations he had created for those who came after him.

The account that follows is preserved in the record of Þorvald, a literate clerk attached to the assembly through a northern bishop’s house, whose duty it was to set down matters of consequence as they were resolved. Þorvald was not a man of Leifr’s mind, nor did he claim to be; he recorded what he saw, what he was told, and, where he understood it, what followed from it. His limitations are present in the record — he often writes from the vantage of a witness rather than an analyst — yet without his careful keeping, what occurred would have passed into memory without the same clarity of sequence. What is preserved here is drawn directly from his notes, with such shaping as is required to make the action intelligible without altering its substance.

The Account of Þorvald

Set Down That the Matter May Be Preserved in Its Proper Sequence

The smell of the assembly hall in winter is a thing a man does not forget once he has worked within it. Wet wool and woodsmoke, the particular sharpness of pine resin in the wall-joists, the tallow of rush-lights that guttered and spat in the draughts that found their way through the door-gaps despite every effort of the doorman’s stuffed hides. I set this down because it is where the matter began — not dramatically, not with the discovery of a body or the accusation of a name, but with Leifr Holmr standing near the far wall with a cup of something warm that he was not drinking, looking at nothing that I could identify.

I had been clerk to the assembly for three years at that point. I knew him well enough to know when he was looking at nothing and when he was looking at something that only he could see. This was the latter.

“You are counting something,” I said. I had learned that direct inquiry was more productive than waiting.

“Generations,” he said, without looking at me. “Sit down, Þorvald. You may find this useful for your records.”

I sat.

He had been, for some weeks, in a state that I had come to recognize as the counterpart to his more familiar torpor. The torpor came when there was nothing to occupy him — he would sit for whole days in his chair by the eastern post of the hall, barely speaking, eating only when the food was placed directly before him, occasionally asking for the older precedent-records to be read aloud while he stared at the smoke-hole in the roof. He was not, during these periods, pleasant company. He was not, I think, in particularly pleasant company for himself.

But when something had caught in his mind — when some thread had presented itself — he became a different animal entirely. He walked with purpose. He ate with appetite. He spoke quickly and precisely, and the words were worth following. This was one of those periods.

“The families of Gunnarr Hásteinn and Vermundr Blátönn,” he said. “What do you know of them?”

I answered, and he led me through the relations, the land, and the coming death of Hákon the Bald. He described, with calm certainty, a dispute that had not yet occurred — its claims, its tempers, and its likely descent into violence.

“I intend to prevent it,” Leifr said. “Write that down as well.”

He went first to Gunnarr, and then to Vermundr. I accompanied him, and I recorded. He did not argue for victory, but for avoidance — not for who would win, but for what both would lose. He spoke of cost, of uncertainty, of the slow poisoning of relations between families whose lives were already intertwined.

The terms he proposed were simple in form and complex in consequence. The meadow would pass by blood-descent to Gunnarr’s son’s wife. The oak-rights would be shared in perpetuity, divided by the old stone both families already acknowledged. The use would be governed by agreement lodged with the assembly, so that disputes would have recourse before they became quarrels.

Both families resisted, as he had predicted.

“Men are poor at imagining pain they have not experienced,” he told me later. “But a refusal now serves its purpose. It satisfies honour. The acceptance, when it comes, can be the sense.”

Hákon died in the second year.

The morning after the news reached the assembly, Leifr had the document prepared — the same document he had written years before, when no one was grieving.

Both families came. They sat across from one another. The sons now, not the fathers.

Leifr spoke briefly. He placed the document before them. He asked them to consider it not as a concession, but as a preservation.

They read.

There was resistance still — but it was the resistance of men who recognized reason and did not wish to yield to it too easily.

They accepted.

The agreement was lodged with the assembly under formal record. The public account names the families and the land and the terms. It does not name the years of preparation, nor the quiet work that preceded the moment of decision.

These things I record here.

On the evening after the settlement, I found Leifr again at his chair by the eastern post. The work was done. The stillness had returned.

He had not eaten.

I brought him bread and set it beside him.

“Þorvald,” he said. “Do you know the Saltvik family?”

I said that I did.

“Sit down,” he said. “Get your board.”

I got my board.

Outside, the door-hinge sang.
