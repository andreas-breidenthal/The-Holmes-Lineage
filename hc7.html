AWIL-MARDUK

~1870 BC — Babylon, Mesopotamia

Man of Marduk — the name a declaration of allegiance to the god whose cult was rising with the city, whose elevation to king of the gods under Hammurabi would not occur for another generation but whose trajectory was already visible to anyone watching the religious politics of Babylon with the attention they deserved. Awil-Marduk watched them with precisely that attention. He served as a junior official in the expanding Babylonian administration — in the office responsible for the registration of property transactions, a role that placed him at the intersection of law, commerce, and political intelligence, because in Babylon in 1870 BC the disposition of property was a reliable index of the disposition of power.

His father, Ili-iddinam, had built a life on recognising when conditions were being held in artificial balance. Awil-Marduk inherited the same inclination, but his temperament was slower, more cumulative. Where his father moved from anomaly to conclusion with measured confidence, Awil-Marduk preferred to hold the anomaly in place, to extend it across time until it either dissolved or revealed itself as part of a larger structure. He did not rush to understand; he preferred to endure not knowing until understanding could no longer be avoided.

He worked under the supervision of Bel-ēṭir, a senior scribe whose authority rested on long familiarity with the register and an instinctive resistance to questioning patterns that disrupted established administrative expectations. Bel-ēṭir recorded transactions faithfully, but he was cautious in interpretation and impatient with speculation. He permitted Awil-Marduk access to the full archive in part because he considered it impractical to refuse, and in part because he assumed the younger man would tire of it before finding anything of consequence.

The Account Of Bel-Ēṭir

Copied from my private clay into this tablet at the insistence of no one — for I do not yet know whether I am recording a triumph of administration or a conspiracy of two men against a third, and until I am certain, I will record it only for myself.

The air in Babylon in the dry month is marked by the river retreating from its banks — exposed mud, baked brick, the particular sharpness of bitumen where the canal-linings crack in the heat — and it was in this season that Nur-Adad came.

He did not come in the morning, when the clerks were present and the reed-styli moved in their rows. He came at the hour when the lamp-oil burns lowest and the junior officials have gone home to their households. He came, in other words, at the hour when only Awil-Marduk remained.

This was already a thing worth noting. Awil-Marduk was still there because he was always still there, working through the archive by the particular quality of attention that I had initially taken for diligence and had gradually come to recognise as something more unsettling — the patience of a man who does not tire of not knowing, who treats each unanswered question as a kind of nourishment. I myself had gone to the inner room to seal the day's completed tablets, and so I heard what passed between them before Nur-Adad was aware that anyone else remained in the building.

Nur-Adad was a temple official — an accountant for one of the smaller annexes of the Esagila complex, the great temple of Marduk himself, whose priests were not yet the most powerful men in the city but whose ambitions ran considerably ahead of their formal authority. He was a man of middle years, comfortably housed, professionally unremarkable. I knew his file. He appeared in the register twelve times across six years, all transactions in order, all stamps present. He was, in the language of our office, a clean record.

He entered the archive trembling.

I heard him before I saw him — the unsteady footfall, the breathing of a man who had been rehearsing a confession long enough that he had exhausted the rehearsal and now faced the performance with nothing left. He settled onto the stool across from Awil-Marduk's worktable and set something down on the clay surface. I moved slightly to the doorway and saw: a clay tablet, small, unstamped, the writing dense and compressed in a hand that suggested deliberate anonymity — the specific effort of someone who normally writes differently.

"I have received three of these," Nur-Adad said. "Over sixty days."

Awil-Marduk did not look at the tablet immediately. This was characteristic of him — the deliberate delay, the refusal to grant the object its expected centrality. He looked instead at Nur-Adad's hands, which were still arranged on the table as though the tablet might move without warning.

"What do they say?" he asked.

"That certain transactions — in my administration of the annex — have not been reported with complete accuracy to the temple's senior accounts. That this discrepancy is known. That unless I arrange for a particular document to be sealed in a particular way — a land transfer, a field near the second canal — the matter will be brought to the Šangû."

The Šangû. The chief administrator of the Esagila. A man whose recorded patience with irregularity in subordinates was not extensive.

"And have you?" Awil-Marduk asked.

"Have I what?"

"Failed to report with complete accuracy."

Nur-Adad's hands moved. "There are... in the management of a temple annex... small adjustments, over time. Nothing that diminishes the god's portion. Redistribution of surplus. Reclassification of—"

"Yes," said Awil-Marduk quietly, which ended that particular speech. He picked up the tablet and read it without visible reaction, the way a man reads a property description — attending to particulars without committing to interpretation. Then he set it down and folded his hands.

"Sit," he said, though Nur-Adad was already sitting.

"I am sitting."

"Then remain sitting. You will want to sit for what I tell you next." He paused, and the lamp between them made his shadow large on the archive wall, laid over the stacked tablets like a second catalogue. "Leave the letter with me. Return to your household. Speak to no one of this, not your wife, not your deputy, not the annex administrator. Come back in fifteen days."

"Fifteen days — and the next letter may—"

"Will arrive in eleven days," said Awil-Marduk, "on the basis of the current interval. I know. Leave it with me."

He had not, as it happened, known precisely. When I pressed him on this later — I am not too proud to admit I pressed him, curiosity being, in a senior scribe, a professional obligation — he acknowledged that the interval had been estimated. But the estimate, he explained, was not arbitrary.

"Sixty days, three letters. The first at the beginning, the second at thirty days, the third at forty-five. The interval is contracting."

"Meaning urgency," I said.

"Meaning impatience," he corrected. "These are different things. Urgency is external — it belongs to the situation. Impatience is internal. It belongs to the sender."

I considered this. "You already believed you knew who sent them."

"I had a preliminary view."

"Before examining the tablet."

"Before examining the tablet in front of Nur-Adad," he said, which was not quite the same thing, and which I chose not to pursue at that moment.

In the fifteen days that followed, Awil-Marduk did not go to the Esagila. He did not question anyone employed by the temple, or approach the annex, or make any inquiry that could be classified as investigation. He sat in the archive. He worked through the register.

He had been working through the register for, by my rough accounting, nearly two years. Not all of it — the register of our office covered property transactions across the city, and even Awil-Marduk's patience had geographic limits. He was working, specifically, through the transactions connected to the temple complexes: the direct transactions, the secondary transactions of families with temple employment, and — this was the element I had not fully appreciated until later — the transactions of senior officials within those temples, recorded not under the temple's administrative designation but under their private names, as private citizens making private purchases.

It is a feature of our register that it does not distinguish between a man acting professionally and the same man acting personally. Both transactions are recorded. The register has no interest in the man's capacity at the moment of signing; it records only that he signed. This is, for the purposes of administration, a simplification. For the purposes of the kind of reading Awil-Marduk was engaged in, it was something considerably more useful.

I watched him work. He would take out a series of tablets — not sequentially, but according to a logic I could not initially follow — and lay them in arrangements on the worktable, and sit with them, and make no mark and speak no word. Then he would return them to their shelves in a different order from how they had arrived, and I understood that the different order was itself a notation, a physical annotation of a relationship he had identified and was now preserving in the archive's own structure.

On the eighth day, he asked me a question.

"Bel-ēṭir. The field near the second canal — the one the letters require Nur-Adad to seal. What is its history in the register?"

I knew the register better than Awil-Marduk. This had been my source of patience with him — I assumed that whatever he was doing, I could, at the moment it became relevant, locate the relevant record faster than he could. I was generally correct. I went to the section, ran my eye down the tablet column, and told him what I found.

"Three owners in twelve years. The first, a private citizen — deceased. The second, a merchant collective — dissolved. The third is the current holder of record: an official of the Esagila administrative staff."

"Name."

I read it.

Awil-Marduk was quiet for a moment. Then: "And who appointed Nur-Adad to his position at the annex?"

I was less quick with this, but I found it. "The same man."

"And who would receive the field, in the transfer Nur-Adad is being required to arrange?"

I looked at the letter. The designated recipient was a designated intermediary household — a form used in transactions where the true beneficiary wishes not to appear in the register by name.

"The trust cannot be traced through our records," I said.

"No," said Awil-Marduk. "But it can be traced through the pattern of other designated intermediary households that have received property from officials this same man appointed, over a period of four years, all of which trusts have subsequently dissolved and transferred their holdings to a single private residence outside the city walls." He paused. "I have been working through that pattern for some months. The field near the second canal would have been the fourteenth transfer."

I set the letter down on the table.

"He is blackmailing his own subordinate," I said.

"Testing him," Awil-Marduk corrected. "The letters are not from a blackmailer seeking money. The letter does not request money. It requests the completion of a property transaction that the official could, at any point, have simply ordered Nur-Adad to complete by administrative direction. He chose instead to construct this pressure. He wished to know whether Nur-Adad, under duress, would act with discretion or would seek help."

"He wished to know if Nur-Adad could be trusted."

"He wished to know if Nur-Adad could be controlled. These are related but distinct qualities." Awil-Marduk looked at the lamp between us. Its oil was burning with a slight impurity — there was something in the smell, a vegetable sharpness beneath the sesame — and the light it gave was fractionally yellow. "Nur-Adad came to me. This is now part of what the official knows, whether or not he yet knows it. Nur-Adad has demonstrated that when threatened, he seeks outside counsel rather than compliance. He is not, by the official's standards, controllable."

"Then Nur-Adad is in danger," I said.

"Nur-Adad was always in danger. He was in danger the moment this official appointed him, because the appointment was itself a form of possession. Men who are useful to this kind of network are not permitted to simply leave it. They are tested, then used, then disposed of when they cease to be manageable. Nur-Adad came to me at precisely the right time — not to escape the danger, but because his coming here has changed the nature of it."

I looked at him. "How?"

"Because now there is a record," said Awil-Marduk.

On the fifteenth day, Nur-Adad returned.

He was less visibly frightened. The fear had settled into him in the interval and become familiar, which is the way of things with sustained dread — it stops presenting as acute crisis and begins presenting as ordinary life, and ordinary life is something men have a remarkable capacity to endure.

Awil-Marduk received him without ceremony. He returned the anonymous letter, which Nur-Adad received with the caution one extends to a thing that has already caused harm.

"Complete the transaction," Awil-Marduk said.

Nur-Adad stared at him. "You are telling me to comply."

"I am telling you to complete the transaction, yes. Seal the document. Transfer the field."

"Then — the blackmail succeeds? He has what he wanted?"

"He has the field," said Awil-Marduk. "And he has the confirmation that you can be directed. Which is what he wanted from you." He folded his hands on the table. "And I have the transaction, which will now appear in the register under your name and his designated trust, as the fourteenth in a sequence that I have already mapped and filed in supplementary notation. The transaction itself is now part of the record. It cannot be made not to have happened."

Nur-Adad was quiet for a long moment. The sound from the street outside — the gate-hinge of a nearby compound, which had needed oiling for months and which creaked at each hour of significant movement — turned once, and was silent.

"What does that mean?" Nur-Adad asked. "For me."

"It means you are no longer the most dangerous position in this structure," said Awil-Marduk. "You were selected because you were exposed. After you complete the transaction, you are simply another man who has done what he was told. The exposure is no longer unique to you — there are thirteen others in the register with identical profiles. You are now one node in a network that is documented. That documentation is the protection."

"You are protecting me with the evidence of my own wrongdoing."

"I am ensuring," said Awil-Marduk, "that if anything happens to you, the record exists to explain why." He paused. "The official is not going to want that record examined. As long as you are alive and compliant, there is no reason to examine it. You understand the logic."

I understood it. Nur-Adad, after a moment, indicated that he understood it as well. He left — more steadily than he had arrived, though with the particular gait of a man carrying something he cannot set down.

The transaction was completed four days later. It appears in our register in standard form: a field near the second canal, transferred from Nur-Adad, acting in his private capacity, to a designated intermediary household, witnessed and stamped according to procedure.

I sealed it myself.

Awil-Marduk did not report the matter to any superior authority. He did not bring it to the attention of the palace administration, or to the Šangû of the Esagila, or to the senior judges of the city. He made his supplementary notations in the archive — a series of cross-referenced tablets that a careful reader of the register would eventually locate if they were looking for a specific pattern and knew where to begin — and he said nothing.

I asked him why.

"The Šangû," he said, "is not the appropriate authority. The network extends past him. I do not yet know how far."

"Then the palace administration—"

"Is the appropriate authority when I know how far." He returned a tablet to its shelf. "I do not yet know how far."

"And in the meantime, the official continues."

"In the meantime, the official continues," he agreed, "and the register continues, and when I know how far, there will be a complete map rather than a partial one. A partial map is more dangerous than no map, because it allows the mapmaker to believe the territory is smaller than it is."

I looked at him across the stacked tablets and the low lamp and the accumulated smell of old clay and the archive's particular silence, which was the silence of many things that had been said, set down, and sealed.

"You have been working toward this map for two years," I said.

"Longer," said Awil-Marduk, with the equanimity of a man to whom this is not a source of impatience. "But two years in this archive." He looked at the register around us — the tablets in their rows, the records of who had given what to whom and when and in the presence of which witnesses. "The register contains everything. It simply does not yet contain enough."

He sat down at his worktable and picked up his stylus, and he returned to reading.

I sealed the lamp. I went home. I wrote this account on private clay and stored it in my household archive, behind the tablets I do not wish my wife to see and in front of the tablets I do not wish my son to see, in the place reserved for things that are true but not yet safe to be official.

I do not know when that will change.

I am beginning to think Awil-Marduk does.

Here ends the account of Bel-ēṭir, senior scribe, Office of Property Transactions, Babylon.
