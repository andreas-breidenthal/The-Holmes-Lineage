HOLKOS OF ATHENS

~1360 BC — Athens, Greece

Athens in the Mycenaean age was not yet the city of Pericles and philosophy but a palace-centre of the second rank — a citadel on a limestone rock, a wanax and his scribes and his warriors, a Linear B archive recording the obligations of dependent farmers and the contents of bronze-smiths’ stores. Holkos of Athens served in that palace economy as his father had served in Corinth and his grandfather before that in Mycenae itself — as a merchant-interpreter, moving between the Greek palace world and the trading networks that connected it to Cyprus, to the Levant, to Egypt, and to the Hittite empire to the east.

He was a man whose value lay in languages and in the particular intelligence that languages, when truly mastered, convey: not merely the words but the assumptions behind them, the conventions that a speaker of one tongue considers too obvious to articulate to a speaker of another. Holkos of Athens had that intelligence in full, and he used it to move goods and information between worlds that could not otherwise communicate with the precision that commerce and diplomacy required. He raised his son in the same tradition — the same formation, the same alertness, and the same conviction that the most consequential work is usually done at the boundary between two things that do not quite understand each other.

The account that follows is preserved by the scribe Menandros, whose duties in the Athenian archive included the copying of trade disputes and agricultural records. His notes, appended after the resolution of the matter, show both care and limitation: he records what he was shown, but often notes his inability to follow the reasoning until Holkos later explained it to him directly.

The Account Of Menandros

I will say first what every man in the citadel already knows, which is that I am not a clever man. I can copy a tablet without error from the first sign to the last. I can sum a column of barley figures and arrive at the same answer twice. I can distinguish the hand of Arkitas from the hand of Deukalion at twenty paces in poor lamplight. These are the competencies of my office, and I have always considered them sufficient.

It was Holkos of Athens who taught me — if that is the correct word for a process that felt more like having one’s assumptions removed one at a time, with the clinical patience of a man extracting splinters — that to see what is written on a tablet and to read what its writing discloses are operations so different from one another that calling them by the same name is a kind of fraud.

I did not understand this when he first said it to me, in the lower corridor of the archive, while the body of Arkitas lay cooling at our feet.

I understand it now.

The morning had the particular quality that late summer mornings in Athens sometimes acquire: a stillness so absolute that the air itself seems to be waiting. The smell of the citadel at that hour is always the same — limestone dust, the rendered fat of last night’s lamps, and beneath both of those the older smell of the rock itself, which is a smell like the inside of a sealed jar, dry and mineral and slightly airless, as though the hill is slowly breathing out something it has held since before men came to put walls upon it.

The gate-hinge of the archive — I will not forget its sound, a particular iron complaint in two notes, the second lower than the first — made that sound at the moment I arrived to begin the morning copy-work, and I pushed through to find the room occupied more than I had anticipated.

Arkitas lay between the central table and the east shelf, on his back, with his arms arranged in a way that suggested he had not arranged them himself. His oil lamp had burned to nothing on the table beside the tablet-press. The tablets from the previous evening’s session were laid in their drying rack along the north wall, still soft enough that the morning chill had not fully set them.

This was the strangeness that I noticed first and that I could not explain: the tablets were there. Arkitas was dead. But the assessment session from the previous evening — a disputed cargo matter involving two merchants and considerable quantities of copper ingots — had apparently been completed, because the tablets in the rack numbered exactly what they should have numbered, and they bore entries in what appeared to be a proper hand.

The other man who had been present for that session — a merchant called Eruthros, who had sailed with Cretan copper from a Cypriot intermediary and disputed the palace’s assessment of his declared weight — was nowhere in the archive.

I sent for the Lawagetas’s man, who arrived with three palace guards and the expression of a person who has learned that a problem exists but not yet what shape it takes. His name was Theron, a careful, broad-shouldered official of middle years who had managed the palace’s inspection of cargo movements for longer than I had held my post. He looked at Arkitas. He looked at the tablets in the rack. He looked toward the door.

“The merchant Eruthros,” he said. “Where is the merchant Eruthros.”

This was the correct question, and within the hour Eruthros had been found on the lower slopes of the citadel, near the merchant quarter, breaking his fast with apparent calm at the house of his factor. He was brought back to the archive.

He said: Arkitas had been alive when he left. The assessment had been completed. He had simply gone to sleep. He said this with the particular composure of a man who has rehearsed the sentence.

Theron believed this to be a lie. So did I. But Eruthros was a man of standing in the trading network — Cypriot contacts, Levantine contacts, a factor in Corinth and another, it was said, in Rhodes — and the matter required more than Theron’s intuition and my unease. It required, the Lawagetas’s secretary eventually decided, a fresh pair of eyes.

Holkos of Athens had completed a trading mission to the eastern ports that same month and was present in the citadel for his accounting. He was known to the palace. He was asked.

He came in the late morning.

I had seen Holkos at work before — twice, in minor matters of disputed provenance and one question of a falsified consignment record — but never in a matter of this weight, and I watched him now with the attention I would give to a man performing a craft I did not practice.

He was perhaps fifty, though the age was difficult to assign. A narrow face, deeply browned by travel, with eyes of an unremarkable grey-green that moved across a surface the way water moves across stone: covering everything, exerting a continuous, even pressure, leaving nothing quite dry. He wore a plain wool travelling cloak that he did not remove. He had brought nothing with him except a small polished obsidian disc, which he occasionally turned in his fingers in moments of apparent abstraction. Later I learned this was his habitual occupation for the hands when the brain required them to be still.

He entered the archive and did not look at the body.

He looked instead at the lamp on the table. He looked at the tablet-press. He walked to the drying rack and examined each tablet in sequence, holding them at an angle to the light from the high window. He spent particular time on the last two entries. He set them back carefully, in order.

He looked at the floor near the east shelf. He crouched and examined something I could not see — I was standing too far back, and Theron’s guards filled most of the corridor-mouth. He straightened without comment.

Then he looked at the body.

He did so for approximately the time it takes to recite a short prayer — unhurried, complete — and then he stood and turned to the wall where the current-year assessment tablets were stored in their cubbies, and he began reading them. Not the fresh tablets. The old ones. The records of previous assessments in which Arkitas had served as the recording scribe.

Theron shifted his weight. I could feel his impatience from across the room. He was a man who reasoned in straight lines, and he had already drawn his straight line from Eruthros to the body, and he wanted the formality of confirmation so that he could send his guards to make an arrest.

“Can you tell us,” he said, “whether the merchant’s account is false or true?”

Holkos did not turn around. “I can tell you rather more than that,” he said. “But I prefer to tell it all at once. It would lose something, divided.”

He assembled us finally — Theron, myself, two of the guards, and Eruthros, who had been permitted to remain on the understanding that his cooperation was expected — near the north wall, where the drying rack held its tablets in morning light.

He picked up the second-to-last tablet.

“You are a scribe, Menandros,” he said to me, not unkindly. “What do you see?”

“An entry,” I said. “A cargo assessment. The standard format — commodity, declared weight, assessed weight, the differential, the palace’s share.”

“Whose hand?”

I looked. “Arkitas,” I said. “The hand of Arkitas. I know it well.”

“And this one?” He handed me the last tablet — the final entry of the evening.

I looked. I said, with confidence: “Also Arkitas.”

Holkos smiled, very slightly, in a way I did not care for, because it was not the smile of a man who has been agreed with. It was the smile of a teacher whose student has just provided the demonstration the lesson requires.

“Menandros,” he said, “you know the hand of Arkitas because you have worked alongside him for years and you see its forms as a unified whole, a single impression. I am looking at it for the first time this morning, which means I am looking at it as a collection of individual decisions — where a sign-maker chooses to begin a sign, how he completes the downstroke, whether he lifts the stylus between the horizontal wedges or draws them in a single motion.” He set the two tablets side by side on the table edge. “The hand of Arkitas, across your archive records, is a hand formed in the Mycenae tradition with one peculiarity: he begins the sign for ko — the syllable that opens the commodity notation for copper, ko-pe-re-ja — with an upward motion. Not unusual; it is one of the variant forms. Consistent, across twenty tablets I examined. He has done it this way since he first learned the sign.”

He tapped the final tablet.

“The last entry of the evening was made by a person who begins ko with a downward motion. It is the more common form. It is not the form Arkitas used. The hand is an imitation — a good one, made by someone who had studied Arkitas’s records carefully enough to reproduce the proportions and the spacing, but who could not entirely suppress their own ingrained habits when they reached a sign they had always written differently.”

The room was quiet.

I looked at the two tablets again. And I — who had read them as identical — now saw it. The last entry’s ko sign descended first. All of Arkitas’s others ascended. It was a small thing. It was, once seen, completely impossible to unsee.

“The final entry,” Holkos continued, “was made after Arkitas was dead. It was made to complete the record — to give the appearance that the assessment session had concluded in proper form, that both parties had settled their figures, that Eruthros’s cargo had been assessed at a weight favorable to Eruthros, and that Arkitas had certified it. A record that certified it meant Eruthros would owe the palace considerably less bronze than the true weight demanded. A record made by Arkitas’s own hand — or what appeared to be his hand — could not be disputed.” He set the tablet down. “Eruthros killed him. He studied Arkitas’s sign-forms well in advance — probably from public records, from previous assessments involving his cargo. He came prepared. He brought his own stylus, which is why the depth of the impression on that final tablet is slightly heavier than Arkitas’s own entries: a different instrument, a different grip. He made the entry. He arranged the body. He left.”

Eruthros had not moved. He was looking at the tablet with an expression from which something had just departed — the composure, I think, like water running from a cracked vessel.

“The body,” Theron said. “The cause.”

“A blow behind the left ear. The floor has a scuff-mark near the east shelf consistent with a man falling backward against it. Arkitas was struck while working, possibly in a moment of dispute over the figures, possibly by prior arrangement that was never intended to resolve peacefully. Either way, he fell, and he did not rise, and Eruthros had his opportunity.” Holkos picked up the obsidian disc from the table where he had set it. “The lamp had burned low by then — you can see by the residue that it had been burning for many hours. Eruthros worked by its final light to complete his entry. That is why the impression pressure varied: he was working in near-darkness, pressing harder than necessary to be certain the signs were legible.”

Theron was already looking at his guards.

What happened next is the part I find most difficult to record without distorting it, because I am not certain I fully understood it as it occurred, and I have only Holkos’s later explanation to supplement my own account.

Before the guards moved, Holkos said: “A moment, Theron.”

Theron paused.

“Eruthros,” Holkos said, in a different register — still quiet, but with something in it that was not quite the same as the preceding clinical tone. Something that suggested the clinical portion was finished and something less comfortable had begun. “The cargo that was disputed. The copper. Where is it from, precisely?”

Eruthros looked at him.

“Not the answer you gave the archive,” Holkos said. “The actual answer.”

A long silence. The second lower note of the gate-hinge drifted in from the corridor, where someone had opened the door to look in at us.

“Alashiya,” Eruthros said at last. This was Cyprus, or the kingdom on Cyprus — significant because it was under some degree of Hittite influence, and movements of large copper quantities from that source carried political implications beyond the commercial.

“And the man who arranged the contract,” Holkos said. “In Corinth.”

Eruthros’s jaw shifted.

“I know his name,” Holkos said pleasantly. “I have worked those routes for thirty years. I know who coordinates the larger movements of Alashiyan copper through the Aegean without palace knowledge. I know how that coordination works, and I know approximately how many palace officials in how many citadels have received gifts to facilitate it.” He paused. “I am not going to speak that name in this room.”

Theron turned to look at him sharply.

“Because,” Holkos continued, as though Theron had asked a question rather than merely looked at him, “if I speak it in this room, the matter becomes a matter for the Lawagetas, and then for the Wanax, and then — quite rapidly — for people considerably more powerful than any of us. Arkitas died because he assessed honestly and would not amend his figures. He was a competent man doing his work with integrity. He deserves justice. Eruthros will give him justice. But the name behind Eruthros, and the network behind that name, is a matter for another day, in another room, and I will take it up directly with the appropriate party when I am ready to do so and not before.” He looked at Theron. “Eruthros killed Arkitas. He falsified a palace record. The case against him is complete and requires nothing further. Take him.”

Theron took him.

I do not believe Theron understood what had just been withheld from him, and why. I did not understand it myself until Holkos explained it to me that evening, walking back through the lower citadel in the long amber light.

“The network,” he said, turning the obsidian disc, “is not dismantled by naming one man in one archive to one Lawagetas’s official. It disperses. It learns. It finds new men in new archives. Eruthros is caught, and Eruthros will pay for Arkitas. But the man in Corinth is of more value to me unaware of my knowledge than he would be if I had named him today and watched him vanish to Rhodes before nightfall.” He looked sideways at me. “Do you understand?”

“I think so,” I said.

“You don’t yet,” he said, without unkindness. “But you will write it down accurately, which is the more important quality.”

I should record, for the purpose of completeness and because I believe it will be useful to whoever reads these notes after me, what Holkos said when I asked him how he had known to examine the sign-forms specifically — rather than the content of the tablets, or the weight of the stylus impressions, or any number of other details he might have examined first.

He was quiet for a moment, in that particular way he had of being quiet that was not the quiet of a man who does not know what to say but of a man who is deciding precisely which part of a larger answer to offer.

“Forgery,” he said, “requires the forger to become someone they are not. And the places where a person cannot fully become someone else — cannot abandon their own formations, their own ingrained motions — those are exactly the places where the forgery speaks loudest. Not where it is most similar to the original, but where it slips.” He tapped two fingers against his temple. “A man’s hand is not merely a skill. It is twenty years of habit compressed into motion. You cannot borrow another man’s twenty years, however carefully you study his results. You can borrow his proportions. You cannot borrow his direction.”

He turned the disc once more.

“I look,” he said, “for the moment a man stops pretending and starts, without realizing it, being himself.”

Eruthros of the Cypriot trade-route was brought before the Lawagetas and bound over for palace judgment. The record certified his guilt in the matter of the death of Arkitas, scribe of the first rank, and in the falsification of a cargo assessment tablet. The archive record has been corrected. The falsified tablet has been preserved in its incorrect state alongside this account, as Holkos requested, on the grounds that it is of greater documentary value intact than destroyed.

The name Holkos declined to speak in the archive, I also decline to write here. It is known to me. It will be useful, in time, to one who knows how to use it.

Menandros, Scribe of the Second Rank

Athens, Forty-Third Year
