HOLKOS OF ATHENS

~1360 BC — Athens, Greece

The Matter of the Death of Arkitas

Being a Record of the Manner in Which the Truth Was Established

I.

The gate-hinge of the archive makes a distinctive sound — a bronze shriek, rising at the top of the swing — and I heard it at the precise moment I pushed through to begin the morning copy-work and found the room more occupied than I had anticipated.

Arkitas lay between the central table and the east shelf, on his back, with his arms arranged in a way that suggested he had not arranged them himself.

I will say here, before anything else, that I am not a clever man. I can copy a tablet without error from the first sign to the last. I can sum a column of barley figures and arrive at the same answer twice. I can distinguish the hand of Arkitas from the hand of Deukalion at twenty paces in poor lamplight. These are the competencies of my office, and until that morning I had considered them sufficient. What I had not understood — what Arkitas's death taught me, through the agency of one of the strangest and most precise minds I have encountered in thirty years of archive work — is that to see what is written on a tablet and to read what its writing discloses are operations so different from one another that calling them by the same name is a kind of fraud.

I did not know this yet. I knew only that Arkitas was dead, and that the oil lamp on the table had burned to nothing, and that the tablets from the previous evening's assessment session were laid in their drying rack along the north wall, still soft enough that the morning chill had not fully set them. The session had concerned a disputed cargo matter involving a merchant called Eruthros and considerable quantities of copper ingots. The tablets numbered exactly what they should have numbered.

This was the strangeness I could not explain. Arkitas was dead. But the session appeared complete.

I sent for the Lawagetas's man.

II.

His name was Theron — a careful, broad-shouldered official of middle years who had managed the palace's inspection of cargo movements for longer than I had held my post, and whose reputation in this citadel rested entirely on the assumption that what passed through his archive was what it appeared to be. He arrived with three palace guards and the expression of a person who has learned that a problem exists but not yet what shape it takes. He looked at Arkitas. He looked at the tablets in the rack. He looked toward the door.

I recognised his expression because it matched mine: the face of a man whose professional certainty has just stepped into open air and found nothing beneath it.

"The merchant Eruthros," he said. "Where is the merchant Eruthros."

Within the hour, Eruthros had been found on the lower slopes of the citadel, near the merchant quarter, breaking his fast with apparent calm at the house of his factor. He was brought back to the archive. He said Arkitas had been alive when he left. The assessment had been completed. He had simply gone to sleep. He said this with the particular composure of a man who has rehearsed the sentence.

Theron believed this to be a lie. So did I. But Eruthros was a man of standing in the trading network — Cypriot contacts, Levantine contacts, a factor in Corinth, another in Rhodes — and the matter required more than intuition. It required, the Lawagetas's secretary eventually decided, a fresh pair of eyes. Eyes that could not be said, afterward, to have had any reason to reach a particular conclusion.

I understood the subtext. Theron's eyes were not fresh. His reputation — his position, the work of twenty careful years — depended on this archive being orderly and above reproach. If the fraud had passed through his inspection and the murder been committed under his watch, he needed a resolution that was unimpeachable, conducted by a man who owed him nothing. He was a careful man, and a careful man's worst hour is the morning he discovers that his care was not sufficient. He sent for Holkos of Athens.

III.

I had encountered Holkos twice before — once in a matter of disputed provenance, once in a question of a falsified consignment record — but I had never seen him at work in anything of this weight. He was a merchant-interpreter by formation, a man whose value lay in languages and in the intelligence that languages, when truly mastered, convey: not merely the words but the assumptions behind them. He had been moving between the Greek palace world and the eastern trading networks for thirty years, inheriting the routes and the formation from his father before him, who had worked from Corinth, and his grandfather before that from Mycenae itself. He was perhaps fifty, though the age was difficult to assign.

He came in the late morning. He wore a plain wool travelling cloak he did not remove. He carried a small polished obsidian disc, which he turned in his fingers as he crossed the threshold.

He entered the archive and did not look at the body.

What he did instead — and I watched him with the attention I would give to a man performing a craft I did not practise — was move. Not still, not meditative: he moved with the slightly compressed energy of a man who has already begun thinking and whose body has not yet caught up with his conclusions. He looked at the lamp on the table — crouching to examine the residue in the bowl at close range, his cloak pooling on the floor, before straightening abruptly and crossing to the tablet-press with quick, decisive steps. He pressed two fingers into the clay-bed, tested its resistance, and made a sound under his breath that was not quite satisfaction and not quite its opposite.

Then he went to the drying rack.

He examined each tablet in sequence, holding them at an angle to the light from the high window — and here I must record what I observed, because it matters, and because I did not understand its significance until much later.

On the second-to-last tablet, he slowed. He tilted it once, then again, bringing it very close to his eye. The sign he was examining — I was near enough now to see — was a ka sign, the syllable that opens the commodity notation for bronze. I know this sign well. I have written it ten thousand times. He tilted the tablet a third time, and something shifted in his face: not a frown exactly, but a quality of focused attention so concentrated that it had the appearance of one.

He set that tablet back and took up the last one — the final entry of the evening — and performed the identical examination. This time the shift in his face was different: a brief, controlled thing, quickly suppressed, that I could not read at all.

He set the last tablet back carefully, in order.

Then he began to pace.

This was unexpected. Three steps toward the east shelf, a turn, three steps back — not anxious, not uncertain, but with the rhythmic deliberateness of a man who thinks better in motion than in stillness, who uses movement the way I use a clean tablet, as the surface on which thought takes shape. Theron watched him with visible impatience. Eruthros, I noticed, watched him with something else: a stillness that had, at its edges, the quality of held breath.

"Can you tell us," Theron said at last, "whether the merchant's account is false or true?"

Holkos stopped pacing. "I can tell you rather more than that," he said. "But I need the old records first. Previous assessments — in which Arkitas served as recording scribe."

Theron made a gesture toward the storage wall. Holkos was already moving. He pulled the cubby tablets — not the current season's records but the older ones, a year back, two years — and began reading them at speed, carrying two or three at a time to the window light and running his eye down their surfaces with the same focused, rapid attention. Occasionally he set one aside. Occasionally he returned one to its cubby with what I can only describe as a dismissive precision, as though it had confirmed something he had already suspected.

I moved closer as he worked, curious now rather than merely apprehensive. I looked at the tablet nearest to his hand — one of the older assessments, a copper cargo from eight months prior. The scribal hand was Arkitas's. I knew it as I know my own. The ka signs ran down the left column of each entry, opening the bronze notation: ka-ko, ka-ko, ka-ko. Each one begun, as I had always unconsciously registered without ever consciously remarking, with the stylus making its first motion upward before completing the sign.

Holkos set this tablet down and picked up one of those he had set aside — an older record from a different session, one I did not immediately recognise as involving Eruthros.

I looked at the records on Eruthros's prior visits. I looked at the ka-ko entries that appeared in those older tablets wherever bronze-related goods appeared in the cargo notations that Eruthros had either disputed or settled.

I could not, then, have said what I was looking for. But I remember that I looked.

Holkos turned from the wall. He looked, for the first time since he had entered, at Eruthros.

It was a brief look — five seconds, perhaps — but it had the quality of a measurement being completed. Eruthros met it without flinching, with the same rehearsed composure he had displayed since his return from his factor's house. Yet something in the set of his shoulders, something I might not have noticed had I not been watching specifically, suggested that the look had cost him something to receive.

Holkos glanced at me. "Menandros," he said, quietly. "The lamp."

"Yes?"

"When I ask you to bring me the last tablet from the rack — the final entry — I would like you to carry it past Eruthros on your way to the table. Hold it where he can see the surface clearly." He said this in a tone of mild administrative instruction, as though he were asking me to fetch a stool. "And then set it down beside me."

I did not understand the instruction. But I had seen enough by then to do what he asked without requiring an explanation.

Holkos turned to Theron. "I believe," he said, "that given the nature of what I am about to demonstrate, it would be prudent to have your guards positioned near the door and near the rack. Not obtrusively. Simply near."

Theron frowned, but he was a careful man, and careful men recognise the instruction of another careful man when they hear it. He moved his guards quietly.

I brought the final tablet from the rack. I carried it, as instructed, past Eruthros — close enough that he could see its surface, that he could see exactly what it bore and exactly what condition it was in. I saw his eyes move to it. I saw something behind that composed surface shift and reset, the way a man resettles his footing on uncertain ground.

I set the tablet on the table beside Holkos.

And Eruthros moved.

He moved fast, and without announcement, and entirely toward the rack — not toward the door, not toward Holkos, but toward the other tablets, both hands extended, and in that single moment I understood with absolute clarity what he intended: that if the tablets did not exist, the evidence did not exist, and if the evidence did not exist, he was simply a merchant who had found a dead scribe and gone home for his breakfast. I was closest to the rack. I did not think. I put myself between his hands and the tablets and took the impact of his forearm across my chest hard enough to drive me back against the shelf.

The guards had him before he reached me again.

He stood in their grip with an expression from which everything had departed — the composure, the rehearsed ease, all of it gone at once, like water from a cracked vessel — and he looked at Holkos with the eyes of a man who has just understood the nature of the ground he is standing on.

Holkos had not moved during any of it. He stood where he had been standing, turning the obsidian disc in his fingers, watching.

IV.

"You arranged that," Theron said. His tone was not precisely accusatory. It was the tone of a man recalibrating.

"I arranged for the truth to arrange itself," Holkos said. "There is a distinction, though I grant it is a fine one." He picked up the second-to-last tablet and the final one and set them side by side on the table. "Come here, Menandros."

I came.

"You are a scribe," he said. "Tell me what you see."

I looked at both tablets. "Cargo assessments," I said. "The standard format — commodity, declared weight, assessed weight, the differential, the palace's share."

"Whose hand?"

"Arkitas," I said. "Both of them. The hand of Arkitas. I know it as well as my own."

"Look at this sign." He tapped the ka-ko entry on the second-to-last tablet — the one I had seen him examine at the rack. "How does it begin? Follow the first motion of the stylus."

I looked. The first stroke of the ka sign moved upward before completing the form. "Upward," I said.

"This sign." He tapped the same notation on the final tablet.

I looked. The first stroke moved — I stared at it. The first stroke moved downward. The rest of the sign was identical in proportion, identical in spacing. But the first motion was reversed.

I had read both tablets as identical. I looked at them again and saw what had been there to see from the first moment I had entered the archive that morning, had any of us known how to look.

"The hand of Arkitas," Holkos said, "is a hand formed in the Mycenae tradition with one peculiarity: he begins that sign with an upward motion. Not unusual — it is one of the variant forms. But it is consistent. Across twenty tablets I examined, across two years of records, he has done it this way since he first learned the sign." He tapped the final tablet. "The last entry of the evening was made by a person who begins ka with a downward motion — the more common form; his own ingrained form. The hand is an imitation. A good one, made by someone who had studied Arkitas's records carefully enough to reproduce the proportions and the spacing, but who could not entirely suppress his own habits when he reached a sign he had always written differently."

The room was very still.

"The final entry was made after Arkitas was dead. Made to complete the record — to give the appearance that the session had concluded in proper form, that Eruthros's cargo had been assessed at a weight favourable to Eruthros, and that Arkitas had certified it. A record in Arkitas's own hand — or what appeared to be his hand — could not be disputed by the palace. Eruthros came prepared. He had studied the archive records in advance. He brought his own stylus — a different instrument, a different grip, which is why the impression on that final tablet is slightly heavier than Arkitas's own entries. He completed the entry. He arranged the body. He left."

I looked at Eruthros. He was looking at the tablet with the focus of a man who has nothing left to calculate.

"The body," Theron said. "The cause."

"A blow behind the left ear. The floor near the east shelf has a scuff-mark consistent with a man falling backward against it — I examined it when I came in. Arkitas was struck while working, at a moment when the lamp had burned low enough that the mark would be difficult to observe. He fell, and he did not rise." Holkos set the obsidian disc on the table. "The lamp residue tells me it had been burning for many hours. Eruthros worked by its final light. That is why the impression pressure on the last tablet is heavier than his earlier work: near-darkness, pressing harder than necessary to be certain the signs were legible."

Theron turned to look at his guards.

"A moment, Theron," Holkos said.

Theron paused.

Holkos looked at Eruthros — not with the quality of measurement he had used before, but with something more direct. "The copper cargo. Where is it from, precisely?"

Eruthros said nothing. His jaw was very still.

"Not the answer you gave the archive," Holkos said. "You have a factor in Corinth. I have worked those routes for thirty years. I know who coordinates the movement of Alashiyan copper through the Aegean without palace knowledge, and when I examined your previous assessments on this archive's shelves just now, I found that the notations for your cargo sources have been, on four separate occasions, recorded as Aegean intermediary when the declared commodity weight and grade are consistent only with direct Alashiyan origin. Someone in this trading network has been arranging that notation for you, and that someone is not in Cyprus." He paused. "Your factor in Corinth is not merely a factor."

Eruthros's jaw moved. It was the smallest possible motion, and it was enough.

"I know his name," Holkos said pleasantly. "I am not going to speak it in this room."

Theron turned to look at him sharply.

"Because if I speak it in this room," Holkos continued, as though Theron had asked a question, "the matter becomes a matter for the Lawagetas, then for the Wanax, then — rapidly — for people considerably more powerful than any of us. Arkitas died because he assessed honestly and would not amend his figures. He was a competent man doing his work with integrity. He deserves justice. Eruthros will give him justice. But the name behind Eruthros, and the network behind that name, is a matter for another day, in another room, and I will take it up directly with the appropriate party when I am ready." He looked at Theron. "Eruthros killed Arkitas. He falsified a palace record. The case against him is complete. Take him."

Theron took him.

V.

The smell of the citadel at that late-morning hour had the quality that high summer sometimes produces — a stillness so absolute the air itself seems to be waiting. Limestone dust. The rendered fat of the burned-out lamp. Beneath both, the older smell of the rock: like the inside of a sealed jar, dry and mineral and slightly airless, as though the hill is slowly breathing out something it has held since before men came to put walls upon it.

I registered this only as the guards and their charge departed and the archive fell quiet, and I stood among the tablets with Holkos of Athens and understood, for the first time since I had pushed through that gate that morning, that the room was not still because something was waiting. It was still because something was over.

VI.

He explained it to me that evening, walking back through the lower citadel in the long amber light.

"The network," he said, turning the disc, "is not dismantled by naming one man in one archive to one Lawagetas's official. It disperses. It learns. It finds new men in new archives. Eruthros is caught, and Eruthros will pay for Arkitas. But the man in Corinth is of more value to me unaware of my knowledge than he would be if I had named him today and watched him vanish to Rhodes before nightfall." He looked sideways at me. "Do you understand?"

"I think so," I said.

"You don't yet," he said, without unkindness. "But you will write it down accurately, which is the more important quality."

I asked him — because the question had been sitting in me since the moment he had instructed me to carry the tablet past Eruthros — whether he had known what the man would do.

"Not precisely," he said. "I knew he had spent considerable preparation on his alibi and his forgery. A man who has prepared that carefully does not abandon his investment without a fight. And a man whose investment is a physical object — a tablet — will fight for the physical object when he believes it is about to be used against him." He turned the disc. "I made certain he could see it clearly. I made certain he understood that I had examined it. The rest was his own decision."

"And if he had not lunged?"

"Then I would have shown the tablets to Theron in the ordinary way, and Theron would have had his evidence, and Eruthros would have denied everything, and the matter would have gone to palace judgment on the strength of the scribal analysis alone." He paused. "Which it still will, since the analysis stands regardless of what Eruthros did with his arms. But his lunge was — additional."

"Additional," I repeated.

"It confirmed guilt in a manner that required no interpretation," Holkos said. "A composed innocent man does not attempt to destroy the evidence of his innocence."

I thought about this for several paces.

I asked him, then, the other question — how he had known to examine the sign-forms specifically, above all other details he might have examined first.

He was quiet for a moment in that particular way he had: not the quiet of a man who does not know what to say, but of a man deciding precisely which part of a larger answer to offer.

"Forgery," he said, "requires the forger to become someone they are not. And the places where a person cannot fully become someone else — cannot abandon their own formations, their own ingrained motions — those are exactly the places where the forgery speaks loudest. Not where it is most similar to the original. Where it slips." He tapped two fingers against his temple. "A man's hand is not merely a skill. It is twenty years of habit compressed into motion. You cannot borrow another man's twenty years, however carefully you study his results. You can borrow his proportions. You cannot borrow his direction."

He turned the disc once more in the amber light.

"I look," he said, "for the moment a man stops pretending and starts, without realising it, being himself."

Eruthros of the Cypriot trade-route was brought before the Lawagetas and bound over for palace judgment. The record certified his guilt in the matter of the death of Arkitas, scribe of the first rank, and in the falsification of a cargo assessment tablet. The archive record has been corrected. The falsified tablet has been preserved in its incorrect state alongside this account, as Holkos requested, on the grounds that it is of greater documentary value intact than destroyed.

The name Holkos declined to speak in the archive, I also decline to write here. It is known to me. It will be useful, in time, to one who knows how to use it.

Menandros, Scribe of the Second Rank Athens, Forty-Third Year
