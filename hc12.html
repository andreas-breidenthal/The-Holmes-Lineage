DJEHUTY-MES

~1720 BC — Thebes, Egypt

The Matter of Amenhotep-sa-Hapu

In the seventeenth year of the reign of Merneferre, may he be granted life, stability, and dominion

I am Hori, assistant to the cataloguer Djehuty-mes in the House of Life at Waset. I set down this account because I observed it, and because I have come to believe that what is observed and not recorded is, in the reckoning of this place, the same as what never occurred. That belief was not mine before this matter. It became mine during it.

It began, as the more consequential things often do, with a thing absent that should have been present.

The morning of the fourth day of the second month of Akhet: I arrived at the catalogue room to find three things missing from their customary places. A junior observer's papyrus, which should have been on my table before the second meal of the previous day. A pair of sandals, which should have been at the door of the junior observers' quarters. A reed-case, which should have been on its hook.

I knew what the first absence meant immediately. The papyrus belonged to Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, Third Watch observer, eleven months in our section, and constitutionally incapable of tardiness. He had never once been late. He was, if I am honest, somewhat irritating in his punctuality — a man of approximately thirty years, thin and scholarly, with an unfortunate habit of correcting his seniors' pronunciation of astronomical terminology, and a genuinely diligent mind that cared about the work the way the work deserves to be cared about. Not late. Never late.

The second absence — the sandals — I noted without yet understanding what I was noting. The reed-case likewise.

I went to find Djehuty-mes.

He was at the roof platform, lying on his back on the painted observation deck with his eyes open and a small clay tablet pressed under his forearm, watching the morning sky as though reading a text. This was not unusual. It was his version of what other men accomplished with wine or with sleep — the long, vacant contemplation that I had learned, after two years of working alongside him, to disturb only at genuine cost.

I disturbed it.

"Amenhotep-sa-Hapu's entries are absent," I said. "He himself is absent."

He said nothing for the length of time it took a kite to cross from the eastern pylon to the river. Then, without sitting up: "The Third Watch log for the third and fourth nights is also incomplete."

I had not yet checked the third night.

"You—" I began.

"I noticed it when I came up this morning. The alignment of Sopdet's rising does not accord with my own observation from the previous month, and the entry for the third night explains why — it was entered by someone other than Amenhotep-sa-Hapu." He sat up then, brushing limestone dust from his kilt in the deliberate way of a man who is thinking about something else entirely. "The reed-stroke is different. The determinatives for transit and conjunction are formed in the manner of Kha-nefer, who covers the watch when the regular observers are ill." He stood, and the sudden conversion of stillness into motion that always slightly startled me occurred. "Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was therefore absent from his watch on the night of the third day. Today is the fourth. He has been missing, if that is what this is, for at least two nights."

"If that is what this is," I said. "What else might it be?"

He descended the roof stair without answering. I followed, as I generally found myself following, with the specific feeling of a man who has begun walking a road before noticing that it has no obvious destination.

He was perhaps forty years old, angular in the way of men whose bodies have decided that flesh is largely unnecessary, with eyes of that particular stillness that made some of the newer scribes nervous — not unkind eyes, but eyes that were perpetually doing something other than simply looking at you. His shaved head was customary for the House of Life. The clay tablet under his forearm was less so: he worked partial astronomical calculations on it in no obvious order, erasing and rewriting continuously, in the manner of a man who could not bear to leave a surface empty. I had once asked him what calculations they were. He had looked at me with mild surprise, as though the question had not previously occurred to him as a question anyone might ask, and said: All of them.

That was Djehuty-mes.

The office of Chief Astronomer Nebwenenef occupied the ground floor of the eastern wing, adjacent to the storage rooms for the festival calendars. Nebwenenef himself was a man of approximately sixty, broad in the chest and face in the way of men who had been powerful when young and had merely thickened rather than diminished with age. He wore the leopard-skin of a sem-priest over his white linen even in his administrative functions. This was, I had always thought, a statement of priority rather than comfort. He wished you to know what he was before you knew anything else.

He received Djehuty-mes with the particular cordiality that officials offer to people they consider useful and slightly alarming.

"Amenhotep-sa-Hapu," he said, when Djehuty-mes named the missing man. "The young observer. Diligent. Perhaps excessively enthusiastic about his opinions."

"He had expressed opinions?" Djehuty-mes's voice was entirely neutral.

Nebwenenef arranged the pens on his writing board with the deliberateness of a man choosing each word. "He raised a concern. About the festival calendar entries. The positioning of the Opening of the Year festival relative to the heliacal rising of Sopdet." A pause. "The concern was noted and addressed."

"By whom?"

"By myself."

"And Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was satisfied with that address?"

The pause this time was longer. "He was respectful."

Djehuty-mes nodded slowly, as though this answered a different question than the one he had asked. "I will need access to the festival calendar records for the past three years," he said, "and the provincial correspondence archive — specifically any dispatches from Khemenu and Abydos in the last four months."

Something moved in Nebwenenef's expression then — not alarm, precisely, but its immediate predecessor. "The provincial correspondence is not within the mandate of the astronomical section."

"No," Djehuty-mes agreed pleasantly. "But a discrepancy in our observation logs may relate to the provincial records, and I cannot determine whether our records are accurate until I have compared them. I am a cataloguer. I catalogue." He spread his hands in a gesture of transparent innocence. "If there is no problem, the comparison will confirm it."

Nebwenenef held his gaze for a long moment. Then he called for the archive boy.

In the corridor afterwards, before we descended to the archive room, I said quietly: "He knows you are not simply a cataloguer."

"Yes," said Djehuty-mes. "That is rather the point."

I have sat in many rooms alongside Djehuty-mes while he worked, and I have learned that there are different qualities of his silence. There is the idle silence of a man at rest. There is the concentrated silence of a man reading. And there is a third kind — rarer, and notably uncomfortable for anyone in the vicinity — which is the silence of a man who has found something that does not fit, and is rebuilding the arrangement of facts around it, and will not speak until the new arrangement is complete.

He was silent in that third way for the remainder of the morning.

The archive room smelled of dust, dried ink, and the faint sweetness of the cedar chests. I occupied myself with copy work and watched him when I thought he was not aware of it. The provincial dispatches were spread before him in careful rows. The festival calendar lay open to his left. The running observation log from the past eight months was to his right. He did not eat the bread I brought from the stores at midday. The kyphi-and-castor smell of the oil lamp — the House of Life burned something lower and smokier in those weeks than the clean winter flame, as though even the fire had absorbed the lethargy of the inundation season — pressed down on everything like warm water.

I offered what I considered a reasonable reconstruction. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was young and had embarrassed a senior priest with his calculations. Embarrassed men found ways to enforce embarrassment in return. He had perhaps decided that a position under Nebwenenef's displeasure was not worth keeping, and had gone to seek a post at one of the provincial temples.

"The sandals," Djehuty-mes said.

I had thought he was not listening.

"A man who chooses to leave takes his sandals. A man who is removed does not always have the opportunity." He turned a papyrus without looking up. "Though the distinction matters less than you might think. The sandals tell us the departure was rapid. They do not yet tell us why, or in which direction."

"Surely north or south along the river," I said. "There is nowhere else to go."

"There is the west," said Djehuty-mes.

He said it without drama. That absence of drama made the words considerably more unpleasant than drama would have.

He meant the desert, and beyond it the necropolis, and beyond that whatever lies beyond necropolises. I said nothing else until he was ready to speak.

He spoke in the late afternoon, when the light through the archive grille had turned the colour of cheap oil.

"Come and look at this."

The papyri were arranged before him with the precision of a man who has sorted objects into a pattern he wishes someone else to perceive without prompting. He leaned back and folded his arms and watched me look — rather in the manner of a sculptor who has unveiled something and is interested in whether you understand what you are seeing.

I looked. I saw the observation logs, the festival calendars, the provincial dispatches. Numbers, dates, the names of stars.

"The festival calendar," I said slowly. "It does not accord with the observation log."

"More precisely."

I studied the figures. "The Opening of the Year festival is dated in the calendar to the twenty-eighth day of the third month. But the observation log for the tenth month of last year places the heliacal rising of Sopdet at—" I calculated, aware of him watching me with the patient tolerance of a man who knows the answer and is waiting to see how long it takes his companion to find it, "—at the third day of the fourth month. A difference of five days."

"A difference of five days," he agreed. There was something in his voice — not satisfaction precisely, but the controlled acknowledgment of a man who has found the answer he suspected and does not enjoy being right. "And the dispatch from the sanctuary at Khemenu?"

I found it. Read it. "They also observed the rising on the third day of the fourth month."

"Abydos?"

"The fourth day."

"So." He unfolded his arms and leaned forward. "The festival calendar, maintained by the office of the Chief Astronomer, places the Opening of the Year five days earlier than the actual observed rising of Sopdet — as recorded in our own observation log, and confirmed by two independent provincial sanctuaries." He paused. "Now consider what that means for the calendar of ritual obligations over the past three years. The festival scheduling. The allocation of temple income to the associated celebrations. The offerings budget drawn against each of those festival dates."

I sat very still.

The income attached to a major festival was not inconsiderable. The offerings, the staff payments, the supplementary distributions to the affiliated priests — drawn five days before the observationally correct date, every year, for three years. The difference between what was spent and what should have been spent, against an artificially early date that no one was supposed to notice because the observation logs were maintained by the same office that maintained the calendar — was the kind of difference that, over three years, could sustain a very comfortable secondary existence for whoever controlled the accounting.

"Amenhotep-sa-Hapu noticed," I said.

"He noticed. He had the misfortune of being genuinely competent at work that required genuine competence. He cross-referenced the provincial records as part of his duties, found the discrepancy, raised it with Nebwenenef, and was told it had been addressed." Djehuty-mes's voice remained level — the voice of a man describing the properties of a geometric figure, though I noticed his hands had tightened on the edge of the table. "Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was the kind of man who corrected his seniors' pronunciation. He would not have let it rest. He went to the dispatches archive himself, obtained the Khemenu and Abydos observations, and wrote a formal notation in the observation log citing the discrepancy." He stood abruptly. "Two nights later, a man arrived at his quarters with a private message from the Chief Astronomer, and struck him across the face when he arrived at the canal path."

"Then he is—"

"He is in this compound," said Djehuty-mes. "Come."

I will say this for Djehuty-mes: he moved, when he finally moved, with a sureness that left no room for hesitation on the part of anyone in his vicinity. He went directly north through the compound toward the canal that fed the temple gardens, past the outer wall where an ancient sycamore had long ago disrupted the mudbrick and created a gap that had been imperfectly repaired and had over the years become imperfectly repaired again.

"He ran," said Djehuty-mes, examining the gap in the wall without touching it, "but could not get through. Too exposed. He went back into the compound."

The storage magazine at the north end held surplus materials — worn linen wrappings, broken furniture, extra weights for the balance scales. It was locked from the outside with a wooden bolt. Djehuty-mes examined the bolt, the frame, the ground before it. Then he slid the bolt and opened the door.

The smell was what struck me first — human occupation, stale water, fear-sweat. The darkness resolved slowly. A man sat in the corner behind a stack of old wooden carrying frames, knees drawn to his chest, blinking at the light with the expression of someone who has spent enough time in darkness to have lost confidence in his eyes.

He had a clay water jug, half empty. He had no food. He had a bruise along his left cheekbone that had gone yellow-green at the margins — a contusion perhaps three days old, the kind that results from a sharp blow rather than a fall, the shape consistent with a fist rather than an open hand. He was alive, and upon observing that it was Djehuty-mes and myself rather than anyone sent by Nebwenenef, he began to shake.

I went for bread and water. When I returned, Djehuty-mes was sitting cross-legged on the floor of the magazine — which for Djehuty-mes was a significant concession to human feeling — and Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was still shaking but no longer silent.

The account he gave was substantially what Djehuty-mes had already reconstructed. He had raised his concerns. He had been told they had been addressed. He had asked to see the correction. He had been told there was no written correction yet, but one was being prepared. He had — with what I can only describe as the magnificent and catastrophic stubbornness of a man who is entirely right and entirely unable to stop being entirely right — gone to the dispatches archive himself, obtained the independent observations from Khemenu and Abydos, and written a formal notation in the observation log.

Two nights later: the message, the canal path, the blow. He had managed to run. He had not managed to get further than the magazine before his legs failed him and darkness seemed the safest option.

"What notation," Djehuty-mes said, "did you write in the observation log?"

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu described it. The notation was precise, citation-complete, and entirely accurate. It was also, as Djehuty-mes already knew, still in the log — because removing it would require acknowledging that there was a reason to remove it.

"You understand," Djehuty-mes said quietly, "that the notation is irrevocable. It is in the archive. Cross-referenced now in my own hand to the dispatches from Khemenu and Abydos. Whatever happens next, the record exists."

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu said: "I wanted it to exist. That was the entire point."

Djehuty-mes looked at him for a long moment with an expression I had rarely seen on his face — not admiration precisely, but recognition. The look of a man who has encountered, in unexpected circumstances, something that functions the way he believes things ought to function.

"Yes," he said. "It was." He stood. "Eat the bread. Drink the water. We have one more call to make this evening, and after that we will discuss your future arrangements."

I confess I did not immediately understand what he intended. I understood when we arrived at the door of the Chief Astronomer's office and he walked through it without announcement.

Nebwenenef was at his desk in the lamplight, writing. He looked up. Something moved in his face — quickly controlled, but not quickly enough.

"Djehuty-mes," he said. "It is late for administrative matters."

"Yes," Djehuty-mes agreed. He settled into the visitor's chair with the easy authority of a man who does not require invitation. I stood. "I have spent the day in the provincial dispatches archive. I found the letter from the astronomer Khay at Khemenu, three months old, filed without response. He writes with considerable precision regarding the heliacal rising of Sopdet — specifically, that the festival calendar circulated from this institution places the Opening of the Year five days in advance of the astronomical event it is supposed to celebrate. He cites his own observations, and asks whether the discrepancy is an error or a revision he ought to incorporate." He paused. "I found his letter filed between two storage requisitions for the magazine supplies. An interesting place for provincial astronomical correspondence."

Nebwenenef said nothing. His face had settled into the stillness of a man who is deciding something.

"I have also found," Djehuty-mes continued, in the same unhurried tone, "Amenhotep-sa-Hapu."

The stillness in Nebwenenef's face cracked, very briefly, like a thin glaze over old clay. Then it sealed again. "The missing observer. I am glad to hear he is found. Where—"

"In the storage magazine at the north end of the compound, where he has been for two days without food, having been struck across the face by a man he did not recognise who appeared at his quarters bearing a private message attributed to this office." Djehuty-mes's voice remained entirely, unnervingly level. "He has a bruise consistent with a blow delivered approximately three days ago. Which is to say: on the night after he wrote his notation in the observation log."

Nebwenenef set down his pen. "A junior scribe whose claims about astronomical records—"

"Are confirmed in triplicate," said Djehuty-mes, "by this office's own logs, by the sanctuary at Khemenu, and by Abydos. The mathematical derivation of a five-day discrepancy across three years of festival scheduling — and the consequent irregularity in the offerings budgets drawn against artificially early festival dates — is a matter of elementary arithmetic. I have done the arithmetic. It is in my own notation in the catalogue, cross-referenced to the dispatches." He tilted his head very slightly. "I am not a priest of the Vizier's court. I am a cataloguer. But I catalogue with considerable thoroughness."

The silence stretched.

Nebwenenef was sixty years old, broad and powerful, a man of thirty years in this institution with relationships that reached through three senior priests to the temple revenue committee. He looked, in that moment, at Djehuty-mes — angular, shaved-headed, sitting in his visitor's chair with the clay tablet still tucked under his forearm as though he had not thought to set it down — and I watched him weigh what he was being offered.

"What is it," he said at last, "that you want?"

"Several things," said Djehuty-mes. "Amenhotep-sa-Hapu will receive a letter of introduction from this section, recommending him to the astronomer Khay at Khemenu. He will leave with the morning cargo-boats, and the catalogue record will note that his position was vacated by voluntary transfer. The man who struck him will not come near him again. These are not subject to discussion." He paused. "The notation in the observation log remains. The cross-references remain. The provincial dispatches, including Khay's letter, are now indexed in the archive under the astronomical section's catalogue — also not subject to discussion."

"And the inquiry?" Nebwenenef's voice was very controlled.

"I am not the Vizier's office," said Djehuty-mes. "I am not making a report tonight. What I am doing is telling you precisely what the archive now contains and precisely what cannot be removed from it without making the removal itself visible." He stood, unhurrying, and there was something in the rising that made Nebwenenef, despite himself, draw fractionally back. "The truth is in the record. Records persist. These are not comforting observations — they are statements of arithmetic." He picked up the clay tablet from the arm of the chair. "Good evening."

We walked back through the compound in the dark, between the outer wall and the first of the storage magazines, past the ancient sycamore with its disrupted roots. The canal smell was in the air — mineral and faintly cool, the Nile's exhalation already gathering in the south. Ibises moved in the reeds somewhere ahead of us, unseen but audible, with the self-important patience of their kind.

I said, after a while: "You have not destroyed him."

"No," said Djehuty-mes.

"The fraud continues. He is warned now. He will be more careful."

"He will be more careful," Djehuty-mes agreed, "and the archive is more complete than it was this morning, and Amenhotep-sa-Hapu will be at Khemenu watching Sopdet rise at the correct time and writing it down accurately. And Nebwenenef knows, with considerable precision, what the archive contains and what I know it contains." He was quiet for a moment. "Fear of a complete record is a more reliable governor of conduct than formal inquiry, in the short term. Formal inquiry in this institution, at this moment, would last eighteen months and conclude with a disciplined junior scribe and an old priest with thirty years of relationships intact. I preferred the arithmetic."

"He may still act against Amenhotep-sa-Hapu."

"He will not." The certainty in his voice was not the certainty of a man hoping — it was the certainty of a man who has calculated. "I have told him precisely what I know and precisely what exists in the archive. If anything further happens to Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, it happens against a background of documentation that was placed there by me and that I will make available. Nebwenenef has survived thirty years in this institution by understanding arithmetic." He paused. "He understands it now."

That night I watched Djehuty-mes write two documents.

The first was the letter of introduction for Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, addressed to the astronomer Khay at the Thoth-sanctuary of Khemenu. It was precise, commendatory, and complete. It cited the work in the observation logs, the independent cross-referencing of provincial data, the intellectual rigour of the notation. It did not mention bruises or storage magazines or men appearing at canal paths after dark. Letters of recommendation have no use for such things, and Amenhotep-sa-Hapu's abilities were in no way diminished by the fact that he had spent two days in a magazine with a half-empty water jug.

The second was his own entry in the catalogue record: Position of Junior Observer, Third Watch, vacated by transfer to the provincial sanctuary of Khemenu. Recommendation provided. Archive of observations and notations to be retained in the standard manner and indexed under the current section's catalogue.

He set down his reed and looked at the lamp.

"You are not satisfied," I said.

"I am rarely satisfied. That is not the relevant question."

"What is the relevant question?"

He considered this seriously, in the way he considered most things seriously. "Whether what was done was the best that could be done with the materials available. I believe it was. I do not believe it was enough." He paused. "Those are not the same thing."

I said: "The law has blunt edges."

"Very blunt edges," he agreed, "and the guilty are not always caught by them, and the guilty who are not caught continue to cause harm in the interval. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu deserved to see his work vindicated in his own lifetime, in public, before witnesses, with consequence for the man who had him struck." He looked at the sealed letter. "What he will have instead is Khemenu, and the work, and the knowledge that the notation stands. I find this inadequate and preferable to the alternative." He allowed himself something that was, by his standards, close to a rueful acknowledgment. "The problems change. The records do not. I have always preferred the side of this arrangement that does not change."

He turned down the lamp.

I saw Amenhotep-sa-Hapu to the river stairs in the grey hour before dawn. He had his reed-case. He had his sandals. He had the sealed letter in his satchel and the observation notation — still in the log, irrevocable, correctly dated — behind him in the archive.

"The truth is in the record," he said, when he stepped onto the boat, and I could not tell whether he was comforting me or himself or simply, in the manner of a man trained to observe, noting an observable fact.

The morning river took him north. The ibises along the bank stood in the reeds and watched him go with their usual expression of theological indifference.

I went back to the catalogue room and completed the entries.

So I, Hori, have set down this account in full, noting what was observed, what was deduced, and what was chosen — including the choice that left a guilty man in his office with a warning rather than a warrant, and the choice that placed a good observer on a cargo-boat north rather than at the centre of an eighteen-month inquiry into an old priest with excellent connections. Whether those were the right choices I still cannot say with entire confidence. I have decided that my inability to say so with confidence is itself a part of the honest record.

The formal records of the House of Life make no mention of any irregularity in this period. The notation made by Amenhotep-sa-Hapu remains in the observation log, correctly indexed, cross-referenced in my own hand to the provincial correspondence from Khemenu and Abydos, and cross-referenced again — as of the evening of that fourth day — to Djehuty-mes's own notation citing the arithmetic of the discrepancy. The Chief Astronomer Nebwenenef served in his office for a further seven years before retiring due to ill health. What the quiet pressure of a thoroughly documented archive accomplished in those seven years, or failed to accomplish, is a matter for other records than mine.

The man who walked into the west was not dead. He was in Khemenu, watching Sopdet rise at the correct time and writing it down accurately.

This, I think, was the better record to leave — though I am aware that better and sufficient are not, in the reckoning of honest men, the same word at all.
