DJEHUTY-MES

~1720 BC — Thebes, Egypt

Thoth is born — the name’s root the same that the Greeks would render as Thutmose, the name of pharaohs and priests, but its theological meaning precise: an invocation of the ibis-headed god of wisdom, of writing, of calculation and the moon, whose sacred function was the recording of all things and the adjudication of their truth. There was no more appropriate patron for a member of this lineage. Djehuty-mes served in the House of Life — the per ankh, the scribal library and research institution attached to the great temple complexes — where the accumulated knowledge of Egyptian civilisation was housed, copied, studied, and extended by the scholars and scribes who constituted the closest thing the ancient world had to a permanent institution of accumulated and transmissible knowledge.

He worked in the astronomical and mathematical section, applying to the problems of celestial observation and calendrical calculation the same analytical precision that his ancestors had brought to grain accounts and temple land records. He was slower than his father in arriving at conclusions, preferring to turn a problem over repeatedly before committing himself, and this caution defined both his strength and his limitation. Where Kha-em-waset had moved with decisive certainty, Djehuty-mes required confirmation from multiple sources before he was satisfied, and it was this habit that made him particularly suited to work where error would propagate through many hands. The recognition that the same gift served equally in commerce, in law, in administration, and in the observation of the natural world — that the capacity to find the true pattern in a set of data was a single transferable faculty rather than a collection of distinct skills — was the great intellectual inheritance of his family, and he understood it as such, though he did not speak of it in those terms. The problems change. The method does not.

Among those who worked alongside him was a junior scribe named Hori, whose task was to assist in copying the observation logs and maintaining the catalogues of celestial events. Hori served as both witness and counterweight, his interest in the work shaped less by abstraction than by concern for the temple’s stability and his own position within it. His record, written in a careful but slightly hurried hand, survives alongside Djehuty-mes’s own annotations and preserves not only what was observed, but how the conclusion was reached, and where doubt remained until the final moment of clarity.

The Matter Of Amenhotep-Sa-Hapu

Recorded by Hori, his assistant, in the seventeenth year of the reign of Merneferre, may he be granted life, stability, and dominion

The season of Akhet had begun heavy that year.

I say heavy, and I mean it as one means it when speaking of the air itself — thick with the Nile’s exhalation, with the mineral breath of the inundation already gathering in the south like a held thought. The lamp oil in the House of Life burned with a particular quality in those weeks: not the clean yellow flame of winter, but something lower, smokier, more insistent, as though even the flame had absorbed the lethargy of the season and could not be bothered to burn properly. The smell of it — black kyphi resin mixed with the cheaper castor oil the temple purchasing office had substituted this year without announcement — clung to every papyrus, every linen wrapping, every corner of the vaulted catalogue room where I spent my mornings transferring the previous night’s star-observations into the running record.

It was in this room, on the morning of the fourth day of the second month of Akhet, that I first became aware something was wrong with the observation logs.

The junior observer Amenhotep-sa-Hapu had not completed his entries.

This requires explanation for those unfamiliar with the rhythms of our section. The Third Watch observers work from the second hour of night until the fifth, recording the positions of the imperishable stars against the transit markers painted on the rooftop platform. Their logs are due in the catalogue room before the second meal of the following day. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu had never once been late in the eleven months he had served under our section. He was, if I am honest, somewhat irritating in his punctuality — a man of approximately thirty years, thin and with an unfortunate habit of correcting his seniors’ pronunciation of astronomical terminology, but genuinely diligent, genuinely clever, and genuinely interested in the work in a way that the work deserves to be interesting.

His papyrus was not on my table. His sandals were not at the door of the junior observers’ quarters. His reed-case was not on its hook.

I reported this to Djehuty-mes.

He was at the roof platform when I found him, lying on his back on the painted observation deck with his eyes open, watching the morning sky as though reading a text. This was not unusual. It was his version of what other men accomplished with wine or with sleep — the long, vacant contemplation that I had learned, after two years of working alongside him, to disturb only at genuine cost.

I disturbed it.

“Amenhotep-sa-Hapu’s entries are absent,” I said. “He himself is absent.”

Djehuty-mes said nothing for the length of the time it took a kite to cross from the eastern pylon to the river. Then he sat up.

He was perhaps forty years old, angular in the way of men whose bodies have decided that flesh is largely unnecessary, with eyes of that particular stillness that made some of the newer scribes nervous — not unkind eyes, but eyes that were perpetually doing something other than simply looking at you. His shaved head was customary for the House of Life; less customary was the small clay tablet he kept pressed under his forearm even when lying down, on which he had worked partial astronomical calculations in no obvious order, and which he erased and rewrote continuously in the manner of a man who could not bear to leave a surface empty.

“The Third Watch log for the third and fourth nights is also incomplete,” he said.

I had not yet checked the third night.

“You—” I began.

“I noticed it when I came up this morning. The alignment of Sopdet’s rising does not accord with my own observation from the previous month, and the entry for the third night explains why — it was entered by someone other than Amenhotep-sa-Hapu. The reed-stroke is different. The determinatives for ‘transit’ and ‘conjunction’ are formed in the manner of Kha-nefer, who covers the watch when the regular observers are ill.” He stood, brushing limestone dust from his kilt. “Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was therefore absent from his watch on the night of the third day. Today is the fourth. He has been missing, if that is what this is, for at least two nights.”

“If that is what this is,” I repeated. “What else might it be?”

He descended the roof stair without answering immediately. I followed, as I generally found myself following, with the specific feeling of a man who has begun walking a road before noticing that it has no obvious destination.

The office of Chief Astronomer Nebwenenef occupied the ground floor of the eastern wing, adjacent to the storage rooms for the festival calendars and the archival copies of the observation records stretching back forty years. Nebwenenef himself was a man of approximately sixty, broad in the chest and face in the way of men who had been powerful when young and had merely thickened rather than diminished with age. He wore the leopard-skin of a sem-priest over his white linen even in his administrative functions, which I had always thought was a statement of priority rather than comfort.

He received Djehuty-mes with the particular cordiality that officials offer to people they consider useful and slightly alarming.

“Amenhotep-sa-Hapu,” he said, when Djehuty-mes named the missing man. “Yes. The young observer. Diligent. Perhaps excessively enthusiastic about his opinions.”

“He had expressed opinions?” Djehuty-mes said. His voice was entirely neutral.

Nebwenenef arranged the pens on his writing board with the deliberateness of a man choosing each word. “He raised a concern. About the festival calendar entries. The positioning of the Opening of the Year festival relative to the heliacal rising of Sopdet.” He paused. “The concern was noted and addressed.”

“By whom was it addressed?”

“By myself.”

“And Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was satisfied with the address?”

The pause this time was longer. “He was respectful.”

Djehuty-mes nodded slowly, as though this answered a different question than the one he had asked. “I will need access to the festival calendar records for the past three years, and the provincial correspondence archive — specifically any dispatches from Khemenu and Abydos in the last four months.”

Nebwenenef’s expression shifted slightly — not alarm, but the early intimation of it. “The provincial correspondence is not within the mandate of the astronomical section.”

“No,” Djehuty-mes agreed. “But the discrepancy in our observation logs may relate to the provincial records, and I cannot determine whether our records are accurate until I have compared them. I am a cataloguer. I catalogue.” He spread his hands in a gesture of transparent innocence. “If there is no problem, the comparison will confirm it.”

Nebwenenef looked at him for a long moment, and then called for the archive boy.

I have worked with Djehuty-mes long enough to know that when he becomes very quiet and very still, something has found its way into the arrangement of facts that did not fit, and the arrangement is now being rebuilt around it. He sat in the archive room for the remainder of that morning with the provincial dispatches spread in careful rows before him, the festival calendar rolled open to his left, and the running observation log from the past eight months to his right. He did not speak. He did not eat the bread I brought from the stores at midday. The kyphi-and-castor smell of the oil lamp mixed with the older smell of the archive — dust, dried ink, the faint sweetness of the cedar chests — and the silence pressed down on everything like water.

I occupied myself with the copy work I had brought, and I watched him when I thought he was not aware I was watching him.

At one point I offered what I considered a reasonable reconstruction. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was young and had embarrassed a senior priest with his calculations. Embarrassed men found ways to enforce embarrassment in return. The young observer had perhaps decided that a position in the House of Life under Nebwenenef’s displeasure was not worth keeping, and had gone to seek a post at one of the provincial temples — Khemenu perhaps, or the Thoth-sanctuary at the oasis.

“The sandals,” Djehuty-mes said.

I had thought he was not listening.

“A man who chooses to leave,” he continued, still not looking up from the dispatches, “takes his sandals. A man who is removed does not always have the opportunity.” He turned a page. “Though the distinction matters less than you might think. The sandals tell us the departure was rapid. They do not yet tell us why, or in which direction.”

“Surely north or south along the river,” I said. “There is nowhere else to go.”

“There is the west,” said Djehuty-mes, and his voice was entirely without drama, which made the words considerably more unpleasant than drama would have.

He meant the desert, and beyond it the necropolis, and beyond it whatever lies beyond necropolises.

I said nothing else until he was ready to speak.

He spoke in the late afternoon, when the light through the archive grille had turned the color of the cheap oil in the lamps.

“Come and look at this.”

I came. The papyri were arranged before him with the care of a man who has sorted objects into a pattern he wants someone else to perceive without prompting. I looked. I saw the observation logs, the festival calendars, the provincial dispatches. I saw numbers and dates and the names of stars.

“Well?” he said.

“The records,” I said slowly. “They are—” I looked more closely. “The festival calendar does not accord with the observation log.”

“More precisely.”

“The Opening of the Year festival is dated in the calendar to the twenty-eighth day of the third month. But the observation log for the tenth month of last year places the heliacal rising of Sopdet at—” I calculated, slowly, aware of him watching me with the patient tolerance of a man who already knows the answer and is merely waiting for me to find my own way to it, “—at the third day of the fourth month. A difference of five days.”

“A difference of five days,” he agreed. “And what does the dispatch from the sanctuary at Khemenu say?”

I found the relevant document. Read it. Read it again. “They also observed the rising on the third day of the fourth month.”

“And Abydos?”

I found that one. “The fourth day.”

“So.” He leaned back. “The festival calendar, maintained by the office of the Chief Astronomer, places the Opening of the Year five days earlier than the actual observed rising of Sopdet as recorded in our own observation log, as confirmed by two independent provincial sanctuaries.” He paused. “Now consider what that means for the calendar of ritual obligations over the past three years, the festival scheduling, the allocation of temple income to the associated celebrations, and the offerings budget that was drawn against each of those festival dates.”

I sat very still.

The income attached to a major festival was not inconsiderable. The offerings, the staff payments, the supplementary distributions to the affiliated priests — drawn five days before the observationally correct date, every year, for three years. The difference between what was spent and what should have been spent — against an artificially early date that no one was supposed to notice because the observation logs were maintained by the same office that maintained the calendar — was the kind of difference that, over three years, could sustain a very comfortable secondary existence for whoever controlled the accounting.

“Amenhotep-sa-Hapu noticed,” I said.

“He noticed. He was a young man who had been working in this archive for eleven months, cross-referencing observation logs as part of his duties, and he had the misfortune of being genuinely competent at his work.” Djehuty-mes’s voice remained flat and even, the voice of a man describing the properties of a triangle. “He raised the concern with Nebwenenef. Nebwenenef told him the concern had been addressed. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was respectful.” He paused. “But he was also the kind of man who corrected his seniors’ pronunciation of astronomical terminology. He would not have let it rest.”

“So Nebwenenef—”

“Made certain he could not continue raising it.” Djehuty-mes stood abruptly, with the sudden conversion of stillness into motion that always slightly startled me. “We need to find him. Come.”

The House of Life’s compound extended to the north toward the canal that fed the temple gardens, running between the outer wall and the first of the storage magazines. It was not a part of the precinct that received much traffic after dark — the gardeners worked at dawn, the water-haulers at midmorning, and by the afternoon heat the canal path was empty except for the occasional ibis moving through the reeds with the self-important patience of its kind.

It was also, I discovered, walking behind Djehuty-mes as the sun moved toward the western cliffs, the place where the canal path passed under an ancient sycamore tree whose roots had long ago disrupted the mudbrick of the outer wall, creating a gap that had been imperfectly repaired and had over the years become imperfectly repaired again, and which someone of thin build and desperate motivation might conceivably have used as a temporary—

“There,” said Djehuty-mes.

The storage magazine at the north end of the compound was one of three that held surplus materials — extra weights for the balance scales, worn linen wrappings awaiting reuse, broken furniture from the scribal hall awaiting repair or disposal. It was locked with a wooden bolt from the outside. Djehuty-mes examined the bolt, examined the frame around it, examined the ground before it, and then slid the bolt and opened the door.

The smell was what I noticed first — human occupation, stale water, the particular quality of fear-sweat. The darkness resolved slowly. A man was sitting in the corner behind a stack of old wooden carrying frames, knees drawn to his chest, blinking at the light with the expression of a person who has spent enough time in darkness to have lost confidence in his eyes.

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu had been in the storage magazine for two nights and most of two days. He had a clay water jug, half empty. He had no food. He had a bruise along his left cheekbone that had gone yellow-green, which placed its infliction at approximately three days prior. He was alive, and upon observing that it was Djehuty-mes and myself rather than anyone sent by Nebwenenef, he began to shake.

The account he gave, once I had fetched bread and water and the shaking had subsided to something manageable, was substantially what Djehuty-mes had already reconstructed. He had raised his concerns about the calendar discrepancy. He had been told they had been addressed. He had asked to see the correction. He had been told there was no written correction as yet but one was being prepared. He had, with what I can only describe as the magnificent and catastrophic stubbornness of a man who is entirely right and entirely unable to stop being entirely right, gone to the provincial dispatches archive himself, obtained the independent observations from Khemenu and Abydos, and written a formal notation in the observation log citing the discrepancy.

Two nights later, a man he did not recognize had come to his quarters, informed him that Nebwenenef wished to speak with him at the canal path regarding a private administrative matter, and struck him across the face when he arrived.

He had managed to run. He had not managed to get further than the magazine before his legs failed him and the darkness seemed the safest available option.

He had not known what to do next. He had been there for two days making this decision without reaching it.

“What notation,” Djehuty-mes said, “did you write in the observation log?”

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu described it. The notation was precise, citation-complete, and entirely, irrefutably accurate. It was also, as Djehuty-mes already knew, still in the log — because removing it would require acknowledging that there was a reason to remove it.

Djehuty-mes stood for a moment with his hands clasped behind his back, looking at the storage magazine wall as though it were a papyrus requiring decipherment.

“What will you do?” I asked him. “This should go to the Vizier’s office. The calendar fraud alone—”

“The Vizier’s office,” said Djehuty-mes carefully, “will open an inquiry. The inquiry will require witnesses. The witnesses include a junior observer with a bruised face and no corroboration, and a catalogue scribe whose access to the provincial dispatches was granted at Nebwenenef’s own discretion and therefore subject to dispute. The inquiry will last months. Nebwenenef has functioned in this institution for thirty years. He has relationships with three other senior priests, two of whom sit on the temple revenue committee.” He paused. “Amenhotep-sa-Hapu will be at the centre of a process that may destroy Nebwenenef, and may equally destroy Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, and will certainly make both their lives extremely unpleasant for the duration.”

“But the fraud—”

“The notation is in the log,” he said. “It will remain in the log. In forty years, when the next Amenhotep-sa-Hapu in the next generation of this House opens the archive and compares the records, they will find it. The truth is in the record. Records persist.”

He turned to look at the young observer sitting in the corner of the magazine.

“The sanctuary at Khemenu has an astronomical section,” he said. “Their senior observer, Khay, is a man of considerable ability and independent disposition. He is not affiliated with Nebwenenef’s network. He has corresponded with this House of Life regarding the same calendar discrepancy — I found his letter this morning among the unanswered provincial correspondence, three months old, filed without response.” He paused. “A letter of introduction from this section, citing your notations and your independent verification, would be a substantial recommendation. Khemenu is eight days north by river.”

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu stared at him.

“You are suggesting,” the young man said slowly, “that I leave.”

“I am observing,” said Djehuty-mes, “that a man who chooses to accept a position in a provincial sanctuary is not a missing person. He is a man who has changed his posting. The log notation remains. The record is complete. The investigation it implies will unfold in time, or it will unfold in forty years, or it will unfold when Nebwenenef makes one error too many. These things find their level eventually.” He looked down at him steadily. “The question is what serves the work. You are a competent observer. Khemenu needs competent observers. The observation of the stars continues regardless of who administers the calendar fraud.”

Silence in the magazine. The smell of old linen and stale water. Somewhere outside, an ibis made its sound.

“I will write the letter this evening,” Djehuty-mes said. “You will leave with the morning cargo-boats. I will note in the catalogue record that your position has been vacated by voluntary transfer.”

That night, I watched Djehuty-mes write two documents.

The first was the letter of introduction for Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, addressed to the astronomer Khay at the Thoth-sanctuary of Khemenu. It was precise, commendatory, and complete. It cited the work in the observation logs, the independent cross-referencing of provincial data, the intellectual rigor of the notation. It did not mention bruises or storage magazines or men appearing at canal paths after dark.

The second was his own entry in the catalogue record: Position of Junior Observer, Third Watch, vacated by transfer to the provincial sanctuary of Khemenu. Recommendation provided. Archive of observations and notations to be retained in the standard manner and indexed under the current section’s catalogue.

He set down his pen and looked at the lamp for a while.

“You are not satisfied,” I said.

“I am rarely satisfied,” he said. “That is not the relevant question.”

“You could have gone to the Vizier.”

“I considered it.” He was quiet for a moment. “The truth is in the record. The notation stands. What I am not willing to provide is a young observer as the instrument through which an old priest destroys himself — or fails to, which is the more likely outcome in the short term. Justice administered through the proper channels in this institution, at this moment, will produce eighteen months of inquiry and a disciplined junior scribe. Justice administered by patience and record-keeping will produce, in time, the same consequence, without the cost.” He paused. “And Amenhotep-sa-Hapu will be at Khemenu, doing work that matters.”

“Nebwenenef will continue.”

“For now,” said Djehuty-mes. “He has falsified records in three years of archived correspondence. In forty years, those records will still be there. My annotation cross-referencing the provincial logs is now also in the archive.” He allowed himself something that was, by his standards, close to satisfaction. “The problems change. The records do not.”

He sealed the letter. He turned down the lamp.

I record this as I observed it. I record also that I did not entirely agree, and do not entirely agree now, with what he chose to do. The law has blunt edges and the guilty are not always caught by it, and the guilty who are not caught continue to cause harm in the interval before the records find their level. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu deserved to see his work vindicated in his own lifetime. The junior scribes who came after him would be copying falsified festival calendars for years, perhaps decades, because one old priest had an arrangement no one could prove and a junior observer chose a cargo-boat north over a bruising inquiry.

But Amenhotep-sa-Hapu himself, when I saw him to the river stairs in the grey hour before dawn, did not seem to think he had been failed. He had his reed-case. He had his sandals. He had the sealed letter in his satchel and the observation notation — still in the log, irrevocable, correctly dated — behind him in the archive.

“The truth is in the record,” he said, when he stepped onto the boat, and I could not tell whether he was comforting me or himself or simply noting an observable fact.

The morning river took him north. The ibises along the bank stood in the reeds and watched him go with their usual expression of theological indifference.

I went back to the catalogue room and completed the entries.

So I, Hori, have set down this account in full, noting what was observed, what was deduced, and what was chosen. The formal records of the House of Life make no mention of any irregularity in this period. The notation made by Amenhotep-sa-Hapu remains in the observation log, correctly indexed, cross-referenced in my own hand to the provincial correspondence from Khemenu and Abydos. The Chief Astronomer Nebwenenef served in his office for a further seven years before retiring due to ill health. What the inquiry of those years found, or failed to find, is a matter for other records than mine.

The man who walked into the west was not dead. He was in Khemenu, watching Sopdet rise at the correct time and writing it down accurately.

This, I think, was the better record to leave.
