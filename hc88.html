HOLMAR OF JUTLAND

~640 AD — Jutland, Denmark

The Frisian trading routes extended north into the Danish peninsula, and Holmar of Jutland followed them — or was sent along them, as a young factor establishing the family’s commercial connections in the markets of the north. He settled in Jutland, married a Danish woman of good family, and spent his adult life as a merchant of the middle rank: prosperous enough to be comfortable, obscure enough to be safe, well-connected enough to be informed.

He was a collector of information in the way that other men collected silver or land — systematically, with an appreciation for quality, and with a clear sense of what a given piece of knowledge was worth and to whom. The Danish chieftains of his acquaintance learned that Holmar knew things — about the movements of rival jarls, the reliability of trading partners, the temper of distant markets — and that what he knew was accurate. He became, in time, a man whose counsel was sought on matters well beyond the price of a shipment of amber, and who gave that counsel carefully, understanding that the value of a reputation for accuracy is destroyed the moment it is compromised.

Among those who recorded his work was Ketil, a younger man in the service of the jarl’s lawman, tasked with keeping written accounts of disputes brought before the assembly. Ketil’s records were kept on treated parchment, folded and stored in a wooden chest lined with waxed cloth to protect them from damp. He was not always certain what mattered most in what he witnessed, and his account reflects both his attention and his uncertainty. It is through his notes that the following matter is preserved.

The Account of Ketil

Holmar had not yet opened his shutters when Sigurd Factor came to his door.

He heard the man before he saw him. Sigurd's sandals were new, the leather still stiff, and they struck the wooden step outside with a sharp, anxious rhythm — not the patient knock of a man with business to conduct, but the rapid, uneven beat of a man who had been rehearsing his words since before dawn and was no longer certain any of them were right.

Holmar was at his table. He had been sorting a collection of small clay tablets that had been handed down as family heirlooms — old things, badly fired, with marks he was attempting to identify as either a regional variant of a common accounting notation or something else entirely. The work was delicate and he was not especially glad of the interruption, but he rose, set the tablet he was holding face-down, and went to the door.

Sigurd was a broad man, well-made, with the kind of face that inspired confidence in creditors — square jaw, clear grey eyes, skin weathered to an honest brown. He was perhaps forty years old, and he had served Jarl Hrothmund's household as factor for nearly fifteen of them. Holmar had done business with him on six separate occasions and found him careful, reliable, and almost entirely without imagination, which in a factor is not a defect.

This morning his face was wrong. The jaw was set too hard, and the grey eyes had the particular flatness of a man who has not slept.

"Come in," Holmar said, stepping aside.

Sigurd came in. He stood in the middle of the room and did not sit, though there were two stools available. He held his cloak closed with one hand even though the morning, while cool, was not cold. The cloak was holding something, or concealing it — the pressure of his hand was too deliberate to be unconscious.

Holmar returned to his table and sat. He poured two cups of the thin barley drink that his wife Astrid kept in a clay jar near the window, and placed one within Sigurd's reach. Then he waited.

Sigurd's eyes moved to the cup. Then back to Holmar. Then to the cup again, as though the cup were a question he hadn't expected and wasn't prepared to answer.

"I have received a letter," Sigurd said finally.

The letter had arrived four days prior, carried by a boy whom Sigurd had never seen before and who, when questioned, proved to have received it from another boy, who had received it from a woman at the market, who was not found when sought. The letter itself was written in a competent hand on a small piece of good parchment, folded twice and sealed with plain wax bearing no mark.

It accused Sigurd of selling information during a winter shortage to the agents of a rival merchant, Edvar Broadhand, enabling the manipulation of supply. It named a price for silence and a place for payment.

Holmar listened without interruption. When Sigurd had finished, he asked only: "Was the account accurate?"

"Substantially," Sigurd said.

Holmar examined the letter — the parchment, the ink, the folds — and began to ask questions not about guilt, but about knowledge. Who knew. Who had seen. Who could remember.

Over the next three days, he walked the quay, spoke in alehouses, visited counting houses, and asked one question in the correct place. From parchment, habit, and memory, he identified the writer: a young clerk named Ferold, recently come from the southern markets.

He confronted the clerk directly. Not with accusation, but with reconstruction — laying out the path by which the letter had come to exist, and the calculation behind it. Ferold admitted it. He had intended to extract payment and leave the region. He had seen enough of Edvar’s operations to understand that he did not wish to remain entangled in them.

But in speaking, he revealed more: that Sigurd’s arrangement had not been singular. It had been continuing. And that Edvar’s network operated beneath ordinary trade, in ways not easily named.

Holmar returned to Sigurd with this understanding. The letter was no longer the matter. It was a symptom.

Sigurd confessed fully. The arrangement had persisted across seasons. Information had flowed outward; influence had flowed inward.

Holmar considered this, not in terms of law, but in terms of weight.

He resolved three things. The clerk would be paid modestly and sent south, removed from the web he had entered. The letter would vanish with him. Sigurd’s arrangement would end gradually, without disruption that might provoke attention.

And the truth — not the letter, but the larger truth — would be written down.

Holmar composed an account and delivered it to Ketil without attribution. It was placed in the wax-lined chest, indexed only by season and year.

Sigurd continued in his role, altered. Known, in a way he had not been known before. Managed.

Whether this was justice or utility, Ketil does not say. He records only that the scale was balanced in such a way that nothing fell — and nothing was fully resolved.
