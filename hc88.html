HOLMAR OF FRISIA

~610 AD — Frisia, North Sea coast

The Frisian traders of the seventh century were the arteries of the northern world — their ships moving between the Rhine delta and the Thames, between the Seine and the Humber, carrying wool and wine and slaves and pottery and the thousand other commodities that a fragmented post-Roman Europe still needed to move between its scattered centres of production and consumption. Holmar of Frisia was born into this world and mastered it with the thoroughness of someone who understood that trade, at its root, is a problem of information — who has what, who needs it, at what price, and whether the man offering to broker the transaction can be trusted.

He could not always be trusted, in the way that all successful merchants cannot entirely be trusted, but he was consistently more honest than his competitors, which amounted to the same thing commercially and was considerably more comfortable to live with. He assessed cargoes with an accuracy that became locally legendary — running his hands over bales of wool in the dim holds of Frisian trading vessels and pronouncing on their quality, origin, and probable value with a confidence that was almost never wrong, and that he could not, when pressed, entirely explain. He simply knew. The family had always simply known.

Among those who kept account of his work was Eadric, a clerk in the service of the port-reeve at Dorestad. Eadric wrote in a careful hand on wax tablets before transferring his notes to parchment when the matter seemed worth preserving. His account of Holmar was not written as praise, nor as suspicion, but as record, though his own interpretations and hesitations are visible in the way he chose to arrange what he saw. What follows is preserved from his surviving tablets and fragments, which came into Holmar’s possession late in life and were copied into the family record.

The Death of Sigvard Hammerhand

The morning the body was found, the mist had not yet left the river.

It lay on the Waal as it always lay in autumn — not the clean sea-fog that rolls in from the west with salt on its tongue, but the inland kind, which has no taste at all and smells of reed-rot and cold mud and the particular damp of timber that has been wet too many times to ever again be truly dry. The trading quarter of Dorestad was still dark inside it. The post-Roman quays — stone footings the Franks had inherited and never quite maintained — emerged from the whiteness at a distance of perhaps fifteen paces, and the hulls of the vessels tied there appeared before their masts, which remained invisible above.

I was at my tablets when the boy came.

He was one of the dock-workers’ sons who served as message-runners for the port-reeve’s office, and he arrived breathless and with river-mud on his legs to his knees, having apparently waded some portion of the distance in his haste. He said that Sigvard Hammerhand, master of the Gull-Wing, had been found dead in the hold of his own vessel by his crew when they went to stow provisions for the morning tide. He said the crew were waiting, and that Radulf, the port-reeve’s officer for the eastern quays, had already gone down.

I took my tablets and my stylus and followed.

The Gull-Wing was a trading vessel of the middle sort — not one of the great cargo ships that ran the Frisian route to the Thames, but substantial enough, with a hold that could take twenty or thirty bales and still leave room for a man to stand and work. She was moored at the third post along the eastern quay, and she smelled, as all the wool-ships smell, of lanolin and salt and the particular mineral tang of fleece that has crossed the North Sea more than once in its existence.

Radulf was already aboard. He is a careful man, Radulf, in the way that careful men are sometimes also slow men, and he was standing at the hatch to the hold with his arms crossed when I arrived, wearing the expression he wears when he has encountered something he does not know how to write down.

The crew stood apart on the quay — four men, Frisian all of them, saying nothing to each other and not quite looking at the ship. One was very young. He had his back to the water.

“Eadric,” said Radulf, when I came aboard. “Write this down.”

Sigvard Hammerhand lay on his back among the wool bales, in the narrow working space between the cargo and the hull. There was a bruise at his temple, dark and already purpling, and the angle of his neck said what the bruise confirmed.

The wool bales were stacked as I remembered them from the previous day, when Holmar had assessed them. Fourteen bales, each sealed with the merchant’s cord and Holmar’s distinctive knot.

I wrote down what I saw. Radulf spoke uncertainty. I said: “Send for Holmar.”

He arrived before the mist had fully lifted.

He descended into the hold, looked at the body, then at the bales — and something in his expression sharpened.

He began to work.

After examining several bales, he paused. “This one,” he said. Then later: “And this one.”

He explained: wool that has travelled compresses; wool that has been opened and restuffed does not. Two bales had been disturbed.

He opened them.

From the second, he drew out a wrapped package.

Inside was silver. Beneath it, a sealed letter.

The letter concerned a concealed debt and an unlawful shipment. It bore the mark of Osbert the Weigher — an official with access to every cargo on the quay.

Holmar reasoned it plainly: only such a man could tamper with sealed bales unnoticed. Sigvard had discovered the hidden cargo. He had sought explanation. He was silenced.

We went to Osbert.

Holmar laid out the facts: the tampered bales, the concealed silver, the letter, the method. The reasoning was sufficient. Osbert was taken.

The courts resolved the matter as unlawful trade and killing. The official record credited Radulf. This account preserves what occurred.

Later, I asked Holmar how he had known which bales to examine.

He said: “The smell.”

Fourteen bales should smell the same. Two did not. They smelled of open air. That was enough to begin.

“The family has always simply known,” he said. “I prefer to use it.”
