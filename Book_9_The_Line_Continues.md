# BOOK IX: THE LINE CONTINUES
## *1893 – 2021*
### London · Boston · United States

---

*A note on the record:*

*The chronicler who has followed this line from its beginning — from the scribe of Ur who first bent his gift toward the hidden discrepancy in the grain tally — arrives now at the period closest to his own time, and finds the record both more abundant and more difficult than any that preceded it. More abundant because the modern world documents everything; more difficult because the modern world also obscures everything, and the members of this line, from the second Sherlock onward, appear to have understood this and acted accordingly. What follows is assembled from private memoranda, legal archives, a handful of unpublished intelligence summaries, and one annotated copy of a family document that passed through several hands before reaching this account. Where the record is thin, the chronicle says so. Where it is absent entirely, the chronicle says that too.*

*The organisation that the great detective knew as Moriarty's empire did not die with its Napoleon. It had not died with any of its previous commanders, across the four thousand years this chronicle spans. It reorganised. It found new instruments, new languages, new legitimate fronts behind which the same essential structure — hierarchical, durable, insulated from prosecution by layers of apparent respectability — continued its operations. The Whitechapel counting house that appears in the records of the second Sherlock's time bore a different name in each subsequent generation. The address did not change. Addresses, unlike names, are harder to fabricate from nothing.*

### SHERLOCK HOLMES II
**born 1893 — London, England**

He was born in the autumn of the Great Hiatus, in the months when London mourned a detective who was not, in fact, dead — which meant that the boy's father was alive somewhere in the world, and entirely unavailable, and known to the public only as a man who had gone over a waterfall in Switzerland with the most dangerous criminal mind in Europe. His mother, Miss Falkland, was a woman of considerable intelligence and deliberate social invisibility, who had managed, in the years preceding the birth and in the years following it, to keep her son's parentage a matter of inference rather than record. She raised him in Holborn, in respectable obscurity, among books and the careful habits of a woman who understood that knowledge, properly ordered, was the only reliable protection a person of limited means could acquire. The boy grew up knowing, in the way that such things are known — not told but observed, deduced, confirmed by the particular quality of his own mind when set against the minds of those around him — exactly who his father was.

Watson encountered him once, in the autumn of 1903, when Holmes II was ten years old and already possessed of that quality of watchful attention that the old doctor, who had spent twenty years in its company, could not mistake. He noted, in a private memorandum that survived among his papers, that the boy's eyes were his father's exactly — and that when introduced to a stranger, he had spent the first thirty seconds in a silence that was not shyness but assessment. Watson did not record what conclusion the boy had reached. He recorded that the stranger, a solicitor of his acquaintance, had been visibly unsettled by it.

Holmes II established himself in the first years of the new century as an inquiry agent of quiet distinction in the Holborn district, operating under his own name without publicly connecting it to the Baker Street legend — an omission that suited him perfectly, since the legend invited expectations he had no interest in managing, and the work, in his experience, went better when the subject of an inquiry did not know they were being observed. He was not his father; the flamboyant reconstruction, the dramatic disclosure, the audience arranged for maximum effect — these were not his methods. He was quieter, more patient, content to allow the truth to arrive at its own pace so long as it arrived. His clients were merchants, solicitors, insurance companies, and occasionally individuals who had found themselves in circumstances they could not account for and did not know where else to turn.

It was the insurance work that brought him, in the spring of 1912, to the case that would occupy the next two years of his professional life and confirm, beyond any further personal doubt, that the organisation his father had faced at Reichenbach had not ended there.

The matter began with a routine referral from a marine underwriter at Lloyd's — a firm of Holborn shipping factors, T. Hargreave & Sons, had filed three substantial claims in eighteen months for cargo lost in transit between the Port of London and Rotterdam. The losses were well documented, the manifests in order, the captain's depositions consistent and detailed. The underwriter had paid the first claim without suspicion, made enquiries before paying the second, and declined the third pending investigation. The enquiry that followed was, on its face, unremarkable: goods did vanish in transit, captains were not always honest, port officials were occasionally compromised. Holmes II was asked to review the documentation and offer an opinion.

He began, as he always began, not with the claims themselves but with the pattern of the claims. The first loss had occurred on a Tuesday in March; the second on a Thursday in October; the third on a Tuesday in February. He noted this without yet attaching significance to it. He noted also that all three vessels had been loaded at the same Thames-side wharf, by the same gang of stevedores, under the supervision of the same wharfinger — a man named Crale, twenty-two years in the trade, unremarkable record, lodgings in Bermondsey. He noted that the manifest for each lost cargo had been countersigned, at the factor's office, by the same clerk. He noted that the clerk had resigned his position three weeks after the third claim was filed, giving no forwarding address.

The resigned clerk was, ordinarily, where the matter would have ended — a dishonest employee, a crooked wharfinger, a straightforward fraud. But Holmes II was his father's son, and his father's son had been left, among his mother's papers, a single document: a letter, written in 1895 and never sent, in which the original Sherlock Holmes had set down, for his own reference, the structural characteristics of the Moriarty organisation as he had observed them over three years of close study. Among those characteristics was this: the organisation never operated through a single corruptible employee. It always operated through a corruptible employee, a legitimate commercial front, and a directing intelligence at a third remove from the actual fraud — insulated by two layers of apparent innocence from any consequence that might follow discovery. The clerk was layer one. Crale the wharfinger, Holmes II suspected, was layer two. Layer three was elsewhere.

He found it by following the money, which led not to the clerk's lodgings in Bermondsey but to a bill-discounting house in a court off Bishopsgate, which led in turn to a counting house in Whitechapel, the senior partner of which was a man named Gruner — not the Baron Gruner his father had dealt with in 1902, but a cousin, it emerged, a man of careful commercial reputation and no criminal record, whose name did not appear in any public connection with the Hargreave frauds or with any of the eleven other insurance claims that Holmes II identified, over the following eight months, as sharing the same structural signature. The Whitechapel address, when checked against the records his mother had kept of his father's papers, was the same counting house that had appeared in the background of two Moriarty-adjacent cases from the 1880s — different names both times, same address.

He brought his analysis to the underwriter at Lloyd's, who brought it to a King's Counsel, who brought it before a special examiner appointed by the Board of Trade. Holmes II did not appear at the examination. His name does not appear in any of the proceedings. Gruner's firm was wound up in December 1913; Gruner himself left for the Continent and did not return; Crale the wharfinger was convicted of fraud at the Old Bailey in February 1914 and sentenced to seven years. The network, as always, contracted but did not cease. The address in Whitechapel stood empty for six months, then acquired new tenants and a new name.

He lived quietly, worked methodically, and died in 1946, having seen the world he had been born into become something he could not entirely have imagined. He never sought public acknowledgement of his parentage. That was, one imagines, exactly as he intended.

### SHERLOCK HOLMES III
**born 1914 — Sussex, England**

He was born in the year his grandfather came out of Sussex retirement to break a German intelligence network — which meant that his first year of life coincided with the outbreak of a war that would kill a generation, and that his childhood was shaped by the long aftermath of that killing: the silence in households where the men had not come back, the particular English habit of not speaking about things that had happened, the sense, pervasive in the twenties and thirties, that something fundamental had been broken and not yet repaired. He grew up in Holborn, in his father's household, a spare and watchful boy who read everything and spoke selectively, and who demonstrated the family gift early enough that his father, recognising it, did the thing his own father had apparently not done with him: he explained it. He told the boy what it was, where it came from, what it was for, and what it had cost the men who had carried it before them. It was, by all accounts, an unusual education.

He did not attend university. He was recruited instead, in the spring of 1936, by a department of the Foreign Office whose precise functions were not described in his letter of appointment, which was brief and which he was asked to destroy. He spent the next three years learning languages — German, principally, and enough French and Italian to be useful — and the related disciplines of close observation, documentary analysis, and the careful management of what other people believed about you. He was good at all of these. He was particularly good at the last.

The wartime record, such as it is accessible, suggests that he operated primarily in the counter-intelligence capacity — the identification and neutralisation of German agents and networks operating within Britain — from 1940 onward, working in coordination with the Intelligence Inner Council and, on specific occasions, with the British Security Coordination in New York and Washington. His companion through much of this period was Dr. James Watson, grandson of the original, a physician of the Royal Army Medical Corps who had been seconded to intelligence duties in 1941 and who possessed, in addition to his grandfather's loyal practicality, a fund of medical knowledge that proved unexpectedly useful in the assessment of what a man's physical condition could disclose about where he had recently been. The partnership attracted, in certain circles, precisely the kind of half-suppressed recognition that Holmes III would have preferred to avoid: people who encountered them noted that the tall, spare, hawk-featured consulting detective and his somewhat rotund, affable doctor companion presented a silhouette that the popular imagination had been trained, by forty years of Watson's chronicles, to associate with Baker Street. Holmes III dealt with this by the simple method of never addressing it. The Intelligence Inner Council, for their part, found the association useful; it gave their consulting asset a quality of legendary invulnerability that an unknown operative would have lacked.

The case that this chronicle selects from among those years — not for its dramatic surface but for what lay beneath it, and for the thread it contributed to the long record — concerned not a German intelligence operation but something older, and in some respects more dangerous.

It began in the winter of 1942, when a set of intercepted communications between a London-registered import firm and a banking correspondent in Lisbon revealed a pattern that Holmes III recognised as anomalous in a specific way: the transactions were not moving money toward Germany, as a conventional enemy financial operation would have done, but were moving it into a neutral account structure in Switzerland — accessible, in principle, to either side, or to neither. The firm, Meridian Trade and Finance, occupied an office in the City; its registered address for correspondence was a counting house in Whitechapel. Holmes III noted the Whitechapel address in his file, remembered where he had seen it before, and began to attend to the matter with a focus that went beyond its immediate intelligence value.

What he found, over the following four months, was a financial structure of considerable sophistication: a network of nominally independent firms in London, Lisbon, and Geneva, connected by overlapping directorships and shared banking correspondents, that had been in operation — under varying names, with varying personnel, but with a consistent underlying architecture — since at least the early years of the century. The London anchor was the Whitechapel counting house. The Swiss account structure was administered by a Geneva fiduciary whose client list was, officially, confidential, but whose correspondence with Lisbon disclosed at least three clients with addresses traceable, by Holmes III's meticulous cross-referencing of commercial registers, back to directorship structures that appeared in his father's files on the Hargreave fraud of 1912.

The organisation was not working for Germany. It was not working for Britain. It was working for itself — sustaining the war by ensuring that both sides continued to have access to certain categories of strategic materiel, routed through neutral intermediaries, at prices that reflected the operational costs of a network that had been maintaining this kind of service, across conflicts and across generations, for longer than anyone currently directing it could personally remember. Holmes III spent a weekend with his father's files, read his grandfather's 1895 letter, and sat for some time in silence.

In March 1943 he committed to paper what he had spent four months assembling. The memorandum ran to forty pages. Its first thirty-eight described, with the precision the Council required, the network's current structure, personnel, and mechanism — the nominee directorships, the Lisbon correspondent, the Geneva fiduciary, the pricing arrangements that kept both sides supplied through neutral channels. The final two pages he marked sealed, addressed to no named recipient, and clipped separately. They contained one thing the Council had not asked for and would not have known to ask for: a structural diagram tracing the organisation's architecture backward through its previous incarnations — the 1912 Hargreave fraud, the 1890s Whitechapel counting house, the dissolved City firms of the 1870s — with a note, written in his own hand, that the Whitechapel address had not changed in sixty years and that the Geneva fiduciary's founding partner had trained in the same Chancery chambers as the London solicitor named in his grandfather's 1895 letter. He left those two pages among his private papers rather than with the Council's copy. They were, he had concluded, not intelligence. They were inheritance. The Meridian firm was wound up; its directors were interned under Defence Regulation 18B; the Swiss account structure was frozen through diplomatic channels. Holmes III's name did not appear in any of the proceedings.

He was in Washington six weeks later, on a separate matter connected to a stolen document and the protection of an allied intelligence asset — a case that the record, characteristically, does not detail — when he met Eleanor Marsh of Boston, Massachusetts, at a reception at the British Embassy. Eleanor Marsh was a woman of intelligence, directness, and an immunity to oblique conversational management that Holmes III found, after a professional lifetime of dealing with people who concealed things, entirely refreshing. She told her grandchildren, many years later, that she had known within ten minutes of meeting him that he intended to marry her, and had found this less alarming than it perhaps ought to have been, because it was, she had concluded by the end of the evening, the correct outcome.

They married in London in June 1944. After the war he followed her home to America, where he lived quietly in Massachusetts and died in 1977. The Yorkshire connection, such as it still was, passed to a cousin of his father's branch. Eleanor outlived him by twelve years and described him, in a letter to her eldest grandchild, as the most completely honest man she had ever known — noting, with the dry precision that her grandchildren recognised as a family inheritance on both sides, that this was consistent with his being also the most completely certain man she had ever known, which was occasionally less comfortable to live with than honesty alone.

### SHERLOCK HOLMES IV
**born 1945 — Boston, Massachusetts**

He was born in September 1945, six weeks after the Japanese surrender, into a household where the war's end had produced not relief but a particular quality of English silence that his American relatives found difficult to interpret and his mother had long since learned not to attempt to explain. His father's silences were not unhappy; they were the silences of a man who had spent a professional lifetime reading what other people concealed, and who had made, on arriving in America, a private decision to direct that faculty inward rather than outward — to observe his own life, for once, with the same attention he had previously given to the lives of others. He was, by his son's account, an exceptional father: present, attentive, and constitutionally incapable of pretending not to know things he knew, which meant that the usual parental fictions were unavailable to him and that the boy grew up in a household of considerable, occasionally startling, transparency.

He inherited the gift fully and expressed it in the language of his time and place, which was the language of the law. He read at Harvard, was called to the bar in 1969, and spent the early years of his career in a Boston firm of commercial solicitors before moving to the federal practice that would occupy the remainder of his professional life. His particular facility was financial fraud — cases built not on witness testimony or physical evidence but on documentation: the ten thousand pages of ledgers, correspondence, incorporation filings, and banking records that told, to a reader with the patience and the precision to follow them, a story entirely different from the one their authors had intended. He was not, his colleagues noted, a brilliant advocate; he did not perform in court with any of the theatrical authority that juries respond to. He was something rarer and, in the federal context, considerably more useful: a man who could read a fraudulent financial structure from its documentary residue the way his great-great-grandfather had read a criminal from his boots and his cuffs, and who could explain what he had found in terms precise enough to survive cross-examination by the best commercial barristers in New York.

He married in 1972, late by his generation's standards, and said very little about it then or afterward. His wife, a woman of considerable private force, died young; and Holmes IV, who had not been demonstrative in his happiness, was similarly unrevealing in his grief, which his son later understood to have been considerable.

The case that defined the middle period of his career came to him indirectly, in the winter of 1977, through a referral from a colleague at the Securities and Exchange Commission who had noticed, in the wreckage of a collapsed Boston pension fund, something he could not quite account for — a structural anomaly in the fund's investment allocations that suggested, to his colleague's less developed but genuinely alert instinct, that the collapse had not been accidental.

Holmes IV took the files home over a weekend and read them with the quality of attention he reserved for problems that interested him — which was, as his son observed in later life, the quality of attention other men reserve for very little. What he found in that first weekend was the anomaly his colleague had noticed: a pattern of investment decisions that had systematically directed the fund's assets toward instruments that were, by any reasonable analysis, almost certain to lose value, in a way that was too consistent to be incompetence and too subtle to be immediately legible as fraud. What he found in the three months that followed, as he acquired the files of four more collapsed pension funds in New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut, was that the same structural signature appeared in all five — and that each fund, at the moment of its collapse, had been holding a significant position in instruments issued by a cluster of connected financial vehicles that traced back, through a series of nominee directorships and shared legal counsel, to a single New York investment management firm: Aldgate Capital Partners.

The name Aldgate was not, on its face, significant. But Holmes IV had his father's papers, and his father's papers included the sealed memorandum of 1943, and the sealed memorandum of 1943 included, in a footnote, the observation that the London anchor of the wartime financial network had maintained a New York correspondent under a series of names, all of them featuring either a reference to London postal districts or a reference to the trade routes along which the network's historical operations had been conducted. He noted this. He did not yet conclude from it.

He concluded from it six weeks later, when a subpoena to Aldgate Capital's incorporation documents revealed that the firm's registered legal counsel in its 1958 incorporation filing was a London solicitors' firm, now dissolved, whose senior partner's name appeared in a 1946 Board of Trade enquiry into the winding-up of a City of London import company called Meridian Trade and Finance.

He sat with this for a day. Then he assembled his analysis — five pension fund collapses, one New York investment firm, one London legal connection, one wartime precedent — and delivered it to the United States Attorney for the District of Massachusetts in a memorandum of forty-seven pages, accompanied by twelve lever-arch files of supporting documentation. He did not include the historical analysis; he included only what was legally actionable. Seventeen individuals associated with Aldgate Capital and its connected vehicles were indicted in 1979; eleven were convicted; the firm was dissolved. Holmes IV was never called as a witness. His name does not appear in the indictment or in the trial record.

He retired in 1998, having spent fifty years in the law, and died in 2009 at sixty-four — too young, his son thought, for a man who had always seemed to him to have more of the work left to do.

### SHERLOCK HOLMES V
**born 1970 — Boston, Massachusetts**

He was his father's son in the way that the line had always produced its sons: not by any particular intention on either side, but by the operation of something in the blood that expressed itself, in each generation, as the same fundamental faculty in a new set of circumstances. His father had explained it to him, as his great-great-grandfather had explained it to Holmes III, as a thing to be understood rather than celebrated — a tool with specific purposes and specific limitations, not a general superiority but a particular kind of sight that came with the obligation to use it for something other than personal advantage. Holmes V absorbed this instruction and, by his own account, failed to live up to it in approximately the ways that his father had predicted he would: he was impatient with people who did not follow his reasoning; he was poor at concealing what he had concluded; and he had, as his daughter Charlotte observed in her less guarded moments, the intermittent quality of being entirely present and the more frequent quality of being present in body while absent in everything else, which was difficult to live with and impossible to argue with because he was, when challenged about it, always able to explain precisely where he had been and why.

He was educated at Harvard, like his father, read law, and then did not practice it — turning instead to the adjacent but less constrained world of policy analysis and congressional consultation, where the analytical faculty he had inherited could be applied to the large, complex, poorly documented systems of power and money that his generation had inherited from the Cold War and were dismantling, or believed they were dismantling, with varying degrees of success. He worked as a consultant to several Senate committees through the nineties and into the first years of the new century, with a reputation for finding the thing in a document collection that everyone else had looked at and not seen.

In the autumn of 2001, in the weeks immediately following the attacks of September 11th, he was engaged by the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence to assist in a review of pre-attack intelligence failures — one of several such engagements across multiple departments and committees, overlapping and partially duplicating one another in the way that governmental responses to catastrophe tend to do. Holmes V accepted the engagement with the private reservation that intelligence failures of that scale were rarely simple failures and that the more productive question was usually not what had been missed but what had been noticed and not acted upon, and why.

He found what he was looking for in the second month, in a sub-collection of interagency communications that had been marked for the review's attention as evidence of missed connections. What he found was not, in itself, incriminating: a pattern of threat assessments, originating from different agencies and concerning different threat streams, that had been routed through a Washington-based interagency coordination unit and had each, at that unit's level, received the same processing designation — a designation that, in practice, resulted in the assessment being forwarded to a secondary queue rather than the primary action queue, and consequently being acted on with a delay that, in three specific cases, had meant it was not acted on at all. The unit had existed since 1995. Its director had retired in August 2001.

That was, on its face, administrative incompetence of the kind that large bureaucracies produce habitually and that reviews of this kind document regularly without consequence. What made it not that — what made Holmes V sit with the sub-collection for three days and read it again from the beginning with a different set of questions — was the pattern of what had been delayed. The delayed assessments were not random. They were, without exception, assessments that, if acted upon, would have exposed the operational activities of a cluster of private security and intelligence contractors who held clearances for access to the same category of information the assessments concerned. The delay had systematically protected those contractors from scrutiny. The protection had the structural signature of design rather than accident.

He spent another month tracing the contractors. They led, through the familiar mechanism of nominee directorships and shared legal counsel, to a Delaware holding company, which led to a Cayman Islands beneficial ownership structure, which led — at the third remove, through a document he obtained from the SEC's historical filings under a FOIA request — to a New York investment management firm that had been dissolved in 1979 following a federal indictment. The name in the dissolution filing, as legal representative of the dissolved firm's residual interests, was the same Washington attorney who had incorporated the Delaware holding company in 1987.

He submitted a sealed memorandum to the committee chairman in January 2003. The contractors in question lost their security clearances in the subsequent review cycle; the precise grounds were not made public. Holmes V's name does not appear in any public record of the committee's proceedings. He was, by then, accustomed to this.

He recognised the gift in both his children before either of them recognised it in themselves, and handled the recognition imperfectly in the ways that men of his temperament handle most intimate things — with precision about the what and considerable uncertainty about the how. He loved them in the way that the line had always loved: completely, silently, and with an attention to their qualities that they experienced, alternately, as the most sustaining and the most unsettling thing about him.

### SHERLOCK HOLMES VI
**born approximately 1999 — United States**

Of all the members of this line since the first Sherlock, he is the most difficult to write about. The difficulty is not a function of the record's scarcity, though the record is scarce; it is a function of a quality in the man himself that the record, where it exists, consistently reflects. He moves through documentation as he apparently moved through rooms: leaving the minimum necessary trace, attending to everything, committing to nothing that would fix his position for the benefit of anyone who might, at a later point, wish to know where he had been.

His father observed the gift in him early — earlier, perhaps, than in any generation since the first Sherlock — and transmitted to him, as his own father had transmitted it to him, the instruction that had accompanied it through the line: what it was, where it came from, what it was for. Holmes VI received this instruction and drew from it a conclusion that no previous member of the line had quite reached, or had reached and not acted on so completely: that the gift, properly exercised, operated most effectively in advance of the event rather than in response to it. His predecessors had been detectives in the fullest sense — readers of what had happened, tracers of what was hidden, resolvers of crises already in progress. Holmes VI was something different. He was a preventer.

In the spring of 2021, a series of coordinated intrusions into the settlement infrastructure of several major financial institutions — attributed by the public and official record to a state actor whose name appeared in the relevant intelligence assessments with a specificity that Holmes VI found, on reflection, too convenient — came to his attention through a channel that this record does not identify. The attribution, he concluded after three days of analysis, was the point of the operation, not its by-product. Someone had constructed an attack whose most valuable feature was not what it compromised but whom it implicated; the actual data exfiltrated was, by any reasonable assessment of the attributed state actor's intelligence priorities, of negligible value to that actor. It was of considerable value to a financial network that needed, for reasons Holmes VI had to reconstruct from his father's sealed memorandum and his grandfather's 1943 document, to know the settlement positions of those specific institutions at that specific moment.

He identified the actual origin of the intrusion through three convergent observations: the timing of the attacks, which followed a pattern inconsistent with a state actor's operational security discipline but consistent with a commercial entity operating across multiple time zones; the specific nature of the data extracted, which mapped to the settlement exposure of a cluster of financial vehicles that appeared, under current names, in a corporate genealogy he had assembled from regulatory filings going back to 1979; and a cryptographic signature embedded in the intrusion's command-and-control infrastructure that had appeared, in a slightly different form, in a 2003 classified annex to the Senate intelligence committee report that his father had been involved in producing — an annex that had never been made public, and whose appearance now, repurposed as an operational tool, indicated that whoever had designed the current operation had access to material that had been classified for twenty years.

He routed his findings through a single trusted contact within the relevant federal authority, accompanied by documentation sufficient to allow that contact to act without requiring further explanation. The correct attribution was established in classified channels within six weeks; the financial vehicles lost a major operation; the state actor's name was quietly removed from the public assessments. No announcement was made. No name was attached to the analysis.

His name appears in no public document. That, as this chronicle has noted before of him, is almost certainly deliberate. The gift, in his hands, became finally and completely what it had always been at its most essential: not a performance, not even a service in the acknowledged sense, but a form of attention — sustained, precise, and directed always at the thing that most required it, regardless of who received the credit for the consequence.

### SHERLOCK HOLMES VII
**born 2021 — United States**

Son of Sherlock Holmes VI. The seventh to bear the name; the latest in a line that stretches back, through England and Rome and Greece and Egypt and Mesopotamia, to a scribe in the temple of Nanna at Ur who could read discrepancy in a grain tally as other men read weather, and who understood, in whatever language available to him across four thousand years of distance, that the gift was not his own — that it had come to him from somewhere behind him and was going somewhere ahead of him, and that his obligation was to exercise it faithfully and pass it on intact.

He is, at the time of this writing, a child.

*The chronicle ends here, for now. The organisation whose operations this record has traced across four millennia — from the temple fraud at Ur to the financial networks of the present century — has not been destroyed. It has not been destroyed in any generation. It has contracted, reorganised, changed its name, moved its address, found new instruments, and continued. It will continue. The line that has opposed it has also continued, generation by generation, without public acknowledgement and without any formal appointment, sustained by nothing more and nothing less than the inheritance of a particular quality of mind and the understanding, transmitted from each member of the line to the next, of what that quality was for.*

*The scribe of Ur did not know that his grandson would carry what he had discovered. The seventh Sherlock does not yet know what he will discover, or what his children will carry forward from it.*

*The record waits.*
