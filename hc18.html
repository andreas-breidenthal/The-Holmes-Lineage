HANNO-BAAL

~1540 BC — Cyprus (Alashiya)

THE ACCOUNT OF DANELU, SON OF ZAKARBAAL

I am the third scribe to have served a man of the line of Melqart-Azar, and I was the first to understand that the position was not a commercial appointment. My predecessors had recorded transactions. I recorded something else — I did not know its name for a long time, and I am not certain I know it now.

Hanno-Baal's father had been the most reliable assessor at the Tyrian port for thirty years. His grandfather had been trusted by the most demanding merchant council in the Levant. When the Tyrian house assigned me to their factor on Cyprus, they told me I was to maintain correspondence between Tyre and the island, to keep the weight-records, and to copy the commercial tablets in a hand legible to the receiving merchants at Byblos and Ugarit. What they did not tell me — what I discovered for myself, in the manner of a man who steps into a river and only then notices the current — was that Hanno-Baal had inherited from his father and his father's father a faculty that operated at a different level from commercial competence. He knew things before he knew how he knew them. He saw shapes in evidence that other men saw as noise. The gift ran in the blood unbroken. He did not speak of it. He did not need to.

I record this now because what happened at Tamassos cannot be understood without understanding the man, and the man cannot be understood without understanding what he came from, and that inheritance has its own weight — as consequential, in its way, as the copper ingots whose measure was at the centre of everything.

The harbour at Enkomi smells of dung and copper and the sea-rot of old timber, and in the late afternoon, when the wind comes off the land rather than the water, it smells also of the smelting furnaces to the north, whose smoke is the colour of old blood and settles into the throat like a bad decision. I record this because Hanno-Baal remarked on it the first morning we arrived, lifting his long nose as a hunting dog lifts its muzzle, and said: "Three furnaces working at half-capacity. Either the ore is poor this season, or someone is rationing the charcoal. Either answer is interesting."

I wrote it down. I was not yet sure why I wrote down the things he said, but I had begun to understand that his observations had a tendency to become important later.

He was not, in those first weeks on Alashiya, a man who attracted attention. He was lean and quieter than most Canaanite factors, with the long-boned face of his family and eyes that moved across a room the way a merchant's fingers move across a table of goods — not grasping, but cataloguing. He slept little. He ate when reminded. During the torpor between cases — for I had already begun to think of his periods of inactivity in those terms, though I could not have explained why — he had a habit I found, at first, deeply unsettling: he would take from a small cedarwood box a pinch of dried herbs whose name I eventually learned was kyphi, the Egyptian temple-blend, and he would let it smoulder on a clay dish and sit in the smoke with his eyes half-closed and his long fingers pressed together at their tips, perfectly motionless, for an hour or two hours or until something shifted in his thinking and he rose without warning and reached for a tablet. The smell of it — resin and honey and something underneath that I could not name and did not entirely trust — came to mean, for me, that the case had not yet begun. The moment it stopped, I knew to have my stylus ready.

He spent the hours of high sun, when all sensible men rested from the heat, crouched over the copper weight-records of the previous three seasons, cross-referencing the ingot tallies against the ship manifests with a concentration I found unnerving to witness. When I asked him once what he was looking for, he said: "Holes. A true record has no holes. A managed record has small ones, very carefully placed. The difficulty is learning to see the careful ones."

This was, I now understand, the condition in which he lived during those early weeks — the slow torpor of a mind insufficiently occupied. He was patient with the merchants, correct with the harbour administrators, courteous to the Cypriote officials who greeted our house's arrival. But I could see it: the energy coiled behind his eyes like rope under tension, waiting for the case that would release it.

The body of Rib-Hadda the overseer was found at the mouth of the third shaft of the Tamassos workings on the morning of the fourteenth day of the second month of our residence.

He had been struck once, very hard, behind the left ear. The instrument was not recovered. He was face-down in the spoil-heap at the shaft entrance, and he had been there, the mine physician estimated, since some point in the darkness before dawn.

By midday, the Cypriote port-captain Ahmose-mer-Ra — an Egyptian-named man of Cypriote birth who administered the royal mine interests and who wore the title of his office like armour against the complexity of his actual duties — had arrested our factor, Aziru son of Bomilcar of Tyre.

The logic was, as Hanno-Baal later noted, not without a certain municipal poetry. Aziru had argued loudly with Rib-Hadda in the market three days before the death. The argument concerned the weighing of a consignment of copper ingots — specifically, whether the weights applied to them matched the weights recorded in the official tablets. Aziru said they did not. Rib-Hadda said that our factor was impugning the honour of a man appointed by the Alashiyan administration. The harbour square had been full of witnesses.

I did not see the port-captain's men come for Aziru. I heard them. Hanno-Baal and I were at our morning work in the upper room of the house when the sound reached us from the street below — the flat, authoritative percussion of bronze-capped staves on packed earth, the kind of sound that says: this is now official. Hanno-Baal was at the window before I had risen from my mat.

Below, in the lane, four men in the port-captain's colours had Aziru between them. He was not a large man, but he fought with the particular, useless fury of someone who knows exactly how unjust the thing being done to him is — twisting in their grip, his voice rising in a tirade of Canaanite invective that the port-captain's men absorbed with professional indifference. His wrists had been bound behind him with a cord of the kind used for cargo, and the casual efficiency of that binding — the fact that someone had thought to bring the right rope — was more frightening than the shouting.

Then he looked up. He had, perhaps, some instinct for where help might be standing, or perhaps it was simply the movement at the window that caught his eye. Whatever the cause, his gaze found Hanno-Baal in the upper room, and the shouting stopped as suddenly as if a lamp had been snuffed.

"Hanno-Baal." His voice was different then — smaller, stripped of its anger, with something underneath that I had not heard from him before. Not grief. Not pleading. The sound a man makes when he realises he has one chance and must not waste it on noise. "You know the weights. You know I was right about the weights."

The port-captain's men pulled him forward, and he stumbled and caught himself, and turned his head back as far as the cord and the grip would allow, and said, as clearly as he could manage: "Get me out."

Then he was gone around the corner. The lane was quiet. The sound of the staves receded.

Hanno-Baal stood at the window for a moment longer, not moving. His face was, as always, unreadable by any means I possessed. Then he turned back into the room.

"Find me everything you can carry of the last two seasons' weight-records," he said. "We have work."

He did not reach for the kyphi box. That, in itself, told me something.

The port-captain came to us on the second day after the arrest. He was not, I should say, a foolish man — but he was something more dangerous than a fool. He was a man of system. He had served the Alashiyan administration for fifteen years, and in that service he had learned — or perhaps decided — that the function of official process was to resolve uncertainty efficiently, not accurately. Accuracy was expensive. Efficiency was rewarded.

He came because the Tyrian merchant house was an important commercial relationship and because he required, as he delicately expressed it, "the cooperation of the Canaanite community in bringing this matter to a swift resolution."

What he meant was: confirm that your man did it, and we will proceed.

Hanno-Baal received him in the reed-mat shade of our house's courtyard, on a bench near the small olive tree that grew there with improbable persistence. He had been sitting motionless for the better part of the morning in the kyphi smoke, which had stopped half an hour before the port-captain arrived — early enough to suggest that some piece of the puzzle had resolved itself in the interior where I had no access. He rose when Ahmose-mer-Ra arrived, offered the appropriate courtesies, and listened with every appearance of respectful attention.

Ahmose-mer-Ra was a broad man, going to heaviness in the way of officials who have grown comfortable in their authority. He had a civil servant's hands — unmarked by labour, stained by ink — and he held them in front of him when he spoke, as though presenting his own arguments as exhibits. He had brought two guards to the courtyard gate. They wore short bronze blades at their belts. This I wrote down. I wrote down also that when he named the Tamassos workings in passing, his eyes moved briefly to Hanno-Baal with an attention that was slightly too controlled — the particular vigilance of a man checking whether a word has landed.

He had been to Tamassos recently, I thought. More recently than his office required.

"You have the public dispute," Hanno-Baal said, when the port-captain finished. "You have the timing. You have no witness to the act itself?"

"A man seen near the Tamassos road before dawn has no business on the Tamassos road before dawn."

"He was seen?"

"A shepherd reports a figure. The description—"

"Is consistent with approximately every man of working age on this island," Hanno-Baal said pleasantly. "Port-Captain, I would like to examine the body, the shaft entrance, and the weight-records of the Tamassos overseer's office for the past two years."

The reaction was small and immediate and entirely disproportionate: Ahmose-mer-Ra's hands, which had been resting open on his knees in the posture of a man conducting a routine interview, closed. Not tightly. Carefully. The movement of someone applying a deliberate check to an involuntary response.

I wrote this down also.

"The records of the overseer's office are an administrative matter—"

"They are the reason the argument occurred. A weight dispute is not an insult to a man's honour unless the weights are carrying something heavier than copper." Hanno-Baal tilted his head. "You might prefer to know what they contain before this proceeds to formal adjudication. If I am wrong, nothing is lost. If I am right, you will have been very wise to look."

The port-captain was a man who understood the architecture of self-interest even when it was dressed in the language of civic virtue. But I saw, now, that there was a second architecture beneath the first — a private one, with load-bearing walls he could not afford to have examined.

"I will authorise an inspection of the shaft entrance," he said. "The records are a separate matter."

"Of course," said Hanno-Baal, in a tone that suggested he had already determined how to approach the separate matter.

After Ahmose-mer-Ra left, I described to Hanno-Baal what I had observed — the controlled hands, the eyes moving at the mention of Tamassos.

"Yes," he said. He was looking at the courtyard gate through which the port-captain had departed. "He did not come here merely to secure cooperation. He came because he needed to know how much we know." He was quiet for a moment. "The weight-records at Tamassos will tell us about Rib-Hadda. But a fraud of that scale and duration — two years, consistent, never investigated — requires someone above the overseer to be incurious about the accounts. Deliberately incurious."

"The port-captain certified the weights."

"The port-captain certified the weights," he agreed. "And has, I think, been drawing his own margin from the arrangement. Which is why Aziru's public argument was not merely inconvenient to Rib-Hadda. It was dangerous to both of them." He rose. "We have perhaps three days. Possibly fewer. Come."

The Tamassos workings were two hours north of Enkomi, in the dry hills where the copper ore ran through the rock in seams the miners followed by instinct and long experience. The landscape there is the colour of old rust — the soil stained by millennia of mineral seepage — and the scrub oak grows in shapes that suggest argument, all angular limbs and leaning trunks. We went the following morning in the early hours, before the heat rose, and Hanno-Baal spent the journey in silence, watching the road.

The mine was already alive when we arrived, though the sun had barely cleared the eastern ridge. The shaft wheels were turning, the ropes creaking with their loads, and the men emerging from the lower workings had the blunted, inward look of people who have spent hours in darkness and whose eyes take time to remember that the world has a horizon. They were thin — not the thinness of poverty exactly, but the stripped-down economy of bodies that spend themselves daily in confined spaces and are fuelled to precisely the level required for the next descent.

One of them did not go with the others to the water-jars. He sat apart on a flat stone near the second shaft entrance, turning something small in his wide-palmed hands. He was perhaps forty years, with the forward slope of the shoulders that comes from a working life underground, and his skin was the colour of the Tamassos hillsides. When Hanno-Baal and I passed within a few paces of him on our way to the body, he looked up briefly, assessed us with the quick, flat attention of someone accustomed to evaluating strangers for threat, and looked away.

Hanno-Baal's step did not slow. But I saw his eyes move to the man's feet and remain there for a fraction of a moment. I looked also. The man's sandals were pale with dust — the particular fine-ground pale dust of the spoil-heap, which is different from the red-brown of the road and the grey of the mine-floor. I noted it without yet understanding why I noted it.

We were taken first to the body, which had not yet been moved. While the escort-men stood back, I crouched beside Rib-Hadda and looked at him with the careful attention that Hanno-Baal had taught me by example.

He had been, in life, a heavyset man of perhaps fifty years. His right hand was open, fingers slack. His left hand was closed — not in the convulsion of death, but with the deliberate grip of a man who had been holding something when he died and had not released it. I pried the fingers back. A short bronze stylus, of the kind used for impressing marks into fresh clay. It was worn smooth on the left side of the grip — the wear-pattern of a left-handed man.

I showed it to Hanno-Baal. He looked at it briefly, confirming rather than discovering, and said: "Note which side."

"The left only."

"Good." He moved to the spoil-heap.

At the shaft entrance, he moved with the attentiveness of a man reading a surface others see as blank. He crouched over the spoil-heap for a long time. He examined the stone lip of the shaft. Then he crouched over the area thirty paces to the east, where the ground changed from spoil to natural surface, and I watched him study the drag-marks with the minute attention of a jeweller examining a suspect gem.

"The dust," he said, without looking up. "The spoil-heap dust here — you see the colour of it. Not the road-dust, not the mine-floor. That particular whitish grey. It is the crushed tailings from the third shaft specifically — the ore there has a higher calcite content. It does not occur naturally elsewhere on this site."

I looked. He was correct. The dust kicked over the drag-marks — the deliberate obscuring — was that pale, specific white-grey.

"So the person who kicked the dust over these marks," I said, "was standing in the spoil-heap of the third shaft. They would have that dust on their feet afterward. Specific to this place and this morning."

Hanno-Baal looked at me with the expression he reserved for moments when I had surprised him. He said nothing, but rose and turned slowly to look at the working shed, and then toward the flat stone where the miner sat, and then back at the shed.

"Someone for whom the road to Tamassos before dawn required no special explanation," he said, quietly. "Someone who had been walking it for years."

I thought of the pale dust on the miner's sandals. Not road-dust. Not mine-floor. The specific calcite-white of the third shaft's tailings.

I wrote it down. I did not yet know whether I was recording an observation or an accusation.

Hanno-Baal did not go immediately to the shed. He went to the mine foreman.

His name was Ili-Rapi — a compact, quick-eyed man of perhaps thirty-five with the foreman's particular manner, simultaneously superior to those beneath him and anxious about those above. He had found the body that morning, and he wore the agitation of that discovery like a fresh stain he could not stop touching. When Hanno-Baal asked him, gently, about the morning's events, Ili-Rapi produced an account with the over-precise fluency of a man who has been rehearsing it since dawn.

Too precise. Too unbroken.

"You found him alone?" Hanno-Baal said.

"I am first to the shaft every morning."

"Then you were on the Tamassos road before dawn." A pause. "Did you see anyone else?"

"No one."

"And did Rib-Hadda have enemies among the men? Anyone who had argued with him — a dispute over pay, a short measurement?"

The foreman named two men, with the precision of someone pruning a larger list. "Baal-Eser had a dispute last month. He accused the overseer of a short tally. Loudly." A pause. "The overseer reduced his gang's allocation as a correction."

Hanno-Baal spoke next with Baal-Eser himself — a young man of perhaps twenty-five, raw-boned, his anger present-tense and outward-facing. He had been in Enkomi two nights ago and could name four witnesses. His grievance was real and recent and burning.

Hanno-Baal thanked him and walked away.

"Not Baal-Eser," he said to me, before I could ask.

"Because of the witnesses in Enkomi?"

"Because of the anger. Baal-Eser's anger wants to be seen. The man I am looking for has a different relationship with what he carries. Older. Settled. He has finished arguing." He glanced, briefly, at the miner on the flat stone — at the pale dust on the man's sandals, which I could see clearly from where we stood. "Baal-Eser argues in the market. The man I am looking for arrived, alone and in silence, at his own arithmetic. And then he acted on it, on a road he had walked his whole working life." He paused. "The foreman saw someone on that road this morning, and has decided not to say so. He is protecting himself from the knowledge. If he never said it clearly, he never had to act on it."

"What does he know that the records will confirm?"

"That the short tallies were not Rib-Hadda's private enterprise." He was already moving toward the shed. "Someone above him kept the accounts clean from the Enkomi end. Someone whose seal authenticated the consignment records. Someone who, if the fraud is exposed, loses not his position but his life." He said it quietly, without inflection, the way a man states an arithmetic fact. "Come."

The keeper of the record-shed was the thin boy Shipti-Baal, perhaps sixteen, who had been assigned as guard and recorder by Rib-Hadda himself. He straightened when he saw us, his hand moving to the door's locking-bar with mechanical certainty.

"The records," he said, with effortful dignity, "are under the authority of the Alashiyan administration and may not be examined without—"

"Who told you to say that?" Hanno-Baal asked pleasantly.

The boy faltered. "The port-captain's office sent a rider this morning—"

"Before or after we left Enkomi?"

Silence.

"Before," said Hanno-Baal, to no one in particular. He looked at the escort's senior man with an expression of mild academic interest. "The port-captain sent a rider at dawn to bar the records before he had any reason to suppose we were interested in them. That is a remarkable act of administrative foresight." He turned back to the boy. "What is your name?"

"Shipti-Baal, sir."

"Shipti-Baal. The man who assigned you to this post is dead. His records are now part of an investigation into that death. If those records are damaged or lost before the adjudication of our factor's case, the questions about who gave the instruction to damage them will be asked at a level considerably above the port-captain's court." He said this without emphasis, as though explaining a commercial procedure. "Step aside."

Shipti-Baal stepped aside. I moved first through the door, took up my position against it from the inside with my hand on the bar, and looked at the escort's senior man steadily until he decided, after a long moment, that the threshold was not worth testing. I heard him speak to his colleagues and their footsteps move away.

I held the door and waited.

Inside, Hanno-Baal went to the most recent stack and began.

The tablets were organised by season and consignment — each load of copper ingots recorded by weight, source shaft, and destination vessel. Two columns ran parallel within each record: the shaft output tally on the left, the outgoing consignment weight on the right.

I watched over his shoulder. I had been prepared by the courtyard conversation, and looked specifically at the columns.

"Here," he said, after ten minutes. "Read them."

I leaned forward. Shaft output: forty talents. Outgoing consignment, same date, same load: forty-two.

"And here." Summer record. Shaft: fifty-three. Outgoing: fifty-five and a fraction.

"And here." Autumn. Shaft: thirty-one. Outgoing: thirty-two and a half.

"Always higher," I said. "Always in the same direction. The same proportion."

"Between one-fortieth and one-thirtieth. Across two full years." He set the tablets side by side. "Random variation distributes evenly — sometimes high, sometimes low. This is structured. Stone weights introduce error, but they do not introduce error that is always in the same direction and always of the same order."

He reached for a third tablet, deeper in the stack — a consignment record from eighteen months prior — and it was at precisely this moment that the boy Shipti-Baal came through the door.

He moved faster than I would have credited him. He had not, as I had supposed, gone anywhere — he had waited outside, gathered himself, and come back with the particular desperate velocity of someone who has been given an instruction he dare not fail to execute. He went not for Hanno-Baal, who was between him and the shelves, but for the stack of tablets on the mat between them — he reached for them with both hands and swept them sideways, and three of them struck the beaten-earth floor and one shattered.

Hanno-Baal caught the fifth with one hand before it followed the others.

Shipti-Baal stood breathing hard, looking at what he had done, and then at Hanno-Baal, and then at the intact tablet in Hanno-Baal's hand. His face held the specific expression of a boy who has just understood that the act he was instructed to perform has failed and that he now bears its consequences alone.

"The rider told you to destroy them if anyone read them," Hanno-Baal said. It was not a question.

The boy said nothing.

"What did they tell you would happen if you did not?"

Still nothing. But his hands, at his sides, were shaking.

Hanno-Baal looked at the tablet in his hand, then at the shattered fragments on the floor, then at the boy. His expression did not change — it remained the expression of a man who has just watched a mechanism confirm precisely what he expected of it — but there was something in it, briefly, that was not calculation. He set the intact tablet down carefully on the mat and crouched beside the shattered pieces, turning them over with his long fingers.

"The break is clean," he said quietly. "The impression survives in the larger fragments. Danelu."

I had already moved to the floor beside him. I fitted the pieces together on the mat — four of them, a fifth too small — with the careful hands of a man who has spent his life handling fragile things. The columns were legible across the break: shaft output thirty-eight talents, outgoing consignment forty and a quarter.

"Write it," Hanno-Baal said.

I wrote. While I was writing, Hanno-Baal looked up at the boy Shipti-Baal, who had not moved.

"You were given an instruction by a man who has already placed you between himself and the consequences of his own acts," Hanno-Baal said. "That is not a position you chose. But it is a position you are currently in." He paused. "The port-captain's name is on the order the rider brought you this morning. Is it not?"

The boy's silence was answer enough.

"Then the order exists. You have not destroyed it. And the records, as you can see, are substantially intact." Hanno-Baal rose, dusted the clay-dust from his hands. "Go and sit outside, Shipti-Baal. Do not run. There is nowhere on this island that the official record cannot follow you."

The boy went. I was not entirely sure he would not run, but I heard his footsteps stop a short distance from the door and stay there, and I attended to the tablets.

"He was stealing from the miners," I said, when I had the full picture.

"From men who have no tablets and no advocates. Men who count in sacks and rough measures and depend on the overseer's tally because they cannot read the overseer's tablet." His voice was precise, informational. "Rib-Hadda held the fraction at the shaft. But the consignment records — which went to the receiving merchants, who have their own assessors — had to show the true quantity shipped, or the discrepancy would appear at the other end. So the consignment records show what was actually shipped. The shaft output records show the false quantity the miners received payment for."

"And the port-captain authenticated the consignment records."

"And drew his share of the margin. Which is why Aziru's public argument about the weights was not merely embarrassing. It was an existential threat." He picked up the two most legible tablets and the fragments we had reassembled. "A man who has been taking a cut of a two-year fraud, when the fraud is exposed, does not face a fine. He faces the kind of administration that appointed him — which, in this instance, answers to Egypt." He looked at me directly. "He will not negotiate quietly at the docks, Danelu. He will try to end this before it reaches any dock."

I thought of the guards at the courtyard gate. The hands that had closed when the weight-records were mentioned. The rider sent before dawn.

"Then we should not let him choose the ground," I said.

Hanno-Baal looked at me for a moment with something that was as close as he came to approval. "No," he said. "We should not."

We chose the harbour-front at the hour of the outgoing tide, when the dock was full of workers and merchants and sailors and the two ships loading for Ugarit and Byblos respectively were still at their moorings. I had spent the previous evening preparing: two sealed letters, each a complete copy of the tablet-columns with my notations, one in the keeping of the shipmaster Abdi-Milki, one in the keeping of the Byblos vessel's purser, both with instructions to open and present them to the receiving merchants' councils if neither Hanno-Baal nor I appeared to reclaim them before sailing. I am a scribe. Letters are my instrument. This was, I understood, the role I had been prepared for.

Ahmose-mer-Ra came with six men. More than before. Four of them had their hands close to their blades.

He looked, when he saw us waiting for him in the open, like a man who has arrived at a meeting expecting to set its terms and has found that the terms are already set. His composure was professional — fifteen years of administration had given him a face that could absorb surprises without visibly registering them — but his six men were not so practiced, and two of them looked at the crowd of dock-workers and sailors with the calculating expression of men revising a plan.

"Port-Captain." Hanno-Baal spoke at a volume that carried easily to the dock-workers, the vessel crews, and the merchants at their nearby post. "I am grateful you came. I have found something in the Tamassos records that I believe requires your assessment."

He laid the tablets on the dock-post between them and spoke. Shaft output forty talents, outgoing consignment forty-two. Season after season. The mechanism of the fraud — the false shaft tally, the accurate consignment record, the fraction held and accumulated. The arithmetic of it: how much copper, over two years, from how many men. He said all of this at the same carrying volume, to the open air, with the dock-workers and merchants as his audience.

Ahmose-mer-Ra listened with a stillness that had nothing of patience in it.

"These figures," he said, when Hanno-Baal finished, "come from records removed from an administrative facility under seal."

"Copied under the authorisation your office provided for the inspection — the same authorisation that preceded, by some hours, the rider your office sent to have those records destroyed." Hanno-Baal's tone remained pleasant. "Shipti-Baal the record-keeper has the rider's written order in his possession. Your seal is on it."

The stillness in Ahmose-mer-Ra shifted quality. It was no longer the stillness of a man containing a response. It was the stillness of a man arriving at a decision.

"Take them," he said, to his men. And then, more quietly: "Both of them."

Two of the men moved. They drew their blades — not to threaten but to act, the movement of men who have been told that this is the moment and who are committed to it. The dock-workers nearest us scattered back. The merchants at the post went absolutely still. The blade-points came up.

I said, loudly and clearly, in the carrying voice of a trained scribe accustomed to reading legal documents to assembled merchants: "The consignment record for the season of the barley harvest reads: shaft output, thirty-eight talents of copper. Outgoing consignment, forty and a quarter talents. The difference of two and a quarter talents, valued at—"

"Stop," said Ahmose-mer-Ra.

I did not stop. I had the tablet in my hands and I read from it, deliberately and at full voice, the column figures for every season I had copied — forty against forty-two, fifty-three against fifty-five, thirty-one against thirty-two and a half — and around me the dock went quiet with the specific quiet of people who are listening and will remember what they have heard.

The men with the blades had stopped moving. They were looking at Ahmose-mer-Ra. He was looking at me and at the tablet in my hands and at the dock-workers standing in the open, who were not moving away.

Hanno-Baal spoke, quietly, for Ahmose-mer-Ra alone: "The letters to Ugarit and Byblos sail with the tide regardless of what becomes of us. Every merchant on this dock has now heard the figures. The record already exists outside this harbour. The only question remaining is whether you are the man who silenced the inquiry or the man who resolved it." He held the port-captain's gaze without effort. "Those are not the same man, in the administration's accounting."

The silence held for a long moment that I will carry for the rest of my life. I held the tablet and breathed and did not look at the blades.

Then Ahmose-mer-Ra made a very small gesture, and his men lowered their weapons, and the dock breathed again.

"Aziru son of Bomilcar," the port-captain said, in the voice of a man reading a conclusion from a tablet he has not written, "will be released on the ground that the witness account was insufficient."

"A sound administrative decision," said Hanno-Baal.

Before we left the dock, Hanno-Baal turned back to Ahmose-mer-Ra with the particular look he reserved for moments that pleased him aesthetically — not warmth, not triumph, but the satisfaction of a craftsman examining a completed joint.

"There is one further matter," he said. "For your information, since the adjudication record may require it." He spoke the next part with a precision that was, I recognised now, its own form of theatre. "Rib-Hadda was left-handed. The bronze stylus found in his closed left hand shows wear only on the left grip-face — the mark of a lifetime's use. A man struck in anger by someone facing him receives the blow on the right side of his head. The wound was on the left. Therefore: the man who killed him was not facing him. He was walking beside him, on his left, close enough not to be questioned. A man Rib-Hadda trusted. A man for whom the road before dawn required no explanation." He paused. "The shepherd's figure on the Tamassos road was not your factor, Port-Captain. It was never your factor. You knew this, I think, before you arrested him."

Ahmose-mer-Ra said nothing.

"I thought so," said Hanno-Baal, and turned away.

Aziru was released the following morning. He came to our house that afternoon, still carrying the specific anger of a man who has been briefly imprisoned for something he did not do, and he sat in the courtyard and drank our wine and said several things about Cypriote justice that I will not copy here. Hanno-Baal listened with patient courtesy and said little.

At some point in the late afternoon, when the worst of the heat had passed and the courtyard's single olive tree was making its small shade, the miner appeared at the gate.

I knew him immediately. The forward-sloping shoulders. The wide-palmed hands, now at his sides and very still. I looked, as I had looked at the mine, at his sandals — but they had been cleaned, or changed, or perhaps I had imagined the pale dust. I wrote in my tablet simply that the man appeared, and noted that I could not determine, in retrospect, whether I had been recording an observation or an accusation, and that Hanno-Baal's certainty on the matter suggested the distinction had not troubled him.

He stood at the gate looking at Hanno-Baal.

Hanno-Baal looked back at him. His face was, as always, unreadable by any means I possessed.

"There is a ship leaving for Byblos at the tide before dawn tomorrow," Hanno-Baal said, without greeting, without preamble. "The shipmaster is called Abdi-Milki. He owes the house of Melqart-Azar a favour and will ask no questions about a passenger presented with this token."

He held out a small cylinder seal — one of our house's secondary marks, used for minor commercial letters. The man looked at it. He did not move immediately. His gaze went, briefly, to me — to the tablet and stylus I was holding — and something passed across his face that had the quality of a man deciding whether the record is something he can trust.

Then he looked back at Hanno-Baal.

"The official record," Hanno-Baal said, "contains no man. It would be untidy if one appeared."

The miner crossed the courtyard. He took the seal. He looked at it for a moment in his wide palm, and he said, in the rough Cypriote of the working mines: "The weight was always wrong."

"Yes," said Hanno-Baal. "I know."

The miner left without speaking again. His sandals on the packed earth made almost no sound.

Aziru watched all of this and then looked at Hanno-Baal with an expression that was mostly bafflement and a small, reluctant admiration. "Do you know what you just did?"

"I balanced an account," said Hanno-Baal. He picked up his wine cup, examined it, and set it down again without drinking. "The weight was never right. Now it is somewhat less wrong. That is, in my experience, the most that can usually be achieved."

He said nothing else. After a while I heard him open the cedarwood box, and the smell of kyphi drifted across the courtyard — resin and honey and that underthing I could never quite name — and I understood that the case was over and the torpor had begun, and that he was already somewhere I could not follow.

I thought about what he had said and I thought about his father at the Tyrian port, and his grandfather before the merchant council, and the gift running through the blood of the family like the copper seams through the Tamassos rock: unbroken, unremarked, invaluable to those who knew how to follow it. I did not know, then, what the accumulated observations of this family amounted to, or what the recurring pattern in their history signified. I only knew that I had been part of the record now — not merely its keeper but, at the dockside, briefly its instrument — and that this was a different thing from what I had supposed my work to be when I arrived on this island.

I wrote it all down. I do not know, even now, whether I was recording commerce or justice or something that has no name in any of the languages I know. I recorded it because it seemed to me that the weight of what he did — the precise calibration of it, the decision about what truth was worth releasing into the world and what truth was worth holding back — deserved to exist somewhere outside his own silent head.

The smoke from the Tamassos furnaces drifted south all that evening. Three furnaces, working. Full capacity, this time — or near enough to make no difference to a man who was not counting.

I noted this too, and cannot say exactly why.

Lower margin, later hand, ink partially flaked: the miner's name is not recorded. The seal was found in a house in Byblos some years later. No connection was drawn.
