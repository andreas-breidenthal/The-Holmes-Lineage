HANNO-BAAL

~1540 BC — Cyprus (Alashiya)

Favoured by Baal — and the favour expressed itself as direction: west, across the water to Cyprus, which the Canaanites called Alashiya and which was, in the middle of the second millennium BC, the most important copper-producing territory in the world. The Cypriote copper mines supplied the bronze industry of the entire eastern Mediterranean — every palace, every arsenal, every ship’s fittings — and the island was therefore the object of intense commercial and political attention from Egypt, from the Hittites, from the Aegean palace cultures, and from the Canaanite trading cities whose prosperity depended on the free movement of the metals trade.

Hanno-Baal had grown up in Tyre in the knowledge — not yet articulated as a formal inheritance, not yet recorded in language that acknowledged what it was — that the family’s facility with evidence and inference was something more than a professional competence. His father had been the most reliable assessor at the Tyrian port for thirty years. His grandfather had been trusted by the most demanding merchant council in the Levant. The gift ran unbroken. He knew he had it. He did not yet know what it was for, in the largest sense — what the accumulated observations of his family amounted to, what the recurring pattern in the family’s records signified. He went to Cyprus as a trading agent for the Tyrian merchant house he served, and the answer began to come clear in the island’s copper economy.

The record of his time in Cyprus survives in fuller form than those of his predecessors, preserved in a set of commercial tablets copied and recopied by his scribe, Danelu son of Zakarbaal, who was assigned to him by the Tyrian house to maintain correspondence between Tyre and the island. Danelu was younger than Hanno-Baal and more impressionable than his predecessors’ scribes, and his account reflects both his admiration for Hanno-Baal’s methods and his uncertainty about their meaning. What follows is drawn from his tablets, with later marginal notes indicating where the original surface had been damaged or partially erased.

The Account of Danelu son of Zakarbaal

The harbour at Enkomi smells of dung and copper and the sea-rot of old timber, and in the late afternoon, when the wind comes off the land rather than the water, it smells also of the smelting furnaces to the north, whose smoke is the colour of old blood and settles into the throat like a bad decision. I record this because Hanno-Baal remarked on it the first morning we arrived, lifting his long nose as a hunting dog lifts its muzzle, and said: “Three furnaces working at half-capacity. Either the ore is poor this season, or someone is rationing the charcoal. Either answer is interesting.”

I wrote it down. I was not yet sure why I wrote down the things he said, but I had begun to understand that his observations had a tendency to become important later.

He was not, in those first weeks on Alashiya, a man who attracted attention. He was lean and quieter than most Canaanite factors, with the long-boned face of his family and eyes that moved across a room the way a merchant’s fingers move across a table of goods — not grasping, but cataloguing. He slept little. He ate when reminded. He spent the hours of high sun, when all sensible men rested from the heat, crouched over the copper weight-records of the previous three seasons, cross-referencing the ingot tallies against the ship manifests with a concentration I found unnerving to witness. When I asked him once what he was looking for, he said: “Holes. A true record has no holes. A managed record has small ones, very carefully placed. The difficulty is learning to see the careful ones.”

This was, I now understand, the condition in which he lived during those early weeks — the slow torpor of a mind insufficiently occupied. He was patient with the merchants, correct with the harbour administrators, courteous to the Cypriote officials who greeted our house’s arrival. But I could see it: the energy coiled behind his eyes like rope under tension, waiting for the case that would release it.

The body of Rib-Hadda the overseer was found at the mouth of the third shaft of the Tamassos workings on the morning of the fourteenth day of the second month of our residence.

He had been struck once, very hard, behind the left ear. The instrument was not recovered. He was face-down in the spoil-heap at the shaft entrance, and he had been there, the mine physician estimated, since some point in the darkness before dawn.

By midday, the Cypriote port-captain Ahmose-mer-Ra — an Egyptian-named man of Cypriote birth who administered the royal mine interests and who wore the title of his office like armour against the complexity of his actual duties — had arrested our factor, Aziru son of Bomilcar of Tyre.

The logic was, as Hanno-Baal later noted, not without a certain municipal poetry. Aziru had argued loudly with Rib-Hadda in the market three days before the death. The argument concerned the weighing of a consignment of copper ingots — specifically, whether the weights applied to them matched the weights recorded in the official tablets. Aziru said they did not. Rib-Hadda said that our factor was impugning the honour of a man appointed by the Alashiyan administration. The harbour square had been full of witnesses, and several of them had heard Aziru make remarks that stopped just short of a formal curse.

Aziru was a short, dark-tempered man of forty years who had been working the copper trade between Tyre and Alashiya for two decades and who believed, not without justification, that he knew more about the practical operation of the copper market than any official who had ever issued him a tablet. He was also exactly the kind of man who argues loudly in public and then does nothing whatsoever. I knew this. Hanno-Baal, who had met him twice, said he knew it also.

The port-captain did not know it, or did not find it relevant.

Ahmose-mer-Ra came to us on the second day after the arrest. He was not, I should say, a foolish man — he was simply a man whose function had been designed by a system that valued closure over accuracy, and he had served that system long enough that its values had become his own. He came because the Tyrian merchant house was an important commercial relationship and because he required, as he delicately expressed it, “the cooperation of the Canaanite community in bringing this matter to a swift resolution.”

What he meant was: confirm that your man did it, and we will proceed efficiently.

Hanno-Baal received him in the reed-mat shade of our house’s courtyard, on a bench near the small olive tree that grew there with improbable persistence. He had been sitting motionless for the better part of the morning, which I had learned to recognise as his thinking posture — not sleep, not leisure, but some internal process that used stillness as its fuel. He rose when the port-captain arrived, offered the appropriate courtesies, and listened to Ahmose-mer-Ra’s account with every appearance of respectful attention.

“You have the public dispute,” he said, when the port-captain finished. “You have the timing. You have no witness to the act itself?”

“A man seen near the Tamassos road before dawn has no business on the Tamassos road before dawn.”

“He was seen?”

“A shepherd reports a figure. The description—”

“Is consistent with approximately every man of working age on this island,” Hanno-Baal said pleasantly. “Port-Captain, I would like to examine the body, the shaft entrance, and the weight-records of the Tamassos overseer’s office for the past two years.”

Ahmose-mer-Ra’s expression underwent a small contraction. “The records of the overseer’s office are an administrative matter—”

“They are the reason the argument occurred. A weight dispute is not an insult to a man’s honour unless the weights are carrying something heavier than copper.” Hanno-Baal tilted his head slightly. “You might prefer to know what they contain before this proceeds to formal adjudication. If I am wrong, nothing is lost. If I am right, you will have been very wise to look.”

The port-captain considered this for a long moment. He was, I noted, a man who understood the architecture of self-interest even when it was presented to him in the language of civic virtue.

“I will authorise an inspection of the shaft entrance,” he said finally. “The records are a separate matter.”

“Of course,” said Hanno-Baal, in a tone that suggested he had already determined how to approach the separate matter.

The Tamassos workings were two hours north of Enkomi, in the dry hills where the copper ore ran through the rock in seams the miners followed by instinct and long experience. The landscape there is the colour of old rust — the soil stained by millennia of mineral seepage — and the scrub oak grows in shapes that suggest argument, all angular limbs and leaning trunks. We went in the early morning, when the heat was still manageable, and Hanno-Baal spent the journey in silence, watching the road.

At the shaft entrance, he moved in the way I had come to recognise: not quickly, not dramatically, but with the particular attentiveness of a man reading a surface that other men see as blank. He crouched over the spoil-heap for a long time. He examined the packed earth around the shaft entrance. He studied the stone lip of the shaft itself, running his fingers along it in a way that made two of the port-captain’s men exchange a look.

“He was not killed here,” he said, straightening.

“He was found here,” said Ahmose-mer-Ra.

“He was found here. He was killed approximately thirty paces to the east, where the ground changes from spoil to natural surface, and dragged to this point. The drag-marks have been partially obscured — someone kicked spoil-dust over them — but the direction of disturbance in the debris is plain enough. Also, the wound was to the left rear of the skull. Rib-Hadda was left-handed. A man facing him in anger strikes the right side. A man he trusted, walking beside him on his left, strikes the left side.”

He paused, turning slowly to look at the working shed where the mine’s daily records were kept — a mud-brick structure twenty paces from the shaft, with a heavy wooden door.

“A man he trusted,” he repeated, more quietly, as though reading from a tablet only he could see. “Or a man whose presence he had no reason to question. Someone for whom the road to Tamassos before dawn required no special explanation.”

The port-captain said nothing. I wrote everything down.

The weight-records, as it proved, did not require formal authorisation. The keeper of the shed — a thin boy of perhaps sixteen who had been assigned the task of guard and recorder by Rib-Hadda himself — had, in the shock of the morning’s events, forgotten to lock the door, and Hanno-Baal had observed this on arrival and said nothing. He simply walked to the shed while Ahmose-mer-Ra was occupied examining the drag-marks and opened the door and read.

I stood behind him and tried to see what he saw. The tablets were organised by season and by consignment — each load of copper ingots recorded by weight, by source shaft, and by destination vessel. They were, on their surface, orderly records. The kind that suggest competence.

“Here,” said Hanno-Baal, after a silence of perhaps twenty minutes. He had been working backwards through the seasons, comparing columns with his long fingers and an expression of settled concentration. “And here. And here.”

He showed me. I am no assessor, but even I could see it, once he pointed: the recorded weight of the ingots in the outgoing consignments was, with consistent regularity, fractionally higher than the weight of the same ingots as recorded in the shaft output tallies. The gap was small — small enough to appear as natural variation, as the imprecision inherent in stone weights and reed measures. But it was not random variation. Random variation distributes evenly, sometimes high, sometimes low. This variation was always in the same direction.

“For two years,” Hanno-Baal said, “Rib-Hadda recorded the miners’ output at true weight and then, in the outgoing consignment record, added between one-fortieth and one-thirtieth to each tally. The additional copper was real — he had it — because he had been systematically undercounting the miners’ production at the shaft, paying them for less than they produced, and accumulating the difference. The consignment records show the true quantity shipped. The shaft output records show the false quantity the miners were credited for.”

I understood it slowly, the way cold water reaches the skin. “He was stealing from the miners.”

“From men who have no tablets and no advocates and no harbour square in which to argue publicly. Men who count in sacks and rough measures and who depend on the overseer’s tally because they cannot read the overseer’s tablet.” His voice had not changed. It remained precise, informational. “Aziru saw it because Aziru has been working this trade for twenty years and knows what a standard consignment should weigh against a standard output. Aziru argued in public because Aziru is the kind of man who argues in public. And then someone who lived at Tamassos — someone who walked the road in darkness without irregularity, someone Rib-Hadda trusted enough to stand beside — decided that the argument, and whatever investigation it might provoke, was less tolerable than the man who caused it.”

“One of the miners.”

“Almost certainly. A man who had, perhaps, finally added his own years of short payment and arrived at the true sum.”

The light in the shed was the flat yellow of morning oil-lamps — we had lit two, small things that made our shadows enormous on the tablet-shelves. Outside, the furnace-smoke had begun its drift from the north. I heard, very clearly, the sound of the shaft wheel turning — men beginning the morning’s work below the earth, the same men whose labour had been measured small and sold for less than it was worth.

I thought about what Hanno-Baal had said: a man who had finally added his own years of short payment and arrived at the true sum. I thought about arriving at a sum like that. What it would feel like in the body. What it might make a man do, before dawn, on a road he had walked his whole working life.

He laid it before the port-captain in the courtyard that evening, with Ahmose-mer-Ra seated across from him and the Tamassos weight-records arranged between them on a linen cloth.

He spoke for some time, unhurried. He explained the mechanism of the fraud with the same tone he used when explaining anything — the tone of a patient teacher who has already understood his student’s confusion and is simply providing the path out of it. He showed the port-captain the columns. He explained the arithmetic. He explained what the gap between the two sets of records meant in practical terms — how much copper, over two years, how many men, how many seasons of short weight.

Ahmose-mer-Ra said nothing for a long time after.

Then: “The motive for killing him was, then, the dispute.”

“The dispute was the spark. The kindling was two years old.”

“If this record becomes public—” The port-captain stopped.

“The Alashiyan administration appointed Rib-Hadda. They certified his weights. They accepted his consignment records.” Hanno-Baal’s voice was still entirely neutral. “If the fraud is made public, the questions about administrative oversight become somewhat pointed. More pressingly, the consignment records — which now appear to show the true quantities shipped — contradict the official shaft output records, which means every receiving house in the eastern sea has tablets demonstrating they received more copper than was officially produced. The accounting difficulty would be considerable.”

Ahmose-mer-Ra looked at him for a long time. “What do you propose?”

“Aziru is innocent. I propose you know this, and that you act accordingly, and that the matter of who struck Rib-Hadda remains an open question in the official record — a robbery, perhaps, or an unknown enemy. Rib-Hadda was not, on examination, a man without enemies.” He paused. “I further propose that the weight-standards at Tamassos be quietly revised, and that the new overseer be a man whose arithmetic is both accurate and whose honesty is monitored with rather more attention than his predecessor’s.”

“And the man who—”

“There is no man, in the official record. There is an open question. The official record, as I understand it, is your domain entirely.”

Another silence, longer.

“Aziru,” said the port-captain finally, “will be released on the ground that the witness account was insufficient.”

“A sound administrative decision,” said Hanno-Baal.

Aziru was released the following morning. He came to our house that afternoon, still carrying the specific anger of a man who has been briefly imprisoned for something he did not do, and he sat in the courtyard and drank our wine and said several things about Cypriote justice that I will not copy here. Hanno-Baal listened with patient courtesy and said little.

At some point in the afternoon, when the worst of the heat had passed and the courtyard’s single olive tree was making its small shade, a man appeared at the gate. He was perhaps forty years, with the particular build of someone who has spent his working life underground — shoulders that slope forward, hands that are wide in the palms, a skin the colour of the Tamassos hillsides. He wore a miner’s kilt and carried nothing. He stood at the gate for a moment, looking at Hanno-Baal.

Hanno-Baal looked back at him. His face was, as always, unreadable by any means I possessed.

“There is a ship leaving for Byblos at the tide before dawn tomorrow,” Hanno-Baal said, without greeting, without preamble. “The shipmaster is called Abdi-Milki. He owes the house of Melqart-Azar a favour and will ask no questions about a passenger presented with this token.”

He held out a small cylinder seal — one of our house’s secondary marks, used for minor commercial letters. The man at the gate looked at it. He did not move.

“The official record,” Hanno-Baal said, “contains no man. It would be untidy if one appeared.”

The man at the gate crossed the courtyard, took the seal, and left without speaking. His sandals on the packed earth made almost no sound.

Aziru watched all of this and then looked at Hanno-Baal with an expression that was mostly bafflement and a small, reluctant admiration. “Do you know what you just did?”

“I balanced an account,” said Hanno-Baal. He picked up his wine cup, examined it, and set it down again without drinking. “The weight was never right. Now it is somewhat less wrong. That is, in my experience, the most that can usually be achieved.”

He said nothing else. He sat very still for a long time, and I could see the energy going back into him — the coil tightening again, the torpor settling over him like the smoke from the furnaces to the north. The case had been solved. The account balanced. There would be another one, or there would not, and the interval between was a kind of small death that he endured by habit if not by choice.

I wrote it all down. I do not know, even now, whether I was recording commerce or justice or something that has no name in any of the languages I know. I recorded it because it seemed to me that the weight of what he did — the precise calibration of it, the decision about what truth was worth releasing into the world and what truth was worth holding back — deserved to exist somewhere outside his own silent head.

The smoke from the Tamassos furnaces drifted south all that evening. Three furnaces, working. Full capacity, this time — or near enough to make no difference to a man who was not counting.

I noted this too, and cannot say exactly why.

Lower margin, later hand, ink partially flaked: the miner’s name is not recorded. The seal was found in a house in Byblos some years later, in a context the Byblos records do not explain. No connection was drawn.
