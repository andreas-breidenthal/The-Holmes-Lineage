AKAR-WOS

~1480 BC — Knossos, Minoan Crete

The name was the family’s old name-element — Akar, the Semitic thread that had run through the lineage since the great-grandfather’s migration to the Tyrian coast — grafted onto a Minoan sound that made it pronounceable to the palace functionaries among whom he grew up. Akar-wos was born on Crete to a Canaanite father and a Minoan mother, and grew up as the thoroughgoing hybrid that the island’s cosmopolitan culture made possible — Semitic in his commercial instincts and his forensic cast of mind, Minoan in his language and his knowledge of the palace economy, and possessed of the interpreter’s particular gift of seeing each world clearly because he was never entirely of either. He worked in the palace economy at Knossos as a merchant-interpreter, facilitating the transactions between the Minoan administration and the foreign traders — Egyptian, Syro-Palestinian, Cypriote, early Mycenaean — who came to the island in the considerable numbers that Knossos’s commercial dominance attracted.

Linear A, the Minoan script, is still undeciphered by modern scholars; Akar-wos read and wrote it as a matter of professional necessity, in the same way that his ancestors had read Sumerian or demotic Egyptian — as the local language of record that the gift was applied to, rather than as an end in itself. The tablets he produced in the course of his work — recording the terms of transactions, the quantities of goods, the obligations of counterparties — were among the administrative documents of a palace culture that would be destroyed within two generations, its script falling silent with it. He did not know that. He knew only that the accounts balanced, that the transactions were recorded accurately, and that a man who maintained the archive with honesty in a world where the archive was the institution was performing a service without which everything else failed.

The account of his work is preserved through the records maintained by his scribe, Lycon of the inland estates, a functionary assigned to assist with agricultural accounting who was drawn into Akar-wos’s orbit when discrepancies in storage and distribution required broader oversight. Lycon’s training was practical rather than scholarly, and his writing reflects a concern with outcomes rather than theory. He records events as they affected the functioning of the estate and the palace, and his account, though fragmentary, preserves the sequence of Akar-wos’s reasoning with unusual clarity.

The Account of Akar-wos

Recorded by Lycon of the Inland Estates, in the Matter of the Boy Kirwa-na

The oil had been wrong for three days before Akar-wos said anything.

I had not noticed it myself — I am a practical man, trained in the movement of grain and the counting of sheep, and I confess that the lamps of the western corridor held no particular meaning for me beyond the fact that they burned. But Akar-wos had noticed from the morning of the boy's disappearance, and it was only later, when he had assembled everything into its terrible and inevitable shape, that he explained what the smell had told him. The lamps in the storage wing were burning the second-grade pressed oil — the oil from the inland estates, thick and slightly bitter, the kind that leaves a particular heaviness in the air, the kind that the palace reserve never used except in the agricultural outbuildings. Someone had substituted it for the good oil without filing the exchange in the archive. And Akar-wos, who had read the archive that morning as he read it every morning, had known at once that the substitution was unrecorded, which meant it was not a clerical error. Which meant it was deliberate. Which meant that whoever had made the substitution had access to both the palace stores and the inland supply, and had reason not to wish the exchange documented.

He said nothing of this to me until the end. That was his way. He gathered and he waited, and when the moment came, he arranged the facts with the precision of a man who has already known the answer for some time and is now simply allowing others the experience of arriving there.

But I am moving ahead of myself. I should record the matter as it unfolded, for that is my function here, and I have learned that Akar-wos is poorly served by accounts that begin at the resolution rather than the beginning.

The boy was called Kirwa-na.

He was perhaps fifteen years, the apprentice and property of a Canaanite merchant named Baal-sharru, who operated under the palace's licensed trading arrangements and kept a small set of rooms in the eastern quarter. Baal-sharru had been at Knossos for three seasons — long enough to have established the useful relationships, short enough that his accounts had not yet been comprehensively audited — and he traded primarily in dyed cloth and the smaller luxury goods that moved easily along the Egyptian and Syro-Palestinian routes. He was not a man I had paid particular attention to. He was not a man, I think, that anyone had paid particular attention to, which was, as I later understood, precisely the condition he had cultivated.

Kirwa-na had been seen at the evening distribution on the fourth day of the month. He had not been seen on the fifth. Baal-sharru reported his absence to the palace administration with what I initially read as appropriate anxiety, and it was in the course of that report — filed with the duty functionary in the proper manner, assigned a tablet entry in the proper manner, allocated to the proper channel of inquiry — that Akar-wos first became involved.

He had not been assigned to the matter. I want to be clear on this point, because it bears on what came afterward. The duty functionary was a capable man named Orea-ko, and it was Orea-ko who held the formal warrant for inquiry. Akar-wos had simply been present in the archive room when Baal-sharru's report was delivered — he was there for his own purposes, the reconciliation of a Mycenaean cargo against the palace's import tallies — and something in the report had caused him to look up from the tablets he was reading.

I had been assigned to assist Orea-ko as a secondary recorder, which was how I came to be present. And I saw Akar-wos's eyes move to Baal-sharru's face with the particular attention he reserved for things that had snagged against his understanding of how the world was arranged.

He said nothing. Orea-ko completed his recording of the report, assured Baal-sharru that the matter would be pursued through the standard channels, and dismissed him. Akar-wos watched him go.

"He described the boy as his apprentice," he said, when Baal-sharru had passed out of the corridor. "Three times."

Orea-ko looked at him. "He is his apprentice."

"He is his apprentice," Akar-wos agreed. He had already turned back to his tablets. "Three times in a report of perhaps a hundred words is a frequency that suggests the word is doing work the speaker does not wish us to examine."

Orea-ko was a good functionary — methodical, fair, and concerned with procedure. He was not, I think, constitutionally inclined toward the lateral movement of thought that Akar-wos performed as easily as breathing. He noted Akar-wos's observation with professional courtesy and returned to the standard channels. The standard channels led, as they generally did in cases of missing persons of low standing, toward the harbor: a runaway, probably. A boy who had found passage on an outbound ship, or fallen into bad company among the sailors. Orea-ko sent his inquiry to the harbor registrar and settled down to wait for a response.

Akar-wos went to the archive.

I followed him, which perhaps requires explanation.

I had been assigned to assist with the inquiry, but Orea-ko had dismissed me with the observation that there was nothing to be done until the harbor registrar responded. I was free until the afternoon. I had been working alongside Akar-wos for two months by this time, long enough to understand that when he moved with that particular quality of focused quietness — the slight tightening around the eyes, the hands that ceased their habitual tablet-smoothing and hung still at his sides — he had seen something that repaid attention. I am a practical man, as I have said. I followed.

The archive at Knossos is a room that rewards patience. The tablets are stored in clay bins along the walls, organized by category and season, and the smell of the place is the smell of the administrative work of the palace — the particular dry-dust smell of baked clay, and beneath it, always, the faint residue of the good oil that keeps the lamps burning through the long sessions of recording. Except that on this particular morning, as I have noted, the smell was wrong. The second-grade inland oil, thick and slightly rancid. I noticed it only because Akar-wos paused in the doorway and breathed deliberately, and in doing so directed my attention to what I would otherwise have passed through without registering.

He did not remark on it then. He went to the storage tallies.

The storage tallies record the movement of goods into and out of the palace's warehouses — the great magazines along the western wing, and the subsidiary storage facilities that served the estate network. They are not, in the ordinary course of administration, interesting documents. They record quantities and categories and the identifying marks of the officials who authorized each movement. I had spent considerable portions of the previous two years working with them, and I can attest that they are thorough, accurate, and very nearly entirely tedious.

Akar-wos read them with the absorption of a man engaged in a text of great pleasure.

After perhaps an hour, during which I occupied myself with a re-examination of the Mycenaean cargo tallies that had occupied him before Baal-sharru's arrival, he looked up and said, without any particular emphasis:

"How many estates does the inland network maintain?"

I told him. Fourteen principal estates, seven subsidiary holdings.

"And the estates that supply the secondary lamp oil — the pressed oil, not the cold-extraction — how many of those?"

Three, I said. The holdings of Tarwi-sa, of the southern valley estate network, and the estate managed by a man named Dawu-rimi whose family had held the management contract for the better part of a generation.

"Dawu-rimi," Akar-wos said. He looked at the tally tablet in his hand for a moment, then set it down with the care he gave everything that had become evidence. "And what is the distance from the palace to Dawu-rimi's estate?"

Half a day, I told him. Perhaps a little more in the late-season heat.

"Then we should start now," he said, "if we are to have light for the return."

I should record what Orea-ko said when we informed him of our intention, because it is illustrative of the procedural difficulty that attended Akar-wos's methods throughout his career at Knossos.

Orea-ko pointed out, correctly, that he was the assigned investigator. That the standard inquiry was proceeding through the harbor registrar. That an excursion to a private estate required either invitation or administrative authorization from a superior, and that neither was currently in hand. That Dawu-rimi's family held a long-standing relationship with the palace that made precipitous action politically inconvenient. And that if Akar-wos had an observation to contribute to the inquiry, the appropriate method was to record it on a tablet and submit it through the standard filing process.

Akar-wos listened to all of this with the attentiveness he brought to everything.

"The boy may be there now," he said.

"The boy may be at the harbor now," Orea-ko said.

"The harbor registrar's response will arrive tomorrow morning at the earliest. If the boy is at the harbor, he will still be findable tomorrow morning. If the boy is at the estate, tomorrow morning is not the same question."

There was a silence. Orea-ko was a fair man. I believe he recognized the logic. But he was also a careful functionary in an administration that rewarded careful functionaries, and private estates with long-standing palace relationships were precisely the territory where careful functionaries did not tread without authorization.

"I cannot authorize the excursion," he said finally.

"I am not asking for authorization," Akar-wos said, with a mildness that contained nothing mild in it. "I am informing you of my movements, as a courtesy."

He turned and walked out. I followed him. This was becoming a habit.

The road to the inland estates follows the river valley out of Knossos, and in the late-season heat it is a road that demands respect. The dust rises with every step and settles on the skin like a second garment, and the olive trees along the verges give a broken, inadequate shade that teases rather than relieves. We walked in the company of a supply driver who was making the return journey with an empty cart — Akar-wos had arranged this in perhaps four minutes of conversation at the transport yard, with a facility that always slightly surprised me, given his general disdain for unnecessary social commerce.

He was quiet for much of the journey. This was not the quiet of a man with nothing to say, but the quiet of a man whose mind was fully occupied and who had simply suspended the social portion of his functioning until the work was complete. I had learned to read the distinction, and to provide conversation only when he showed the particular slight restlessness that meant he wanted to think aloud.

Somewhere past the midpoint of the journey, in the deep shade of a cork oak stand where the driver had stopped to water the oxen, he spoke.

"Tell me what you know of Baal-sharru's trading arrangements," he said.

I told him what I knew, which was not extensive. Licensed trader, cloth and luxury goods, three seasons established, accounts in reasonable order.

"And his relationship with the estate network?"

None that I was aware of. His operations were coastal and maritime, not agricultural.

"Then why," Akar-wos said, with the quality of a man producing something he has been holding for some time, "does a quantity of inland lamp oil — second-grade pressed oil of the variety produced exclusively at Dawu-rimi's estate, in a quantity consistent with perhaps three large storage jars — appear in the palace magazine records as received against a transaction authorized by Baal-sharru's trading license, two days before the boy disappeared?"

I stared at him.

"The authorization is irregular," he continued, with the particular pleasure he took in arrangements of fact. "Baal-sharru holds a coastal trading license. He has no authorization to participate in the inland estate supply chain. The transaction was recorded, which is why it exists in the archive — but it was recorded in such a way as to require cross-referencing three separate tally categories to find it, which is not how an authorized transaction would be recorded. An authorized transaction is simple. This transaction was complex. Complexity of that kind, in a record-keeping system as standardized as the palace archive, is not the result of confusion. It is the result of effort."

He had spent the hour in the archive not verifying whether a transaction existed, I understood, but following backwards the chain of what that transaction implied about relationships that should not exist.

"Baal-sharru," I said slowly, "received oil from Dawu-rimi's estate."

"Received oil from Dawu-rimi, yes. In a quantity that would represent a significant portion of the secondary estate's discretionary surplus. The question is what Dawu-rimi received in return, and why neither party wished that exchange to appear straightforwardly in the archive."

The driver was re-harnessing the oxen. We rose and followed the cart back into the dust and the heat.

"The boy," I said.

"The boy," Akar-wos agreed. "Who is described, three times in a hundred words, as Baal-sharru's apprentice. Which is the legal category that carries the most extensive obligations of personal custody and the most legally awkward questions if the custodian can be shown to have surrendered that custody as surety."

I understood then. Not the full architecture of it — that came later — but the shape. Baal-sharru had a debt to Dawu-rimi, or an obligation of some kind that he did not wish recorded in the normal fashion. The oil was payment, or part of payment. And the boy, the apprentice over whom Baal-sharru held legal custodial responsibility, had been sent to the estate as surety for some portion of the arrangement that the oil had not yet settled.

"He reported the disappearance," I said. "Wouldn't that—"

"Expose him? Only if the report were properly followed. The harbor registrar's inquiry leads outward, to the ships and the sailors. It does not lead inland. Baal-sharru knew this. Reporting the disappearance was the act of a man establishing his own innocence within the system he was confident would not find the answer."

He paused, and then said something I have remembered precisely in all the years since, because it seemed to me to contain something essential about how he understood his work.

"He forgot that the archive remembers everything. Every tablet. Every authorization. Every gram of oil. He thought the complexity of the arrangement made it invisible. But complexity in a standardized system is not invisibility. It is a signature."

Dawu-rimi's estate announced itself before we saw it: the smell of pressed olives and the sharper smell of the secondary oil in production, and then the low stone walls of the outbuildings appearing through the heat haze, and the gate with its particular bronze hinge that gave a two-note creak as the driver's cart passed through — the lower note first, then the higher, a sound so specific to that gate and that hinge that I can still produce it exactly in memory, thirty years later, as clearly as if I stood before it now.

The estate manager himself came out to meet us. He was a compact, capable-looking man of perhaps fifty, with the weathered look of someone who had spent his working life outdoors, and his expression when he saw Akar-wos held a particular quality I recognized — the expression of a man who had prepared a set of answers for a question and was now calculating whether the question he was being asked was the question he had prepared for.

Akar-wos gave him no time to calculate.

"I am here on behalf of the palace administration," he said, with a formality that was technically accurate and substantively somewhat elastic. "I am inquiring after a boy named Kirwa-na, an apprentice of the trader Baal-sharru, who has been reported missing from the palace quarter. The storage tallies record a transaction between this estate and Baal-sharru's trading license two days ago. I would like to reconcile the record."

Dawu-rimi said, with the steadiness of a man who had thought about this possibility, that he had no knowledge of any boy. That the transaction was a routine supply arrangement. That the archive would confirm everything was in order.

"The archive does confirm everything," Akar-wos said pleasantly. "That is why I am here and not at the harbor."

The silence between them was the particular silence of two men who understand each other precisely.

Then, from somewhere in the interior of the main outbuilding — behind the heavy linen hanging that served as a door in the heat of the season, behind the smell of the pressed oil and the dust of the threshing floor — came the sound of someone moving. A small, careful movement, the movement of a person who has been very still for a long time and has made the error of shifting weight at the wrong moment.

Akar-wos did not look toward the sound. He kept his eyes on Dawu-rimi, and he said, very quietly:

"The boy."

It was not a question.

Kirwa-na was fifteen years old and had been at the estate for four days. He was not, in the physical sense, harmed. He had been housed in the outbuilding, fed adequately, kept away from the other estate workers. He was, in every sense that the arrangements between Baal-sharru and Dawu-rimi had structured, a piece of security — a pledge held against payment, the same function that a jar of oil or a bolt of dyed cloth might serve, but with the distinction that he was also a person, and that the palace administration's laws regarding the custody of apprentices were considerably more explicit than its laws regarding the custody of jars.

When the hanging was pulled aside and he came blinking into the afternoon light — slight, dark-eyed, with the look of someone who has spent four days being carefully frightened — Akar-wos looked at him for a moment with what I can only describe as a rigorous absence of sentiment. It was not coldness, exactly. It was the look of a man confirming that the last element of a calculation has resolved correctly.

Then he said, in the local Canaanite tongue — which I understood only partially, and which I record here as best I could reconstruct — something that I believe translated approximately as: You are found. Do not speak yet. There will be time.

The question of what happened afterward is one I have considered carefully in deciding how to record this account, because the resolution was not, in the strict procedural sense, the resolution that the palace administration would have sanctioned.

Orea-ko arrived the following morning with the harbor registrar's response — negative, as Akar-wos had anticipated — and with, I suspect, a degree of private chagrin at having been outmaneuvered. He interviewed the boy, interviewed Dawu-rimi, and prepared the formal inquiry documents.

Baal-sharru was questioned. The transaction in the archive was scrutinized. The debt arrangement between the two men — which had its origins in a cargo dispute of the previous season, the details of which I never fully understood — was examined.

And here is what I must record carefully: the formal outcome was, on the face of it, a resolution in favor of all parties. The boy was returned to the palace quarter. The transaction was re-filed under the appropriate category with appropriate documentation. Dawu-rimi paid a fine consistent with procedural irregularities in the supply chain. Baal-sharru received a formal notation in his trading license regarding the improper deployment of an apprentice as surety, a notation that would complicate but not end his operations at Knossos.

No one went before the palace court. The arrangement between the two men was acknowledged to exist, its irregular elements corrected, its core terms permitted to stand.

I asked Akar-wos, on the evening of Orea-ko's formal resolution, whether this was justice.

He considered the question with the attention it deserved.

"Baal-sharru is a bad man," he said, "in the specific and limited sense that he was willing to use the boy as a token in a transaction. He is not a singular criminal. He is a merchant who encountered a debt he did not wish to acknowledge openly and resolved it through a method that the systems of the archive do not easily accommodate. Dawu-rimi received a boy instead of oil and kept him uncomfortable but intact for four days, which tells me that Dawu-rimi was making a point, not building a habit. The boy is returned. The debt is resolved through proper channels. The archive is corrected."

"And Baal-sharru?"

"Baal-sharru will be more careful with his archives in future." He paused. "Which is perhaps more useful than a court proceeding that would involve the boy testifying to arrangements that he only partially understood, that would require Dawu-rimi's family's palace relationships to be examined in ways that would disrupt several legitimate supply agreements, and that would result in Baal-sharru's license being revoked, his operation concluded, and the boy — who has nowhere else to go — left without a master or a livelihood."

He picked up the tablet he had been working on, the eternal reconciliation of one account against another.

"The archive is the institution," he said. "I serve the archive. The archive records what happened. What happened is that a boy was held improperly and then returned. The record reflects this. That is what the archive can do."

I wrote all of this down. It is my function. I record what I observe, and I leave the question of what it means to those who read it.

What I observed was this: a boy came blinking into the afternoon light at Dawu-rimi's estate, and Akar-wos looked at him with rigorous, careful attention, and said in the local Canaanite tongue that there would be time.

There was time. The boy was found.

The oil in the western corridor lamps was replaced with the correct grade the following week. The smell went back to what it should have been. I noticed it only because Akar-wos paused in the doorway on the morning it changed and breathed, once, with what I recognized, by then, as satisfaction.

He went in and began to read the archive.

Here the account ends. The tablets from which this record was drawn were recovered from the ruins of Knossos after its final destruction. The script (Linear A) remains undeciphered, but the sequence and context preserved in later Mycenaean administrative layers allow this reconstruction. The archive, as Akar-wos served it, outlasted the palace that housed it.
