SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅳ

1945 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was born in September 1945, six weeks after the Japanese surrender, into a household where the war's end had produced not relief but a particular quality of English silence that his American relatives found difficult to interpret and his mother had long since learned not to attempt to explain. His father's silences were not unhappy; they were the silences of a man who had spent a professional lifetime reading what other people concealed, and who had made, on arriving in America, a private decision to direct that faculty inward rather than outward — to observe his own life, for once, with the same attention he had previously given to the lives of others. He was, by his son's account, an exceptional father: present, attentive, and constitutionally incapable of pretending not to know things he knew, which meant that the usual parental fictions were unavailable to him and that the boy grew up in a household of considerable, occasionally startling, transparency.

He inherited the gift fully and expressed it in the language of his time and place, which was the language of the law. He read at Harvard, was admitted to the Massachusetts bar in 1969, and spent the early years of his career in a Boston commercial law firm before moving to the federal practice that would occupy the remainder of his professional life. His particular facility was financial fraud — cases built not on witness testimony or physical evidence but on documentation: the ten thousand pages of ledgers, correspondence, incorporation filings, and banking records that told, to a reader with the patience and the precision to follow them, a story entirely different from the one their authors had intended. He was not, his colleagues noted, a brilliant advocate; he did not perform in court with any of the theatrical authority that juries respond to. He was something rarer and, in the federal context, considerably more useful: a man who could read a fraudulent financial structure from its documentary residue the way his great-great-grandfather had read a criminal from his boots and his cuffs, and who could explain what he had found in terms precise enough to survive cross-examination by the best commercial attorneys in New York.

There is, attached to his papers, a private record — compiled across several decades, in the spare and precise prose he brought to everything — of his childhood visits to the Sussex cottage. His father had taken him to England several times during the first decade of his life, and Holmes Ⅳ had understood, even as a small boy, that these journeys were not ordinary ones. The record he kept is not a diary; it has no dates and no sequence of entries. It is instead a set of recollections, written down at intervals as they became clear to him in retrospect: the smell of the cottage; the quality of the light over the Downs in different weathers; the old man's hands, which he remembered as precise and still; the bees, which were everywhere in summer and which his great-grandfather discussed with an earnestness that a child could follow, and that Holmes Ⅳ came to understand, much later, was not a simplification for the child's benefit but the genuine thing — the same complete attention brought to the same subject that had once been brought to criminal networks and ciphers and the unrecorded history of four thousand years.

What the record preserves most precisely is the quality of conversation. The Great Detective, in his final decade, did not perform for visitors, not even for the smallest ones. He asked questions. He listened to the answers. He asked more questions. Holmes Ⅳ, who was three or four or five years old, answered them with the directness of a child who had not yet learned the adult habit of managing what he disclosed. The old man noted what the boy said with the concentration of a man still in practice — not indulging a great-grandchild, but attending to an intelligence he had taken the measure of and found, in the characteristic manner of the line, worth attending to. Holmes Ⅳ could not have articulated this at the time. He articulated it, with considerable precision, in the private record he kept, across the long years of his career in the law. It was, he wrote, the most complete attention he had ever been given. He did not think he had ever been given it again, by anyone, in quite the same way.

In later years, Holmes Ⅳ's records note visits from Dr. Amelia Watson, a contemporary of his own generation and a direct descendant of John H. Watson. She had, for some months, been keeping a personal chronicle of Boston medical and legal affairs, which she shared with Holmes Ⅳ during discussions that ranged from clinical ethics to forensic procedure. It was in these sessions, often held over the clatter of a distant hospital ward or the rustle of financial files, that she first began to record the remarkable acuity with which he approached complex human behaviors and financial structures. The account she maintained — her own voice — would become a primary repository for the observation of Holmes Ⅳ in action, capturing his methods and temperament from a witness uniquely positioned to note both the gift and its occasional blind spots.

The Matter of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer

From the private chronicle of Dr. Amelia Watson, maintained in parallel with her clinical rounds at Massachusetts General Hospital, and not intended for publication during the lifetimes of those named herein.

I.

There are cities whose November is merely seasonal, and there is Boston. Those who know it well will understand the distinction without further elaboration; those who do not may be told that the harbour wind off the water carries, at that time of year, not merely cold but a quality of institutional judgement — as though the city had reviewed one’s conduct for the preceding eleven months and found the file wanting in several particulars. The elm trees along Commonwealth Avenue had shed their leaves with what I can only describe as professional relief.

I had spent the morning in the trauma unit reviewing cases that bore, in their human incompleteness, an uncomfortable resemblance to the criminal proceedings I had been, for the preceding eighteen months, permitted to observe at closer range than most physicians enjoy. I arrived at the McCormack Building at half past one carrying two coffees from the cart in the lobby — a precaution I had learned was not hospitality but clinical necessity, since the man I was coming to see had almost certainly not eaten since before six and would not remember to do so until considerably after dark, if at all.

Holmes was not in the outer office.

I was still removing my coat when the outer door was flung open behind me with a violence that sent it cracking against the wall and set the coat-stand rocking on its base. The man who stumbled through it was well-dressed, or had been that morning — his overcoat hung open despite the cold, his collar was askew, and his face was the particular shade of grey I have observed in patients who have arrived at hospital already suspecting their situation is considerably worse than they have yet been told.

“Holmes!” he said, and then, finding the room apparently empty, turned to me with the wild, reddened eyes of a man who has not slept. “Where is he? I was told — I must see him — my name is DiMarco, Anthony DiMarco, I was lead counsel on the Aldrich, Voss case, and I have perhaps forty-eight hours before—”

The interior door opened. Holmes stood in the frame, and whatever he had read in the sound of that entrance — and I have no doubt he had read a considerable amount — his face showed nothing but sharp, forward attention.

“Mr. DiMarco,” he said. “Come in.”

He steered the man into the chair by the desk with a firm efficiency and stood over him with his head at the angle I have recorded, in these pages, as indicating active and hungry focus. I set one of the coffees before DiMarco. He did not notice it.

Holmes, however, did not immediately invite him to speak. He stood quite still for a moment, his gaze moving from DiMarco’s shoes — dark with recent rain — to the pale creases in his overcoat where it had been gripped and released repeatedly, to a faint smear of toner on the inside of his right cuff, dark grey and slightly waxy, of the kind produced by high-volume laser printers rather than standard office equipment. Then he straightened.

“You came on foot from the federal courthouse,” said Holmes. “The last three blocks, at least — Congress Street is impassable at this hour and you could not wait for a cab. You have spent the better part of the morning in the basement printing room of this building, reviewing your own discovery submissions.” He paused. “You have not eaten since yesterday. Sit back, Mr. DiMarco. You are hyperventilating, and it is impairing your account.”

DiMarco stared at him. Then, with the particular obedience of a man who has run out of resistance, he sat back.

“Tell me everything,” said Holmes. “From the beginning. Leave nothing out, however trivial it appears. The trivial detail, Mr. DiMarco, is nearly always the load-bearing wall.”

DiMarco told us.

The substance of it was this: three days prior, the U.S. Attorney’s office had received an anonymous tip alleging that certain documents in the Aldrich, Voss discovery record were forgeries — introduced by DiMarco himself to strengthen a case that, without them, he could not prove to the required standard. The tip had included a technical analysis of the documents’ embedded metadata, purporting to show that timestamps had been manipulated to make recently-created forgeries appear to date from the early 1980s. The case had been suspended pending investigation.

“I swear to you, Holmes,” said DiMarco, leaning forward with his hands gripping the arms of the chair, “I never touched those documents. I could not have produced them — I hold a first-generation licence for Document Management Suite ’91, which records timestamps in an entirely different embedded format from the one cited in that tip. But the evidence as it stands is damning, and the case has been suspended, and if someone does not establish within the week that I am being framed—” He stopped. “My career. My freedom. Everything.”

Holmes, who had been pacing the length of the room with his hands thrust into his coat pockets, halted abruptly. His eyes were bright — the particular brightness I had learned to associate not with pleasure but with the first clear scent of a problem worth solving.

“Watson,” said he, without turning. “Your coat.”

“My coat?”

“Fetch it. We are going to the financial district.”

He was already at the door. He paused, turned, and fixed DiMarco with a look that was, in its way, more reassuring than any spoken comfort.

“The documents,” he said, “will tell us who made them. They always do. Sit here until we return. Mrs. Hendricks will bring you tea.”

And he was in the corridor, and I was scrambling for my coat and bag, and the door was swinging shut behind us.

II.

Anthony DiMarco. I knew the name from the files I had been permitted to review. He had been Holmes’s lead attorney on the Aldrich, Voss case — a decade’s work, a securities collapse that had consumed the retirement contributions of several thousand textile workers in the Connecticut River Valley, and which Holmes had been patiently, document by document, connecting to a pattern of deliberate misrepresentation at the senior partnership level of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer, one of the investment houses that continued to treat the concept of federal oversight as a matter of ongoing philosophical negotiation rather than settled law.

In the cab, Holmes was silent, his eyes fixed on the middle distance, his fingers tapping a rapid, irregular rhythm against his knee. I had learned that this particular rhythm meant the mind was sorting at speed.

“You believe him,” I said.

“I believe the software licence.” He glanced at me. “The tip specified embedded metadata fields that do not appear in the legal literature on document fraud. That is not a detail a lawyer would know to include. It will matter.”

“Who benefits,” I said, “from the case being suspended?”

He looked at me then with the expression I have catalogued, in these pages, as the one he wears when a person has asked precisely the right question without quite understanding the weight of what they have asked.

“Now,” he said quietly, “you are beginning to observe.”

He stood — insofar as one can stand in a Boston cab — and reached, unnecessarily, for his coat.

“One more thing,” he said, without looking at me. “When we are in Pryce’s office and I ask about the software version — you will drop your coffee.”

“I beg your pardon?”

“Your coffee. On the floor. Make a considerable business of it. Apologies, efforts to contain the damage, the full performance. I need approximately sixty seconds in which Pryce is not looking at the terminal.”

He said nothing further. I looked at the coffee in my hand — the one I had brought from the lobby cart, which I had been carrying for the better part of an hour — and reflected that I had, over eighteen months, been asked to do stranger things in the service of a case.

“We have an errand,” he said.

III.

The offices of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer occupied three floors of a financial district tower built in the late 1970s in the architectural style I think of as Aspirational Granite — a building that had wished to communicate permanence and communicated instead the anxious striving of a decade that had not yet learned to trust its own prosperity. The lobby smelled of forced-air heating and the carpet cleaner used in every institution that considers itself adjacent to federal authority. There were no windows visible from reception. I had been in enough such buildings to understand that this was not an accident.

We had not been invited. That, too, was not an accident.

Holmes, however, did not go immediately inside. He stopped at the corner of the block, at a public payphone mounted to the wall beside a dry-cleaner’s, and stood for a moment looking at it with his hands in his coat pockets. The phone was positioned such that a man standing at it could look directly up at the third floor of the tower — at the bank of windows, partially obscured by blinds, that corresponded to the compliance department’s position in the building’s floor plan.

“The tip was placed from a financial district payphone,” I said, understanding.

“From this one, I believe. The location is in the access records.” He looked up at the windows for a moment longer. “He would have been able to see whether the lights were on. Whether anyone was working late.” He turned from the phone and moved toward the entrance. “Come.”

In the corridor outside the compliance department, a man came to meet us whom Holmes had named in the cab, without elaboration, as Gerald Pryce. He was a man of careful surfaces — a suit of conservative good quality, a face composed, with evident effort, into an expression of cooperative inconvenience. He held a procedural manual with the unconscious possessiveness of a man who has, over eleven years, found in procedure a reliable substitute for instinct.

Holmes presented his credentials. He asked, in the measured tone of a man exercising a right he could, if pressed, document exhaustively, to use the firm’s document retrieval system to verify several items in discovery. He asked this simply, pleasantly, and with a patience that suggested he had all afternoon and no particular feelings about how it was spent.

Pryce consulted the manual. Then he closed it.

“I’m afraid that won’t be possible, Mr. Holmes. Outside access to discovery materials requires a formal written request, processed through legal counsel, with a minimum turnaround of ten working days. Those are the firm’s procedures and I’m not in a position to vary them.”

Holmes received this with a pleasant nod, as though Pryce had said something eminently sensible that required only a moment’s reflection to accommodate.

“Of course,” said he. “Entirely understood.” He turned, as if to go — and then stopped, patting his coat pockets with a mildly distracted air. “Forgive me — before we go, could you confirm one small administrative detail? The software version currently running on your compliance workstations. It will help us specify the correct metadata format in the formal request and spare everyone a second round of correspondence.”

Pryce frowned. This was, he supposed, a clerical question — reasonable enough. He turned to the filing cabinet at his left and drew open the top drawer.

I dropped my coffee.

It struck the edge of the desk and went comprehensively across the carpet and the lower shelf of Pryce’s procedural manuals, and I fear my apologies were as voluble as the situation demanded. I produced tissues from my bag and crouched over the manuals with what I hope was convincing distress, and Pryce, whose instinct for the preservation of institutional documents was apparently stronger than his suspicion, crouched with me.

When we straightened, Holmes was standing precisely where he had been, consulting his notebook with an expression of mild professional abstraction.

“My apologies,” said Holmes, pleasantly, when Pryce turned. “I see the documentation is here after all. We shall submit the formal request as you suggest.” He smiled. “Thank you for your time, Mr. Pryce. You have been most helpful.”

In the corridor, walking toward the lifts, I glanced back.

Pryce stood in the doorway of the compliance department. His face had been re-composed into an expression I could not quite read — not relief, not alarm, but something that lay between them, with calculation having rather the better of it.

“Well?” I said, when the lift doors had closed.

“We have what we need.”

“Which is?”

“Data,” said Holmes. And the lift descended.

We did not, however, leave the building.

Holmes stopped at the ground floor, stood quite still for a moment, and turned back toward the stairs. “No,” said he, as though concluding an internal argument. “There is one thing more.”

IV.

What followed occupied approximately forty minutes.

I will say plainly that I understood, during those forty minutes, perhaps a third of what Holmes was doing — and that third only in the most general sense. The first five minutes, however, I understood entirely.

Holmes went back up the stairs and pushed open the compliance department door without knocking. Pryce, who had returned to his desk, looked up with an expression that began as irritation and passed through several intermediate stages with considerable speed as Holmes crossed the room and stood before him.

“I must ask your forgiveness,” said Holmes, pleasantly. “I said I had found the documentation I needed. That was not entirely accurate. I found something rather more interesting.” He drew out his notebook and set it open on the desk, turned so that Pryce could read the single figure he had copied. He said nothing further for a moment. He simply waited.

Pryce looked at the notebook. His polished face went the colour of old parchment.

“March 1987,” said Holmes, quietly. “The background timestamp. The system remembered.” He let that sit. “Systems are reliable in that way.”

Pryce said nothing. His hand moved, very slightly, toward the telephone on the desk.

“Watson — the door, if you would.”

I moved to it. Pryce’s eyes went there and back, and something in them had passed through several intermediate stages to arrive at a place beyond calculation — a still, cold recognition that whatever architecture he had spent eleven years constructing had just been read, in its entirety, by a man who had been in this room for less than two minutes.

“I have no authority to detain you,” said Holmes, with the manner of a man completing a document, “and I want to be clear on that point. But I have everything I require for a submission to the federal prosecutor, and I intend to make it within forty-eight hours. What you do in the interim is your affair entirely.”

He collected his notebook and thanked Pryce with a courtesy that was entirely genuine and entirely opaque.

We left Gerald Pryce sitting perfectly still behind his desk, with the expression of a man who has spent eleven years waiting for a particular moment and has discovered that all the waiting in the world is not the same as being prepared.

The remaining thirty-five minutes were the methodical work. I watched him move through the system with the unhurried focus he brings to any structure he is reading for the first time: not quickly, not slowly, but at the pace of comprehension rather than the pace of performance. He paused at intervals to copy figures into his notebook in the script he uses for his own use rather than for the record — small, compressed, written as though space itself were a resource to be conserved.

He examined, at several points, information that appeared to lie at the edges of the documents themselves — header fields, technical notations, dates. The years 1981, 1983, and 1985 appeared with some regularity. The year 1987 appeared once, and Holmes was still for a moment when it did, in the manner of a man who has found a word in a foreign text whose meaning he had been waiting to confirm.

He made no remark. When I asked, once, what he was looking for, he said “Dates,” and returned to the screen with an expression that made clear this was the complete answer.

When he was satisfied — and I knew he was satisfied because he closed his notebook and straightened his cuffs, which was what he did when a thing was finished — he collected his coat and we left without further exchange with anyone.

V.

Back at the McCormack Building, he spread the printouts across the floor — chronologically by document creation rather than by document date, a distinction I noted without yet understanding — and sat in the low leather chair and looked at them without speaking. I reviewed my own notes. The heating ticked. Outside, the harbour was going grey in the early dark.

“Holmes,” I said, at last. “What is March 1987?”

He looked up.

“You said it to Pryce and the man went white. You wrote it in the notebook and set it before him like a verdict. I have been turning it over for the last hour and I cannot construct the mechanism. What does the date mean?”

He rose and went to the whiteboard.

“The documents,” he said, taking up the marker, “are forged.” He wrote a date. “I want to be precise about this, because there is a tendency, when a fraud is exposed, to retroactively characterise it as obvious. That is dishonest, and it serves no analytical purpose. The forgeries are skillfully done. Paper stock correct for the period. Typeface correct. Letterhead an accurate reproduction. The figures cited are internally consistent — and more critically, they are consistent with the authentic record. They do not contradict what legitimately exists. They supplement it. They tell a story that supports the fabrication’s intended narrative, and they tell it convincingly.”

“But they are still forgeries.”

“They are forgeries because of when they were made.” He wrote a second date beside the first. “They entered discovery as scanned files — digitised documents. The metadata embedded in those files shows creation timestamps consistent with the dates printed on the documents. 1981. 1983. 1985. The anonymous tip alleged that these timestamps had been manipulated — that someone had adjusted the clock of the scanning workstation before scanning recently-made forgeries, to make them appear decades old.”

“And had they been?”

“No.” He set down the marker. “The timestamps are genuine. They accurately record when the scanning occurred.” He let that sit for a moment. “The scanning occurred in 1987. Which means the documents were created in 1987. They bear dates of 1981, 1983, and 1985. The forger did not alter the clock — because the forger did not know, or did not remember, that the version of document management software installed on the firm’s own system in 1987 recorded creation timestamps as a background process. The previous version had no such function. The 1987 update introduced it. It is not mentioned in the user manual. It appears in the technical release notes, which run to fourteen pages, in a register that does not invite casual reading.”

I worked through this carefully. “Then — the forgeries were created on the firm’s own system.”

“On the workstation assigned to the compliance department.”

“And DiMarco—”

“DiMarco holds a licence for the 1991 version of the same software, which records timestamps in an entirely different embedded format — a format that would have been immediately visible in the metadata of these files, had he used his own system to produce them. He did not produce them. He could not have produced them. The documents were made in 1987. DiMarco did not join the case until 1988. The software licence he is alleged to have exploited did not exist commercially until 1991.”

The heating made a sound like a long, slow exhalation.

“Pryce,” I said.

“Pryce.” He did not seem satisfied. He seemed precise, which is a different condition. “The anonymous tip was placed from a public telephone in the financial district at a time when the compliance department’s after-hours access log records Pryce as present in the building. The technical analysis in the tip references specific embedded metadata fields — fields that do not appear in the legal literature on document fraud, that are not intuitive, and that require precisely the secondary knowledge of financial systems technology that a person who obtained the relevant qualification in 1983 would possess.” He paused. “He processed every discovery request I submitted. He knew which documents I had seen and which I had not. He knew the date range I intended to examine next — because my request pattern told him so.”

I looked up. “He read your request pattern.”

“As a document,” Holmes said. “Yes. He read it as a document, identified the gap, and inserted the forgeries into the next production batch with surgical precision.” He was quiet for a moment. “He is not unintelligent. Under other circumstances—”

He did not complete the sentence. I did not ask him to.

VI.

The matter of Gerald Pryce occupied the U.S. Attorney’s office for seven months. The charges against DiMarco were withdrawn within a week of Holmes’s submission to the federal prosecutor — sixty-one pages, which those competent to judge have described as a document of some forensic elegance. The charges against Aldrich, Voss & Mercer’s senior partners were reinstated. The pension recovery proceedings resumed. Pryce entered a plea.

Holmes’s technical analysis — the demonstration of the forgery through the timestamp evidence — is part of the public federal record. The observation that Pryce had read Holmes’s investigative sequence as itself a structure, and had introduced the forgery into the precise gap between requests, appears nowhere in the official submission. It did not need to appear. The metadata was sufficient. Holmes, who has a lawyer’s understanding that sufficiency is its own discipline, did not volunteer the additional layer.

I have recorded it here.

VII.

On the evening before his submission, Holmes sat for a long time in the low leather chair without speaking. I was working on my own notes. The window behind him showed the harbour lights through rain, and the room had that particular November cold of old federal building windows — metal and damp stone and the ghost of decades of institutional paper.

At length he rose and went to the telephone on the corner of the desk. He made two calls, brief and quiet, in the manner of a man verifying rather than inquiring. When he set the receiver down he stood for a moment with his hand still resting on it, and the expression on his face was the one I have learned to read as the closing of a file.

“He was protecting someone,” Holmes said, without preamble.

I looked up.

“Pryce.” He was looking at the middle distance with the expression of a man reviewing a completed calculation and finding it unchanged. “The forgeries were made in 1987. The case that would require them did not exist until 1989. He made them in advance. Which means he knew, in 1987, what the investigation would eventually look for. Which means someone inside the firm told him — in 1987 — what the documentation would eventually reveal.” He paused. “You noticed the photograph on his desk? When we were in the compliance office?”

I thought back. “A sailing yacht.”

“A sailing yacht. Named The Mary Mercer.” He returned to the low leather chair and sat, and I noticed, for the first time that evening, that at some point in the preceding hour he had sent out for food — and that the container on the side table was half empty. He was eating. The case was closing. “The first call confirmed it: the vessel is co-registered to Arthur Mercer — senior partner of this firm — and to one Gerald R. Pryce.” He paused. “The second call was to a contact in the Suffolk County records office. Pryce’s mother’s maiden name was Mercer. Arthur Mercer’s half-sister — a connection never publicly acknowledged. He did this to protect that person. He was placed in that compliance department by Mercer in 1978 — eleven years with full access to the documentation, and loyalty of the kind that is not, in my experience, purchased. It is inherited.” He set down his pipe. “When Mercer understood, in 1987, that the firm’s older documentation contained what a sufficiently thorough investigation would eventually recognise as evidence of deliberate misrepresentation, he did not destroy it — destruction leaves traces. He did something more elegant. He had Pryce create a parallel record: convincing forgeries that would, if the need arose, discredit whichever investigator came closest to the truth by appearing to have been planted by that investigator. The trap was set in 1987 for a case that would not begin for another two years.” He was quiet for a moment. “I don’t think the investigation will find Mercer at the end of that thread. I think he will have been careful. I think that part of it — the human part, the reason — will remain unrecorded.”

“Does it matter?” I asked. “For the outcome?”

“For the outcome, not at all.” He stood and collected his coat from the stand by the door, which is where it lives when he remembers to put it there. “It matters to me,” he said, simply, “because I do not like unread documents. Even human ones.”

He buttoned his coat. He seemed, briefly, on the verge of saying something further. He did not.

I sat for a while after he had gone, listening to the rain against the glass, thinking of a man who had spent eleven years in a compliance department with a forgery in a drawer and a secret he was keeping for someone who had perhaps, by now, forgotten that they had ever told him.

Holmes, who had not eaten since breakfast, went home — and I noted, though there was no one left to say it to, that he had taken the elevator rather than the stairs, which always meant the same thing: the case was finished, and the machine was running down toward stillness, and by tomorrow he would be impossible and brilliantly, predictably himself again.

I have given the technical account as fully as I am capable of rendering it. I leave the matter of Gerald Pryce’s motive for my readers to consider, as Holmes left it for me — not as an answer, but as the shape of a question which the official record, in its preference for the sufficient over the complete, has declined to ask.
