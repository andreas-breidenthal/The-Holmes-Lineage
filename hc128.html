SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅳ

1945 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was born in September 1945, six weeks after the Japanese surrender, into a household where the war's end had produced not relief but a particular quality of English silence that his American relatives found difficult to interpret and his mother had long since learned not to attempt to explain. His father's silences were not unhappy; they were the silences of a man who had spent a professional lifetime reading what other people concealed, and who had made, on arriving in America, a private decision to direct that faculty inward rather than outward — to observe his own life, for once, with the same attention he had previously given to the lives of others. He was, by his son's account, an exceptional father: present, attentive, and constitutionally incapable of pretending not to know things he knew, which meant that the usual parental fictions were unavailable to him and that the boy grew up in a household of considerable, occasionally startling, transparency.

He inherited the gift fully and expressed it in the language of his time and place, which was the language of the law. He read at Harvard, was admitted to the Massachusetts bar in 1969, and spent the early years of his career in a Boston commercial law firm before moving to the federal practice that would occupy the remainder of his professional life. His particular facility was financial fraud — cases built not on witness testimony or physical evidence but on documentation: the ten thousand pages of ledgers, correspondence, incorporation filings, and banking records that told, to a reader with the patience and the precision to follow them, a story entirely different from the one their authors had intended. He was not, his colleagues noted, a brilliant advocate; he did not perform in court with any of the theatrical authority that juries respond to. He was something rarer and, in the federal context, considerably more useful: a man who could read a fraudulent financial structure from its documentary residue the way his great-great-grandfather had read a criminal from his boots and his cuffs, and who could explain what he had found in terms precise enough to survive cross-examination by the best commercial attorneys in New York.

There is, attached to his papers, a private record — compiled across several decades, in the spare and precise prose he brought to everything — of his childhood visits to the Sussex cottage. His father had taken him to England several times during the first decade of his life, and Holmes Ⅳ had understood, even as a small boy, that these journeys were not ordinary ones. The record he kept is not a diary; it has no dates and no sequence of entries. It is instead a set of recollections, written down at intervals as they became clear to him in retrospect: the smell of the cottage; the quality of the light over the Downs in different weathers; the old man's hands, which he remembered as precise and still; the bees, which were everywhere in summer and which his great-grandfather discussed with an earnestness that a child could follow, and that Holmes Ⅳ came to understand, much later, was not a simplification for the child's benefit but the genuine thing — the same complete attention brought to the same subject that had once been brought to criminal networks and ciphers and the unrecorded history of four thousand years.

What the record preserves most precisely is the quality of conversation. The Great Detective, in his final decade, did not perform for visitors, not even for the smallest ones. He asked questions. He listened to the answers. He asked more questions. Holmes Ⅳ, who was three or four or five years old, answered them with the directness of a child who had not yet learned the adult habit of managing what he disclosed. The old man noted what the boy said with the concentration of a man still in practice — not indulging a great-grandchild, but attending to an intelligence he had taken the measure of and found, in the characteristic manner of the line, worth attending to. Holmes Ⅳ could not have articulated this at the time. He articulated it, with considerable precision, in the private record he kept, across the long years of his career in the law. It was, he wrote, the most complete attention he had ever been given. He did not think he had ever been given it again, by anyone, in quite the same way.

In later years, Holmes Ⅳ's records note visits from Dr. Amelia Watson, a contemporary of his own generation and a direct descendant of John H. Watson. She had, for some months, been keeping a personal chronicle of Boston medical and legal affairs, which she shared with Holmes Ⅳ during discussions that ranged from clinical ethics to forensic procedure. It was in these sessions, often held over the clatter of a distant hospital ward or the rustle of financial files, that she first began to record the remarkable acuity with which he approached complex human behaviors and financial structures. The account she maintained — her own voice — would become a primary repository for the observation of Holmes Ⅳ in action, capturing his methods and temperament from a witness uniquely positioned to note both the gift and its occasional blind spots.

The Matter of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer

From the private chronicle of Dr. Amelia Watson, maintained in parallel with her clinical rounds at Massachusetts General Hospital, and not intended for publication during the lifetimes of those named herein.

Boston had been wearing its particular November face for three weeks — a face that those who know the city recognize as distinct from the November of, say, Chicago or New York: colder in intent than in temperature, the harbor wind carrying not merely cold but a kind of institutional disapproval, as though the city itself had reviewed your conduct for the year and found the file wanting. The light off the water was the color of old pewter. The elm trees along Commonwealth Avenue had surrendered their leaves with what I can only describe as relief.

I had spent the morning in the trauma unit reviewing cases that, in their human wreckage and their narrative incompleteness, bore a closer resemblance to criminal proceedings than most members of the public would find comfortable. I arrived at the offices of the Federal Public Corruption Unit on the sixth floor of the McCormack Building at half past one, carrying two cups of coffee from the cart in the lobby — a precaution I had learned, over the preceding months, was not hospitality but necessity, since the man I was coming to see would not remember to acquire one himself and would, by mid-afternoon, have been running on nothing but inference and cold case files since before six in the morning.

Holmes was not in the outer office.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was the quality of the silence beyond the interior door — not the productive silence of concentrated work, which had its own density and rhythm I had come to recognize, but something flatter and more dangerous. I knocked. There was a pause of the kind that suggests the person within is deciding something.

"Come in, Watson."

He was standing at the window. This was significant. In the eighteen months during which I had been keeping this chronicle, I had rarely seen him stand at the window. He sat at the desk, or he sat on the floor surrounded by banker's boxes arranged in systems of his own devising, or he stood at the whiteboard on which the financial structures of various cases had been rendered in blue marker in a script so compressed it required a second reading to distinguish numbers from letters. He did not stand at the window. The window was for days when the mind had nothing productive to do with itself.

I set his coffee on the desk.

"They've charged DiMarco," he said, without turning.

Anthony DiMarco. I knew the name from the files I had been permitted to review. He had been Holmes's lead attorney on the Aldrich, Voss case — a decade's work, a securities collapse that had consumed the pension contributions of several thousand textile workers in the Connecticut river valley, and which Holmes had been in the process of tying, document by document, to a pattern of deliberate misrepresentation at the senior partnership level of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer, one of the three or four investment banks that still conducted themselves as though the concept of federal oversight was a matter of ongoing negotiation rather than settled law.

"Charged with what?" I said.

"Evidence fabrication." He turned then, and I saw what I had been half-expecting: the face not of the man who had destroyed three fraud networks in the preceding decade, but of the man who existed between cases — the hollowed-out, almost dangerous quality of a precise machine with no problem to run on. Except that this time, I understood, it was worse than ennui. "The charge, specifically, is that DiMarco introduced falsified banking records into discovery — records purporting to show wire transfers that did not occur, timed to coincide with the dates I have been arguing were central to the fraudulent structure."

I sat down. "And the actual charge against Aldrich, Voss?"

"Suspended. Pending the outcome of the investigation into the fabrication." He finally moved away from the window and sat, though not at the desk — on the low leather chair in the corner that he used, as far as I could tell, only when he wanted to think without the distraction of being near paper. "The U.S. Attorney's office received an anonymous tip three days ago. The tip included a technical analysis of the metadata embedded in the disputed documents, purporting to show that the creation timestamps had been manipulated — and that the manipulation was consistent with software DiMarco had licensed for document management."

"You look," I said carefully, "as though you've already solved it."

"I look," he said, "as though I am deciding whether to be angry first or useful first. I find the two states incompatible." He picked up the coffee. "Anthony DiMarco has been building federal fraud cases for twenty-two years. He is methodical, cautious to the point of conservatism, and possessed of an ego that expresses itself entirely through the quality of his documentation. He would not fabricate evidence. Not because he is a paragon of legal virtue — I have no particular investment in paragons — but because fabrication is imprecise, and DiMarco finds imprecision physically distressing."

"Who benefits from the case being suspended?"

He looked at me with the expression I had catalogued, in these pages, as the one he wore when a person had asked the exactly correct question without realizing quite what they had done. "Now you are beginning," he said, "to observe."

The offices of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer occupied three floors of a tower in the financial district that had been built in the late 1970s in the architectural style I think of as Aspirational Granite — a building that wished to communicate permanence and communicated instead the anxious striving of a decade that had not yet learned to trust its own prosperity. The lobby smelled of forced-air heating and the particular brand of carpet cleaner used in every federal building and every institution that considers itself adjacent to federal authority. The receptionist's desk was rosewood, or something engineered to suggest rosewood. There were no windows visible from the lobby. This was, I had come to understand, not an accident.

We had not been invited. This, too, was not an accident.

The compliance officer's name was Gerald Pryce. Holmes had asked me to note the name when he first mentioned it, three hours earlier, in the manner of a man who has filed a conclusion and is now marshalling the evidence to display it. Pryce had been with the firm for eleven years. He had a law degree from Fordham and a secondary qualification in financial systems technology that he had obtained, Holmes had established through a single telephone call to the Bar Association, in 1983 — the year that the document management software at the center of the fabrication allegation had first been introduced to the commercial market.

The call we made at the compliance department was brief. Holmes asked to use the firm's document retrieval system to verify several items in discovery. He asked this with the measured courtesy of a federal officer exercising a right he could, if pressed, document — and Pryce, who had clearly anticipated something, and who had perhaps not anticipated this, made the error of compliance officers throughout history and consulted his procedural manual rather than his instincts.

We were shown to a terminal.

What followed occupied approximately forty minutes, and I will confess that I understood, during those forty minutes, approximately a third of what Holmes was doing. I understood the rest when he explained it afterward, and I will render the explanation as he gave it, because it is the clearest account of the method and because, in honesty, it is the part of these chronicles that I find most worth preserving — not the conclusion, which was remarkable, but the architecture of the reasoning that reached it.

"The fabrication," he said, when we were back in the McCormack Building with the relevant printouts spread across the floor in the arrangement he preferred — chronological by document creation, not by document date, which is a distinction that requires a moment's thought to fully appreciate — "was skillfully done. Genuinely skillfully done. I want to be precise about that, because there is a tendency, when a fraud is exposed, to retroactively characterize it as obvious, and that characterization is dishonest and serves no analytical purpose."

I was sitting on the corner of the desk, which he permitted during working sessions. The overhead light was doing something unkind to the printouts.

"The documents themselves are convincing," he continued. "The paper stock is correct for the period. The typeface is correct. The institutional letterhead is an accurate reproduction. The figures cited are internally consistent and, more importantly, are consistent with the known figures — they don't contradict the legitimate record; they supplement it in a way that supports the fabrication's intended narrative." He picked up one of the printouts. "A competent forger. Someone who has studied the authentic documents with considerable care."

"But the metadata."

"The metadata. Yes." He set the printout down and stood, moving to the whiteboard. "The metadata embedded in the digitized versions of these documents — which is how they entered discovery, as scanned files — shows creation timestamps consistent with the dates printed on the documents themselves. 1981. 1983. 1985. This is what the anonymous tip alleged had been manipulated: that the timestamps had been set to those dates artificially, using a technique that involves adjusting the system clock of the scanning workstation before scanning the documents."

"And had they been?"

"No." He wrote something on the board — a date, 1983, and beside it, two other dates. "That is where the fabrication of the fabrication becomes visible. The timestamps were not manipulated. They are genuine creation timestamps, recorded by software that was installed on the firm's document management system in 1987." He let that settle. "Documents created — as the timestamps record, accurately — in 1987. Bearing dates of 1981, 1983, 1985. The forger did not adjust the clock. The forger either did not know that the software recorded creation timestamps, or — and I think this more likely — did not know that the software version installed in 1987 did so. The 1985 version did not. The 1987 update introduced the timestamp function as a background process. It is not documented in the user manual. It was documented in the technical release notes, which are fourteen pages long and written in a register that discourages casual reading."

I held the thought for a moment. "So the documents were forged. But not by DiMarco."

"DiMarco's document management license is for the 1991 version of the same software. Which records timestamps in an entirely different embedded format — a format that would have been immediately visible in the metadata of the disputed documents, had he used his own system to create them." Holmes turned from the board. "The disputed documents were created on Aldrich, Voss & Mercer's own system. On the workstation assigned to the compliance department. In 1987 — a year before DiMarco joined the case, and four years before the software license he allegedly used was commercially available."

The heating system made a sound like a long exhalation.

"Pryce," I said.

"Pryce." He did not seem satisfied, exactly. He seemed precise, which is a different thing. "The anonymous tip was sent from a public telephone in the financial district, at a time when the compliance department's after-hours access log records Gerald Pryce as present in the building. The tip referenced a technical analysis of the metadata that requires knowing which specific embedded fields to examine — knowledge that is not intuitive, that is not in the legal literature on document fraud, and that is precisely the knowledge that a person with Pryce's secondary qualification in financial systems technology, obtained in 1983, would possess." He picked up his coffee, found it cold, set it down again without apparent disappointment. "He prepared the forgeries in 1987. I don't yet know why he waited. That question is for the investigation. What matters now is that he knew — he must have known, when I began requesting documents — exactly which records I had seen and which I had not yet requested, because it is the compliance department that processes discovery requests. He introduced the fabricated documents into the next production batch, in the gap between my penultimate and final requests, targeting precisely the date range I had signaled, through my request pattern, that I intended to examine next."

I thought of the man we had met for twelve minutes in the compliance department. The careful suit. The procedural manual. The face that had arranged itself, with some effort, into an expression of cooperative inconvenience.

"He read your request pattern," I said. "As a document."

Holmes looked at me for a moment with an expression I did not often see — something between acknowledgment and, I think, genuine surprise at being understood. "Yes," he said. "Precisely that. He read it as a document. And he was not wrong to do so. I read structures; he read me reading them and inserted a false signal into the record at the precise moment it would cause maximum disruption." He was quiet for a moment. "He is not unintelligent. Under other circumstances—"

He stopped. He did not complete the sentence, and I did not press him to. I have learned, in the course of keeping this chronicle, which silences are the productive kind and which are the kind that belong to a man working through something privately, without witnesses. This was the latter sort.

The matter of Gerald Pryce occupied the U.S. Attorney's office for the following seven months. The charges against DiMarco were withdrawn within a week of Holmes's submission of his analysis to the federal prosecutor — a submission that ran to sixty-one pages and that I have been told, by those competent to judge, was a document of some forensic elegance.

The charges against Aldrich, Voss & Mercer's senior partners were reinstated. The pension recovery proceedings, which had been suspended, resumed. Gerald Pryce entered a plea.

The documents Holmes prepared, which established the fabrication through the metadata of the document management system's 1987 update, are part of the public federal record. The analysis of the request pattern — the observation that Pryce had read Holmes's investigative sequence as itself a documentary structure and had introduced the forgery into the gap — appears nowhere in the official record. It did not need to appear. The metadata was sufficient. Holmes, who had the lawyer's understanding that sufficiency is its own discipline, did not volunteer the additional layer.

I recorded it here.

There is one further matter I should set down, because I think it belongs to an understanding of the case that the official record does not provide.

On the evening before his submission, Holmes sat for a long time in the low leather chair without speaking. I was reviewing my own notes. The window behind him showed the harbor lights through rain, and the room smelled of the particular cold that comes through old federal building windows in November — metal and damp stone and the faint residue of decades of institutional paperwork.

"He was protecting someone," Holmes said, without preamble.

I looked up.

"Pryce." He had not moved. He was looking at the middle distance with the expression of a man reviewing a calculation he has already completed and finding it unchanged. "The forgeries were prepared in 1987. The case that would require them did not exist until 1989. He made them in advance. Which means he knew, in 1987, what the case would eventually look for. Which means someone inside Aldrich, Voss told him, in 1987, what the documentation of the pension structure would eventually reveal." He paused. "He did this to protect that person. I don't know who. I don't think the investigation will find who. I think that part of it — the human part, the reason — will remain unrecorded."

"Does it matter?" I asked. "For the outcome?"

"No. For the outcome, it doesn't matter at all." He stood, finally, and moved to collect his coat from the stand by the door, which was where it lived when he remembered to put it there. "It matters to me," he said, simply, "because I do not like unread documents. Even human ones."

He buttoned his coat. He appeared to consider saying something further. He did not.

I have given, in this chronicle, the technical account as fully as I am capable of rendering it. I leave the matter of Gerald Pryce's motive for my readers to consider, as Holmes left it for me — not as an answer, but as the shape of a question that the record, in its official form, has declined to ask.

The harbor was raining. The McCormack Building was warm in the way of buildings that keep their warmth despite themselves. Holmes, who had not eaten since breakfast, went home — and I noted, though I did not say so, that he took the elevator rather than the stairs, which meant the case was finished and the machine was running on its reserves, and that by tomorrow he would be impossible, and brilliantly, predictably himself again.

A.M.W. — Massachusetts General Hospital, Department of Internal Medicine. November, 1989. This account to be held, per mutual agreement, for a period of not less than thirty years.
