SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅵ

1999 — United States

Of all the members of this line since the first Sherlock, he is the most difficult to write about. The difficulty is not a function of the record’s scarcity, though the record is scarce; it is a function of a quality in the man himself that the record, where it exists, consistently reflects. He moves through documentation as he apparently moves through rooms: leaving the minimum necessary trace, attending to everything, committing to nothing that would fix his position for the benefit of anyone who might, at a later point, wish to know where he had been.

His father observed the gift in him early — earlier, perhaps, than in any generation since the first Sherlock — and transmitted to him, as his own father had transmitted it to him, the instruction that had accompanied it through the line: what it was, where it came from, what it was for. Holmes Ⅵ received this instruction and drew from it a conclusion that no previous member of the line had quite reached, or had reached and not acted on so completely: that the gift, properly exercised, operated most effectively in advance of the event rather than in response to it. His predecessors had been detectives in the fullest sense — readers of what had happened, tracers of what was hidden, resolvers of crises already in progress. Holmes Ⅵ was something different. He was a preventer.

The record also notes a contemporary presence: Olivia Watson, a great-great-granddaughter of Dr. John Watson, who maintained a parallel account of Holmes Ⅵ’s work. She observed his methods with the rigor and perspective of a trained chronicler, noting both his brilliance and the edges of his endurance. Her detailed records, which form the basis for the following account, reveal the gift in action, the surprises he provokes, and the human calculations he uncovers.

The Feld Matter

Account by Olivia Watson

Washington in October has a particular quality of light — not the oppressive, fog-soaked darkness that London so reliably provides its investigators, but something arguably worse: a clean, crystalline brightness that illuminates everything and obscures nothing, so that the lies have to work considerably harder. The trees along the Mall were burning orange and rust, and the air smelled of fallen leaves composting against the marble bases of monuments, that particular American smell of grandeur slowly returning to rot.

I note at the outset that I was wrong about almost everything.

That is, I have come to understand, something of an occupational condition for those of us who keep company with this family.

I.

He called me at six in the morning. His voice had the particular flatness of a man who has been awake long enough that sleep has become a theoretical construct.

“Sherlock here. Foggy Bottom. Come now. Bring nothing.”

I did not ask why he had called me rather than any of the intelligence community’s more official channels. I had grown up knowing his family well — my father’s sister was a close ally and assistant to his grandfather, and my older brother Edward had been working alongside his father for over thirty years. That was explanation enough.

The address was a third-floor walkup above a dry cleaner. The smell of chemical solvent rose through the floorboards with a persistence that spoke to decades of slow permeation. The rooms were spare to the point of severity: a chair, a desk, a corkboard that held nothing, a coffee table on which sat a laptop, three burner phones in various states of disassembly, and a mug of something that had long since gone cold.

He was crouched over the table when I arrived. Arranged in a precise row before him were seven antique padlocks of escalating complexity, and he was working the last of them with a pick, eyes closed, head slightly tilted — the posture of a man listening to something inaudible to others. After approximately twelve seconds the lock opened with a sound like a small, satisfied exhale, and he set it down beside the others and stood.

He was twenty-six years old. He looked like a theorem someone had bothered to dress.

“You’re shorter than Edward described,” he said, without turning.

“I beg your pardon?”

“He said you were formidable. I had formed a taller impression.” He turned from the window. “The intelligence work was yours, not his suggestion. He would have steered you toward medicine.”

I sat in the chair he hadn’t offered. “He did.”

“And yet.” He looked at me with the brief, cataloguing attention of a man confirming a hypothesis. “The DIA was the compromise. Something that used the same instincts without requiring you to be in the same room as suffering.”

The room was quiet.

“Edward talks too much,” I said.

“Edward talks precisely enough,” he said. “That’s rather the problem.”

He moved to the desk and sat.

“I need you to help me find a missing man,” he said.

The case, as he presented it, was this.

A diplomatic aide named Marcus Feld. Attached to a mid-level trade advisory office. Unremarkable by the public record: forty-three years old, twelve years in the service, a single commendation for work on a bilateral logistics agreement that nobody outside two departments had ever read. He had, according to a colleague’s passing remark in a filed deposition, the habit of arriving precisely four minutes early to every meeting — not five, not three — which his colleagues had come to regard as a form of mild eccentricity. Missing for eleven days. Missing, according to the President’s Counsel office, which had retained Holmes through two intermediaries in the peculiar Washington fashion of urgency conducted at arm’s length.

He turned the laptop to face me and let me read. After a moment he said: “Note the dates.”

I noted them.

A leak had occurred on the fourteenth of August. A congressional subpoena had been filed on the twenty-eighth of September. Feld had disappeared on the third of October.

“Leak triggers removal,” I said. “Someone in his office discovered Feld’s vulnerability, filed an internal report, and word reached the wrong ears. The subpoena confirmed their fears and they moved him before he could testify.”

“State the sequence precisely,” he said.

I did. Leak, then subpoena, then disappearance.

He waited.

I went back to the dates.

The leak had occurred on the fourteenth of August. The subpoena had been filed on the twenty-eighth of September.

Six weeks later.

“The leak precedes the subpoena,” I said slowly.

“By six weeks.”

“Advance warning, then. Someone with access to the sealed proceeding—”

“There was no sealed proceeding. At the time of the leak, no legal process had been initiated. No jurisdiction established. No subpoena in preparation.” He closed the laptop. “You cannot leak the existence of something that has not been created.”

The room was very quiet. Outside, a bus moved down the street below.

“Then what was leaked?” I asked.

“That,” he said, “is the correct question.”

He proposed two rules before we left the apartment.

The first: I was to offer my interpretation of everything we found, and I was not to withhold it out of caution or deference. The second: he would not correct me until the correction would be useful. Both conditions, he explained, were operational requirements rather than courtesy.

I agreed to both. I have since concluded that the second rule was the more important of the two, and not especially to my advantage.

II.

The first thread he pulled was administrative.

We went together to Feld’s office building — a federal annex on G Street, glass and limestone, the kind of structure that announces its own importance without committing to a reason. Holmes had arranged access through a channel he did not specify and I did not ask about. The building’s security office was on the lower level, staffed by a young man named Garrett who had the alert, cooperative manner of someone who has recently been told, by someone considerably senior to him, to be helpful.

Holmes asked to see the building access log for Feld’s credentials.

Garrett produced it without hesitation. There was a single entry dated four days after Feld’s disappearance: a badge swipe at the main entrance, 11:47 PM, with no follow-through to any interior door.

“The credentials were cloned,” I said. “Someone accessing his office after he vanished — to retrieve documents, or a device.”

“Then why only once?” Holmes said.

“Perhaps they found what they needed on the first attempt.”

“Cloned credentials, used for a single swipe, at a public entrance, late at night, with no subsequent interior access.” He looked at the log for a moment. “A real retrieval operation uses the credentials repeatedly, or not at all. Repeated use suggests operational need. Non-use suggests the credentials were never the point.”

He thanked Garrett and led me to the stairwell.

The lobby floor was polished terrazzo, clean and maintained. Holmes held the stairwell door, let it close behind us, and crouched. I stood behind him and saw nothing — the floor was concrete, utilitarian, unexceptional.

“There,” he said.

I crouched beside him. In the corner where the door sweep met the baseboard: a smear of rust-coloured soil, recent enough that its edge was still sharp rather than ground down by foot traffic.

“Piedmont red clay,” he said. “Highly acidic. Found in the undeveloped industrial land of northern Virginia.” He straightened. “The colour alone is suggestive — but look here.” He pointed to a second mark, fainter, on the inside face of the door. “Vehicle residue. Whoever made the swipe came directly from an unpaved site. The clay transferred first to the vehicle floor, then to the shoe, then here. Someone walked from a specific rural location to this entrance, swiped the card, and left.” He looked at me steadily. “The procurement records I reviewed yesterday show a shipping log for construction materials going to a facility in northern Virginia. The same county.”

“To establish that the credentials were used,” I said. “To create a record.”

“Yes. The swipe was not access. It was a signal. Someone wished to be followed.” He was quiet a moment. “The question is whether that someone is the same party who took Feld, or a different one entirely.”

I filed the question and did not yet have the means to answer it.

III.

The second thread was financial — or rather, budgetary, which in government is rarely the same thing.

Holmes had spent the previous two nights working through procurement records from a sub-agency I had never heard of — a trade logistics coordination office whose annual budget was sufficiently modest to fall below any meaningful oversight threshold. He walked me through the numbers at the desk in the Foggy Bottom apartment that afternoon.

A single line item. A figure of $340,000. Allocated to a facility in northern Virginia, listed as a decommissioned logistics depot.

“Three hundred and forty thousand dollars,” I said. “For a decommissioned facility.”

“For modification of an existing facility. Note the category — not construction, not maintenance. Modification. And note the precision: not a round number, not a standard contractual figure. This was costed from actual scope. Someone knew exactly what they needed and how much it would cost.”

“A holding facility,” I said.

“A temporary one. A black site requires ongoing operational infrastructure — security rotation, communications, supply lines. This is a one-time cost for a specific adaptation. Something already existed there. This money made it serve a new function.” He looked at the screen. “Feld is in northern Virginia. I am confident of it.”

I believed him. What I did not yet understand was that knowing where Feld was held was, in this particular case, almost beside the point.

IV.

By the fifth day, I believed I understood the case.

The elements arranged themselves into a shape that was difficult to argue with. Internal security — a division within the Counsel’s apparatus itself, or adjacent to it — had identified Feld as a liability before the legal machinery had formalised the threat. They had watched the subpoena take shape in real time through a source embedded in the congressional process. They had moved Feld pre-emptively and engineered the appearance of external interference — the staged credential swipe, the leaked timeline — to direct scrutiny outward. The facility in northern Virginia was their containment solution.

It was coherent. It was supported by the evidence. It explained the sequence.

When I said as much to Holmes that evening, he listened with the particular attention of a man being courteous.

There was a pause.

“That is the intended interpretation,” he said.

“Meaning it’s wrong.”

“Meaning it accounts for everything except the one anomaly we identified on the first morning.” He stood and moved to the window. “It does not account for the leak.”

“It does,” I said. “The leak was misdirection — to direct suspicion toward external actors before Feld disappeared.”

“Then who generated it? And how did they know, six weeks before a subpoena existed, that a subpoena would exist?”

The room was quiet.

“Step through it,” he said. “The leak occurred in August. At that moment, no legal process was underway. No one in the congressional process knew there would be a subpoena, because the impetus for that subpoena had not yet been formally acted upon.” He turned. “For someone within the Counsel’s apparatus to generate a misdirective leak in August, they would need to know, in August, that a subpoena would be coming in late September.”

“Prediction,” I said. “Based on intelligence about congressional intentions.”

“Possible. Now explain why they would leak that prediction rather than simply move Feld immediately. In August, they had six weeks. Ample time for a quiet extraction. Why draw attention at all?”

I had no answer.

He let the silence do its work.

“There is a further detail,” he said, “that I have been waiting for you to raise.”

I reviewed what I knew. The facility. The credential swipe. The clay. The budget line. The dates.

And then I saw it — or rather, saw that I had set it aside when I should not have.

“The newsletter,” I said.

“Yes.”

The leak had appeared in a single trade publication — a newsletter of the sort that circulates among a few hundred staffers, rarely read outside a specific community. Not the vehicle one would choose for a misdirection aimed at the general press.

“It’s the wrong audience,” I said slowly. “If you want to create noise, you go to the press. If you go to a newsletter with four hundred readers, all of them government-adjacent—”

“You are not creating noise,” Holmes said. “You are delivering a signal to a specific community. The community that would eventually generate a subpoena.” He was very still. “Someone in August knew that a particular congressional office was building toward an inquiry — not yet formalised, but advancing. By placing the leak in that newsletter, they accelerated the process. The congressional staff read it, took it as confirmation of what they were already investigating, and moved. The subpoena that followed was induced.”

“The leak created the subpoena,” I said.

“It pushed it past the point of hesitation. Yes.” He sat. “And once the subpoena existed, the internal security apparatus — the people who genuinely had reason to fear Feld’s testimony — moved him. They were not part of the original design. They were its instrument.”

The room was very quiet.

“We are not looking at reaction,” he said. “We are looking at orchestration. The leak and the subpoena did not produce the disappearance. All three were produced by a single intelligence — one that has used the machinery of suppression to do the work of exposure.”

“Then Feld was not hidden from discovery,” I said.

“He was hidden for it.”

V.

It was at this point that Holmes did something I did not expect.

He reached into the desk drawer and placed a flash drive on the table. He looked at it for a long moment — not with the detached attention of a man reviewing evidence, but with the careful consideration of a man weighing a decision he cannot un-make.

“I have been thinking,” he said, “about the mechanisms available to us.”

He had identified the facility. He had identified the architecture. What he had not yet done was establish an exposure route that the apparatus could not suppress.

“The men who hid Feld have access to every official channel,” he said. “Any report filed through the Counsel’s office will be buried. Any direct approach to the facility will precipitate removal before retrieval. Whatever Feld carries in his testimony requires a jurisdiction that the Counsel’s apparatus cannot reach.” He looked at me. “You still hold your Q-clearance from your time at the Defense Intelligence Agency. And you are having lunch tomorrow with the Deputy Inspector General.”

I looked at the drive.

“The drive contains a precise selection,” he said. “The procurement record, the credential log, the timeline. Assembled not as an accusation but as an anomaly — the kind that triggers mandatory review by its own bureaucratic gravity. The IG’s office is legally required to initiate upon receipt. It cannot decline. It cannot route the matter back through the Counsel’s office. It is, functionally, sealed against the very apparatus that is holding Feld.”

“You want me to introduce this into an active oversight audit,” I said.

“I want you to drop it on the floor of his car. Unattributed. Without explanation.” He was quiet for a moment. “It is an irregular method.”

“Is it legal?”

“Scrupulously. Every document on that drive is a matter of government record.”

I looked at the drive. I looked at him.

“Someone else may have anticipated this step,” I said carefully.

“Perhaps. But I am choosing it regardless.” He held my gaze without apology. “Whoever constructed this situation understood that Feld’s evidence required an unimpeachable channel. I agree with that assessment. The difference is that I am not willing to be guided to a conclusion I have not examined. We go in with clear eyes or we do not go.” He was quiet for a moment. “The design anticipates an investigator. It does not require me — but it has me.”

I took the drive.

VI.

On the tenth night, he told me to drive.

We parked a quarter-mile from the facility — what the procurement record had listed as a decommissioned depot. The treeline was overgrown in the way that suggests deliberate rather than neglectful growth: a screen, not an oversight. Through the gaps I could see a low concrete structure, lights off, fencing recent enough to still be bright.

We sat in the freezing dark for three hours. Holmes did not speak. He had brought nothing to read, nothing to occupy himself with. I found this, by then, more unsettling than the locks.

At half past one he said, without lowering his gaze from the treeline: “If the channel holds, they will come before two.”

At two in the morning, the floodlights snapped on.

A convoy of black SUVs with federal plates breached the gate. Through binoculars I watched the Inspector General’s team flood the facility — methodical, purposeful, the bureaucratic machinery of accountability doing precisely what it was designed to do and almost never does.

Feld was in the second room of three. He was thin and unshaven, and when the agents opened the door he stood immediately, which struck the lead agent as unusual enough to note in his preliminary report. A man expecting rescue stands. A man who has given up does not.

The other two rooms held individuals who were not, it quickly became apparent, fellow detainees.

Beside me, Holmes was not watching the raid.

He was watching a single unmarked sedan slip out the back perimeter and accelerate into the dark, moving without lights for the first hundred metres.

“There goes the origin,” he said quietly.

I lowered the binoculars. “Should we—”

“No.” He watched the tail-lights until they were gone. “That is not settled yet.”

VII.

He came to me the following morning, at a coffee shop on Pennsylvania Avenue that he had chosen — he said — for its unremarkable sight lines.

“Feld is in protective custody,” he said. “The facility held three individuals, all of whom have been transferred to the IG’s jurisdiction. The men who arranged the concealment are under investigation.”

He drank his coffee.

“Now,” he said, “you will want the full account.”

I said I would.

“Begin with the newsletter,” he said. “Everything else proceeds from it. I will walk you through the chain.

“Step one: a particular congressional office is building toward an inquiry into Feld’s former office. Not yet formalised — a matter of open questions, internal memos, quiet conversations. At this stage, the inquiry might stall or advance. It requires a catalyst.

“Step two: someone with foreknowledge of both the inquiry and of Feld’s situation places a leak in that newsletter. Not in the press — in the newsletter. Because the press would generate noise, and noise invites counter-measures. The newsletter reaches the specific staffers who are already asking questions. No one else mattered. They read it as confirmation. Their hesitation dissolves.

“Step three: the subpoena is filed. It would have come eventually — the leak ensured it came in time.

“Step four: the existence of the subpoena reaches the internal security apparatus — the people who have genuine reason to fear Feld’s testimony. They move him. Quickly, quietly, to a facility they control. They believe they are acting to protect themselves. They are, in fact, doing precisely what was required of them.

“Step five: the credential swipe is planted — not by the internal apparatus, but by the same hand that placed the leak. Clay on the shoe, a single swipe, no interior access. Precisely the pattern that would draw an investigator of sufficient capability. It is a thread left for pulling. It leads here, to this conversation, to this morning.

“Step six: the IG route. The only jurisdiction the internal apparatus cannot suppress, cannot reclassify, cannot reach. Testimony taken under the IG process is sealed against the Counsel’s office by statute. It is, in effect, a vault. Left alone, this mechanism might have failed at any of a dozen points. Completed, it cannot be undone.”

He set down his cup.

“Now observe what has been accomplished. Feld’s evidence is in that vault. The internal apparatus, in the act of hiding him, delivered him to the one channel they cannot open. And the two individuals found alongside Feld in the facility — they were not detainees. They were waiting. They have testimony of their own, testimony that could not safely be offered through any other channel, and they are now in a jurisdiction where it cannot be suppressed.”

I sat with this for a moment.

“Whoever built this mechanism,” I said, “knew that you would pull the thread. They knew the credential swipe would register as anomalous rather than incriminating. They anticipated your methods specifically.”

“Yes,” he said, without vanity or alarm. “That narrowed the field considerably.”

VIII.

He had chosen the coffee shop for its sight lines.

I thought about that again. Then I thought about the sedan — how Holmes had watched it, how he had said that is not settled yet rather than that is not this case, as he would have said if the origin were truly beyond our reach.

I looked around the room.

The corner booth. A man perhaps sixty-five, with the careful stillness of someone who has spent a career in rooms where careless movement is noted. His coffee was untouched. He wore a grey overcoat that had been expensive once. He was watching Holmes with the expression of a man who has waited a long time for a particular reckoning and is not entirely certain he welcomes its arrival.

“He’s here,” I said.

Holmes did not look surprised. He looked, briefly, as though he had revised an estimate upward.

“The corner booth,” I said. “He’s been here since before we arrived. The sight lines weren’t for your comfort — they were for his.”

A silence.

“Well done,” Holmes said.

We walked to the corner booth together.

“You used us,” Holmes said. Not accusatory. Anatomical.

“I used what you are,” the man said. His voice was unhurried. “There’s a distinction.”

“A fine one.”

“In my experience, the fine ones are the only ones worth making.”

“You relied on me specifically,” Holmes said.

A pause — the first hesitation I had seen in him.

“I relied on what you would do,” the man said. “That is not the same thing. I have been wrong about individuals before. I have not been wrong about methods.”

He had, as Holmes subsequently confirmed, spent thirty-one years in a sub-agency coordinating trade logistics — which is to say, a sub-agency coordinating the movement of things and the paperwork that conceals it. He had watched evidence vanish into the machinery for three decades. He had, two years ago, made a formal complaint through official channels regarding the treatment of two junior analysts whose work had been suppressed and whose careers had been quietly destroyed. The complaint had been received, acknowledged, and buried.

He had retired six weeks before the leak appeared in the newsletter.

“The newsletter,” Holmes said. “You placed the leak before you retired.”

“I had access to the congressional inquiry through my position. I knew the inquiry existed. I knew it was stalling.” He looked at his untouched cup. “I also knew that the moment I retired, that access would close. I had a narrow window.”

“And the two individuals in the facility.”

“They came to me because every other door was closed. They have waited four years for a room where their words cannot be reached.” He straightened his cup on its saucer with a precision that reminded me, disconcertingly, of Holmes. “I owed them a room.”

“The credential swipe,” Holmes said. “You planted it.”

“I needed someone to follow the thread. Someone whose methods I understood well enough to predict.” He looked at Holmes directly. “Your father would have reached the newsletter by day three. I allowed for day two. You reached it on the first.”

“You built in contingency.”

“I always build in contingency. Thirty-one years of contingency.” He looked at his hands briefly. “My retirement was encouraged. Once the testimony surfaces, I will not be difficult to find. I am aware of that.”

Holmes was quiet for a moment.

“You could not approach me directly,” he said.

“If I had approached you directly, you would have known the channel was contaminated. You would have looked for the hand behind it. The mechanism required that you believe, at every stage, that you were investigating rather than being guided. That you were choosing rather than being positioned.” He held Holmes’s gaze. “I am aware of what that cost. I preferred to acknowledge it in person.”

I thought of the flash drive. Of Holmes holding it on the table and saying: I am choosing it regardless.

He had known — or suspected — the full shape of it. He had chosen to complete the mechanism anyway, with open eyes. I was not certain whether that was wisdom or something more stubborn than wisdom. Looking at him now, I suspected he would say the distinction was not operational.

“I cannot decide,” Holmes said at last, “whether you are the most dangerous kind of idealist or the most idealistic kind of dangerous man.”

“Neither can I,” the man said. “I stopped trying some years ago.” He rose, buttoned the grey overcoat, and placed a card on the table — plain, no name, only a number. “If the testimony is interfered with, call that. I have two contingencies I have not deployed.”

He walked out into Pennsylvania Avenue and became immediately unremarkable among the purposeful, incurious crowd.

Holmes looked at the card for a long moment. Then he said:

“He built a mechanism that the machinery could not reach, using materials the machinery provided.” He pocketed the card. “A just result, obtained by methods I would not recommend. That is the most I can say for it.”

“And us?” I said.

He considered it with the seriousness the question deserved.

“We were instruments,” he said. “But we were not blind ones. We saw the shape of it and chose to complete it. That is not nothing.” He stood and buttoned his coat. “Whether it was right — that I will leave to the testimony. If Feld’s evidence is sound, the mechanism was justified. If it was not, we have done a very elegant thing to no purpose.”

“And if the testimony is suppressed after all?”

“Then we call the number.”

He left the coffee shop without ceremony. I let him reach the door.

“Edward was right about you,” I said.

Holmes paused. He did not turn.

“Was he,” he said. Not a question.

“He said you were the most honest person he had ever met. He meant it as a warning.”

A brief pause.

“He was not wrong,” Holmes said. And walked out into the October light.

Reflections

Marcus Feld lives and testifies.

The men who hid him are under investigation.

The retired official has not been publicly named. I have met him once, in a corner booth on Pennsylvania Avenue, and found that the line between architect and instrument is a matter of which direction you are standing when the mechanism fires.

Holmes would say the distinction is not operational.

I find, on reflection, that I disagree — and that this is one of the things that makes us useful to each other.

— O. Watson, Washington D.C., October 2025
