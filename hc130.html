SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅵ

1999 — United States

Of all the members of this line since the first Sherlock, he is the most difficult to write about. The difficulty is not a function of the record’s scarcity, though the record is scarce; it is a function of a quality in the man himself that the record, where it exists, consistently reflects. He moves through documentation as he apparently moves through rooms: leaving the minimum necessary trace, attending to everything, committing to nothing that would fix his position for the benefit of anyone who might, at a later point, wish to know where he had been.

His father observed the gift in him early — earlier, perhaps, than in any generation since the first Sherlock — and transmitted to him, as his own father had transmitted it to him, the instruction that had accompanied it through the line: what it was, where it came from, what it was for. Holmes Ⅵ received this instruction and drew from it a conclusion that no previous member of the line had quite reached, or had reached and not acted on so completely: that the gift, properly exercised, operated most effectively in advance of the event rather than in response to it. His predecessors had been detectives in the fullest sense — readers of what had happened, tracers of what was hidden, resolvers of crises already in progress. Holmes Ⅵ was something different. He was a preventer.

The record also notes a contemporary presence: Olivia Watson, a great-great-granddaughter of Dr. John Watson, who maintained a parallel account of Holmes Ⅵ’s work. She observed his methods with the rigor and perspective of a trained chronicler, noting both his brilliance and the edges of his endurance. Her detailed records, which form the basis for the following account, reveal the gift in action, the surprises he provokes, and the human calculations he uncovers.

The First Case of Sherlock Holmes VI

Editor’s Note: What follows is reconstructed from Olivia Watson’s journal entries, three encrypted voice memos recovered from a device registered to no one, and a single handwritten page found tucked inside a first edition of “The Final Problem” that had been left — whether by accident or design remains unclear — on a bench in Dupont Circle. The gaps in this account are not the result of careless record-keeping. They are the account.

Washington in October has a particular quality of light — not the oppressive, fog-soaked darkness that London so reliably provides its investigators, but something arguably worse: a clean, crystalline brightness that illuminates everything and obscures nothing, so that the lies have to work considerably harder. The trees along the Mall were burning orange and rust, and the air smelled of fallen leaves composting against the marble bases of monuments, that particular American smell of grandeur slowly returning to rot.

I had met him only once before this case, at a mutual acquaintance’s dinner in Georgetown that he had attended for approximately forty minutes before departing without explanation. My family’s connection to his was not something either of us had raised directly, though I suspected he knew it — he had looked at me across the dinner table with an expression that I can only describe as recognition without surprise, as if confirming the location of something he had already catalogued.

When he called me at six in the morning and asked me to come to an address in Foggy Bottom, his voice had the particular flatness of a man who has been awake so long that sleep has become a theoretical concept.

The address was a third-floor walkup above a dry cleaner. The smell of chemical solvent rose through the floorboards and hung in the air of the apartment with a persistence that spoke to decades of slow permeation. The rooms themselves were spare to the point of severity — a chair, a desk, a corkboard that held nothing, a coffee table on which sat a laptop, three burner phones in various states of disassembly, and a mug of something that had long since gone cold.

He was standing at the window when I arrived, watching the street below with the focused inattention of a man cataloguing data points he has already decided are insufficient.

He was twenty-six years old. He looked like a theorem someone had bothered to dress.

“Olivia Watson,” he said, without turning. “Sit anywhere. There’s only one chair, so that’s not as generous an offer as it sounds.”

I sat in the chair. He remained at the window.

“You called me,” I said.

“I did.”

“You didn’t say why.”

“I’m saying it now. I need a record-keeper.” He turned from the window then. “Someone with your particular background. Someone who understands that there are accounts which must be kept and accounts which must appear not to exist.”

He moved to the desk and sat. “I’ve been retained by the President’s office.”

“Which office specifically?”

“Counsel’s office. Through two intermediaries. The matter is presented to me as a missing person case. A diplomatic aide named Marcus Feld.”

He outlined the case with precision: the disappearance, the flawed assumptions of the investigators, and the critical misframing of the question.

“Feld isn’t missing,” he said. “He’s hidden.”

What followed, I record incompletely.

Holmes VI worked largely beyond observation. From fragments — two meetings, scattered messages, and the condition in which I saw him — I reconstruct the direction, not the path.

Feld had been subpoenaed to testify before a sealed federal grand jury. That fact had leaked. The response was pre-emptive: relocation under controlled cooperation.

“The question is not where Feld is,” Holmes said. “The question is who benefits from the specific version of ‘missing’ he represents.”

He identified a government facility whose function had been subtly altered — a procurement adjustment, invisible in isolation, decisive in pattern.

He did not retrieve Feld.

Instead, he ensured that the right person, in the right position, would discover the truth through a channel that left no trace.

The facility was found. Feld was recovered. The investigation widened.

He came to me once more, after the resolution.

He was tired — not emotionally, but mechanically, as though he had exceeded design limits.

“Feld is in protective custody,” he said.

Then: “I underweighted a variable.”

The leak had preceded the subpoena by six weeks.

The problem extended upward.

“That’s someone else’s case,” I said.

“For now,” he replied.

He left without committing to return.

I record this account incompletely by necessity and by choice.

Some gaps are his design. Some are mine.

To reconstruct fully what he did would be to create the very record he avoids.

What can be stated is this:

Marcus Feld lives and testifies.

The men who hid him are under investigation.

A network has been disrupted.

And somewhere — perhaps here, perhaps elsewhere — Sherlock Holmes VI waits, having already arranged what comes next.

I intend to be present when it does.

— O. Watson, Washington D.C., October 2025
