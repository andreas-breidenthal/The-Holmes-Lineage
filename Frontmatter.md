# A BIOGRAPHICAL CHRONICLE
## OF THE ANCESTORS OF SHERLOCK HOLMES

### *~2050 BC – 2021 AD*

---

The record begins in Ur with a scribe in the temple of Nanna, in the forty-eighth year of the Third Dynasty, whose name has survived in the administrative tablets of that civilisation and whose method of reading a grain account has survived, in forms appropriate to each successive era, for four thousand years after the city that employed him burned to the ground.

This chronicle traces one hundred and thirty-one members of the Holmes line from that temple scribe to a child born in 2021. It is assembled from the evidence that each era has left: clay tablets and papyrus, court rolls and ecclesiastical registers, commercial correspondence and legal proceedings, intelligence memoranda and the private papers of men who understood that a document, once written, could be used against its author. Where the record is rich, the chronicle reports it. Where it is thin, the chronicle follows the thread that connects what survives. Where it is silent, the chronicle says so.

The analytical faculty that Watson documented in his accounts of Sherlock Holmes — the methodical observation, the precise inference, the refusal to conclude before the evidence permits — did not originate in Baker Street. It was inherited. Each generation of this line exercised it in the language of its own era: as scribal precision in the temples of Mesopotamia, as commercial intelligence in the Levantine ports, as forensic advocacy in the Roman basilica, as the reading of oral testimony in the Norse assembly, as the detection of forgery in the English manor courts, and as the structural analysis of criminal financial networks in the modern federal courts. The instrument changes with the century. The faculty is constant.

Running alongside the family line, in every generation, is an adversary. Not a succession of unrelated criminals but a method: organised networks, structured for durability, insulated from prosecution by layers of apparent legitimacy, sustaining themselves across centuries by transmitting their practices as faithfully as the family transmits its gift. The network that Sherlock Holmes confronted in the person of Professor Moriarty was not a Victorian invention. The record of its predecessors fills these nine books.

Two principles govern the chronicle throughout. The accounts preserved by Dr. John H. Watson are treated as primary source: nothing in these pages contradicts them, and where Watson speaks, the chronicle defers to him. And in every generation, the gift is exercised in service of mankind, with the authority that can act receiving the credit for the consequences. The family sought no recognition. The record confirms this. It was, in every generation, sufficient that the work was done.

The books differ in register because the centuries differ in the nature of their evidence. The chronicle's voice emerges gradually — present from the first entry but acquiring its fullest self-consciousness only as the line itself acquires the accumulated understanding of what it has been doing and why. The reader who follows the record from its beginning will arrive at Baker Street already knowing what Watson, for all his gifts, could not have known: that what he was witnessing was not the singular achievement of one remarkable man, but the apex of a line.

## BOOK I: THE ANCIENT WORLD
### *~2050 BC – ~1390 BC*
#### Mesopotamia · Egypt · Canaan · Phoenicia · Crete · Mycenae

---

The ancient world does not yield its records easily. The clay tablets of Mesopotamia have survived where papyrus has not; the Egyptian administrative documents are rich where the Phoenician are almost entirely absent; the Mycenaean Linear B archive is largely silent on everything beyond the palace economy it was designed to serve. The entries in this book are accordingly assembled from what survives — the administrative records, the commercial correspondence, the legal and temple documents of each civilisation — read in the light of what is known about how those civilisations organised their affairs and what men of the family's particular faculty found to do within them.

Two threads run through the book from its first entry. The first is the gift itself: the capacity to read a set of facts and find in them the pattern that others have not seen, to move from the particular discrepancy to the general truth, to remain in the uncomfortable position of not yet knowing until the evidence permits the conclusion. It appears in different registers across the centuries — as scribal precision in Mesopotamia, as commercial intelligence in the Levantine ports, as forensic analysis in the Minoan and Mycenaean palace economies — but it is recognisably the same faculty throughout. The reader who has already encountered it in Baker Street will recognise it here, in a different century and a different tongue, bending itself to the problems of a different world.

The second thread is the adversary. From the third entry onward — from the moment a forged tablet appears in the scribal school at Nippur — a shadow accompanies the family line. It is not a single organisation, not yet. It is a method: the use of legitimate administrative and commercial structures as instruments of systematic fraud, the cultivation of official complicity, the insulation of the principal from the evidence that implicates the agent. It appears in the grain accounts of Babylon and the customs houses of Memphis and the copper trade of Cyprus and the palace archives of Mycenae. It changes its language, its geography, its commodity. It does not change its structure. The family, across these eleven hundred years, does not yet fully understand what it is watching. By the time of the final entry in this book, it has begun to.

A note on the name-element. The family carries, through every generation of this book, a name-fragment that recurs across languages and centuries: the sound that begins as a Sumerian administrative title, becomes a Semitic root, enters Greek as a borrowed commercial term, and arrives, eventually, in the Norse world of the fourth book as the syllable that will become Holmes. The chronicle has traced this thread as carefully as the evidence permits. That there is a line, and that the line can be followed, the record itself will demonstrate.
