SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅳ

Born 1945 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was born in September 1945, six weeks after the Japanese surrender, into a household where the war’s end had produced not relief but a particular quality of English silence that his American relatives found difficult to interpret and his mother had long since learned not to attempt to explain. His father’s silences were not unhappy; they were the silences of a man who had spent a professional lifetime reading what other people concealed, and who had made, on arriving in America, a private decision to direct that faculty inward rather than outward — to observe his own life, for once, with the same attention he had previously given to the lives of others. He was, by his son’s account, an exceptional father: present, attentive, and constitutionally incapable of pretending not to know things he knew, which meant that the usual parental fictions were unavailable to him and that the boy grew up in a household of considerable, occasionally startling, transparency.

He inherited the gift fully and expressed it in the language of his time and place, which was the language of the law. He read at Harvard, was admitted to the Massachusetts bar in 1969, and spent the early years of his career in a Boston firm of commercial solicitors before moving to the federal practice that would occupy the remainder of his professional life. His particular facility was financial fraud — cases built not on witness testimony or physical evidence but on documentation: the ten thousand pages of ledgers, correspondence, incorporation filings, and banking records that told, to a reader with the patience and the precision to follow them, a story entirely different from the one their authors had intended. He was not, his colleagues noted, a brilliant advocate; he did not perform in court with any of the theatrical authority that juries respond to. He was something rarer and, in the federal context, considerably more useful: a man who could read a fraudulent financial structure from its documentary residue the way his great-great-grandfather had read a criminal from his boots and his cuffs, and who could explain what he had found in terms precise enough to survive cross-examination by the best commercial barristers in New York.

There is, attached to his papers, a private record — compiled across several decades, in the spare and precise prose he brought to everything — of his childhood visits to the Sussex cottage. His father had taken him to England several times during the first decade of his life, and Holmes Ⅳ had understood, even as a small boy, that these journeys were not ordinary ones. The record he kept is not a diary; it has no dates and no sequence of entries. It is instead a set of recollections, written down at intervals as they became clear to him in retrospect: the smell of the cottage; the quality of the light over the Downs in different weathers; the old man’s hands, which he remembered as precise and still; the bees, which were everywhere in summer and which his great-grandfather discussed with an earnestness that a child could follow, and that Holmes Ⅳ came to understand, much later, was not a simplification for the child’s benefit but the genuine thing — the same complete attention brought to the same subject that had once been brought to criminal networks and ciphers and the unrecorded history of four thousand years.

What the record preserves most precisely is the quality of conversation. The Great Detective, in his final decade, did not perform for visitors, not even for the smallest ones. He asked questions. He listened to the answers. He asked more questions. Holmes Ⅳ, who was three or four or five years old, answered them with the directness of a child who had not yet learned the adult habit of managing what he disclosed. The old man noted what the boy said with the concentration of a man still in practice — not indulging a great-grandchild, but attending to an intelligence he had taken the measure of and found, in the characteristic manner of the line, worth attending to. Holmes Ⅳ could not have articulated this at the time. He articulated it, with considerable precision, in the private record he kept, across the long years of his career in the law. It was, he wrote, the most complete attention he had ever been given. He did not think he had ever been given it again, by anyone, in quite the same way.

In later years, Holmes Ⅳ’s records note visits from Dr. Amelia Watson, a contemporary of his own generation and a direct descendant of John H. Watson. She had, for some months, been keeping a personal chronicle of Boston medical and legal affairs, which she shared with Holmes Ⅳ during discussions that ranged from clinical ethics to forensic procedure. It was in these sessions, often held over the clatter of a distant hospital ward or the rustle of financial files, that she first began to record the remarkable acuity with which he approached complex human behaviors and financial structures. The account she maintained — her own voice — would become a primary repository for the observation of Holmes Ⅳ in action, capturing his methods and temperament from a witness uniquely positioned to note both the gift and its occasional blind spots.

The Matter of Aldrich, Voss & Mercer

From the private chronicle of Dr. Amelia Watson, maintained in parallel with her clinical rounds at Massachusetts General Hospital, and not intended for publication during the lifetimes of those named herein.

Boston had been wearing its particular November face for three weeks — a face that those who know the city recognize as distinct from the November of, say, Chicago or New York: colder in intent than in temperature, the harbor wind carrying not merely cold but a kind of institutional disapproval, as though the city itself had reviewed your conduct for the year and found the file wanting. The light off the water was the color of old pewter. The elm trees along Commonwealth Avenue had surrendered their leaves with what I can only describe as relief.

I had spent the morning in the trauma unit reviewing cases that, in their human wreckage and their narrative incompleteness, bore a closer resemblance to criminal proceedings than most members of the public would find comfortable. I arrived at the offices of the Federal Public Corruption Unit on the sixth floor of the McCormack Building at half past one, carrying two cups of coffee from the cart in the lobby — a precaution I had learned, over the preceding months, was not hospitality but necessity, since the man I was coming to see would not remember to acquire one himself and would, by mid-afternoon, have been running on nothing but inference and cold case files since before six in the morning.

Holmes was not in the outer office.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was the quality of the silence beyond the interior door — not the productive silence of concentrated work, which had its own density and rhythm I had come to recognize, but something flatter and more dangerous. I knocked. There was a pause of the kind that suggests the person within is deciding something.
