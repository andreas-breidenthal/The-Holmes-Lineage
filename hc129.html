SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅴ

Born 1970 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was his father's son in the way that the line had always produced its sons: not by any particular intention on either side, but by the operation of something in the blood that expressed itself, in each generation, as the same fundamental faculty in a new set of circumstances. His father had explained it to him, as his great-great-grandfather had explained it to Holmes Ⅳ, as a thing to be understood rather than celebrated — a tool with specific purposes and specific limitations, not a general superiority but a particular kind of sight that came with the obligation to use it for something other than personal advantage. Holmes Ⅴ absorbed this instruction and, by his own account, failed to live up to it in approximately the ways that his father had predicted he would: he was impatient with people who did not follow his reasoning; he was poor at concealing what he had concluded; and he had, as his daughter Charlotte observed in her less guarded moments, the intermittent quality of being entirely present and the more frequent quality of being present in body while absent in everything else, which was difficult to live with and impossible to argue with because he was, when challenged about it, always able to explain precisely where he had been and why.

He was educated at Harvard, like his father, read law, and then did not practice it — turning instead to the adjacent but less constrained world of policy analysis and congressional consultation, where the analytical faculty he had inherited could be applied to the large, complex, poorly documented systems of power and money that his generation had inherited from the Cold War and were dismantling, or believed they were dismantling, with varying degrees of success. He worked as a consultant to several Senate committees through the nineties and into the first years of the new century, with a reputation for finding the thing in a document collection that everyone else had looked at and not seen.

Among the papers and diaries that survive from this period, there appears a voice distinct from Holmes Ⅴ, that of Edward Watson, a contemporary and great-great-grandson of Dr. John Watson. Edward recorded his impressions and the actions of Holmes Ⅴ with meticulous care, providing his own perspective — at once observant and occasionally constrained by personal concern — and offering the account that follows.

The singular failure of my friend Mr. Holmes V

There are cases in the chronicle of my friend that I have set down with pride, and cases I have set down with admiration, and cases — the rarer kind — that I have set down because not to do so felt like a dereliction of some duty I could not precisely name. This account belongs to the last category. I write it not because it reflects well on anyone, including myself, and not because the resolution satisfies, for there is no resolution. I write it because it happened, and because a record that omits the failures in order to preserve the legend is not a record at all. It is mythology. Holmes himself would be the first to say so, and he has said so, in the months since, in the particular flat and careful voice he uses when he has decided that the truth requires stating regardless of the cost to the speaker.

I will state what I observed. I will state what I failed to observe, and why. I will state what my friend found, and what was done with what he found, and what arrived on the morning of September 11, 2001, and I will trust the reader to draw the only conclusion available.

Washington in the summer of 2001 had the quality of all Washington summers — a heat that was not merely climatic but administrative, a torpor that settled over the capital like a second government, slower and more powerful than the first, resistant to all legislation. I had come down from New York in late June to consult on a project unrelated to Holmes's work, and had called on him at the apartment he kept in Georgetown — a converted floor of a Federal-era row house that he had filled, over the years, with a density of paper that had ceased to alarm me but continued to unsettle his housekeepers, all three of whom had resigned under circumstances he described as "a mutual philosophical incompatibility."

I found him in his characteristic summer disposition, which is to say: inert.

He was lying on the chesterfield with his shoes on — a gesture of civilizational contempt that he performed only during genuine ennui — staring at the ceiling plaster with the focused vacancy of a man inspecting something no one else could see. On the coffee table beside him, three empty coffee mugs had accumulated in a formation that suggested a brief period of energetic thought followed by a longer period of its opposite. The television was on with the sound muted. CNN was showing footage of something — a summit, a ceremony — that he was not watching.

"Watson," he said, without looking at me, "you've driven, not flown, and you left later than you intended because you stopped somewhere on the way. Maryland, I'd say. The place that does the steamed crabs."

"Annapolis," I said. "Yes."

"I could smell it before you reached the door. Old Bay is not a subtle spice." He turned his head then, with the slow deliberateness of someone returning from a considerable distance. "You look well. Healthier than you were in March. You've been running again."

"I have."

"Good. Sit down. I am experiencing what I believe clinicians would call a depressive episode, though I prefer to think of it as a rational response to the quality of the available problems." He gestured at the television. "There is nothing happening. There is, I grant you, a great deal of noise suggesting that things are happening, but noise and events are not synonymous, as this city consistently fails to understand."

This was the Holmes I knew from the intervals between cases — the man who existed in the troughs between the peaks, poorly suited to his own company, sustained by the particular narcotic of the unsolvable problem and poorly equipped when no such problem was available. His great-great-grandfather, I knew from the family papers, had kept the same hours, practiced the same economies of motion, and had the same quality of seeming to occupy the space between states of matter. Watson the First had written about it with the tolerant exasperation of long acquaintance. I had learned to do the same.

"The committee work," I said. "Nothing of interest?"

He sat up, which was progress.

"The committee work," he said, in a different tone entirely — not the tone of ennui, but not quite its opposite. Something in between, which I had learned to pay close attention to. "The committee work is, in fact, the thing I was lying here considering when you arrived."

He stood, which was more progress, and moved to the desk by the window — a surface covered in the organized chaos he called a filing system and which I had long since ceased to attempt to understand — and retrieved a folder. Not from any obvious location. From somewhere in the middle of what appeared to be a geological stratum of documents.

"Sit," he said. "I want to walk you through something. It's been three weeks since I finished it, and I find that the act of explaining it to an intelligent non-specialist helps me identify whether I've made an error in the chain."

I sat.

"I have almost certainly not made an error," he said, which was the thing he always said before explaining something he was not quite certain of, "but I want to be sure."

The Georgetown apartment had one window that faced east and caught the morning light, which by mid-afternoon had tracked around and left the room in a pleasant dimness. Holmes had lit none of the lamps. He preferred to work in what he called "thinking light" — not darkness, not brightness, but the ambiguous grey middle in which, he claimed, the visual cortex stopped trying to process the environment and allowed the analytic faculties to operate without competition.

He spread the folder's contents across the cleared half of the coffee table — I use the word "cleared" loosely, in the sense that he had moved the three coffee mugs to the floor — and I saw what he had assembled.

It was, at first glance, nothing. At second glance, it was still nothing — a collection of pages that appeared to be printouts of various documents, some official-looking, some clearly downloaded from public sources, a handful of what appeared to be spreadsheet tables. I recognized immigration service formatting on some of the pages. Flight school enrollment records from three or four different states. Wire transfer documentation that appeared to have originated from a financial intelligence database.

"Open sources," Holmes said, watching me look at it. "With the exception of those three" — he indicated a small stack to the left — "which the committee gave me access to and which contain nothing that isn't available elsewhere if you know the right databases. The classification on those is, in my opinion, performative. It is the government's way of labeling things 'official' while deriving no actual informational advantage from the label. But I needed them to understand the wire transfer sequence, so I used them."

"What am I looking at?"

"You are looking at a preparation," he said. "Specifically, you are looking at the documentary shadow of a preparation — the pattern that a complex logistical operation leaves in its wake, not because the operators are careless, but because no complex operation can be executed without leaving traces, and traces accumulate into patterns, and patterns are legible to anyone willing to look at them rather than through them."

He sat across from me and folded his hands with the deliberateness of a man who has organized his thoughts into a sequence and intends to deliver them in order.

"Begin with the wire transfers," he said. "Three jurisdictions — Germany, the UAE, and Florida. The transfers are individually unremarkable. Small amounts. Donations to charitable entities of the kind that appear on no watchlist and trigger no automated flags. But the timing is not random. The intervals between the transfers correspond to a cadence — not precisely, because precision would itself be a flag, but approximately. A cadence that suggests coordination between parties who are not in direct contact but who are operating from a shared schedule."

"Someone noticed this?"

"No one noticed this. That is the point of the method. Each transfer, viewed in isolation, is noise. Viewed together — and together is how I am asking you to view them — they are signal. They are the heartbeat of a distributed operation keeping itself alive."

He moved to the next stack.

"The flight school enrollments. Nine individuals across four states — Arizona, California, Oklahoma, Florida — enrolled in commercial aviation training programs in a specific window. Again, individually unremarkable. Students come from abroad, pay with funds from family sources, train and leave. This is a normal pattern. But these nine share characteristics that are not, individually, alarming, and are, in combination, not random."

He began to list them, without consulting his notes.

"They are enrolled in programs that emphasize flight hours and instrument training but not takeoff and landing. In several cases, the training is explicitly focused on large commercial aircraft simulations. None of them has expressed an interest in certification. None of them has applied for any commercial aviation position. They are learning to fly very large aircraft in a very specific way, and they are not learning to land them."

The room was very quiet. The muted television continued its silent business in the background.

"I made a list," Holmes continued, "of the target categories available in the relevant geography that would justify this specific training profile. Not a long list. The method — aerial approach with a large commercial aircraft — reduces the target set considerably. The target must be stationary. It must be symbolic as well as structural. It must produce the maximum economic and psychological impact from a minimum of operational investment."

He looked at me.

"I believe you have already assembled the conclusion I am walking toward," he said.

"Holmes—"

"The report uses the phrase 'critical infrastructure with high symbolic value and concentrated population density,'" he said, precisely. "I was specific but not too specific. I did not name a building. I named a category. The category is not large."

He had delivered the report three weeks before I arrived, through the committee's senior staff director — a man whose name I will render here as Grady, for reasons of prudent discretion — in the form of a twelve-page memorandum with four appendices, all cited, all sourced, all internally consistent. Holmes had, as was his practice, constructed the document with the meticulousness of a legal brief. Every inference was labeled as an inference. Every documented fact was labeled as documented. The chain was explicit and complete.

"What did Grady say?" I asked.

Holmes considered this question with the expression he wore when asked to report on social interactions — a slight contraction around the eyes, a pause to ensure accuracy.

"Grady said, and I am quoting with precision because the phrasing struck me as significant at the time, that the committee 'appreciated the diligence' and would 'include the analysis in the appropriate briefing channel.' He said this while placing the folder into an out-tray."

He was quiet for a moment.

"An out-tray," I said.

"There is a particular ritual in this city," Holmes said, and now his voice had taken on a different quality — not cold, but careful, in the way of a man navigating around something he has chosen not to feel until later. "When an institution receives information that it cannot immediately use — information that does not fit the existing model, information that would require action the institution is not prepared to take — it does not reject the information. Rejection would require a decision. It files the information. Filing is not a decision. Filing is an administrative action that preserves the option of a future decision while committing to nothing present."

"And the briefing channel?"

"I have, in three weeks, received no acknowledgment of receipt. No follow-up questions. No request for supplementary analysis. No indication that the material has traveled further than Grady's out-tray." He looked at the folder. "Which may not be true, of course. I have no visibility into the downstream. It is possible the analysis reached someone capable of acting on it and was evaluated and found insufficient. That is a possibility I must hold."

"But you don't believe it."

"I believe," he said, with the studied neutrality of a man doing his best with an impossible question, "that I have encountered the principal limitation of the analytical method when applied to large institutional systems. The method produces conclusions. The conclusions require an action from the institution to have effect. The institution has its own logic — its own priorities, its own existing commitments, its own hierarchy of what constitutes a credible source for what kind of claim. My analysis contradicts no existing intelligence assessment, which means it cannot be dismissed. But it is also not an existing intelligence assessment — it is the work of an outside consultant operating in the open-source space — which means it does not carry the weight required to generate immediate action. It exists in a category the system has no good protocol for."

He stood, and moved to the window, and looked out at the Georgetown street with its late-afternoon light and its brick and its ordinary human traffic.

"The document is in the system," he said. "Whether the system knows what to do with the document is a different question."

I left Washington at the end of July, and returned to New York, and spent August in the habitual patterns of a man who has heard something he cannot quite put down. I called Holmes twice. On the first occasion, he had nothing new to report and seemed, if anything, more deeply in the languor than before — the energy that had carried him through the assembly of the analysis had dissipated, and the waiting was doing what waiting always did to him, which was to become, in the absence of new data, a kind of static that he could neither process nor silence.

On the second occasion, he said something I have thought about many times since.

"The difficulty," he said, "with a prediction of this kind is that there are only two outcomes, and both of them are in some sense catastrophic. If the event does not occur — because the operation was disrupted by means I cannot observe, or because my analysis was incorrect, or because the timeline was longer than I estimated — then I have delivered a false positive and damaged whatever credibility I have with the committee, which means the next document will be taken less seriously. And if the event occurs—"

He stopped.

"If the event occurs," I said, after a moment.

"Then the document in Grady's out-tray becomes something else entirely," he said. "And there is nothing to be done with that, either."

This is the last conversation I have recorded from the weeks before September. I have sometimes wondered what I might have done differently. I have come to the conclusion that this question is not a useful one, because the honest answer is: nothing material. I was a witness and a biographer, not an actor in any system with the authority to act. I note this not in self-exculpation but in the interest of accuracy.

I was in my apartment on the Upper West Side when the first reports came on. I watched the television with the same mute incomprehension as everyone else, the kind that comes not from a failure of understanding but from understanding arriving faster than the mind can assimilate it. I understood what I was seeing. I understood it because Holmes had walked me through the logic in a Georgetown apartment six weeks earlier, in the particular dimness he called thinking light, with the documents laid on a coffee table between us.

I called him at nine forty-three. He answered immediately, which told me he had not slept.

"You know," I said. It was not a question.

"I know," he said.

"The report—"

"Is in the system," he said, with a flatness that was the sound of something being held at a very deliberate distance. "Where it has been for the last fifteen weeks. I imagine there will be a great deal of interest in it now."

"Holmes."

"Watson." A pause. "There is nothing to be said that is proportionate to what has happened. I am aware of that. I find that I am not, at present, able to feel it properly — the scale of it defeats the instrument. I expect that will change."

It did change. I can record that, for the sake of the record. It changed over the weeks and months that followed in ways that he permitted no one, including me, to observe directly. But I am an observant man, trained in the practice of observation by the best teacher I have had, and I recorded what I could.

I recorded the three days during which he did not eat, and the silence that was different from his usual silence. I recorded the night, six weeks later, when I found him at the desk at three in the morning with the original folder open in front of him and a pen in his hand and nothing written. I recorded the particular quality of stillness he had when the congressional hearings began, and the report's existence became, in the language of official Washington, "one of multiple pieces of analytic work under review," which is a phrase that means everything and is designed to mean nothing.

I recorded his answer when I asked him, in December, whether he believed his analysis had been read before the event.

"I believe," he said, with the care he always applied to questions about belief versus knowledge, "that the document exists. I believe that existence is not the same as reading. I believe that reading is not the same as understanding. I believe that understanding is not the same as the authority to act. I believe that the authority to act is not the same as the will to act. And I believe that the distance between a document in an out-tray and an action commensurate with what the document describes is, in this particular institutional context, considerably larger than I had estimated when I began the work." A pause. "This was a failure of my analysis. I analyzed the operational pattern correctly. I did not analyze the institutional reception correctly. I should have. I did not, and this is the error I have to account for."

"You're accounting for the wrong error," I said, with more heat than I intended.

He looked at me with the expression — rare enough that I had learned to mark it — that meant he was not going to construct a counter-argument, which from Holmes was the closest thing to agreement available.

There will be investigations. There will be, in the language of Washington, findings and recommendations and systemic reforms, the usual scar tissue that institutions generate over the places where they have failed. Holmes will not testify. He has been asked; he has declined; he has offered, in lieu of testimony, to provide any document in his possession upon formal request. No formal request has been made. This is, he says, consistent with how these things work, and he says it without apparent bitterness, which is either evidence of his extraordinary self-control or evidence that he has, in the months since September, achieved some kind of philosophical accommodation with the limits of individual analysis in a world of institutional inertia.

I am not certain which it is. I am not certain he is certain, either.

I will record what I have seen. I will record that the document existed, and was delivered, and was received, and that the event it described arrived on the morning of a clear September day over a city going about its business. I will record that Holmes V, in the tradition of his line, found the thing in the document collection that everyone else had looked at and not seen — and delivered it, correctly labeled, through the appropriate channel, at the appropriate time — and that the appropriate channel was not, in this instance, equal to the task.

The family chronicle has had its failures before. The record shows them without apology. Holmes II, I am told, arrived two hours after the fact in a case he had solved. Holmes IV, by his own account, once reasoned his way to a conclusion so correct and so unverifiable that it was indistinguishable from being wrong.

This is different. This is the largest scale on which the instrument has been tested, and the test result is this: the instrument worked. The instrument found what it was designed to find. The instrument delivered what it found. And the world in which the instrument operates did not, in this instance, know how to receive what the instrument delivered.

I do not know what to make of this. I have been making notes for twenty years, in the tradition of my family, and I do not know what to make of this.

I think, on some mornings, that it means the method is insufficient — that finding is not enough, that the line must develop, in its next iteration, some capacity for institutional translation, for converting analysis into action, for navigating the bureaucratic architecture that stands between a conclusion and its consequences.

I think, on other mornings, that it means nothing of the kind — that the failure was specific and systemic and not a comment on the method but on the moment, and that another moment in another context would have had a different result.

Holmes, when I put this to him, said: "Both may be true. The line does not have the luxury of choosing which to believe. It has the obligation to account for both."

I have written that down. I have written all of it down. That is the only thing I know how to do.

The document exists. This account exists. I have set down what I observed, without selecting for comfort.

That, Watson the First wrote somewhere in his journals, is the deal.

I believe he was right.

— E. Watson, Georgetown, January 2002
