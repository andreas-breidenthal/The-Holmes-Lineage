SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅴ

1970 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was his father's son in the way that the line had always produced its sons: not by any particular intention on either side, but by the operation of something in the blood that expressed itself, in each generation, as the same fundamental faculty in a new set of circumstances. His father had explained it to him, as his great-great-grandfather had explained it to Holmes Ⅳ, as a thing to be understood rather than celebrated — a tool with specific purposes and specific limitations, not a general superiority but a particular kind of sight that came with the obligation to use it for something other than personal advantage. Holmes Ⅴ absorbed this instruction and, by his own account, failed to live up to it in approximately the ways that his father had predicted he would: he was impatient with people who did not follow his reasoning; he was poor at concealing what he had concluded; and he had, as his daughter Charlotte observed in her less guarded moments, the intermittent quality of being entirely present and the more frequent quality of being present in body while absent in everything else, which was difficult to live with and impossible to argue with because he was, when challenged about it, always able to explain precisely where he had been and why.

He was educated at Harvard, like his father, read law, and then did not practice it — turning instead to the adjacent but less constrained world of policy analysis and congressional consultation, where the analytical faculty he had inherited could be applied to the large, complex, poorly documented systems of power and money that his generation had inherited from the Cold War and were dismantling, or believed they were dismantling, with varying degrees of success. He worked as a consultant to several Senate committees through the nineties and into the first years of the new century, with a reputation for finding the thing in a document collection that everyone else had looked at and not seen.

Among the papers and diaries that survive from this period, there appears a voice distinct from Holmes Ⅴ, that of Edward Watson, a contemporary and great-great-grandson of Dr. John Watson. Edward recorded his impressions and the actions of Holmes Ⅴ with meticulous care, providing his own perspective — at once observant and occasionally constrained by personal concern — and offering the account that follows.

The Case of the Fractured Training

Being an Account of the Investigations of Mr. Sherlock Holmes V

As recorded by Edward Watson

The most expensive errors are not the ones made in ignorance. They are the ones made from evidence that was real, from reasoning that was sound, and from a conclusion that was almost entirely true. I have spent three years attempting to improve upon that sentence and have not managed it.

I set it down here at the head of this account because it is the only epitaph I am able to offer. Holmes would not thank me for it. He has not, to my knowledge, offered one himself.

I.

I had encountered Carlisle on two previous occasions — once at a State Department reception in the spring of '98 which Holmes had attended under protest and I had attended out of loyalty, and once in connection with a shipping matter the previous winter that had briefly acquired the dimensions of a political crisis before Holmes reduced it to its actual proportions. He was a man who occupied the useful middle ground of government: senior enough to act without friction, junior enough to be disowned if necessary. Holmes had described him, after the shipping case, as a man who knew which doors opened inward and which required a different approach entirely.

He arrived at the Georgetown offices on a Tuesday morning in June without telephoning ahead. He was shown in by Mrs. Alvarez, who managed the front office with a composed efficiency that Holmes relied upon and occasionally remembered to acknowledge. Carlisle entered carrying a slim briefcase in his left hand, and Holmes, who had been standing at the window with his back to the door, turned and regarded him for a moment before gesturing to the chair opposite his desk.

"Sit down, Carlisle."

Carlisle sat, and placed the briefcase on the floor beside his chair. Holmes remained standing, his grey-green eyes moving over our visitor with the unhurried attention that most people find unsettling on first encounter and merely unnerving thereafter.

"You have come from a meeting at which you were not welcome," Holmes said. "The meeting was this morning, before nine. You were kept waiting, which you had not anticipated, and the outcome was a refusal. You have carried that briefcase for seven years and never once opened it in another man's presence."

Carlisle looked at him steadily. "The briefcase is a reasonable inference. I grant you that. The rest—"

"The briefcase is not an inference at all. The locks are unscratched, the clasps unloosened by habitual use — a case carried everywhere and opened nowhere. Its purpose is not to contain documents but to suggest that documents exist. As for this morning—" Holmes moved to his own chair and sat — "the building you visited issues magnetic key cards to its visitors, which must be returned on departure. You returned yours in some haste, and the clip has left a mark on your lapel that was not there when that suit was pressed, which was yesterday evening. You visited a building that issues such cards, you left it quickly, and you came here without stopping for the breakfast you had planned, since you have a precise and unvarying habit in that regard — I recall you declining a second cup of coffee at the Meridian Club in February on the grounds that you had already had your two. You have this morning had none. The conclusion is that your morning did not proceed as you intended, and that we are your second choice of audience for whatever it is you have in that briefcase you will not open."

There was a silence.

"Is it always like this?" Carlisle asked.

"Only when it matters," I said. "What have you brought us?"

He reached not into the briefcase but into the inner pocket of his jacket and produced a single folded sheet of paper. He smoothed it on the desk between us. It was a letter, handwritten, two careful paragraphs. A flight instructor in Florida — a man named Dekker — had written it some weeks earlier to a contact in the civil aviation authority, describing a discomfort he could not fully articulate. Several of his students, over a period of perhaps fourteen months, had displayed an unusual pattern of study. They were diligent, even exceptional, in most areas of their training. There was one area in which they showed no interest at all.

The letter did not specify which area.

Holmes read it once, set it down, and looked at Carlisle.

"Which phase of training?"

"We don't know. Dekker wouldn't say in writing. He has — thus far — declined to be interviewed."

"Then we will go to Florida," Holmes said, "and he will say it to us."

II.

We found Dekker at the academy the following afternoon. He was a heavyset man in his middle fifties, former Air Force, with the physical self-possession of someone who had once been required to make rapid decisions and now found the civilian pace of things faintly insufficient. He had three qualities I noted at once: the tip of his left index finger was absent, lost to some earlier machinery; he had a habit of setting down whatever he was holding before answering a question, as though clearing the decks; and his eyes, when they met Holmes's, held the specific wariness of a man who has already talked himself out of his own convictions and is not certain he was right to do so.

He received us in his office, which was a small room off the main hangar, smelling of aviation fuel and old coffee. An oscillating fan moved the warm air from one side of the room to the other without improving it.

"Mr. Dekker," Holmes said, settling into the offered chair with the ease of a man who had nowhere else to be. "I understand you have concerns about certain students."

"I wrote what I had to say." He set down his coffee mug. "I don't know that I have more than that."

"Let us examine what you do have," Holmes said pleasantly. "You have been instructing civilian pilots for — eleven years, I think?"

"Twelve."

"Twelve. In twelve years you have developed an understanding of how students engage with the material. The keen ones, the adequate ones, the ones who will never quite feel it. You have a sense, quickly formed, of what a student intends to do with what they learn."

Dekker said nothing, but something in his expression shifted.

"These particular students," Holmes continued. "They were diligent."

"Very."

"Focused."

"Exceptionally."

"And the phase of training in which they displayed no interest?"

Dekker picked up his mug, then set it down again. "Instrument landing. ILS — instrument landing system. Pages 147 to 183 in the training manual. Approach and touchdown procedure. Not a single one of them requested simulator time in that module. They all put in extra hours elsewhere instead."

"May I see a manual?"

Dekker produced one from the shelf behind him. Holmes took it and did not open it immediately. He turned it over in his hands, examined the spine, ran his thumb along the top edge of the pages. Then he opened it at the index, found chapter eleven, and turned to page 147.

The binding cracked.

He turned back to page 60 — in-flight navigation — and the pages fell open of their own accord, as a well-used book will do at its most visited passages. He turned to page 20 — take-off procedure — and the same. He turned again to page 147. The resistance was unmistakable.

He looked up at Dekker. "How many students over the fourteen months?"

"That I noticed? Four. Could have been more. Most of them transferred in from somewhere — I didn't always see the full picture of where they'd come from or what they'd already covered."

"Transferred from other schools?"

"Some. One I know came up from an outfit in Arizona. Chandler, Arizona. Similar setup to ours."

Holmes closed the manual. "And you raised this with your management."

Dekker's jaw tightened. "I mentioned it. They told me student electives weren't my concern. That the hours balanced out on paper, and that was what mattered." He looked at the fan. "They weren't wrong, technically."

"But you are not technically satisfied."

"No," Dekker said quietly. "I am not."

Holmes handed the manual back. "I wonder, Mr. Dekker," he said, in the mild tone he employed when he had already arrived somewhere and was waiting for others to join him, "what you imagine a student intends when he learns everything about flying an aircraft and nothing about bringing it to the ground."

I had formed my own hypothesis by this point and offered it. "Credential fraud," I said. "Partial certification. They want the documentation without the—"

"They want the aircraft," Holmes said. He stood, and picked up his hat from the desk where he had placed it, and nodded to Dekker. "Thank you. I would ask you to say nothing of this conversation to your management. And I would ask you, most seriously, to reconsider your resignation — at least for the present."

Dekker looked at him sharply.

"The mark of the locker key is on your jacket," Holmes said. "You cleaned it out this morning. Clean it back in this afternoon, if you would be so good."

III.

The Chandler Aviation Academy sat in the flat brown heat twenty miles southeast of Phoenix, a more substantial operation than Dekker's, with four hangars and a fleet of twin-engine Cessnas that bespoke a grander ambition. Holmes had said very little on the drive from the airport beyond asking me, once, to confirm the precise wording of Dekker's statement about the transferred students.

Most of them transferred in from somewhere.

Not one. Most.

I noted it at the time, but thought no more of it. Holmes had apparently thought a great deal more of it.

The chief instructor in Chandler was a lean, precise woman named Farris who received us in a conference room with a jug of iced water and the brisk manner of someone who had agreed to give us an hour and intended to give us exactly that.

Holmes asked for the simulator logs. She produced them without hesitation — a more open operation than Dekker's, or a more confident one. He examined them for several minutes in silence while Farris and I maintained the polite, slightly strained conversation of people waiting for someone else to finish reading.

The pattern was there. Different names, different cohort, but the same conspicuous absence in the same module. And here too the manual's pages 147 to 183 might as well have been unprinted.

With one variation.

"These three students," Holmes said, setting his finger on three entries in the logs. "They attended instrument approach sessions."

"That's correct."

"Twice each."

"I believe so, yes."

"But the sign-off on each session is the same instructor." Holmes looked up. "A Mr. —" he glanced at the log — "Reyes."

"He was one of our senior instructors."

"Was."

A fractional pause. "He left us in March. A personal matter, we were told."

"You were told," Holmes repeated thoughtfully. "You were not given a reason."

"Not a specific one, no."

"And Mr. Reyes's forwarding address, in the event that his references were required?"

Farris looked at him steadily. "We were not given one."

Holmes thanked her for her time, and we left with twenty minutes of our hour unused. In the car park, in the ferocious Arizona sun, he stood for a moment looking at nothing in particular.

"Well?" I said.

"In Florida," he said, "they were careless. They left a chapter unread and did not trouble to conceal it." He turned toward the car. "Here, they sent three men to sit with an instructor for two sessions apiece, long enough to leave a mark in the logs, carefully not long enough to acquire the skill. Someone noticed the carelessness in Florida and corrected it here." He opened the car door. "And then, having made the correction, ensured that the man who could describe the correction was no longer available to describe it."

"One directing intelligence."

"One architect," Holmes said. "Yes. Let us go and find him."

IV.

Back in Washington, Holmes set to work on the documents Carlisle's contacts had quietly accumulated — flight school records, financial filings, visa applications, sponsorship letters, and thin folders of information on seventeen names assembled through channels Carlisle preferred not to specify. He spread them across the large table in the back room that had long since ceased to serve any other purpose, and I kept my notes and left him to it.

Three days later he called me in.

The funding chains behind both schools connected, through a series of intermediaries of increasing obscurity, to three financial entities in the Gulf states. The entities were not connected to one another on paper. They were connected by a pattern of transaction timing so precise — payments always issued on the same day of the month, always in amounts that fell just below the threshold of automatic review — that the pattern itself was the signature. No human accountant repeats himself so exactly without instruction. The instruction came from a single source.

That source Holmes had found in the visa sponsorship documents — three different charitable foundations, three different letterheads, one typographic habit. The author of all three used an em-dash where convention required a comma before a subordinate clause. In seventeen documents across three organisations. A man can change his name and his address and his affiliation. He cannot, without extraordinary vigilance, change the way he punctuates.

The transfer student from Arizona had come, in turn, from a school in Dearborn, Michigan, which connected to the same funding signature. Not a courier, Holmes concluded — a test. Sent between schools to confirm the doctrine was being applied consistently. A quality control measure.

The doctrine itself he stated in twelve words.

"Train them to fly. Do not trouble them with landing."

The operational purpose required no elaboration. The navigation training records — the routes studied, the airspace practiced — identified the primary target as the federal courthouse complex in Miami.

"One organisation," Holmes said. "One architect. And I can give Carlisle seventeen names."

He reached for the telephone.

V.

Holmes was absent for the better part of three weeks. The occasional brief communication reached me — a location, a confirmation, once a note in his own hand asking me to have certain documents ready on his return. I did not hear from him directly until he appeared at the Georgetown office on a Tuesday afternoon, thinner, and without volunteering anything of what had occurred.

A communication from Carlisle arrived the following morning. The network as Holmes had described it had been dismantled. The primary target had been secured. The Coral Gables office that had served as the network's domestic nerve centre had been entered, its occupants detained, its records seized.

That evening we opened a bottle of wine that had been waiting in the office for an occasion sufficient to justify it. Holmes took three glasses, which was exceptional. At some point he said something about his ancestor — that the old man would have enjoyed the punctuation, specifically, and would have been characteristically silent on the subject of enjoying it. There was an affection in it that Holmes rarely permitted himself in connection with the name he carries.

I believed, that evening, that we had finished.

VI.

It was twelve days later, on a Sunday night, that my telephone rang. I was asleep. The clock read twenty past midnight.

"Come in," Holmes said. "Now, if you would."

I dressed and took a cab to Georgetown.

He was at his desk when I arrived, the room lit and the street outside dark, surrounded by documents I recognised as those we had long since considered closed. He was holding the Chandler simulator logs, not reading them so much as looking at them — with the particular quality of attention he directs at things that have begun to trouble him.

"Dekker said most of them transferred in from somewhere," he said, without looking up.

"He did."

"Not some. Not several." Holmes set the log down. "Most."

"You followed the transfer student's trail to Dearborn. It connected to the network."

"I followed one trail." He looked at me then, and there was something in his face I had not seen in eleven years together — not uncertainty, but its near neighbour. "I followed the trail I expected to find, and found it, and stopped following."

He laid three documents on the desk in front of me. Records from a flight school in New Jersey and one in Minnesota that I did not recognise. They had arrived in the original package from Carlisle. The funding signature was absent. They did not connect to the network. He had set them aside as irrelevant.

My eye went immediately to the training logs.

Pages 147 to 183.

"Holmes—"

"Yes," he said.

The room was very quiet.

"No connection to the primary network," I said.

"None. Separate funding. Separate student cohorts. Different nationalities, different documentation, different financial origin. No shared personnel. No shared infrastructure." He stood and moved to the window. "I assumed that identical method implied shared origin. I found one organism and mapped it completely and dismantled it completely. I asked whether the pattern was consistent." He turned from the window. "I did not ask whether it was singular."

"A second cell."

"Independent. Autonomous. The same doctrine — because the doctrine is effective, not because they share an architect. Two men may reach the same solution to the same problem without having met." He reached for the telephone. "Get Carlisle on the line. Please."

VII.

The New Jersey and Minnesota schools were raided within eighteen hours. The student cohorts were traced. Twenty individuals were identified as having passed through that secondary network. One was located — already in custody on a visa violation. Nineteen were not.

Carlisle telephoned on the morning of the second day, shortly before nine. Holmes took the call at his desk. I was in the room, watching the early light move across the floor.

The conversation lasted less than a minute. Holmes did not speak at all; he merely held the receiver to his ear, his face becoming a mask of grey stone. When he finally set the receiver down, he did not look at me. He looked at his hands, folded precisely on the blotter.

He said nothing. He did not need to. From the front office, we heard the low, urgent murmur of Mrs. Alvarez's radio, the tone of the broadcaster shifting into that register of controlled alarm that precedes a catastrophe.

We sat in that room for just over half an hour. Holmes did not move. He did not reach for a file; he did not light a cigarette. He sat with the stillness of a man watching a fuse he has discovered too late to extinguish.

It was shortly after nine-forty when the silence was finally broken. It began as a low vibration in the floorboards, followed moments later by the first of the sirens — not the solitary wail of a passing ambulance, but the sustained, rising cacophony of a city's entire emergency apparatus screaming into motion at once.

VIII.

Holmes returned to work.

He moved through it with the same precision and the same economy of attention. But I have observed, in the years since, that whenever a case resolves into a clean and satisfying unity — when the threads draw together and the conclusion presents itself complete — he stops. He goes back. He looks for what he set aside.

He has not said why. He has not needed to.

E.W., 2004
