ROBERT DE HOLMES

~1356 — Yorkshire, England

Robert de Holmes was born in the plague’s aftermath and grew up in a world remade by it — a Yorkshire in which labour was expensive, land was cheap, and the old hierarchies were under pressure from a mobility that the Black Death had made structurally inevitable. He understood this world with the intuitive grasp of someone formed by it rather than merely observing it, and he used his understanding to acquire, steadily and without ostentation, land that less nimble neighbours were letting go at prices that reflected desperation rather than value. The Chronicler observes that his manner differed from his father’s: where Richard acted in response to disruption, Robert anticipated it and positioned himself accordingly.

The Peasants’ Revolt of 1381 passed largely over Yorkshire, but Robert watched it with close attention — not because it threatened him directly, but because it told him something about the condition of the country and the direction in which pressure was building. He had no enemies among his tenants. This was not accident. He had made a careful study, over years, of what men needed in order to remain content, and had provided it, not from generosity — though he was not ungenerous — but from the reasoned calculation that a stable household was worth more than a profitable one. He left his son an estate considerably larger than the one he had inherited, and a reputation in the North Riding that was, by the end of his life, the family’s most valuable asset.

The surviving account of the following case is preserved in the papers of Edmund Marlow, a scrivener of Beverley, who was retained by the borough to keep records of legal proceedings and commercial disputes. Marlow was methodical in his recording but cautious in his conclusions, and his account reflects both his diligence and his reluctance to draw inference beyond what could be proven. What follows is drawn from his notes, supplemented where necessary by the Chronicler to resolve inconsistencies between fragments of the record.

The Papers of Edmund Marlow

A note in the hand of Edmund Marlow, set before the record:

I have been asked, in my capacity as keeper of proceedings for this borough, to set down the matter of Aldwin Pest's silver chest in such form as may serve the interests of record. I do so with the caveat that certain of the reasoning herein attributed to Robert de Holmes I have reconstructed from his own account given to me afterward. I have suppressed the name of the Lombard merchant involved, as the debt in question was voided by agreement and no further proceeding was taken. I have not, however, suppressed any fact or observation by which the truth of the matter was discovered, for the truth belonged to the evidence long before it belonged to us.

It had been a November of particular grey — not the clean grey of frost, but the grey of wool left too long in standing water, a colour that had soaked into the very timbers of Beverley's market buildings and would not be wrung out. The smell of tallow was on everything. The lamps in the Guild hall burned a thick, animal smell that one associated with poverty even in a prosperous room. And the room where Aldwin Pest delivered his complaint was prosperous enough: new rush on the floor, a wall-hanging of some ambition if not skill, and a heavy oak table at which I, Edmund Marlow, sat with my ledgers, the green wax of the borough seal resting beside my inkhorn.

Robert de Holmes stood at the window.

He had not sat since Pest entered. He stood with his back to the room, watching the street below with an attention that appeared indifferent and was not. I had observed this before — this posture of absence that was, in fact, a posture of accumulation. He was collecting something from Pest's voice.

Pest himself was a man of middle years and middle size, his face carrying the particular arrangement of lines that comes to those who have always had enough but have feared, at intervals, that they might not. I noted him particularly as he stood before my table: a careful man, I judged, for a fresh flake of crimson sealing wax—the distinctive hue of the Lombardy brokers—clung to his thumb, and the smooth half of a split hazel tally-stick, notched with the deep double-scores of a wool-contract, protruded from his belt-pouch. Evidently, he was a man diligent in his affairs, having likely just come from a final reckoning with his creditors before presenting his grievance to us.

He told his story with the fluency of a man who had rehearsed it.

The silver: fourteen pounds and some shillings, accumulated over years of careful husbandry, kept in an iron-strapped chest in the solar of his house. The circumstance: the disarray of the years following the Mortality, when his household had shrunk from nine souls to four, when labourers presented themselves for hire at one door and departed without warning by another.

"It was Thomas Wren," Pest declared, his hands gripping the edge of my table. "A labourer I took on in the spring. Three days ago, he vanished in the night. I slept in the chamber directly beneath the solar, and so heavy was the sleep of a weary man that I heard not a sound until the morning light showed me the ruin of my security. I found the chest's heavy iron hasp smashed, the lock broken, and the silver gone. Wren has fled with it."

"Wren fled on Tuesday," I supplied, running my finger down the borough gate-roll, eager to provide the official corroboration required. "The North Gate watch recorded him passing at dawn, paying the base foot-passenger toll. A man bearing no goods of taxable weight. Just a canvas bundle." I felt a surge of vindication on Pest's behalf; the scoundrel's flight was a matter of record.

Robert de Holmes turned from the window.

"Tell me," said Robert de Holmes, "how long before yesterday did you last open the chest?"

Pest blinked. "In August. After the grain harvest."

"And your Michaelmas wool clip?" Holmes asked, his voice entirely level. "A prosperous flock, I hear. You did not add the profits of that sale to your chest?"

"I... I kept it separate," Pest said, shifting his weight. "For buying winter fodder."

"And the lock," Holmes continued. "Smashed with a heavy iron hammer, you say? The iron hasp entirely broken off?"

"Shattered," Pest confirmed, regaining his footing. "A violent blow. The villain showed no care for my property."

"No care at all," Holmes murmured. He walked slowly to the table and tapped a long finger against my open ledger. "Fourteen pounds of silver coin is a volume of metal that cannot be hidden in a sleeve, Master Pest. It is a dead, clinking weight. Yet Master Marlow's immaculate records show that Wren passed the watchmen paying the toll for a man bearing no goods, carrying only a soft canvas bundle. Furthermore, a hammer blow sufficient to shatter an iron hasp makes a sound that echoes through the timbers of a quiet house like a struck bell. Yet you, sleeping in the chamber below, heard nothing on the night Wren departed."

Pest's face paled, the rehearsed fluency suddenly drying in his throat. "He... he must have wrapped his hammer in cloth. He must have hidden the silver in the woods to retrieve later."

"He did neither," Robert de Holmes said, the edge of his voice sharpening. "Because the chest was not smashed by Thomas Wren. You smashed it yourself, yesterday morning, to conceal a theft that occurred months ago. A theft committed by you."

I set my quill down, staring at Holmes. "Robert, the man's silver is gone. Why would he steal his own coin?"

"Because it was no longer his, Edmund," Holmes replied, his eyes fixed on Pest's belt. "He did not open the chest in August because it was already empty. The silver was moved before Michaelmas — to a deposit in Beverley, where it serves as security against a liability he could not meet from his income and could not acknowledge to his household."

"You speak madness," Pest whispered, though his hands shook.

"I speak of the evidence you brought into this room," Holmes said. He pointed to Pest's right hand. "You carry the debtor's half of a split hazel tally-stick in your pouch, notched for a volume of wool your flock has not yet produced. You bear the fresh flake of crimson sealing wax upon your thumb. As Master Marlow well knows, the borough exchequer uses the green wax that sits upon this very table. Crimson is the mark of the Lombardy merchants who control the wool-trade in this borough."

Holmes stepped closer, the structure of the deception fully illuminated. "The Mortality left you short of hands. You borrowed heavily from a Lombard broker to hire men for the spring planting. You surrendered your chest's contents as security. When Michaelmas arrived, you were forced to hand over your entire wool clip merely to satisfy the broker's interest — which is why that coin never went into the chest. Knowing your wife would soon demand silver for the winter provisions, you seized upon the sudden departure of your labourer, Thomas Wren, as a desperate salvation. You smashed your own lock, blamed the fleeing man, and came here to put a false pursuit on the official record."

The room was entirely silent save for the crackle of the tallow lamps. Pest did not deny it. The shape of the truth was too heavy, and he collapsed under it, sinking onto the bench beside my table. His head fell into his hands.

"I would have been ruined," Pest said to the floor. "My land, my tenants. All taken."

When Pest could offer no further defence, Robert de Holmes moved to resolution. He did not call for the guard.

"The debt will be restructured," Holmes said quietly. "I will speak with the Lombard myself; he owes my family a favour regarding the lease of his warehouses. The silver will be returned to your chest within the year, paid off gradually from your harvests. The matter of the theft will be allowed to resolve in Master Marlow's ledger as 'goods unrecovered, culprit unproven.'"

Pest looked up, tears cutting tracks through the dust on his face. "You would do this? Why?"

"In exchange," Holmes said, "Thomas Wren will be found. He will be returned to the district, his name cleared of all suspicion, and you will grant him a small tenancy on your land on exceedingly reasonable terms."

When questioned later by myself as to why he did not let the law take its full and ruinous course upon a perjurer, Robert de Holmes remarked only:

"A man's name is the only thing that costs nothing to take and everything to restore. It seemed sensible to restore Wren's, and in doing so, preserve a household that the plague had already tried hard enough to break. Justice, Edmund, is rarely found at the bottom of a ruined chest. It is found in the balance we strike afterward."
