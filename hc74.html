CARACALLUS HOLCUS

~185 AD — Rome / Byzantium

Caracallus Holcus came to professional maturity in the years of the Severan dynasty and reached maturity as the empire’s third-century crisis began to gather — the decades of plague, invasion, economic disruption, and imperial instability that would kill fifty emperors in fifty years and nearly kill Rome itself. He read the situation, as men of his family read all situations, with cold clarity, and drew from his reading a conclusion that was becoming increasingly obvious to anyone who would face it: the empire’s centre of gravity was moving east.

He moved with it. Relocating from Rome to Byzantium — the ancient Greek city on the Bosphorus that would, within two generations, be transformed into the new capital of the world — Caracallus Holcus established the family in a position from which they could observe, and participate in, the great administrative shift from Latin west to Greek east that was the defining structural fact of the later empire. He was an early man in a city that was already important and would become, in his grandchildren’s time, the centre of the world — which gave him advantages that he was careful to use.

The account that survives of his most significant inquiry in Byzantium is preserved in a set of wax tablets later copied to parchment by a clerk named Menandros, a meticulous but occasionally impatient observer whose annotations suggest both admiration and a persistent difficulty in following the full chain of Holcus’s reasoning. His record, though at times compressed, is sufficiently exact in its details to allow the reconstruction that follows.

The account of Caracallus Holcus

Recorded by Menandros, freedman and clerk, in the year of the consulship of Quintianus and Vettius. Copied from wax tablet to parchment at Byzantium. Several passages condensed where the author’s patience with philosophical digression proved insufficient.

The Bosphorus in autumn has a particular quality of light — grey without being dull, silver without warmth — that falls on the water and the city walls alike and makes both look as though they are being slowly forgotten. The fishing boats that come through the strait from the Euxine bring cold air from the north, and it settles in the streets below the acropolis and between the close-set houses of the Greek quarter, and does not leave until midday, and returns by late afternoon, so that the city exists in a condition of perpetual half-chill that I have come to regard as its natural humour.

It was in such an afternoon that Aemilius Strabo came to the door of the apartment on the street above the old Milesian quarter where Caracallus Holcus had established himself some four years prior — the upper floor of a building owned by a Syrian merchant who asked no inconvenient questions and kept his oil-lamps trimmed with an exceptionally pungent cedar oil that I had come to recognise, even from the street below, as the smell of the place where puzzles were kept.

I say that Strabo came to the door. I should say more precisely that he came to the door, retreated, walked the length of the street, returned, stood for a period I estimate at no less than a quarter of an hour, and then knocked. I observed all of this from my position at the small table near the window where I was engaged in copying a set of commercial accounts that Holcus had been asked to verify and which he had handed to me with the remark that figures told better stories than men, but that I should note any figure that had been corrected and tell him what the original entry appeared to have been.

Holcus himself was lying on the low couch that occupied the far wall, apparently studying the ceiling, which he had been doing since the second hour of the afternoon. He had been in this condition for nine days, following the resolution of the business with the Thracian grain merchant, which I had found fascinating and he had found insufficiently complex to justify the mental effort it had required, and which he had therefore dismissed from his memory with the contempt of a man discarding an overripe fig. He had eaten, in those nine days, four meals. He had slept for perhaps twenty hours in total. He had filled, and I had counted them, eleven wax tablets with writing that he would not permit me to read and that he erased each morning, so that the tablets were a kind of diary of a conversation he was having with himself that he was determined should leave no record.

When Strabo knocked, Holcus said, without moving: “He has come back.”

“He retreated and returned,” I said. “You heard him?”

“I heard him the first time. The sandal-sole of a man who walks on marble floors — the pattern of wear is quite specific, and produces a distinct sound on the stone of the street that differs from the leather worn by a man who walks on earth or tile. He walked past the door. He returned. He stood. His breathing was audible from the moment he returned — he was not cold, he had walked enough to warm himself; he was frightened. Let him in, Menandros. He is more frightened of the street than of me, which means whatever he carries has become too heavy to take back home.”

Aemilius Strabo was perhaps fifty, with the careful face of a man who had survived three imperial transitions by means of an excellent memory for which opinions he had expressed to whom. He was of the middling administrative rank — neither a senator nor a clerk, but one of the indispensable men who carry the actual mechanisms of government in their heads and whom emperors find it more convenient to retain than to replace. His toga was of good quality and had been brushed recently and with care, which I took to indicate a man who understood the value of surfaces. His hands, when he produced the letters from within his robe, were not shaking, but they had been shaking, and recently; the effort of steadying them was visible.

There were three letters. They were written on good papyrus, in a hand that was educated but effortful — the hand of a person writing in a script that was not their most natural, I thought, though I said nothing. The language was Latin. The content was explicit: Aemilius Strabo was accused, in each letter, of having extended formal administrative cooperation to the faction that had supported the claim of a certain general against the imperial house during a period — some eleven years prior — when that support had been, if not treason, then something which, depending on who was presently ascendant in the palace, could be re-described as treason at very short notice.

The letters demanded nothing specific. They did not name a sum. They did not specify an action. They stated only that the writer possessed further documentation, that this documentation would be made available to parties whose interest in it would be self-evident, and that the writer would be in contact again. The closing formula was polite.

Strabo sat in the chair I had placed for him and said: “I require your absolute discretion.”

“You require rather more than that,” Holcus said. He had not moved from the couch. He was examining the letters by holding them toward the cedar-oil lamp on the far wall, which was an awkward angle that would have given any ordinary man a crick in the shoulder, but Holcus appeared to find it entirely natural. “You require that I establish who has written these, what they intend, and why they have not yet specified their demand, which is the most interesting question by a considerable distance. Discretion is implied by the nature of my work. Sit comfortably. You have been standing for the better part of an hour.”

“I walked here from the Strategion,” Strabo said, somewhat defensively.

“You walked here from the Strategion, then past this building, then around the block of the fuller’s shop, then back, and then stood in the street. You walked from the Strategion in less than a quarter of an hour; the rest was indecision. You have made your decision now. Sit.”

Strabo sat. I poured him wine from the jug on my table — it was a Thracian wine, rough but warming — and he took it with the slightly startled gratitude of a man who had not expected kindness.

“The alignment,” Holcus said. “Describe it. Not the official version you have prepared; I have no interest in that. Describe what you actually did.”

A long pause. The lamp hissed. Somewhere below us, in the street, the hinge of the grain-merchant’s side gate produced its characteristic groan — a sound I had come to associate with the approach of evening, since the merchant’s slaves always closed it at dusk. The sound died. Strabo spoke.

What he described was, as he was evidently aware, entirely ordinary. During a period when the succession had been genuinely unclear and two powerful military figures had been manoeuvring for advantage with the court, Strabo had — as virtually every man of his rank had done — performed certain administrative functions in a manner that was technically neutral but practically favourable to one claimant over the other. He had approved the provisioning requests of certain units with unusual speed. He had delayed other requests by a period of days that had, in the event, mattered. He had been careful never to commit himself in writing, but there had been verbal undertakings, and there were men alive who had been present for them. The claimant he had favoured had ultimately lost. The man who had won had, in the subsequent decade, neither pursued Strabo nor rewarded him, which was the administrative equivalent of a conditional pardon: safe, but never secure.

Holcus listened without apparent interest. He had placed the letters on his chest and was now examining the ceiling again.

“When did they begin?” he said.

“Forty days ago. The intervals are irregular. Eight days, then eleven, then twenty-one.”

“And the delivery?”

“Left at my house. The porter sees nothing. Each time, by morning.”

“No demand,” Holcus said, as though confirming a figure in a calculation.

“None. Only the statement of what they possess and the implication that it will be used.”

“And you have told no one.”

“I have told you.”

“You have told me. Who else has access to this information — to the specifics of what you did?”

“Three men were present for the undertakings. Two are dead. The third is Lucius Caelio, who is now—” Strabo stopped.

“Who is now,” Holcus said patiently, “what?”

“Who is now a senior assessor in the financial office of the Prefect.”

Holcus was quiet for a long moment. I continued copying the commercial accounts, because I have learned that Holcus’s silences are not invitations to speak and are best treated as one treats the silence before thunder — as information in themselves.

“The letters,” he said finally. “May I retain them?”

“I would prefer—”

“I understand your preference. I ask whether I may retain them.”

Another pause. “Yes,” Strabo said.

“Good. Come back in three days. Not to this building — to the bookseller’s stall at the north end of the Tetrastoon. At the second hour after midday. You will find me there, or a message from me. Do not send anyone in your place; I will know, and I will tell you nothing.”

Menandros’s note, inserted here: I am condensing what followed, as Holcus’s account to me of his investigation occupied four hours and included a digression on the history of administrative Latin in the Greek east that I found instructive but which would not serve the reader. The essential elements are as follows.

I had worked, in my previous life — before Holcus acquired my contract from the shipping merchant who had acquired it from the estate of my previous employer, a sequence of transactions which Holcus described at the time as “the most efficient method of obtaining a literate assistant in a city where literacy is theoretically universal and practically rarer than good drainage” — I had worked extensively with documents, and I knew something of hands.

The first observation was the one I had made and not spoken: the Latin was not the writer’s most natural script. The letterforms were correct but formed with a slight excess of care, the care of a person writing in a language they had learned thoroughly as an adult rather than acquired as a child. The spacing between words was marginally too regular. These are the habits of translation, of a mind that is composing in one language and transcribing in another.

The second observation was Holcus’s, and he arrived at it by a method I could not follow in its middle stages but whose conclusion, when he stated it, seemed — as his conclusions always seem — not merely correct but obvious. The papyrus on which the letters were written was of Alexandrian manufacture but had been stored, prior to use, in a space where the dominant smell was cedar. Not cedar oil from a lamp, but cedar wood — the smell of a chest or cabinet. Cedar storage is common in certain administrative offices where documents must be protected from insects. It is not common in private houses.

The third observation concerned the seal. The letters were not sealed in the conventional manner but were folded and pressed with a thumb, and the pressure of the thumb had left, in the wax — a cheap, undyed wax — a partial impression that was not a ring-seal but the edge of a thumbnail, and the thumbnail had a specific irregularity, a slight lateral split, that Holcus examined for a long time with the focused expression he wore when other men might be said to be reading.

“We are looking,” he said, on the second day, “for a man who writes formal Latin as an acquired skill, who has access to the financial records of the period in question — which means either a current or former administrative position — and who stores documents in a cedar chest. We are looking, specifically, for a man in the office of the Prefect’s financial assessors. There are fourteen men in that office. Of those fourteen, I need to know which store their personal documents in cedar.”

“How do you propose to discover that?” I asked.

“I propose that you discover it,” he said, “by the straightforward method of visiting the households of fourteen administrative officials on some plausible pretext and observing whether you can smell cedar on any document they have handled recently. You have a nose, Menandros. Use it.”

I will not record the fourteen visits, except to note that the pretext Holcus devised — that I was verifying delivery of a commercial assessment on behalf of a Syrian trader whose name Holcus gave me, and who, I later determined, was entirely fictional — worked in twelve of fourteen cases, and in two cases I was turned away by porters, which was itself information. Of the twelve households I entered, three had the smell of cedar about their document storage. Of those three, one kept his documents in an open basket that would not trap the scent. One used cedar oil in his lamps, which was common and explained nothing. The third had a chest.

The man with the chest was not Lucius Caelio. He was a junior assessor named Philippus, a Greek who had Latinised his professional name and who was, in the relevant period eleven years ago, a senior clerk in the provisioning office — present, therefore, for exactly the transactions Strabo had described. He had since risen two grades, which was respectable but not remarkable, and which suggested a man whose advancement had been earned rather than bought.

I reported this to Holcus. He received it with the expression I had learned to identify as satisfaction that he is performing no external movement to indicate.

“Philippus,” he said.

“The nail of his right thumb,” I said, “has a lateral split. I noticed it when he took the document I brought.”

Holcus looked at me for a moment with what I can only describe as approval, and said nothing, which from him is the equivalent of a considerable compliment.

Menandros’s note: I have debated whether to include Holcus’s full analysis as he delivered it at the Tetrastoon on the third day, since Strabo’s responses were largely monosyllabic and the substance of the reasoning was entirely Holcus’s. I include it because this is the part that I could not follow at the time but which, having thought about it since, seems to me to be the most important thing the case contained.

The bookseller’s stall occupied a good position at the end of the colonnade, catching the light from the south and protected from the harbour wind by the line of the stoa. Holcus was there before us. He was reading a scroll that he returned to its slot without purchasing, and when Strabo arrived he was already seated on the low wall beside the stall as though he had been there for some time and found it entirely comfortable.

“It will stop,” Holcus said, without preamble. “The letters will stop. I would estimate that you will receive no further communication.”

Strabo stared at him. “You have — recovered the documentation? You have spoken to—”

“I have recovered nothing. I have spoken to no one about this matter except my clerk.” Holcus examined the nearest scroll without unrolling it. “Let me tell you what you already know but have not yet acknowledged to yourself. The alignment you made eleven years ago was not singular. It was the alignment that virtually every man of your rank made, in one direction or the other, because the situation made neutrality impossible and survival mandatory. You made your calculation. Others made theirs. The man who has been sending you these letters made his — and the calculation he made was identical to yours.”

A long silence. The sound of the harbour, distant. A vendor of heated wine somewhere in the colonnade.

“He supported the same faction,” Strabo said slowly.

“He supported the same faction. He was present for the same undertakings, not merely as a witness to yours, but as a participant in his own right. The documentation he claims to possess is documentation that implicates you both. This is why there has been no demand. There is no demand he can make — no money he can extract, no political favour he can require — that would not create in you an incentive to expose him in return. The threat is the entirety of his leverage, and the threat is perfectly self-cancelling. He discovered this, I suspect, some time after he dispatched the first letter, which was sent in anger or in fear rather than in calculation. The intervals between letters — eight days, then eleven, then twenty-one — are not the rhythm of a man building pressure. They are the rhythm of a man losing his nerve. The final interval was twenty-one days. I believe you will find that no fourth letter comes.”

Strabo sat with this for a moment. “Then he was — he wrote in panic.”

“He wrote because he believed himself uniquely vulnerable and you uniquely safe. Then he discovered, or remembered, or was reminded by someone wiser than himself, that the ground beneath his feet and the ground beneath yours is the same ground. A man does not drain a swamp by pointing at another man who is also standing in it.”

“Who is he?” Strabo said.

Holcus looked at him with what I recognised as a careful neutrality. “That is not a question I am going to answer.”

“I have a right—”

“You have no right that would survive examination,” Holcus said, not unkindly. “If I tell you his name, you will be in possession of a weapon. You do not currently need a weapon; you need the letters to stop, and they will stop. If you have a weapon, you will be tempted to use it, and if you use it, he will use his, and two men who were both, eleven years ago, doing what every man of their position was doing will destroy each other in a spectacle that will be very satisfying to whoever currently finds it useful to remind the court that the Severan alignment was a long time ago and everyone would like to forget it, but cannot quite do so while certain men remain in certain offices. You understand me.”

Another silence. The grain-merchant’s cart went past in the street beyond the colonnade, the driver shouting at his mule in a mixture of Latin and a dialect I didn’t recognise.

“You’re telling me,” Strabo said, “that the safest thing I can do is nothing.”

“I am telling you that the safest thing you can do is precisely what you have always done,” Holcus said. “Which is to survive by being more useful than you are dangerous, and to leave the management of dangerous men to those whose profession it is.” He stood, with the abrupt energy of a man who has set down a weight he has been carrying and found himself unexpectedly lighter. “My fee is modest. Menandros will discuss it with you.”

He had already turned toward the scroll-seller’s stall before I could protest that this was not, in fact, part of my agreed duties.

Menandros’s concluding note.

No fourth letter came.

Strabo paid promptly and without complaint, which Holcus said was the surest sign that the advice had been correct: men who believe they have received good value do not haggle after the fact.

He filed no report with the office of the City Governor, whose representative — a humourless Pannonian named Bruttius who handled cases involving the upper administrative ranks — had come to Holcus twice during the previous year for consultations whose outcomes he had duly credited to his own department’s investigative acumen. Holcus had permitted this without comment each time. I had found it galling. Holcus said it was the correct tax on the privilege of operating without interference, and that any man who required personal credit for his work was a man who did not yet understand what work was for.

He wrote nothing in his tablets about the Strabo affair. He erased what little he had written — the edge of a nail, the smell of cedar, an equation about leverage — on the morning after the meeting at the Tetrastoon, and returned to examining the ceiling with the focused intensity of a man reading a language written in plaster.

He was in this condition for six days before the next client knocked.

I have chosen to record the affair here because I think it is the truest example I have observed of what he actually does, which is not, as I formerly believed, to solve crimes. It is to locate the precise weight of a situation — the exact point at which the pressure on each side of a balance is equal — and to leave it balanced, rather than tipping it, because a balanced scale tips itself, eventually, and a tipped scale breaks.

He would say that this is elementary. He would say that the world is mostly made of such equations, and that most men are too frightened of the variables to work them through to their conclusion.

He is, I continue to find, correct.

Here the tablet sequence ends. What follows is a separate inquiry, the details of which the present copyist has not yet had occasion to review.
