TRAJANUS HOLCUS

~25 AD — Londinium, Britannia

The field notes of Quintus Marcellus

I

The fog that settles on Londinium in autumn is not the clean mist of the Campanian hills. It comes off the Tamesis like breath from a sick animal — grey, close, pressing down on a town of fresh timber and raw ambition, muffling the sounds of the work-gangs before the second hour and leaving every building looking as though it had been erected the day before, which in most cases it had.

I was at my table before first light — plotting survey distances for the road east toward Camulodunum, as was my work in those years, attached to the Governor's staff as a military surveyor — when I heard feet on the wooden stairs. Not the heavy regulation tread of soldiers but the rapid, decisive step of a man who has already solved a problem in his mind and is moving his body to confirm the solution. I had been in Londinium long enough to know that step.

Trajanus Holcus appeared in my doorway still pulling his cloak across his shoulders, his dark eyes already moving around the room in that manner he had of measuring everything at once, as a carpenter glances at a beam and knows immediately whether it is sound. He was, I should say for anyone who has not encountered him, a man of the Roman administration in the new province of Britannia — not a soldier, though he moved like one, and not precisely a clerk, though he read everything — a man whose specific function defied the boxes the Provincial bureaucracy preferred, which was, I had come to believe, entirely intentional on his part.

"Your stylus," he said. "You will need it."

"It is not yet dawn," I observed.

"Which is precisely why we must move. The town is still quiet enough to read. In two hours the morning traffic will have covered whatever the night has written." He looked at me with a patience that was almost, but not entirely, kind. "Gaius Vettius Rufus has not returned to his quarters. He was due to deliver his tribal accounts to the Governor's secretary at the second hour after sunrise. I received word from his assistant — a frightened boy named Durus — not twenty minutes ago. The meeting cannot be delayed. The Governor leaves for Camulodunum at midday."

Four hours. I set down my stylus and reached for my cloak.

I knew Vettius Rufus by sight: a careful, methodical clerk from Neapolis who kept the records of tribal payment obligations, a small man with ink-stained fingers and the slightly hunted expression of all men who are responsible for other men's money. "A clerk gone missing the night before he submits accounts," I said. "The obvious conclusion—"

"Is obvious, yes," said Trajanus Holcus, with the particular quality of stillness that meant he found the obvious conclusion inadequate. "Which is to say it is wrong, or at least, insufficient. Take your cloak. We are going first to his room."

II

The administrative quarter of Londinium occupied what had been, eighteen months before, a stand of oak. The trees had been felled so recently that the stumps were still pale and their roots were still fighting the paved lanes that had been pressed over them, cracking the stone in places with a slow vegetable stubbornness that I found, privately, impressive. The building that housed the clerical staff was a long timber structure, two storeys, constructed with the speed that characterised everything the army built in this province — serviceable, square, and displaying in every joint the slight desperation of men building civilisation against a deadline.

Outside Vettius's door, the boy Durus was waiting in the corridor — fourteen, perhaps, with the large eyes of someone who has been awake all night with fear. He was from somewhere near Ostia, I guessed from his accent; the son of some minor official who had found his boy a position in the new provincial administration. He was clutching himself in the way of someone who has already decided he is in trouble and is merely waiting to find out what kind.

Trajanus Holcus spoke to him first, before we entered the room. This was characteristic of him: the room would not change, but the boy's recollection would deteriorate with every minute of standing in a cold corridor with his fear.

"When did you last see him?"

"At the ninth hour of night, master. He sent me away. He said he had work to finish and I was to be back before dawn to help him carry the tablets to the Governor's offices."

"Did anyone come to see him yesterday? In the afternoon or evening?"

The boy hesitated. "A man came. In the late afternoon. I didn't know him. Tall. He wore Roman clothes but not quite right — like a man who has learned Roman dress the way you learn a language, correctly but without the ease of habit. He spoke Latin well enough."

"Tribal?"

"His bearing, master. Not his speech. His speech was good. But he looked at the room the way — the way someone looks at a room they are measuring."

Trajanus Holcus glanced at me very briefly. "Did you hear what they discussed?"

"No. Master Vettius sent me on an errand. When I came back, the man was gone and Master Vettius was — he was different. Quiet. He kept checking the tablets, running the same columns again and again."

"Was there anything on the table that is no longer there? A separate tablet, perhaps?"

The boy's eyes widened. "There was a note. On plain wax. Pushed under the door, I think — it was there when I came back from the errand, and it wasn't there before. He picked it up and read it and — yes. His face changed. And then — then he sent me away."

"What did his face do when he read it?"

The boy was quiet for a moment. "He looked," he said carefully, "the way my father looked when someone told him my mother was ill. Not frightened for himself. Frightened for someone else."

Trajanus Holcus was still for a moment in the way that, I had learned, indicated that something had landed precisely where he had expected it. "Go and wait in the survey room downstairs," he said to the boy. "Tell no one what you have told me. Not yet."

III

He moved through Vettius's room with an attention that excluded me entirely for several minutes. He did not touch anything immediately. He stood in the centre of the small space and looked. I have been in his company long enough to know that this stillness is not inactivity. It is the moment in which he reads what is before him the way I read a landscape — taking the distances, the orientations, the shapes — converting the visible into the intelligible.

"Tell me what you see," he said at last.

"A bed, slept in. A table. Two lamps — one burned out, the other still casting a dying, guttering light over a plate of half-eaten bread. The accounts — that stack of wax tablets on the table. His cloak is on the peg. His sandals are beside the bed."

"Good. What do you conclude?"

"That he left in a hurry. Without his cloak or sandals, which is—"

"At night, in a Britannic autumn, extraordinary, yes. What else?"

I looked again. "The tablets. If he had fled with the accounts — if this were an embezzlement, as one might suspect—"

"He would have taken them. Precisely. The accounts are here. Therefore he did not flee with them, which removes the most commercially convenient explanation." He crossed to the table and lifted the first of the wax tablets with careful hands, angling it toward my lamp. He was quiet for a moment. Then he set it down and took up the next. And the next. His expression did not change, but something behind it sharpened — the way a hound's attention sharpens when the wind shifts.

"Come here," he said.

I came. He held the tablet so that I could see the columns: names of tribal client chieftains, their assessed obligations, their payments recorded in careful notation.

"Segovax of the northern Catuvellauni," he said. "Payment this quarter: short by eleven silver units." He set it down and lifted another. "Adminios. Short by nine units. Same pattern — just short. Never egregiously." He lifted the third. "Cunobelin's man, Caratacos — short by fourteen units." He set the tablet down and looked at me. "What does thirty-four silver units, consistently collected short, across three chieftains, across four quarters, suggest to you?"

I looked at the numbers. "Poverty? Resistance?"

"The consistency is the argument against both. Poverty is irregular. Resistance, of the passive fiscal kind, escalates. This does not escalate. It maintains a precise shortfall, repeated, season after season. The precision is a signal, not a symptom." He paused, his finger hovering over a hurried marginal notation in the second tablet: 'Per the Prefect's private discretion.' He did not comment on it, but his eye lingered there for a second longer than the rest. "Someone is collecting that shortfall before it reaches Roman accounts. A parallel levy. A shadow tax, gathered in our shadow, from men who pay us openly and pay someone else quietly. That sum, over four quarters, would arm and supply a significant body of men."

He then turned his attention to the small wooden chest where Vettius kept his personal seals and private correspondence. He traced the dust on the inner lid with a finger, then looked at a narrow gap between the chest and the wall. "Note the void, Marcellus. A small, rectangular space, cleared of dust, where a ledger of perhaps six tablets once sat. Vettius did not take it — he has no sandals — and our intruder would not have stolen the official accounts if he had already secured the private record of the shadow tax. He has the ledger. He has the map of the conspiracy. He has simply come for the clerk to ensure the silence of the official record."

The room felt colder than it had a moment before. "The tribal visitor yesterday afternoon," I said slowly.

"The man in Roman clothes that did not quite fit. Yes." He was already moving back toward the door. "He came to ensure the accounts would not arrive at the Governor's meeting. The note under the door — he needed Vettius to come to him willingly, quietly, without raising alarm. He used something Vettius valued above his own safety." He paused at the threshold. "The boy's description. The way Vettius's face changed when he read the note — frightened for someone else, not himself. He has family in Neapolis. A sister, I believe, from a reference in his personal record. The note threatened her."

"You believe? You are not certain?"

"I will be certain when Vettius tells me. Let us go and find him so that he can."

IV

We were outside in the lane, moving toward the river, before I had fully assembled the logic of what he had told me.

"You said he was compelled rather than frightened," I said. "How do you distinguish them?"

Trajanus Holcus did not slow his pace. The fog was thick enough that the torches at the lane's end were visible only as orange blooms of diffusion, and the smell of the river was stronger now, the tide being out, the Tamesis in the process of revealing its banks with the calm indifference it displayed toward all Roman civic planning.

"A frightened man takes what matters to him. His money. His sandals. The accounts, if he has stolen from them. But look at the room, Marcellus. A lamp left burning to waste the oil, a meal half-eaten on the table. A man fleeing for his life does not leave a light for his return. He extinguishes his presence. A man compelled by a message — by a threat to someone he loves — leaves everything because he intends to return. He is not fleeing. He is answering a summons he considers temporary." He paused at the junction of two lanes, examining the ground with a care that I had learned to take seriously when I could not immediately understand its purpose. "Here. Look at this."

I looked at the mud of the lane, which was, to me, simply mud.

"The timber plank they use as a threshold for that door — see where the edge has been recently disturbed. Someone stood here in the dark and was not entirely steady on their feet. The mark is deep on the right, shallow on the left. A man supporting weight on one side. And here—" he moved three steps further "—something dragged. Not deeply. Not the drag of a body rendered unconscious. The shuffle of a man who is being moved but is ambulatory. He walked, but with assistance he had not entirely chosen."

"He was taken," I said.

"He was conducted," said Trajanus Holcus, with the precise quality of distinction that was characteristic of him. "By someone who needed him present, but not necessarily cooperative."

He produced from within his cloak a small clay tablet of his own and re-examined notes he had apparently already made. "The man who visited him in the afternoon," he said. "He is not merely a messenger. The care with which this operation has been constructed — the precision of the fiscal shortfalls, the timing of the visit to coincide with the eve of submission, the use of a family threat rather than a direct bribe — this is an organised mind. A mind that has been watching the provincial administration long enough to understand how to move within it." He looked at me. "He will not have delegated the warehouse visit to subordinates alone. He will want to be present. He will want to know that Vettius has agreed to destroy the accounts — or that Vettius has been disposed of — before the meeting hour."

"Then he is there," I said. "At the warehouse."

"He is there," said Trajanus Holcus, "or he will be before dawn. Which means we must be there first, and we must give him a reason not to act immediately when we arrive."

I considered what he had said. "You mean to use his caution against him."

"A cautious man's first move, when cornered, is to establish that he is not the man in the corner." He glanced at me. "You are attached to the Governor's staff. Your authority in this province?"

"I can compel access to any structure for the purposes of official survey and assessment."

"Then we will give him a moment in which he believes he can step back into the shadows. And we will make certain," he said quietly, "that there are no shadows left."

He was quiet for several paces. Then: "This man — the organiser — has been operating in the waterfront warehouses for two years. He uses them as a factor for tribal trading. Which means he knows every building in that district. He will have chosen his location not merely for access but for exit — a building with a second egress, away from the main lane, where a man can withdraw into the river-fog without being observed. He thinks, always, in terms of the retreat." Holcus paused. "And a man who thinks in terms of the retreat consolidates what he cannot afford to lose. He will not scatter his evidence. He will keep it in a single location — one he controls, one he can reach and clear quickly if the situation demands it. The ledger and anything else he holds will be together, in the same place, because separation requires trust in other men, and a man of this kind does not extend that trust."

"Then you already know where to look," I said.

"I know what I am looking for. The location will declare itself." He added, with the precision of a man closing an argument: "These warehouses are tribal trading stores, built by men who understand river damp. There is always one wall that holds dry — the wall furthest from the water, backed against the older ground. Any man who has worked in these buildings for two years knows which wall that is. He would not store what he cannot afford to lose anywhere else."

V

He spoke on the way to two men I would not have thought to consult.

The first man we encountered — a water-carrier on his morning rounds — was a compact Gaul who had worked the waterfront quarter since the town's founding. He knew the warehouses the way I know survey ground — not as buildings but as a set of practical problems solved and unsolved. He told us which trading houses used which stores, which factors came and went at irregular hours, and, when Holcus asked with the casualness of a man confirming what he already knows, which of the buildings held dry through winter and which did not.

"The river-damp gets into the western walls by the third month," he said. "The stores worth having are the ones built backing onto the older ground — eastern aspect, away from the water. They are the only ones that stay dry, and in a warehouse, a man keeps his secrets where the rot cannot reach them. Everyone in the district knows it. The tribal factors pay more for those."

Holcus thanked him and moved on without comment.

The second — a night-watchman at the bridge approach — was a former legionary discharged from the Fourteenth with a stiff leg and a comprehensive knowledge of nocturnal movement in his district.

"Two men and a third between them," said the watchman, "going toward the river warehouses. Not quite the third hour of night. I thought it was someone who'd found trouble in the wine."

"One of the two — the man directing it. What did he look like?"

The watchman thought. "Tall. Good cloak. Not a trader's cloak — the quality of a man who wants to look provincial but is not. The way he moved, though — he was not concerned about being seen. He moved like a man who does not expect to be questioned."

Trajanus Holcus pressed a coin into his hand with the ease of a man who regards money as a tool rather than a possession. We moved toward the waterfront.

"You knew to come here," I said.

"A man being conducted somewhere must be taken to a space that is enclosed, away from morning traffic, accessible from the administrative quarter without crossing the main commercial lane, and available to those with money but without Roman property rights. The waterfront warehouses are used by tribal traders as temporary storage. Several of them are, in practice, controlled by the same network that has arranged the payment shortfalls." He paused, then added, with the quality of a man who finds self-correction as natural as breathing: "I should have come here first. I made the error of being thorough when I should have been direct."

It was not, in my experience, common for a man of his capacities to say such a thing. I noted it.

When the warehouse came into view — long, low, unremarkable against the fog and the river-smell of wool and grain and travelled water — Holcus stopped me with a hand at my arm.

"Give me time to circle the building," he said. "Just wait here a few moments before you knock."

"What are you doing at the rear?"

He looked at me for a moment with an expression that was not quite amusement and not quite patience, but contained elements of both. "Confirming what I expect to find," he said. "And removing it from the equation before we begin."

He disappeared into the fog. I stood in the dark and listened to the river move against its banks, and waited the few moments as instructed.

VI

I knocked on the door with the flat of my hand and announced, in the carrying voice I have used to address work parties in wind, that I was conducting an imperial survey assessment and required immediate access under the authority of the Governor of Britannia. There was movement inside. A delay — not long, but considered, the delay of men receiving instructions. The door opened to reveal a man whose expression combined the attempt at innocence with the reality of alarm in a proportion that told me immediately his instructions had not accounted for this particular approach.

I stepped inside.

The warehouse was lit by a single lamp, turned low — enough to work by, too little to be visible at a distance. The smell of grain and lanolin lay beneath everything. Wool bales were stacked along both walls, though the bales towards the rear appeared rather poorly stacked. Behind the first man, his companion was already moving toward the rear of the building, toward a timber partition that divided the space: a door in it, closed, with a line of steadier, brighter light beneath it. I moved to block him, but his path and mine intersected at an angle that briefly favoured him, and in that moment the light beneath the partition door shifted as someone on the other side stood.

The partition door opened.

The man who stepped through it was tall. He wore a good cloak — the quality of a man who understood that presentation was a form of argument. He was perhaps forty, with the kind of face that has learned to present whatever expression is most useful and had settled, in this moment, on the expression of a man entirely at ease with the scene before him. He looked at me. He looked at the open door behind me, at the fog beyond it, at the lane that led back toward the administrative quarter and everything civilised and well-lit.

"Surveyor," he said, in Latin with only the faintest inflection of somewhere else. "I think you have the wrong building."

"I think I have exactly the right building," I said.

He spread his hands — the gesture of a reasonable man, a man with nothing to conceal. "A storage arrangement with some local traders. You are welcome to make your assessment. You will find only wool and grain." He was already moving, with the ease of a man accustomed to moving through situations without leaving marks — toward me, but also toward the space beside me, toward the open door and the dark lane beyond. He was not fleeing. He was withdrawing. There is a difference, and he knew it.

I did not move.

He checked, fractionally. The ease did not leave his face, but something behind it recalibrated. Then the rear door of the warehouse opened, and Trajanus Holcus walked through it.

He did not hurry. He walked to the centre of the warehouse with the ease of a man who has arrived at a place he visited some minutes before, and he was carrying, in his left hand, a small leather satchel. He set it on the wool bale nearest the lamp with the unhurried deliberateness of a man placing a document on a table at the beginning of a meeting. He did not open it. He looked at the tall man, and said nothing.

The tall man looked at the satchel.

I watched him in that moment with the attention I give to ground I am about to cross — reading it for what it will bear. The composure did not shatter. A man of this quality does not shatter. But it reorganised itself around a new and unwelcome set of calculations, and the reorganisation was visible in the way that a change in river current is visible — not the thing itself, but the surface effects of the thing. He looked at the satchel, and then at Holcus, and then back at the satchel. I saw him working through it: what was inside, how it had been found, what the finding meant for the position he had occupied thirty seconds ago and the position he occupied now, and whether those two positions were, in any meaningful sense, the same. They were not. He knew it. Holcus knew it. The warehouse held its breath.

Nobody spoke. Outside, the Tamesis moved against its banks with the indifference it had shown this ground for ten thousand years before the first Roman boot touched it. The lamp between them guttered once and steadied.

Then Togodumnus looked up.

"You are Holcus," he said. It was not a question.

"And you have been in Londinium for two years," said Holcus, with the tone of a man confirming a measurement. "Working as a factor for the tribal trading houses, which gives you access to these warehouses, legitimate reason to move between the tribal territories and the town, and complete knowledge of the payment schedules of every client chieftain in the region. The name you use here is Togodumnus — not the man of that name who fell in the conquest, but someone who has taken it up. Which is, in itself, a statement of intent." He did not look at the satchel. "Adminios funded the operation. The direction is yours."

The tall man — Togodumnus — shed the manner of the trader as a man sheds a cloak he no longer needs. What replaced it was cooler and more considered and, I thought, more honest — the face of a man who has decided that if the game has changed, he will play the new game and play it well.

"You have a bag," he said. "Let us say, for the sake of argument, that it contains what you believe it contains. You have the shortfall in the accounts — a clerical irregularity, ambiguous in its origins. You have a set of private documents whose provenance you cannot establish in front of a magistrate without explaining how you came to be in this warehouse at this hour conducting what is, I think you will find, an unauthorised entry." He paused, with the timing of a man who has been saving something. "And the clerk — the man who could speak to the irregularities — has not yet decided whether to cooperate with the Roman administration or with the men who hold information about his family in Neapolis. I am not certain," he said, "that you hold what you believe you hold."

It was well done. I felt the force of it — not panic but counter-attack, clean and precise, the move of a man who has not finished calculating.

Holcus looked at him for a moment with the settled attention that I had come to recognise as the expression he wore when events were conforming to expectation. Then he opened the satchel.

He removed from it, first, the bound collection of wax tablets — six of them, tied with cord: the private ledger, the shadow record, taken from the void beside Vettius's chest. He set it beside the lamp. Then he removed a second bundle: sealed letters, eight or nine of them, the wax of each seal carrying the marks of men whose names I recognised — men attached to the Governor's inner administration. The Prefect's seal was amongst them. I had seen that seal beside the notation Per the Prefect's private discretion in Vettius's tablets not two hours before.

He set the letters beside the ledger and looked up.

The silence that followed had a different quality from the silence before it. Togodumnus looked at the letters. Not at Holcus, not at the door, not at his two men near the entrance — at the letters. And I understood, watching him, that the letters were not merely evidence. They were the architecture of his safety — the guarantee that if he fell, he would not fall alone, that the weight of what he knew would press back against anyone who moved against him. They had been in the satchel, in the one place in the warehouse where the wall held dry, beneath the rearmost bale, because he was a man who consolidated what he could not afford to lose, and Holcus had known this before he entered the building.

"The accounts," said Holcus quietly, "are already with the Governor's secretary. I sent the boy Durus with them an hour ago, before I came here." He paused. "You were not keeping Vettius long enough."

Togodumnus looked at the letters for a moment longer. Then something behind his eyes moved — not the collapse of a man overwhelmed but the final calculation of a man who has identified the one move remaining to him. He did not reach for the knife at his belt with the suddenness of panic. He reached for it with the deliberateness of a man who has decided. He took one step toward Holcus — specifically toward Holcus, who stood between him and the satchel, between him and the only remaining possibility of recovery — and the blade came up with an economy of motion that told me this was not his first such moment.

It very nearly reached its destination. I crossed the warehouse in the same instant, and what followed was not elegant: the impact took us both hard into the floor, my shoulder driving into his chest, and the cold of the stone came up through my hands as we went down together into the grain dust and the dark smell of old wool. He was strong and he twisted with it, but the wrist was beneath him when we landed and the bone gave way under the combined weight — snapping with the sound of green wood — and the breath went out of him in a single sharp exhalation, and the knife rang away across the stone somewhere behind us and was still. Holcus did not scramble backward. He moved forward, deliberately, placing himself between the fallen blade and the clerk who was bound against the far wall, watching all of this with the hollow eyes of a man who has been frightened for too many hours and is now observing the world from a very great distance.

The two men near the door made their calculation and sat down.

VII

Holcus cut Vettius's bindings with the small iron knife he wore at his belt — a knife that showed, I had noticed before, rather more wear than a man who deals primarily in documents should reasonably account for.

"The note under your door," he said to the clerk, who was rubbing his wrists and staring at him with the expression of a man watching something descend from a height. "It threatened your sister. In Neapolis."

Vettius looked at him. "How did you—"

"What did it say exactly?"

"That they knew where she was. That if I didn't come and speak with him—" his eyes moved briefly to Togodumnus, who sat cradling his broken wrist, his face arranged now into the arithmetic of a man calculating the extent of his losses "— she would be visited. That she would be made to understand the consequences of a brother who causes difficulty." He looked at his hands. "They said if I destroyed the accounts before the meeting, they would leave her alone. I was considering it." He said it plainly, without flinching from it. "I want to be honest with you. I was genuinely considering it."

"I know," said Trajanus Holcus, and there was nothing in his voice that judged this.

Vettius looked up. He had the face of a man who has been braced for judgment and found its absence harder to bear than its presence. "She is all I have left," he said. "Our parents died the year I came to this province. I have not seen her in three years. I send money when I can. She thinks—" he stopped. He tried again. "She thinks the work here is important. She is proud of it. She does not know what it actually consists of — the columns, the shortfalls, the endless recording of other men's obligations." He was quiet for a moment. "When I read the note, I thought: they will go to her and tell her what her brother does. And she will look at her idea of me, and it will be wrong. Both ideas — the one they give her and the one she already holds. Both wrong."

The warehouse was very still. Even Togodumnus, I noticed, had stopped his arithmetic.

"She will receive a letter," said Holcus, after a moment. "Before the next dispatch ship. Written to someone in that city who can be trusted to watch over her quietly, without alarm, without any explanation that will cause her distress." He said it as though it were already done. "What you did tonight — coming here, not destroying the accounts — was not nothing. It will be in the record."

Vettius nodded slowly, with the careful movement of a man whose head is very heavy. "They didn't know about her from me," he said. "I never spoke of her. Not here."

"No. They have been watching this office for a considerable time. Long enough to know the personal details of the clerical staff. Long enough to choose the correct lever for each man." He looked at the satchel on the bale — the ledger, the letters, the map of a thing that had been designed to be invisible. "That precision will go in the report too."

I waited until Vettius had been moved to a safer room and the two men from the door were under guard before I asked Holcus the question that had been shaping itself since he walked through the rear entrance with the satchel already in his hand.

"The satchel," I said. "You knew where it was before you entered."

"I knew where it had to be." He looked at the lamp, which was burning low. "This man has operated in these warehouses for two years. He knows which wall holds dry and which holds damp — any working factor does, after a single winter. The ledger and the letters were evidence he could not replace and could not risk separating: separate storage means separate risks, separate people with knowledge of separate locations. He consolidates. I told you as much on the way here. A man of his habits, in a building he has used for two years, stores what he cannot lose against the dry wall, as far from the river-damp as the structure permits." He was quiet for a moment. "You noted the poorly stacked bales near the rear, but it was that single bale, sitting slightly askew against the wall, that gave the game away. When you have moved something beneath a bale and replaced it in the dark, the replacement is never perfect. The board beneath it showed fresh scoring at its edge, where it had been lifted recently and set back without quite the same care as the original placement. These are not lucky observations, Marcellus. They are the expected consequences of a specific prediction."

"And if the prediction had been wrong?"

"Then I would have entered with nothing in my hand and a different problem." He picked up his cloak. "But the prediction was not wrong. A man cannot be methodical for two years and careless in the moment that matters most. Method is habit. Habit is visible, if you know what you are reading."

He paused, with the quality of a man who has remembered something he had already solved but not yet reported. "The confrontation was never about discovering evidence. It was about compelling a response to evidence, in front of a witness with the authority to make that response legally consequential." He looked at me steadily. "You are attached to the Governor's staff."

"I am," I said.

"Then what happened in this warehouse, tonight, in your presence, is a matter of official record." He turned toward the door. "Which is why you were here."

VIII

We were back in the administrative quarter before the first grey suggestion of dawn had begun to separate itself from the fog. The town was not yet awake. The stumps in the lane continued their slow vegetable resistance to Roman paving.

But before the Governor's meeting began, Trajanus Holcus requested a private audience. I waited in the corridor outside the Governor's receiving room, the silence of the villa pressing in, until I heard the Governor's voice — not the sharp note of indignation I had half expected, but something lower and more controlled, which was, I reflected, rather more dangerous.

I was admitted after perhaps ten minutes.

The satchel's contents were on the desk. The Governor stood behind it, and he was not the man I had seen at yesterday's briefing — brisk, administrative, moving papers with the efficiency of a man who regarded the province as a problem of logistics. He was reading the letters with the stillness of a man counting something he had hoped would not need counting, and the total was not in his favour. The Prefect's seal appeared on three of the letters. The notation Per the Prefect's private discretion had its explanation now, and the explanation reached further into the Governor's household than the Governor had, until this moment, been required to consider.

He set the last letter down. He looked at Holcus.

"You understand," he said, "what you are asking me to do."

"I am not asking you to do anything, Excellency," said Holcus. "I am informing you of what has been done, and by whom, and in whose house. What you do with that information is a question of administration. I am not an administrator."

The Governor's eyes moved to me — to my rank, my attachment to the staff, the fact of my presence as witness. He was, I saw, making calculations of his own: the distance between what he knew and what could be proved, the weight of the letters against the weight of a denial, the question of what a man in his position could afford to do and what he could not afford to leave undone. He was not a stupid man. That, in this moment, was the difficulty.

"This material," he said carefully, "was recovered from a warehouse in the waterfront district. Its provenance is, at best, unclear. A magistrate might question—"

"Its provenance," said Holcus, "is the record of Quintus Marcellus, attached to the Governor's staff, who was present at its recovery under his authority to compel access for official survey purposes. The record is already written." He paused. "It can be submitted to the Legate in Augusta by the next military dispatch, or it can be submitted to your secretary this morning and managed within this administration. Those are the available options. I am content with either."

The Governor looked at him for a long moment. I have seen men look at Holcus that way before — with the specific expression of someone who has just understood that the room was arranged before they entered it, and that the arrangement is not in their favour. It is not a comfortable thing to witness. I did not find it comfortable now, even from the correct side.

"If I were to suggest," the Governor said, very quietly, "that a man in your position — unofficial, unranked, operating on the margins of the administration's structure — might find that position considerably less tenable by the end of the week—"

"You would be correct," said Holcus, without heat. "I hold no rank. I have no legion at my back. I am, as you say, a man on the margins." He reached across the desk and turned the topmost letter so that the Prefect's seal faced upward. "But the dispatch ship leaves in six days. And Marcellus, who does hold rank, has already written his account of the night."

The silence in the room had the quality of a held breath.

The Governor looked at the seal. He looked at Holcus. He looked, finally, at me — and I met his gaze and did not look away. What passed across his face then was not capitulation so much as the recognition that the arithmetic was finished and the total was fixed. He was a man of the administration. He understood, better than most, that some positions, once occupied, cannot be held.

"Get out," he said. The words had the flatness of a man who has closed an account. "Both of you. Send Pollio to me."

Holcus inclined his head with the precise degree of courtesy the moment required — not more, not less — and turned for the door.

We walked back through the whitening fog in silence. When we reached my quarters, Holcus sat for a period of time and looked at the wall.

"The boy Durus," he said at last. "He will need reassurance. Vettius will need to be prepared for the meeting — a shaken man before a Governor is not useful." He paused. "The threat to the sister in Neapolis is real enough that a letter should go with the next dispatch ship. I will write it, if you can identify who to address."

"My brother-in-law has connections there," I said, after a moment.

"Good." He did not write the letter immediately. He looked at the wall. "Togodumnus knows the accounts are with the Governor's secretary. He knows the pattern is visible. The operation is finished in its current form. The question is whether he attempts to rebuild it." He was quiet. "He will conclude, correctly, that we do not yet have evidence sufficient to convict him formally. The men in the warehouse are functionaries. They know his face and his instructions, but they will not speak without protection they have no reason to believe will be offered. He will calculate that his exposure is limited."

"And is it?"

"For the moment. But Pollio will investigate — the Governor's secretary will see the accounts this morning, and Pollio is a thorough man." He considered. "Pollio will move formally, visibly. That will drive Togodumnus out of Londinium before the net closes. This is, in its way, acceptable. A man driven out of a position he has spent two years building has lost his investment and must begin again elsewhere. The damage is done. The immediate threat is removed."

"But not destroyed."

"No." He stood, with the sudden energy of a man whose body has been waiting for his mind to require it. "Dismantled. Which is, in a province eighteen months old, perhaps as much as the machinery of administration can accomplish at speed. Destruction requires time and patience we do not currently have." He looked at me. "Your survey crew."

"Your survey crew," I said.

"You have authority to compel labour for imperial survey work."

"I do."

"A road assessment east of Londinium. Several days of intensive ground-level survey. The two men from the warehouse would benefit from fresh air and purposeful occupation, at a sufficient distance from the town that casual communication is not available to them."

I considered this. It was not precisely legal. It was also not precisely illegal, and it would accomplish what a more conventional approach would not. "I will need to adjust my schedule," I said.

"I expect your schedule has survived worse." He picked up his cloak. Then paused, with the quality of a man who has remembered something he had already solved but not yet reported. "You asked me earlier how I distinguished compulsion from fear. There is a second answer I did not give you. A frightened man, when found, is relieved — but somewhere underneath, he is shamed by his fear. A compelled man is relieved, and then he is angry. Did you see Vettius, when I cut the cord?"

I thought back to the lamplight in the warehouse, to the clerk's face in the moment of release. "He was angry," I said. "After the first moment. He was very angry."

"Angry that they used his sister. That they had studied him carefully enough to find her. He was angry at the precision of it." He turned toward the door. "I knew before we found him that he had not burned the accounts. The anger was an inference, not a fact. But the inference was sound, and in the absence of certainty one must act on the best available inference. In this case, it held."

"And if it hadn't?"

He considered this with genuine seriousness. "Then the accounts would have been destroyed and the morning's meeting would have proceeded without them and the investigation would have been delayed by a season. Which would have been regrettable." He turned up his collar against the fog that came under the door. "Get some sleep if you can. The Governor's meeting is at the second hour."

He left. I heard his feet on the stairs — rapid, decisive, moving toward some other problem that had presumably already presented itself to him and been provisionally resolved.

IX

I wrote this account because the Governor's record of the morning's meeting will note only that the tribal accounts were delivered on schedule and that certain irregularities in the payment records of three client chieftains have been referred for investigation. It will note nothing of the night before. The official record of Londinium, in the sixth year of the reign of Nero, will be silent on the question of where those irregularities were first noticed and by whom, and by what sequence of reading tablets in lamplight and measuring mud and talking to water-carriers and night-watchmen they were prevented from remaining invisible for another season.

It will be silent, too, on the subject of a man called Togodumnus, who left Londinium within the week. Pollio's inquiry found no direct evidence connecting him to the warehouse, as Holcus had anticipated. The two men from the warehouse had spent eleven days on a road survey east of the town, in conditions that did not permit casual communication, by which time the inquiry had proceeded sufficiently that their testimony became a procedural matter rather than an alarm. I do not know where Togodumnus went. I know that he was a patient man who had invested two years in a careful construction, and that the construction was dismantled in a single night by a man who read wax tablets the way I read the earth, and found in the columns of figures the shape of something that had been designed to be invisible.

The shadow tax was not merely stopped. The letters in the satchel did their work quietly, in the hands of a Governor who had been offered, as Holcus had put it, the more sustainable position. The Prefect and two of his associates were reassigned to posts at a considerable distance from Londinium — described in the official record as a routine administrative reshuffle, which is the way such things are accomplished in a province still too young to survive open scandal. Those of us who had seen the letters knew what the reassignment meant.

This is, I think, appropriate. The roads I survey are used by men who do not consider who designed them or at what cost. This, perhaps, is what roads are for.

I add only this note, which I have not recorded elsewhere and do not intend to: when Trajanus Holcus wrote the letter for the dispatch ship — the letter to alert someone in Neapolis, to place a careful eye near the sister of Gaius Vettius Rufus — he wrote it without being asked again and without reference. The name and district of the woman, which Vettius had told him once, in extremis, in a low voice, in the warehouse, he had retained precisely. He retains, I have noticed, everything that pertains to a case he is working. I have been present when he has failed to recognise a senior tribune he had met the previous month, and declined to recall the name of a town he had passed through on the road from Gesoriacum.

When I pressed him on this, he replied with a curious detachment: "I did not forget the Tribune, Marcellus; I simply evicted him. He occupied a space in my mind that was required for the study of the Tamesis river-tides."

He told me once that the mind is a room of finite size, and that a man who fills it with furniture he will never use cannot blame the room when there is no space for what he needs. I did not entirely follow this reasoning, being accustomed, as a surveyor, to the proposition that more measurement is always better than less.

I have begun to think he may be correct. I have also begun to think that he made this calculation so long ago, and so completely, that he is no longer aware he is making it. The room is already arranged. He simply walks through it.

The fog lifted, eventually, as it always does. By midday Londinium smelled only of timber and ambition and the Tamesis going about its old business beneath our new bridges.

Vettius Rufus delivered his accounts. The meeting proceeded.
