TRAJANUS HOLCUS

~25 AD — Rome / Britain

Trajanus Holcus bore the name of the distinguished Ulpian family of Hispania, whose military prominence was already substantial in the Julio-Claudian era — a choice the family noted with satisfaction when history made it appear prophetic. He served in the Roman administration of Britain in the years following the Claudian conquest of 43 AD, a posting that combined the administrative challenges of a newly subjugated and deeply resistant province with the particular intellectual interest of encountering a culture that was, to Roman eyes, entirely foreign.

He was stationed in the south-east initially, in the developing town of Londinium, where the administrative infrastructure of the new province was being assembled from nothing with the systematic energy that Roman colonisation required. He proved useful in the assessment of the Celtic tribal politics of the region — the reading of which chieftains were genuinely pacified and which were performing pacification while waiting for an opportunity — and provided, in 60 AD, an early warning of the Boudiccan revolt that was acted upon too slowly to prevent it but quickly enough to preserve the garrison he was with. He left Britain before the province had been fully secured, having understood it, he maintained, well enough.

The surviving account is preserved in the field notes of Quintus Marcellus, a military surveyor attached to the governor’s staff, who accompanied Trajanus Holcus during part of his circuit. Marcellus was a practical man, attentive to land, distance, and supply, and his record is shaped by those concerns, though he was not insensible to the peculiar quality of mind he was observing.

The field notes of Quintus Marcellus

The fog that settles on Londinium in autumn is not the clean mist of the Campanian hills. It comes off the Tamesis like breath from a sick animal — grey, close, carrying the particular mingling of tidal mud, fresh timber, and woodsmoke from a hundred cooking fires that have not yet learned to draw properly through roofs built by men who were making their first roofs. I have measured roads across three provinces. I know the smell of a new town, of raw ambition laid in straight lines over old earth. Londinium smelled of all of that, and beneath it, always, of something older that would not be covered.

I was at my table before first light, plotting survey distances for the road east toward Camulodunum, when I heard feet on the wooden stairs — not the heavy regulation tread of soldiers but the rapid, decisive step of a man who has already solved a problem in his mind and is moving his body to confirm the solution. Trajanus Holcus appeared in my doorway still pulling his cloak across his shoulders, his dark eyes already moving around the room in that manner he had of measuring everything at once, as a carpenter glances at a beam and knows immediately whether it is sound.

“Your stylus,” he said. “You will need it.”

“It is not yet dawn,” I observed.

“Which is precisely why we must move. The town is still quiet enough to read. In two hours the morning traffic will have covered whatever the night has written.” He looked at me with a patience that was almost, but not entirely, kind. “Gaius Vettius Rufus has not returned to his quarters. He was due to deliver his tribal accounts to the Governor’s secretary at the second hour after sunrise. I received word from his assistant — a frightened boy named Durus — not twenty minutes ago.”

I knew Vettius Rufus by reputation: a careful, methodical clerk from Neapolis who kept the records of tribal payment obligations, a small man with ink-stained fingers and the slightly hunted expression of all men who are responsible for other men’s money. “A clerk gone missing the night before he submits accounts,” I said, setting down my stylus. “The obvious conclusion—”

“Is obvious, yes,” said Trajanus Holcus, with the particular quality of stillness that meant he found the obvious conclusion inadequate. “Which is to say it is wrong, or at least, insufficient. Take your cloak. We are going first to his room.”

The administrative quarter of Londinium occupied what had been, eighteen months before, a stand of oak. The trees had been felled so recently that the stumps were still pale and their roots were still fighting the paved lanes that had been pressed over them, cracking the stone in places with a slow vegetable stubbornness that I found, privately, impressive. The building that housed the clerical staff was a long timber structure, two storeys, constructed with the speed that characterised everything the army built in this province — serviceable, square, and displaying in every joint the slight desperation of men building civilization against a deadline.

Trajanus Holcus moved through Vettius’s room with an attention that excluded me entirely for several minutes. He did not touch anything immediately. He stood in the centre of the small space and looked. I have been in his company long enough to know that this stillness is not inactivity. It is, I think, the moment in which he is reading what is before him the way I read a landscape — taking the distances, the orientations, the shapes — converting the visible into the intelligible.

“Tell me what you see,” he said at last.

“A bed, slept in. A table. Two lamps, both burned out. The accounts — or what I take to be the accounts — that stack of wax tablets there on the table. His cloak is on the peg. His sandals are beside the bed.”

“Good. What do you conclude?”

“That he left in a hurry. Without his cloak or sandals, which is—”

“At night, in a Britannic autumn, extraordinary, yes. What else?”

I looked again. “The tablets. If he had fled with the accounts — if this were an embezzlement, as one might suspect—”

“He would have taken them. Precisely. The accounts are here. Therefore he did not flee with them, which removes the most commercially convenient explanation.” He crossed to the table and examined the tablets without touching them, bending close in the manner of a man reading a text in poor light. “Two lamps burned to nothing. He worked late. He was anxious — look at the scratch marks, the stylus pressed deeply. He was copying something, or checking something, repeatedly.” He straightened. “Now. The boy Durus. Where is he?”

The boy was waiting in the corridor, perhaps fourteen, with the large eyes of someone who has been awake all night with fear. He was from somewhere near Ostia, I guessed from his accent — the son of some minor official who had found his son a position in the new provincial administration.

“When did you last see him?” said Trajanus Holcus, gently but with an attention that I have seen make grown soldiers uneasy.

“At the ninth hour of night, master. He sent me away. He said he had work to finish and I was to be back before dawn to help him carry the tablets to the Governor’s offices.”

“Did anyone come to see him yesterday? In the afternoon or evening?”

The boy hesitated. “A man came. In the late afternoon. I didn’t know him. Tall, with a British — with a tribal — I couldn’t place the tribe, master. He wore Roman clothes but not quite right. He spoke Latin well enough.”

“Did you hear what they discussed?”

“No. Master Vettius sent me on an errand. When I came back, the man was gone and Master Vettius was — he was different. Quiet. He kept checking the tablets.”

Trajanus Holcus looked at me. “His cloak,” he said. “His sandals. He left quickly, but he was not sleeping — the lamps burned out, so this was after midnight, when he finally stopped working. He left as a man leaves when he is compelled by urgency rather than fear. There is a difference.” He turned back to the boy. “Was there anything on the table that is no longer there? A separate tablet, perhaps? Notes that were not part of the official accounts?”

The boy’s eyes widened. “There was a wax note. Pushed under the door, I think. He picked it up when I was leaving and read it and — yes. That is gone.”

We were outside in the lane before I had fully comprehended the direction of his reasoning.

“You said he was compelled by urgency rather than fear,” I said. “How do you distinguish them?”

Trajanus Holcus did not slow his pace. The fog was thick enough that the torches at the lane’s end were visible only as orange blooms of diffusion, and the smell of the river was stronger now, the tide being out and the Tamesis in the process of revealing its banks with the calm indifference it displayed toward all Roman civic planning.

“A frightened man,” he said, “takes what matters to him. His money. His sandals. The accounts, if he has stolen from them. A man compelled by urgency — by a message — leaves everything because he intends to return. He is not fleeing. He is answering a summons he considers temporary.” He paused at the junction of two lanes, examining the ground with a care that I had learned to take seriously even when I could not immediately understand its purpose. “Here. Look at this.”

I looked at the mud of the lane, which was, to me, simply mud.

“The timber plank they use as a threshold for that door — see where the edge has been recently disturbed. Someone stood here, in the dark, and was not entirely steady on their feet. The mark is deep on the right, shallow on the left. A man supporting weight on one side. And here—” he moved three steps further “—something dragged. Not deeply. Not the drag of a body rendered unconscious. The shuffle of a man who is being moved but is ambulatory. He walked, but with assistance he had not entirely chosen.”

“He was taken,” I said.

“He was conducted,” said Trajanus Holcus, with the precise quality of distinction that was characteristic of him. “By someone who needed him present, but not necessarily cooperative.”

He produced, from within his cloak, a small clay tablet of his own, and in the poor light of a wall torch he quickly re-examined a series of notes he had apparently already made. I had not seen him make them, which was not unusual. “The tribal visitor in the afternoon,” he said. “The man in Roman clothes that did not quite fit. This is significant.”

“Because he was tribal? The tribes submit to Roman administration now — there are legitimate envoys, traders—”

“Because of what the accounts contain.” He looked at me with the patience that was, I had come to understand, his form of warmth. “Quintus. You are a surveyor. You measure what is visible. Let me tell you what the accounts of Gaius Vettius Rufus contain that is not precisely visible. Three of the tribal client chieftains in this region have, over the past season, defaulted on their payment obligations. Not egregiously — not enough to trigger punitive response. Just short. Just short, every time, by a consistent margin. I have read the accounts that Vettius was due to present this morning, because it is my business to read anything that pertains to the political disposition of the tribes.”

“And?”

“The consistent shortfall, applied across three chieftains, adds precisely to one sum. A sum large enough to equip a significant number of men. The defaults are not poverty. They are a fiscal arrangement. Someone is collecting a levy from the clients of Rome, a parallel tax, a tribe-within-the-province that the official administration cannot see because it does not know what shape to look for.”

I was quiet for a moment. The fog pressed in. Somewhere distant, a British dog was barking with the particular sustained misery of an animal that has encountered something nocturnal and unpleasant.

“And the report Vettius was to deliver tomorrow morning,” I said slowly, “would make this pattern visible to the Governor.”

“Yes. Which means the people who arranged this pattern were very interested in ensuring the report did not arrive.” He began moving again, toward the river. “But they have not killed him, or he would have been found. They need the report’s absence to appear voluntary — a clerk who has taken money and fled — without the complication of a body that might prompt investigation of the wrong kind. So they have him somewhere. And they need him there until tomorrow’s meeting has passed.”

“After which they will kill him.”

“Almost certainly. We have until the second hour after sunrise. Come.”

What happened next, I will record as a surveyor records — with attention to measurement and detail, trusting that the usefulness of accuracy will outlast my own understanding of its significance.

Trajanus Holcus moved through pre-dawn Londinium with the efficiency of a man who has been measuring a place the entire time he has appeared to be doing something else. He knew which lanes connected to which, which warehouses sat empty pending the next supply ship, which buildings were owned by men whose loyalties he had already assessed. He spoke to a water-carrier who was beginning his morning rounds — a Gaul, he established from three words of exchange, who had worked this quarter since the town’s founding. He spoke to a night-watchman at the bridge approach, a former legionary discharged from the Fourteenth with a stiff leg and a comprehensive knowledge of nocturnal movement in his district.

“Two men and a third between them,” said the watchman, “going toward the river warehouses. Not quite the third hour of night. I thought it was someone who’d found trouble in the wine.”

“Which warehouse?”

The watchman pointed. Trajanus Holcus pressed a coin into his hand with the ease of a man who regards money as a tool rather than a possession, and we went in the direction indicated.

“You knew to come to the waterfront,” I said.

“A man being conducted somewhere must be conducted to a space that is enclosed, away from morning traffic, accessible from the administrative quarter without crossing the main commercial lane where too many people sleep in doorways, and available to those with money but without Roman property rights. The waterfront warehouses are used by tribal traders as temporary storage. Several of them are, in practice, controlled by the same network that has been arranging the payment shortfalls. This is the geography of the problem.” He did not look at me as he said this, but I had the sense that the explanation was offered genuinely, as a teacher explains to a student whose failure to see is not stupidity but inexperience. “I should have come here first. I made the error of being thorough when I should have been direct.”

It was not, in my experience, common for a man of his capacities to say such a thing. I noted it.

The warehouse was unremarkable — long, low, timber, smelling of wool and grain and the particular damp of goods that have come a long way by water. There was a light inside, visible at the base of the door: a lamp, turned very low.

Trajanus Holcus examined the door, the ground before it, the wall beside it, with rapid attention. “Two men inside,” he said quietly. “One near the door — you can hear his breathing if you listen. One further back. Vettius will be toward the rear, where it is darkest.”

“How do you mean to proceed?”

He looked at me with something that was, briefly, amusement. “You are a surveyor attached to the Governor’s staff. What is your authority in this province?”

“I can compel access to any structure for the purposes of official survey and assessment.”

“Then compel it,” he said. “Loudly.”

What followed I will summarise rather than anatomise. I have, as I said, been a soldier; I am not, despite the impression my surveying work sometimes gives, a delicate man. I knocked on the door with the flat of my hand and announced, in the carrying voice that I have used to address work parties in wind, that I was conducting an imperial survey assessment and required immediate access under the authority of the Governor of Britannia. There was movement inside. The door was opened, after a delay, by a man whose expression combined the attempt at innocence with the reality of alarm in a proportion that told Trajanus Holcus everything, he told me later, that he needed to know about the man’s instructions. He had been told to appear unconcerned but had not been given the details that would allow him to construct a convincing performance.

Behind him, in the lamplight, Gaius Vettius Rufus sat on a wool-bale with his wrists bound behind him and the expression of a man who has spent several hours making private peace with an outcome he considered likely and is now adjusting, with difficulty, to the possibility of a different one.

The second man came from the back of the warehouse with his hand at his belt, and this is where the account becomes briefly physical in a manner I will record without embellishment: I broke his wrist before he completed the movement. It is, I have found, the most efficient resolution to such a juncture. He made considerable noise. His companion chose, wisely, to sit down.

Trajanus Holcus crossed to Vettius Rufus and cut his bindings with the small iron knife he wore at his belt — a knife that, I noticed, showed rather more wear than a man who deals primarily in documents should reasonably account for.

“The note under your door,” he said to the clerk, who was rubbing his wrists and staring at him with the expression of a man observing a god descend inconveniently from the sky. “What did it say?”

“That my sister — in Neapolis — that they knew where she was.” Vettius’s voice was steady, though his hands were not. “That if I didn’t come and speak with someone, she would be visited.”

“And when you came, they kept you.”

“They said if I destroyed the accounts — they had fire — if I destroyed the accounts before the meeting, they would let me go and leave my sister alone.” He looked at his hands. “I was — I was considering it.”

“I know,” said Trajanus Holcus, and there was nothing in his voice that judged this. He was quiet for a moment, looking at the two men who had kept the clerk — the one sitting, the one nursing his wrist, both of them with the look of men who are not the top of any organisation but are not the bottom either, who are useful and therefore expected to manage situations that are, ultimately, beyond their managing.

We were back in the administrative quarter before the first grey suggestion of dawn had begun to separate itself from the fog. The town was not yet awake. The stumps in the lane continued their slow vegetable resistance to Roman paving.

Trajanus Holcus sat in my room, which by now felt less like a surveyor’s office and more like the natural gathering-point of problems that wished to be solved, and looked at the wall for a period of time during which I said nothing, having learned that this was generally the more productive choice.

“The two men in the warehouse,” he said at last. “They are not nothing. They know enough to identify at least one layer of what sits above them. If we take them to Rufus Pollio”—this was the officer who served as the Governor’s deputy for internal security, a man of system and thoroughness who had, in my observation, a high regard for Trajanus Holcus and a slightly resentful awareness that this regard was based on the frequency with which Trajanus Holcus proved him wrong—“then Pollio will arrest them, which is correct, but he will also arrest them in the manner of the security apparatus, which is to say visibly, with the assumption that visibility demonstrates authority.”

“And that would alert the organisation above them.”

“The organisation that has spent a year carefully building what looks, on the accounts, like natural irregularity. Yes. If those men disappear into custody today, the men above them — who are watching, who are always watching — will fold the structure and rebuild it in a different shape, and we will begin again.” He was quiet. “The accounts go to the Governor this morning. Vettius delivers them. The pattern becomes visible to the administration, which is what the accounts are for, and that is correct and necessary. Pollio will investigate. He will, with the accounts as evidence, eventually disrupt the collection arrangement.”

“Eventually,” I said.

“Eventually. In the meantime.” He stood, which he did with the sudden energy of a man whose body has been merely waiting for his mind to require it. “The two men in the warehouse. They need to be somewhere that is not a security cell and not free, for long enough that the accounts are submitted and the investigation begins. After which their custody becomes a procedural matter rather than an alarm.” He looked at me. “You have a survey crew.”

“I do.”

“You have authority to compel labour for imperial survey work.”

I saw where he was going. “I do have that authority, yes.”

“A road assessment east of Londinium. Requiring, I would imagine, several days of intensive ground-level survey work. In conditions that do not permit casual communication with the town.”

I considered this. It was, I noted, not precisely legal. It was also, I noted, not precisely illegal, and it would accomplish what a more conventional approach would not. “I will need to adjust my schedule,” I said.

“I expect your schedule has survived worse.” He picked up his cloak. “The boy Durus will need reassurance. Vettius will need to be prepared for the meeting — he will be shaken, and a shaken man before the Governor is not useful. The threat to his sister in Neapolis is real enough that a letter should go with the next dispatch ship, alerting someone trustworthy in that city. I will write it, if you can identify who to address.”

“My brother-in-law has connections there,” I said, after a moment.

“Good.” He moved toward the door, and then paused with the quality of a man who has remembered something he had already solved but not yet reported. “You asked me earlier how I distinguished compulsion from fear. There is a second answer I did not give you. A frightened man, when found, is relieved but he is also — somewhere, underneath — shamed by his fear. A compelled man is relieved, and he is angry. Did you see Vettius, when I cut the rope?”

I thought back. “He was angry,” I said. “After the first moment. He was very angry.”

“He was angry that they used his sister. A man angry on behalf of someone else has not lost himself. He was never going to burn the accounts.” He considered. “Or not entirely probably not. The anger was genuine. In any case, the question does not arise.” He turned up his collar against the fog that came under the door. “Get some sleep if you can. The Governor’s meeting is at the second hour.”

He left. I heard his feet on the stairs — rapid, decisive, moving toward some other problem that had presumably already presented itself to him and been provisionally resolved.

I wrote this account because the Governor’s record of the morning’s meeting will note only that the tribal accounts were delivered on schedule and that certain irregularities in the payment records of three client chieftains have been referred for investigation. It will note nothing of the night before. The official record of Londinium, in the sixth year of the reign of Nero, will be silent on the question of where those irregularities were first noticed and by whom, and by what sequence of reading mud and measuring fog and talking to water-carriers and night-watchmen they were prevented from remaining invisible for another season.

This is, I think, appropriate. The roads I survey are used by men who do not consider who designed them or at what cost. This, perhaps, is what roads are for.

I add only this note, which I have not recorded elsewhere and do not intend to: when Trajanus Holcus wrote the letter for the dispatch ship, the letter to protect Vettius’s sister in Neapolis, he wrote it without being asked again and without reference. The name and district of the woman, which Vettius had told him once, in extremis, in a low voice, in the warehouse, he had retained precisely. He retains, I have noticed, everything that pertains to a case he is working. I have been present when he has failed to recognise a senior tribune he met the previous month, and declined to recall the name of a town he passed through on the road from Gesoriacum.

He told me once, when I pressed him on this, that the brain is a room of finite size, and that a man who fills it with furniture he will never use cannot blame the room when there is no space for what he needs. I did not entirely follow this reasoning, being accustomed, as a surveyor, to the proposition that more measurement is always better than less.

I have begun to think he may be correct. I have also begun to think that he has made this calculation so long ago, and so completely, that he is no longer aware he is making it. The room is already arranged. He simply walks through it.

The fog lifted, eventually, as it always does. By midday Londinium smelled only of timber and ambition and the Tamesis going about its old business beneath our new bridges.

Vettius Rufus delivered his accounts. The meeting proceeded.

Here the field notes of Quintus Marcellus conclude for this period. A marginal notation in a second hand reads: Q.M. reassigned to road survey, eastern district, for a period of eleven days, with a labour detail of two. No further annotation is provided.
