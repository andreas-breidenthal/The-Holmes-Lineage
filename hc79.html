HULMAR THE GOTH

~370 AD — Frankish lands

He was born into the great movement — the churning of peoples across the face of Europe that the Romans called, with characteristic understatement, the migrations, and that those who lived through it experienced as the dissolution of everything that had been fixed. The Visigoths were on the march, pressed from the east by the Huns and pressing in turn against the crumbling frontiers of Rome, and Hulmar moved with them — not as a warrior chieftain but as something the migrations needed equally and acknowledged less: a man who could assess a situation, read the intentions of strangers, and negotiate passage through hostile country without provoking the violence that was always, in those years, the easiest outcome.

His name was a Gothic rendering of the old name-element the family had carried westward from Constantinople — "Hulmar," cognate with the Norse "Holmr" that his descendants would bear, the sound shifting as the languages shifted around it. He settled eventually in the territory between the Rhine and the Meuse, where Gothic and Frankish and Roman remnants were beginning the long process of becoming something new. He left his son a position rather than an estate — which, in a world where estates could be taken in an afternoon, was the more durable inheritance.

The account that follows is preserved not in a formal scribe’s hand but in fragments — a set of leather strips recovered from a travelling kit long after Hulmar’s death, their edges worn smooth, the ink partially faded where rain had seeped through the stitching. The writer is not named, but the tone is distinct: halting where memory falters, precise where the writer trusts his own recollection, and marked by a habit of noting what is seen before attempting to understand it. The author of this record is Hulmar himself, though he would not have described himself as such. He wrote only what he observed, and what he could not yet explain he left unfinished rather than complete.

The Leather Strips of Hulmar

Set Down in the Winter Quarter Between the Rhine and the Meuse, in the Year of the Great Pressing

The body was found before the morning mist had lifted off the river, and by the time I reached it the Frankish chieftain Ragomar had already decided who had done the killing.

This is the way of it, in these years. A man dies, and before the flies have found him the living are already arranging his death into a shape that serves them. Ragomar wanted it to be Chlodwin of the river-Franks, with whom he had a quarrel older than the dead man's boots. He said so three times before I had finished crouching beside the body, which is three times more than a man certain of his reasoning needs to say anything.

I will set down what I saw, because I did not trust myself to say it aloud in that camp, and because I have found that writing a thing fixes it in a way that speaking does not. Words spoken in a camp at dawn, among men who have been cold for two nights and are hungry and looking for permission to do what their hands already want to do — those words travel poorly. They arrive changed. But marks on leather do not change unless the leather rots, and I have oiled this leather well.

The dead man's name was Gaius Felicianus Sertor. He had told me this himself, three days prior, when our band passed through the waystation at the old mill crossing where he had set up his small commerce. He traded in lamp oil — the thick, dark kind pressed from river-reeds, which burns with more smoke than the olive oil of the south but costs a fraction of the price and can be procured without depending on a trade route that the Huns have made unreliable. His hands were stained permanently with it, and his cloak smelled of it in the particular way that reed-oil clings, which is not unpleasant — a low, green smell, like the river itself had been rendered down and bottled.

He was a cautious man, Sertor. I had noticed the way his eyes moved when our band came into the waystation yard: not to our weapons first, as most men's eyes moved, but to our boots, our packs, the condition of our animals. He was reading our capacity for purchase, not our capacity for violence. A trader's reflex, and a refined one. He had survived in the frontier territory for some years by correctly identifying which bands were worth selling to and which were too desperate to bargain with safely.

He had a mule — a grey animal, old but sound — and a horse he used for his own travel. A bay, with a blaze on the left side of its face. I noticed it because it stood unusually still while the mule fidgeted.

I mention the horse because it was the first thing I looked for when I came to the body, and it was not there.

He lay on his back in the shallow depression beside the old Roman road, the stone surface of which had not been repaired in perhaps forty years and had begun to return to its constituent gravel. The body had not been dragged — the soil around him was undisturbed except by his own boot-heels, which had furrowed the mud in a manner consistent with a man falling backward from standing, with some violence. The furrows ran from the road's edge inward. He had been standing on the road when the blow came.

The wound was on his left side, below the ribs and slightly toward the back. This is the detail that I have been turning over since I stood up from the body and looked at Ragomar and said nothing.

A blade enters the left side from the back, below the ribs, when the man wielding it is standing behind and to the right of the victim, or occasionally when the two men are face to face and the attacking hand reaches across the body to strike. It does not, under ordinary circumstances, enter a man from the front when the attacker is left-handed and the victim is facing him, because a left-handed man striking from the front strikes the right side, not the left.

I know this because I am left-handed. I have been thinking about the geometry of violence since I was old enough to notice that the world is built for the right hand, and a left-handed man must always approach a fight from a different angle than the instructors assume.

The wound that killed Sertor entered from the back.

He was struck from behind, on a road he knew well, by someone he had allowed to walk behind him.

This tells me something. Men do not let strangers walk behind them on the frontier roads. It was not a stranger.

Ragomar's quarrel with Chlodwin of the river-Franks concerned a cattle-driving right on the common land near the old Roman ford. It was a genuine quarrel and a serious one, the kind that produces real violence eventually. It was also entirely unconnected to the death of a Roman oil trader on a road that Chlodwin's people did not use, because they approached the waystation from the north, along the river, and Chlodwin himself was known to use a different ford two days' walk upstream.

I did not say this to Ragomar.

I also did not say the following, though I have no difficulty saying it here, on leather, in the privacy of what I hope will be read only by careful eyes:

The mule was still in the waystation yard when we found the body. I had confirmed this with my own eyes before walking out to the road. A trader does not stable his mule and ride his horse to a meeting he intends to survive. He takes the mule for commerce, the horse for flight. The mule remaining in the yard meant Sertor had gone to this meeting expecting it to be brief, local, and conducted by someone he had already agreed to meet. He had not expected to need flight.

The lamp oil in his smaller pack — the travelling pack, not the commercial stock — had been gone through. Not robbed. Gone through and largely replaced, but in the wrong order. The bladders had been repacked with the oldest one on top, and I knew from watching Sertor manage his stock three days earlier that he always put the newest pressing on top, to remind himself to sell the older stock first. He had not packed that bag. Someone else had, after searching it, and had made the small error of not knowing how he organized his merchandise.

They had found what they were looking for, or not found it, and repacked the bag to suggest the trader's goods had not been touched.

What they were looking for, I do not know. I have my suspicions — Sertor had the careful manner of a man who carries more than oil through the frontier territory, and there are organisations that move information and sometimes valuables along these roads, using the commercial traffic as a vehicle, and the frontier years have made such organisations both more necessary and more ruthless in policing their membership. But this is speculation, and I will not write speculation as fact.

What I can write as fact is this: the man who killed Gaius Felicianus Sertor was known to him, walked behind him, searched his pack with some familiarity, and had a reason to want him dead that had nothing to do with Ragomar's dispute about cattle-driving rights on a ford two days' walk from here.

Ragomar sent men to Chlodwin's camp at midday.

I had, by that time, thought carefully about what I would do with what I knew.

The truth, spoken in that camp on that morning, would have produced the following sequence: Ragomar would have felt that I was undermining his authority and protecting his enemy. My band and I would have become the obstacle between him and his desired war. Wars on the frontier do not wait for justification — they simply need permission, and denying a man his permission for a war he wants is a dangerous form of opposition in a season when every band is armed and cold and looking for a reason to move.

Chlodwin, for his part, was guilty of enough that Ragomar's hatred of him was not entirely without foundation. He had raided the mill crossing twice in the previous summer and had made the waystation commerce difficult and dangerous. He was not an innocent man. He was simply not guilty of this particular killing.

If Ragomar attacked Chlodwin and the gods of the frontier roads showed their usual indifference to justice, one of three things would occur: Chlodwin would be killed, which would settle a genuine irritant and leave the truth of Sertor's death untold; Ragomar would be killed, which would settle a different irritant and leave the same truth untold; or both bands would be weakened, the waystation would lose the marginal protection their rivalry provided, and the commerce on that road, already fragile, would collapse entirely for a winter.

In none of these outcomes did speaking the truth serve anyone the truth was capable of serving.

I have thought about this a great deal, walking in the days since.

What I believe — and I set this down not as wisdom but as honest record of my own reasoning — is that Sertor's killer will answer to the organisation he serves before he answers to any man's law in this territory. The frontier roads have their own justice, slower and less visible than Ragomar's, but more precise. The man who searches a dead courier's pack and does not find what he was sent to find, and returns to his masters empty-handed, has already stepped into a current that will carry him to a reckoning I cannot provide and Ragomar cannot provide and the crumbling law of Rome's former territories certainly cannot provide.

He will answer to his own shadow. That is not nothing.

The bay horse with the blaze on its left side was found three days later, grazing loose near the river-bend south of the waystation. Sertor's saddle-bag was still attached. The smaller pack was gone.

I did not report this either.

I have thought about what kind of man writes things down only to keep them secret, only to oil the leather against the rain and store the strips in the bottom of a travelling kit that may never be read by any eyes but his own.

I think it is the kind of man who cannot bear for a true thing to be entirely lost, even when speaking it would be worse than silence. The record is not for justice. Justice in these years is an improvised and local thing, and I have made my peace with that. The record is against the particular darkness of a true thing dissolving as if it never was.

Sertor read his buyers accurately for years and finally misread one. He deserved to have someone note the shape of how he died, even if that someone notes it only in private, only in leather, only for the rain to decide whether it survives.

The mist came off the river again this morning. It smells of reed-mud and cold water and, faintly, of the pressed-reed oil that traders move along these roads, in the year of the great pressing, in the territory between the Rhine and the Meuse, where everything that was fixed is learning to move.

I have written what I saw.

What I could not yet explain, I have left — not unfinished, but open. As a door is left open that the wind has not yet decided to close.

— H., son of H-T., setting down in the winter quarter
