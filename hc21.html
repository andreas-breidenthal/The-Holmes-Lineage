AKAROS

~1450 BC — Mycenae, Greece

The Mycenaeans had displaced the Minoans as the dominant power of the Aegean by the time Akaros arrived at Mycenae — the shaft-grave culture that had been absorbing Minoan influence for a century now asserting its own supremacy, the great palace at Mycenae with its shaft-grave citadel and its Linear B archive replacing Knossos as the centre of the Aegean world. Akaros rendered the family name-element into Mycenaean Greek — the oldest Greek we have, preserved on those clay tablets — and served in the palace economy as his father had served at Knossos: as a merchant-interpreter, the man who could speak to the Canaanite trader and the Egyptian factor and the Hittite envoy in terms each could follow, and render their competing interests into the administrative language of the palace. He was also, and this was not incidental, a reader of men in the Mycenaean court — a world of warrior aristocracy and political intrigue where the ability to assess a man’s true intentions from his conduct rather than his words was a survival skill as much as a professional one.

He had grown up on Crete with the full inheritance of the family’s written record, the private archive that now spanned a hundred and fifty years of observation and accumulated to something that no single mind had encompassed before. His father had been the first to understand the network as a single entity. His father’s father had been the first to suspect it. He was the first to have the complete picture available to him from the beginning of his professional life, and the picture changed how he read the world around him. He arrived at Mycenae not merely as a merchant-interpreter but as a man already looking for the network’s presence in a new palace culture, and he found it within three years.

The record that follows was preserved by the scribe Dorykles, attached to the wanax’s archive, whose duty was to copy and maintain sensitive administrative cases beyond their initial filing. Dorykles was not trained as an investigator, and his account reflects both his diligence and his limits, yet it is from his hand that this particular sequence survives, with occasional marginal notes where he struggled to follow Akaros’s reasoning.

The Account Of Dorykles

Set down in the thirty-first year of the palace’s present administration, in the season of the barley harvest.

I record this at the instruction of Akaros the interpreter, who told me that an account made by a man who does not fully understand what he witnessed is more trustworthy than one made by a man who does. I confess I found this a peculiar reason for choosing me. I record it nonetheless.

The archive room had no window.

This was its purpose and its virtue. The tablets could not be baked by direct sun before their contents were verified; the dust of the citadel could not settle on a fresh inscription and fill the characters before the checker had read them; the traders and factors and military men who passed through the Cyclopean Gate had no angle from which to observe what the palace chose to record and what it chose not to. The archive was a sealed world within the sealed world of the citadel, and the air was full of scents: of damp clay, of lamp-oil pressed from olives grown in the lower fields below the walls, of the particular mineral cold that stone accumulates in darkness even in the months of summer heat. The lamp that burned in the corner when we found it had been burning long enough to blacken the ceiling above it in a half-circle that spread like a stain. The smoke had nowhere to go. Neither, in the end, had Thalos.

He was the senior scribe of the Linear B archive. He had served the palace for nineteen years, and he lay now on his side against the western shelving rack with his face turned toward the door and his reed still gripped in his right hand, as a man who falls in a field grips the tool he was carrying because the hand does not know yet that the work is done. The back of his skull had been struck once. The clay tablet on which he had been working lay on the floor beside him, fallen from the writing board when he fell, not yet dry.

The wanax’s chamberlain, Phrenos, had been the one to call me. He was a thick-necked man who administered the palace with the efficiency of a man who has never in his life been required to imagine what another person might be thinking. He was competent. He was reliable. He was, as I had learned in four years of working alongside him, constitutionally incapable of considering that a situation might contain more than one explanation.

“The slave Naxios,” he said, as we stood outside the archive waiting for the guard to unlock it, “was found this morning with clay on his hands and a lamp-wick that had burned to nothing. He has no account of his movements after the second watch.”

“Then why,” said the voice behind us, “did you send for me?”

Neither Phrenos nor I had heard Akaros approach. The interpreter had the quality — I had noticed it in the three years since he had arrived at Mycenae — of existing in a room for some time before other people became aware of him, as though he had first to decide whether the room was worth announcing himself in. He was leaning against the citadel wall with his arms folded, watching Phrenos with the expression of a man who has been told a story he recognises and is waiting for the teller to reach the part where they realise they have omitted something essential. He was not a large man. He had the colouring of his Cretan origins, darker than the Mycenaean aristocracy preferred, with a scholar’s stillness that sat incongruously on a frame that suggested, when he moved, that the stillness was a choice rather than a physical fact.

“Protocol,” Phrenos said. “A death in the wanax’s archive is a matter of the palace record. You are engaged to review matters of the palace record when —”

“When the answer is not obvious.” Akaros unfolded himself from the wall. “You have a slave with clay on his hands and no alibi. You have a dead scribe. You have concluded the one killed the other. You have sent for me, which means some part of you suspects that the obvious answer has a fault in it, or you would have simply had the slave questioned and not wasted this particular morning. Show me the room.”

The three of us entered the archive.

Akaros stood in the doorway for what seemed, in the confined and smokeful dark, a very long time. He did not move toward the body. He turned, slowly, to take in the shelving, the lamp, the door-hinge, the position of the single writing board that stood on its stand in the centre of the room, and then he crouched where he was and examined the threshold.

“There is clay on the floor here,” he said. “Fresh. From a tablet that was carried, not from the clay store, which is behind the eastern rack and therefore — where are you standing, Phrenos?”

“I am standing in the doorway.”

“You are standing on the only clay trail that connects this threshold to the body. Please move.” He waited while Phrenos stepped aside with the expression of a man who is being deliberately insulted. “The trail goes from the body to the door. Not from the door to the body.”

“The slave —”

“If the slave had entered to commit a murder, the clay would travel from the door to the body and then back again, as his sandals picked up whatever had spilled from the impact and redistributed it on his way out. This clay travels in one direction only, outward, which means it came from the tablet.” He rose and moved to where Thalos lay. He did not touch the body but crouched beside it at close range, examining the hand that still held the reed, the angle of the fall, the position of the head relative to the shelving. “He was struck from behind and to the left. He was working when it happened. He fell to his right. The tablet fell from the board.”

He picked up the fallen tablet.

I watched his face when he read it.

He read it twice. Then he stood very still, and I saw something happen behind his eyes that I cannot describe precisely: a narrowing, and then a releasing, as though something that had been held under tension had been permitted to run. It was the only moment in that room that I would describe as anything other than cold.

“Phrenos,” he said, “what does this tablet contain?”

Phrenos crossed to look. “It is an inventory record. The Iph-section, third register. Holdings of the textile division.”

“Read me the third line.”

Phrenos read: wo-ka-de a-re-pa-te-ja so-we-no we-re-ke. “To the work-parties: oil-allocated persons, the sowing-workers.”

“Now read the fourth.”

“me-ri-to a-re-pa-to-jo a-mo-ra-ma.” “Honey of the un-oiled — no. That is not a proper formulation. A-re-pa-to-jo is the genitive of a-re-pa-te, which is —” Phrenos paused. I could see him assembling the grammar. “A scribe of the oil-allocation? But the genitive construction here is malformed. The possessive does not agree. And a-mo-ra-ma is not —” He stopped again. “That is not a word.”

“No,” Akaros said. “It is not. Thalos was a scribe of nineteen years service. He had been copying Linear B records since before you arrived at this palace, Phrenos, and his hand is visible throughout the archive. I have read enough of his work in the course of my duties to know that he did not make errors of grammatical agreement. He certainly did not invent words.” He set the tablet down on the writing board with care. “This line was not a mistake. It was a record. He knew he was dying — the blow, perhaps, was not immediately fatal, or perhaps he heard the footstep and understood what was coming — and he used the only instrument he had available.”

“He wrote something into the tablet,” I said.

“He wrote a name into the tablet.” Akaros looked at me with an attention that was, I confess, briefly uncomfortable, as though I had done something unexpected. “Poorly. Under duress, with a blow already taken or a blow imminent, using a cipher that any trained scribe would eventually unravel but that would survive a cursory reading by someone who was not. The word a-mo-ra-ma does not exist in the Linear B lexicon. The syllable cluster, however — a-mo — is recognizable as the first element of a name, if you read the fourth line not as a sentence but as an interruption. He was mid-word when he fell, or chose to stop mid-word because stopping mid-word would make it look more like a dying error and less like a deliberate inscription.”

Phrenos had gone very still.

“A-mo,” Akaros continued, examining the tablet again, though I believe by then he had it complete in his mind and was consulting it only as a courtesy to those of us who needed to see him look at something before we could follow his reasoning, “as the first syllabic element of a Mycenaean name. The grammatical error in the preceding line is not, in fact, an error. A-re-pa-to-jo is not the genitive of a-re-pa-te in the form Phrenos initially read it. It is the genitive of Arepatos. Thalos was not constructing a sentence. He was constructing a name in the genitive — the name of a possessor, a master, the man on whose instruction or in whose presence this work was being done. The a-mo-ra-ma is the beginning of a second name, unfinished. Two names. The first complete. The second cut short.”

I looked at Phrenos.

Phrenos did not look at me.

“Arepatos,” Akaros said, “is the name of the second-ranking administrator of the textile division, who would have had legitimate reason to visit the archive in the evening to check the accuracy of an inventory submission before it was sealed. He would have been admitted without question. He would have been alone with Thalos for as long as he required.” He set the tablet down. “The slave Naxios was in the textile division storehouse until the third watch; I spoke with the storehouse keeper before I came here this morning because the storehouse keeper is an honest man who records everything and I thought his records might be useful. Naxios cannot have been in this room at the time the lamp-oil suggests this tablet was last inscribed. The clay on his hands came from an entirely different source, which I suspect Phrenos already knew when he arrived at his conclusion, but a slave without an alibi is a satisfying answer, and satisfying answers are the chamberlain’s professional preference.”

Phrenos said nothing. His face had the specific quality of a man who has calculated that there are no remaining useful words.

“The second name,” I said. “The a-mo-ra-ma. Did he mean —”

“I have a theory about the second name,” Akaros said. “I will not record it here. A theory is not a name, and a name set down without evidence is an accusation, and an accusation against certain names within this palace is an action with consequences that require consideration before I commit them to a tablet that Dorykles here will copy three times and file in different sections of the archive.” He gathered his cloak and moved toward the door, and then stopped with his hand on the door-frame and looked back at the tablet where Thalos had left his final record. “He was a careful man. Nineteen years without an error in the register. I wonder if he knew, when he heard the footstep, that someone would eventually be careful enough to notice.”

He did not say this to me or to Phrenos. He said it to the room.

Arepatos was brought before the wanax’s tribunal four days later. The evidence of the clay trail, the grammatical inscription, and the storehouse records that disproved the case against the slave Naxios were presented by the chamberlain Phrenos, in the manner of these things, as the product of the palace’s investigative process.

Akaros was not present. He was, as I learned when I went to find him, sitting on the eastern wall of the citadel in the late afternoon, watching the plain below, in the particular state of motionless inattention that he sometimes fell into between cases — not sleeping, not working, simply absent in some interior way, his mind browsing through its own contents like a factor checking a warehouse. He had, I was told by the palace cook, eaten nothing since the previous morning, which the cook found offensive. A cup of the Cretan wine he favored sat beside him, untouched.

“They have convicted Arepatos,” I told him.

“Yes,” he said.

“And Naxios is released.”

“Yes.”

I sat beside him, which he permitted without acknowledgment. Below us the plain ran gold and dry to the hills in the long light. The evening was beginning to carry the smell of cook-fires from the lower town.

“The second name,” I said. “The a-mo-ra-ma.”

He looked at me then, and I had the brief sensation — not unpleasant, but disorienting, the way that looking over a great height is disorienting — of being read with some precision.

“Dorykles,” he said, “you are a careful man. That is why I asked you to write this account. I want you to understand that the careful man’s virtue is also, in certain circumstances, his greatest danger.” He picked up the wine cup, found it empty, and set it down again. “There is a second name. It is the name of a man with whom Arepatos was transacting business that the textile inventory was intended to conceal. Arepatos did not kill Thalos because Thalos had made an error. He killed Thalos because Thalos was too good a scribe to make the error that Arepatos needed the record to contain. Thalos had noticed the alteration and was preparing to report it.”

“Then the man Thalos was trying to name —”

“Is known to me.” He said it with the particular flatness that, I had learned, indicated that a subject was adjacent to something very large. “He is not a man who can be brought before the wanax’s tribunal on the evidence of an incomplete syllable-cluster in a dying inscription. He is also not a man who can be allowed to know that I know what his name’s first two syllables would have been. These are, you understand, competing constraints.”

“What will you do?”

He was quiet for a moment. The cook-fire smell was stronger now, and somewhere below the citadel gate a cart-wheel had caught on the road-stone and was crying against it in a long, rhythmic complaint.

“I will do,” he said, “what can be done with the evidence I can use in the forum I have available. Which means Arepatos dies for Thalos’s death, and the business arrangement that produced that death becomes slightly more expensive and difficult for its architect, which is not justice but is the closest available approximation of it.” He rose from the wall. “Record that, Dorykles, at the end of your account. Not the name — I will not give you the name — but the principle. That the full truth is not always the truth that can be spoken. That a good scribe knows the difference between what the tablet contains and what it means. And that Thalos knew it too.”

He went inside.

I sat for a while longer, watching the plain go dark. Then I came back to the archive — to this archive, the one that has no window, though the lamp had been replaced and the floor cleaned and Thalos’s writing board moved to the storage rack where the closed accounts are kept — and I began to write.

This record is set down complete and filed in the third section of the sensitive administrative cases, per standing instruction. Dorykles, scribe of the second rank, in the season of the barley harvest, thirty-first year.

The account of the second name is not filed. It is kept. There is a difference, as I have been taught.

Here the account ends.
The record was preserved as part of the sensitive administrative cases of the Mycenaean palace archive at Mycenae.
