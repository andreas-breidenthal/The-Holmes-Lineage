AKAROS

~1450 BC — Mycenae, Greece

The Account of Dorykles

Set down in the thirty-first year of the palace's present administration, in the season of the barley harvest.

I record this at the instruction of Akaros the interpreter, who told me that an account made by a man who does not fully understand what he witnessed is more trustworthy than one made by a man who does. I confess I found this a peculiar reason for choosing me. I record it nonetheless.

They were leading the slave Naxios past the archive door when Akaros appeared at my shoulder. I had been watching from the colonnade. The slave was not a young man — he moved with the particular stillness of someone who has decided that the body's protests are no longer worth attending to — and the two guards on his arms were unnecessary in every practical sense, which is perhaps why they held him as tightly as they did. He had clay on his hands. He did not look up.

As they drew level with the archive door, he said, quietly and to no one in particular: "I was in the storehouse. I was in the storehouse all night." Neither guard responded. The three of them continued along the colonnade at the same pace, as though the words had not been spoken. Perhaps, for the guards, they had not.

"They will execute him by evening," I said. The words were barely out of my mouth when a boy collided with me from behind — fourteen, perhaps, reed-thin and wearing an archive tablet-case strapped across his chest, his eyes reddened in the way of someone who has been weeping with thoroughness and has not yet had the occasion to stop. This was Sesos, Thalos's junior apprentice. He had been copying administrative accounts under Thalos's instruction for the better part of two years, and I had seen him in the archive almost every morning of my own service, quiet and diligent in the corner where the new scribes were placed. He was not quiet now.

"They are going to kill him," he said, without preamble, his voice controlled by the particular effort of a boy who knows that losing it entirely will help nothing. "Naxios. They are going to kill him by evening and he had nothing to do with it — he delivered clay to us two days ago and he has been in the storehouse ever since, I know he has been in the storehouse, everyone knows it, and Phrenos will not listen to me because I am an apprentice and apprentices do not give testimony, and Thalos is —" He stopped. His voice, which had been managing very well, did not quite survive the name. "There is no one else who will go inside that room," he said, at last, very quietly. "They have all decided what happened. I need someone to go inside and look."

He was staring at me. He was, I realised, past the point of embarrassment; he had gone to everyone of any standing in this citadel and had been turned away from each, and I was what remained. I looked at Akaros.

"No," said Akaros, behind me, "they will not." I had not heard him approach. This was not, I had learned in the three years since he had arrived at Mycenae from Crete, where his family had served the palace at Knossos for generations before the Mycenaeans displaced their masters, entirely a matter of quiet feet. It was something more deliberate than that, as though he had first to decide whether a situation was worth the announcement of himself, and the decision required his full attention. He was watching the slave's retreating back, and I saw him clearly for the first time that morning: lean and dark, with the kind of frame that looks, in repose, like an instrument stored rather than a man resting — all potential and suspension, nothing wasted. His hands were at his sides. One finger, the index of his left hand, was tapping against his thigh with the rapid, unconscious rhythm of a loom shuttle, three beats and pause, three beats and pause, in a pattern that had nothing to do with the pace of his breathing or the movement around him. He had the quality — I had noticed it more than once — of thinking with his entire body, though most of his body had not been informed of this and was simply experiencing the consequences.

He looked at Sesos directly, and something in his gaze had the character of measurement — not unkindly, but completely, as though the boy were a text being read for comprehension rather than content. "You are Thalos's apprentice. You have worked in that archive for two years."

"Yes," said Sesos.

"Then you have read Thalos's hand every day for two years. You know how he forms his genitive constructions."

The boy blinked. This was evidently not the question he had prepared for. "I — yes. He taught me to form them the same way. He said it was the old way, the right way."

"Good." Akaros had already turned toward the archive. "Come." He moved with an abruptness that was slightly startling — the stillness had been so complete that the transition to motion registered almost as sound. Sesos and I followed. In the passage between the colonnade and the archive entrance, he paused at a fragment of painted plaster on the wall — a decorative border, running gazelles above a register of tribute goods — and touched the painted surface briefly with two fingers, the way a man touches a seal to check the impression. He said nothing. He simply noted it and walked on. I had no idea what he had noted. I was to learn this was not uncommon.

The archive room had no window. This was its purpose and its virtue. The tablets could not be baked by direct sun before their contents were verified; the dust of the citadel could not settle on a fresh inscription before the checker had read them; the traders and factors and military men who passed through the Cyclopean Gate had no angle from which to observe what the palace chose to record and what it chose not to. The archive was a sealed world within the sealed world of the citadel, and the air was full of scents: of damp clay, of lamp-oil pressed from olives grown in the lower fields below the walls, of the particular mineral cold that stone accumulates in darkness even in the months of summer heat. The lamp that burned in the corner had been burning long enough to blacken the ceiling above it in a half-circle that spread like a stain. The smoke had nowhere to go.

Neither, in the end, had Thalos. He was the senior scribe. Nineteen years of service, and he lay now on his side against the western shelving rack with his face toward the door and his reed still gripped in his right hand, as a man who falls in a field grips the tool he was carrying because the hand does not know yet that the work is done. The back of his skull had been struck once. Beside his outstretched left hand lay two things: the clay tablet on which he had been working, fallen from the writing board when he fell, not yet dry; and a small fragment of harder, fired clay, reddish in colour, the kind used for sealing submissions from the textile division. It had broken cleanly, as seal-clay breaks when it strikes stone.

At the threshold, where the archive floor met the corridor, there was a smear. I would not have noticed it if Akaros had not dropped to one knee to examine it — quickly, decisively, as though the crouching were merely an inconvenient position he was obliged to adopt and intended to occupy for the minimum necessary time — but when I brought the lamp closer I could see it clearly: reddish clay, the same warm ochre as the fragment beside the body, not the grey of the archive's own store.

Behind me, I heard Sesos make a small sound. He was looking at the writing board, not the body — at the fallen tablet on the floor beside it, visible from where he stood. His jaw had tightened. He had been an apprentice here for two years, and in those two years he had presumably carried water, cleaned the shelving, ground the pigments, swept clay dust from the floor, and watched the senior scribe's hand move across tablet after tablet in its unhurried and perfect formation. He said nothing. He stood very still, the tablet-case on his chest, and looked at Thalos's final inscription from across the room with an expression that was past grief and into something harder.

Phrenos stood at the body's head, arms folded, his face composed into the expression he used when he had already arrived at a conclusion and was performing patience for the benefit of those who had not yet caught up. He was a thick-necked man who administered the palace with the efficiency of someone who has never in his life been required to imagine what another person might be thinking. He was competent. He was reliable. He was, as I had learned in four years of working alongside him, constitutionally incapable of considering that a situation might contain more than one explanation.

"The slave Naxios," he said, "was found this morning with clay on his hands and a lamp-wick burned to nothing in his cell. The wanax will expect a resolution presented before the evening meal. We do not have the luxury of a slow inquiry."

"A lamp-wick burned to nothing in his cell," Akaros said. He was still at the threshold, not looking up from the smear.

"He has given no account of his movements after the second watch."

"Because he was in the storehouse until the third watch, which Ithakos's records will confirm." Akaros stood with a sudden, clean motion — there was something almost impatient in it, as though the floor had kept him long enough. He had been still a moment before; now he moved through the archive with a directness that made the room feel smaller, crossing to the body and crouching beside it — not touching, examining: the angle of the fall, the hand with the reed, the reddish seal-clay fragment beside the outstretched left hand. His index finger resumed its unconscious tapping against his knee.

He picked up the fragment. Held it at the lamp's level. Then he set it down in exactly the position he had found it. "Phrenos," he said, "was there a sealed textile submission on this writing board last night?"

Phrenos blinked. "There may have been. The archive receives submissions from every division for verification before sealing."

"Yes." Akaros looked at the fragment, then at the smear on the threshold. "And this fragment here — the seal-clay from such a submission — would be reddish, ochre, distinct from the grey clay your own scribes use." He did not frame it as a question.

"The textile division uses ochre-pigmented clay for its seals. It is how the submissions are distinguished on the rack."

"Mm." Akaros rose and moved to the writing board. The fallen tablet lay on the floor beside it. He picked it up — and here I saw the thing the critique in my memory of that morning will always carry: the way his hands took the tablet, not gingerly, not with reverence, but with a sudden proprietary certainty, as though the object had been waiting for him specifically and both parties had known it. He turned it once, sharply, into the best angle of the lamp's light. He became entirely motionless. I watched his face when he read it. He read it twice. Then the finger stopped tapping. It was the first time I had seen that happen.

He stood very still, and I saw something happen behind his eyes — a narrowing, and then a releasing, as though something held under tension had been permitted to run. He did not speak immediately. When he did, it was not to me or to Phrenos, but in the quiet of a man thinking aloud in a room he has temporarily forgotten is occupied.

"Nineteen years," he said. "And not one error in the register." I had read enough of the archive to know this was true. Thalos's hand was throughout the record — the clean, unhurried inscription of a man who had been doing the same thing for so long that correctness had ceased to require effort. I had sometimes used his older entries as the standard against which I checked my own copies. He had taught me, without knowing he had taught me, simply by having existed in the same archive and left his work behind him.

Akaros set the tablet carefully on the writing board and looked at me. "You knew his work."

"Everyone who uses this archive knew his work."

"Yes." He looked at the tablet again. Then he looked at Sesos, who had not moved from his position near the door. "Come here, boy. Read me the fourth line. Read it as Thalos would have formed it — in the tradition he taught you."

The door opened before Sesos could respond. The man who entered was perhaps forty, broad across the shoulders in the way of men who were once soldiers and have since transferred their discipline to other instruments — but the soldiering was not far beneath: it was present in the way he occupied the doorway for a fraction of a second before entering, taking the room's dimensions and contents before committing his body to the space. He wore the robes of a senior administrator. His eyes moved across the archive with a practised efficiency that was almost beautiful in its completeness — body, tablet, Phrenos, me, the boy, Akaros — and settled on Akaros with an expression that was almost welcoming. Behind that welcome, behind the specific quality of the warm voice and the easy bearing, was something I can only describe as the willingness to do what was necessary. He wore it the way some men wear an old scar — simply, as part of the terrain of the face.

"Arepatos," Phrenos said, with evident relief. "The interpreter is reviewing the room."

"Of course." Arepatos's voice was measured and warm, which is to say it was neither. He stepped into the room. "A terrible affair. Thalos had served this palace with distinction. If I can assist the interpreter in any way." His eyes had not left Akaros. "You are the Cretan. The family that served at Knossos."

"My family served at Knossos," Akaros said. He appeared to be consulting the tablet again, his attention partly elsewhere. "Before that, at various ports along the Levantine coast. We have always served palace economies. We understand how they work."

"I imagine you do." Arepatos moved further into the room, and I noticed — because Akaros had taught me, without intending to, to watch faces at the moment a man receives unexpected information — that his gaze had found the writing board. The tablet on it. He looked at it with the attentiveness of a man who would very much like to read what is written there, but would very much prefer that no one notice him looking.

"The inscription is unfortunately damaged," Akaros said. He turned the tablet slightly, as though settling it more safely on the board, in such a way that its face was no longer visible from where Arepatos stood. "The scribe appears to have made errors in the final lines. Grammatical agreement failures. A word cluster that does not correspond to any formulation in the lexicon. He was a man of long service — the hand is sometimes the last thing to fail." He paused. "It reads, for now, as a dying error."

Something moved in Arepatos's face. It was very small and it was gone in an instant. What I had expected to see was grief, or at least the performance of it, for a colleague of nineteen years. What I saw instead was the specific quality of a tension releasing. Relief, precise and involuntary, in the half-second before a man who is accustomed to controlling his expressions remembers to control them.

"A shame," Arepatos said. "He was always meticulous." He withdrew with the efficiency of a man who has accomplished what he came to do. The door closed.

I became aware that Sesos, across the room, had been watching Arepatos with an expression I had not seen on a boy's face before — not fear exactly, not grief, but the particular rigidity of someone who has found the thing he was looking for and would very much prefer to have been wrong. Akaros stood motionless for a moment. Then he turned, and his expression had the quality of a man who has placed a piece on a board and is now prepared to watch the game proceed. "Now," he said. "The fourth line."

Sesos read: his voice was controlled and clear, the voice of someone trained to read inscriptions aloud for verification. He read the third line first, the oil-workers and their allocation, the Mycenaean administrative record moving in its practised channels. Then the fourth: me-ri-to a-re-pa-to-jo a-mo-ra-ma. He paused over the grammar. He assembled it. He found it wrong.

"A-re-pa-to-jo," he said slowly, "should be the genitive of a-re-pa-te in the palace standard. But Thalos never used the palace standard for the genitive. He used the old form. He said it every time he corrected my copying: the old form is the right form, never mind what the palace requires. In the old form —" He stopped. He looked at the tablet with a new expression, one I recognised: the expression of someone who has understood a thing and does not yet know whether to be relieved or devastated by having understood it.

"In the old form," Akaros completed, "it is not the genitive of a common noun. It is the genitive of a name." He picked up the tablet. "Arepatos." Phrenos's face did something I had not seen it do before. The performance of patience departed from it entirely, leaving something unguarded underneath.

"The name sits in the fourth line," Akaros continued, "embedded in a construction that reads as a malformed inventory entry to any man who does not read these records every day with Thalos's particular formation of the genitive in his memory. To such a man it is a grammatical failure — a dying scribe's hand betraying him. Which is precisely what it was intended to look like." He set the tablet down. "Thalos did not write the name plainly. A plain inscription would have been the first thing checked, and Arepatos — a man whose entire operation depended on records containing exactly the errors he needed them to contain — would have destroyed the tablet before leaving. Thalos knew this. He had been watching those errors enter the textile submissions for weeks, perhaps longer. He knew how Arepatos worked, which meant he knew that a plain name would not survive. So he did not write a plain name. He wrote an anomaly that a careful reader would find wrong, and having found it wrong, might eventually find right."

The name lay in the room now. Phrenos was not looking at the tablet. He was looking at the door through which Arepatos had departed. Sesos was looking at the tablet. His face had settled, finally, into something that was not grief and not anger but the specific expression of a man — for he was, in that moment, past boyhood — who has understood that his teacher's last act was an act of craft, and has resolved to be worthy of it.

"The second name," Akaros said. "A-mo-ra-ma. Incomplete. He was mid-inscription when he fell, or chose to stop because stopping mid-word made it look more like an accident and less like a testimony. Two names. The man who struck the blow, and the man on whose instruction the blow was struck."

Phrenos found his voice. "The clay trail — you said it travels outward only."

"It does. Look at the threshold, and then look here." Akaros moved to the reddish fragment beside Thalos's left hand. "The seal-clay did not come in on Arepatos's sandals. He was already in this room when Thalos died — legitimately, as he had every right to be: a senior administrator reviewing a textile submission before it was sealed, alone with the scribe as he had been alone with scribes a hundred times before. The submission tablet was on the writing board. Its seal-clay was in Thalos's left hand, or on the board beside him, as it would be during verification. When Thalos fell, the fragment fell with him. When Arepatos crouched over the body — checking, retrieving, satisfying himself that the night's work was complete — he pressed his knee into the fragment. And when he left, the ochre clay left with him, transferred to his sandal, smeared on the threshold as he stepped into the corridor." Akaros looked at the smear by the door, the reddish trail that did not return. "One direction only, because the clay source was at the body, not at the entrance. He did not bring the clay into the room. He carried it out."

The silence that followed had a particular quality — not empty, but full, in the way that a room is full when the last piece of a structure settles into place and everyone present understands, in the same moment, that it is not coming apart.

"The slave Naxios," Akaros said, "will be returned to the textile storehouse. The clay on his hands is grey — archive clay, from the consignment he delivered to this room two days ago, as Ithakos's records will confirm when we consult them, which we will do now, before the morning advances any further." He looked at Phrenos steadily. "You knew this, or you would not have sent for me. A slave without an alibi is a satisfying answer. You sent for me because you were not quite comfortable enough with that answer to proceed without cover." Phrenos said nothing. His face had the specific quality of a man who has calculated that there are no remaining useful words.

"I am going to need the archive until the fourth watch tonight," Akaros said. "For what purpose?" "Arepatos came here this morning because he believed the tablet might still condemn him. I told him it read as a dying error." He looked at the tablet on the writing board. "He will come back tonight to confirm it. A man who has spent years ensuring that palace records contain exactly what he requires them to contain will not leave a damaged inscription unverified. He will need to see it with his own eyes." He turned to me. "I will need Dorykles."

He turned to the boy. "Sesos. You are free to go. You have done what you came to do." Sesos looked at him for a moment. He looked at the writing board — at the tablet that bore the last inscription of the man who had taught him the old form, the right form, never mind what the palace requires — and then he straightened and went out without speaking, which was, I thought, the correct response.

I looked at the door through which Arepatos had departed with his warm, efficient voice and his soldier's shoulders and his involuntary half-second of relief. "Yes," I said.

We did not wait in darkness. Akaros had the lamp trimmed and burning — the same lamp, the same corner, the black half-circle spreading above it as it had spread the night before — because a dark archive when the archive should be sealed was a sign, and Arepatos was not a man who would miss signs. We sat with the writing board between us: Akaros with a reed and a fresh tablet, myself with this account, because Akaros had said that genuine occupation is the best concealment and that if I intended to write the account regardless I might as well begin it now. I set down everything from the morning. I found it steadied me.

At some point in the long, suspended hours before the fourth watch, he rose and moved to the shelving rack. He ran one finger along the edge of three tablets from the textile division's recent submissions — not reading them, or not only reading them — with an attention that was close and peculiar, the way a physician attends the texture of the skin rather than its appearance. He tilted one to the lamp. He set it back. He resumed his seat without explanation.

After a silence, he spoke. "Do you know how the Cretans fired their administrative seals? The specific clay body they used — the mineral composition?" "I do not," I said. "Different from the mainland tradition. The ochre pigmentation is present in both, but the Cretan seal-clay has a finer grain structure — it breaks along a different line when it strikes stone. The fragment beside the body broke along the coarser line. Mainland clay." He dipped his reed. "Which means the submission that clay came from was prepared here, not imported. Someone with access to the mainland supply was managing both the submission and its seal." He wrote something. "That is the second constraint on the second name. Add it to the two syllables."

He had told me what Arepatos would do, as we waited. "He will come late. He will not bring a weapon — he has already committed one violent act in this room and violence is not his preferred instrument; it was an emergency measure taken when Thalos made clear he intended to report what he had found in the submissions. He will come to read. He will be satisfied or unsatisfied by what he reads. If satisfied, he will leave. If unsatisfied, he will destroy the tablet. In either case we will have what we came for." "Which is?" "His presence. Here. At night. After a scribe's death. Documented." He dipped his reed. "And his face, when he understands that he has verified his own guilt by coming to verify its concealment."

I wrote. Akaros wrote. The lamp burned. The room had the quality that rooms acquire after violence — not coldness exactly, not weight exactly, but something between the two that presses inward in the dark. The western shelving rack was clean; they had moved Thalos by then. But the ochre smear at the threshold was still visible from where I sat, and the archive clay still held the shape of the seal-fragment's impact on the floor. Akaros's finger had resumed its tapping — three beats and pause, three beats and pause — and I had come to recognise it as the sound of a problem being turned over, examined from its different faces, settled.

The tapping stopped. At the door: a sound, and then silence, and then the latch. Akaros did not look up from his tablet. The door opened fully and Arepatos stepped through. He had come without a guard. In the lamp's light he looked larger than he had in the morning — the shoulders more apparent, the ease with which he moved through a small space more deliberate — and his eyes went to the writing board first, then to me, then to Akaros, then back to the writing board. His path into the room would bring him past me, and I understood this, and I understood what a man with a soldier's build and an administrator's patience was capable of in a sealed room at the fourth watch, with a lamp for light and a problem that was one unsatisfying inscription away from resolution.

I set down my reed. I placed my hands flat on the writing board. I held my place. There are moments in which a man discovers things about himself that he would have preferred to learn in less definitive circumstances. I discovered, in that moment, that fear has a specific texture: not hot, as I had always assumed, but cold and very precise, concentrated in the hands and the back of the throat, leaving the mind oddly clear. I did not move because Akaros had told me not to. I also did not move because Sesos had come to find someone willing to go inside and look, and I had come inside and looked, and the act of completing a thing one has begun has its own form of authority.

Arepatos stopped. Not because of me. Because Akaros had set down his own reed, precisely and without hurry, and risen — and in the rising there was the same quality I had seen that morning, the transition from suspension to motion that registered almost as sound. He was not a large man. He was, in that moment, something other than a matter of size.

"The genitive," Akaros said, "is the interesting form. In the Cretan tradition — the tradition in which Thalos was trained before this palace was his home — the genitive of a man's name embedded in an inventory line takes the older form. It looks, to a man reading quickly in lamplight, like a grammatical failure. A dying scribe's hand betraying him." He picked up Thalos's tablet from the writing board — where it had sat, unaltered, since morning — and held it so that the inscriptions were visible in the lamp's light. "Your name, in that older form, sits in the fourth line. It has sat there since the night before last. I told you this morning it was illegible because I wished to see whether you would come back to confirm it."

Arepatos looked at the tablet. He looked at Akaros. Something moved in his face — the same small motion I had seen in the morning, but without the relief behind it. What was there instead was the specific expression of a man conducting, with great efficiency and no wasted movement, a final assessment of available options. "You are a long way from Crete," he said. "My family has always been a long way from somewhere," Akaros said. "We have found it clarifying. A man who has served three different palace economies understands that the mechanism is always the same: someone with access to the record, someone with authority over the record, and the belief that a record altered carefully enough is a record that no longer exists." He set the tablet down with the deliberateness of a man placing the final stone in a wall. "Thalos understood that too. He understood it well enough to know that writing your name plainly would not survive your inspection. So he wrote it in the only form that would — the form that reads as error to everyone except the man who has spent four years reading his work."

Arepatos said nothing. He assessed: the door, the guards he could see now through the gap that Phrenos had left deliberately ajar, the tablet, Akaros. The calculation was visible in his face, and it had the quality of a good calculation — thorough, unhurried, accurate. It arrived at its conclusion. "The second name," he said. "The incomplete inscription. You have it." "I have the first two syllables," Akaros said. "And the clay body of the seal. And the grain structure of the break." He let that sit for a moment, in the particular way of a man who has more cards than he intends to display. "They are sufficient."

Arepatos looked at him for a moment longer with the expression of a man professionally acknowledging the quality of work he would have preferred not to encounter. Then he turned and went with the guards in the manner of a man who understands the difference between resistance that is useful and resistance that is not. Akaros sat back down at the writing board. After a moment he said, without looking up: "You may continue your account."

Arepatos was brought before the wanax's tribunal four days later. The evidence of the clay trail, the seal-fragment's position, the grain structure of the break, the grammatical inscription, and the storehouse records that disproved the case against Naxios were presented by the chamberlain Phrenos, in the manner of these things, as the product of the palace's investigative process.

Akaros was not present. He was sitting on the eastern wall of the citadel in the late afternoon, watching the plain below, in the particular state of motionless inattention that he sometimes fell into between cases — not sleeping, not working, simply absent in some interior way, his mind moving through its own contents. A cup of Cretan wine sat beside him, untouched. His left hand's index finger tapped its rhythm against the stone of the wall, three beats and pause, three beats and pause, attending to a problem I did not know the name of.

"They have convicted Arepatos," I told him. "Yes," he said. "And Naxios is released." "Yes." I sat beside him, which he permitted without acknowledgment. Below us the plain ran gold and dry to the hills in the long light. The evening was beginning to carry the smell of cook-fires from the lower town.

"The second name," I said. "The a-mo-ra-ma." He looked at me then, and I had the brief sensation — not unpleasant, but disorienting, the way that looking over a great height is disorienting — of being read with some precision. "Dorykles," he said, "you are a careful man. That is why I asked you to write this account. I want you to understand that the careful man's virtue is also, in certain circumstances, his greatest danger." He picked up the wine cup, found it empty, and set it down. "There is a second name. It is the name of a man with whom Arepatos was transacting business that the textile inventory was intended to conceal. Arepatos did not kill Thalos because Thalos had made an error. He killed Thalos because Thalos was too good a scribe to make the error that Arepatos needed the record to contain. Thalos had seen the alterations in the textile submissions — had been watching them, I think, for some time — and had finally understood what they represented. He was preparing to report it."

"Then the man Thalos was trying to name —" "Is known to me." He said it with the particular flatness that indicated adjacency to something very large. "He is not a man who can be brought before the wanax's tribunal on the evidence of an incomplete syllable-cluster in a dying inscription. He is also not a man who can be allowed to know that I know what his name's first two syllables would have been, or what the grain structure of his seal-clay confirms. These are, as you will understand, competing constraints."

"What will you do?" He was quiet for a moment. Somewhere below the citadel gate a cart-wheel had caught on the road-stone and was crying against it in a long, rhythmic complaint. "I will do what can be done with the evidence I can use in the forum I have available. Which means Arepatos dies for Thalos's death, and the business arrangement that produced that death becomes slightly more expensive and difficult for its architect — which is not justice, but is the closest available approximation of it." He rose from the wall. "Record that, Dorykles, at the end of your account. Not the name — I will not give you the name — but the principle. That the full truth is not always the truth that can be spoken. That a good scribe knows the difference between what the tablet contains and what it means."

He paused, looking out over the plain, at the gold light dissolving across the Argolid. "And that Thalos knew it too. He knew that a plain name would not survive the night. He knew that a hidden name might. He trusted that someone, eventually, would be careful enough to find it — and that the careful man would understand why it had to be hidden, which is almost the same as understanding everything." He went inside.

I sat for a while longer, watching the plain go dark. Then I came back to the archive — to this archive, the one that has no window, though the lamp had been replaced and the floor cleaned and Thalos's writing board moved to the storage rack where the closed accounts are kept — and I completed what I had begun.

Sesos was not there. He had been reassigned, by the palace's own efficient processes, to a senior scribe in the eastern administrative block. I am told he copies well. I am told he still uses the old form of the genitive, which his master corrects, and which he reverts to the following morning, because some instruction settles into the hand and does not come out. I find I am glad of this. Naxios, I am told, went back to the storehouse. I do not know if anyone told him why he was released, or if the reason was explained to him, or if he understands that a dead man's knowledge of his own killer's methods was what kept him alive. I find I hope someone told him. It seems the kind of thing a man should know.

This record is set down complete and filed in the third section of the sensitive administrative cases, per standing instruction. Dorykles, scribe of the second rank, in the season of the barley harvest, thirty-first year. The account of the second name is not filed. It is kept. There is a difference, as I have been taught.

Here the account ends.

The record was preserved as part of the sensitive administrative cases of the Mycenaean palace archive at Mycenae.
