SIR RICHMOND HOLMES

~1670 — Yorkshire, England

Sir Richmond Holmes moved to London after his father’s death and discovered, as a number of clever men have discovered before and since, that cleverness in the company of the wits and gamblers of the coffee-houses of St James’s is an insufficient protection against their pleasures. His friendship with men of similar gifts and similar appetites was expensive in every sense of the word, and Sir Richmond pursued it with an enthusiasm that his estate could not sustain. He could read men well enough; he simply chose, with the clarity of someone who knows exactly what they are doing, to read them toward his own entertainment rather than his own advantage.

He possessed, however, a faculty that would not be entirely quieted by indulgence. When a thing appeared that did not fit, he saw it, and once seen, it held him with a persistence that no pleasure could wholly obscure. The gift in him did not assert itself as a profession or a calling; it surfaced as a disturbance — a small, unwelcome pressure against the smoothness of his chosen life.

Among the papers preserved by his descendants there is a written account by a later family scribe, Samuel Carrow, whose task was to gather and preserve these fragments. Carrow wrote with the patience of a man who understood that he was not recording a hero but a tendency — something that surfaced intermittently, imperfectly, and yet unmistakably when it did appear. What follows is taken from his record, though where the account bears marks of omission or hesitation, that too has been preserved, for it reflects the limits under which it was made.

The Card at White’s

Set Down by Samuel Carrow from the Recollections of Those Present

The night in question was a night in November, and November in London is a particular kind of dark. Not the clean dark of the countryside, which Sir Richmond had known as a boy in Yorkshire and which he would speak of occasionally when deep in his cups, with an expression that sat uneasily between contempt and longing. London's dark is a working dark. It has the smell of coal smoke and river mud and the particular sweetness of the oil in the parish lamps, which are never quite sufficient and which gutter in the river wind that comes up from the Thames and finds its way through every seam in every coat.

Sir Richmond had come to White's that evening with no particular intention beyond the intention that governed most of his evenings: to be among clever men and to let the night resolve itself into whatever the night chose to become. He had dined indifferently. He had drunk enough to smooth the edges of himself without dimming what lay beneath the edges, which was his perpetual habit of observation. The room was warm with bodies and tobacco and tallow candles, and the basset table was already occupied.

He had not been there long — I had procured us glasses and settled into the chair beside his — before I noticed that he had ceased to speak, and that his attention had fixed itself on the far end of the table with the particular quality of stillness that I had come to recognise as the precursor to something worth attending.

I followed his gaze to Mr. Albinus Crayle.

Crayle was a man who understood that appearance was a form of credit. He was well-dressed without extravagance, well-spoken without warmth, and he played badly at first, losing with a well-judged composure that suggested neither theatre nor collapse. The table accepted him as a man whose luck was temporarily impaired.

Sir Richmond, I could see, accepted something else.

"What do you observe?" I asked him, in a low voice.

"A performance," he said, without looking at me, "with a conspicuous absence at its centre."

I confess I saw nothing remarkable. I said as much.

"The losses," he said, "are not landing." He paused, and I waited, for I had learned that with Sir Richmond, patience was the better part of comprehension. "A man who loses money loses it in his body before his face has been consulted. The shoulders, the hands, the angle of the neck — these are honest witnesses. Crayle's face performs grief at every reverse. His body registers nothing at all. He is not losing. He is conducting."

"Conducting?" I said.

"Observe the man two places to his left."

I looked. The figure in question was a compact, unremarkable man of middling years, whose name I later learned was Gilbey, though at that moment he seemed to me merely a part of the room's furniture — a man who might have been anywhere without improving or diminishing the scene. He played cautiously, winning little and losing less, and gave every appearance of moderate concentration.

"Watch his hands," said Sir Richmond. "Specifically, his right hand. Watch when it withdraws from the table and when it does not."

I watched. It required several minutes before I perceived what Sir Richmond had already mapped in its entirety: that Gilbey's right hand drew back from the edge of the table at specific and recurring intervals, not when his own play demanded it, but when the deal passed through Crayle's fingers. Each withdrawal was brief. Each coincided with a pause in Gilbey's customary arrangement of his cards — a pause too short to attract notice, too regular to be accidental.

"The cards?" I said.

"The cards," Sir Richmond agreed. "Gilbey handles them twice. Once as an apparent player, once as a mechanic. The deck has been prepared — a preparation that requires periodic renewal as fresh cards are introduced. Gilbey refreshes it. Crayle reads it. The losses are engineered to fall upon particular positions at the table, and to fall lightly enough that no single man cries ruin."

"Then they are partners."

"They have been partners for, at my estimate, several years. The system is too well practised for novelty. Gilbey's withdrawal is timed to the third deal in every sequence — not the second, not the fourth. The timing is memorised, not calculated. That is the mark of long habit."

I was about to ask how he intended to proceed when the matter was resolved for him by young Peverell, who was hot-blooded and less observant, and who had been losing steadily for the better part of an hour — losing, I understood from what Sir Richmond had quietly observed to me earlier, at a rate that had carried him past inconvenience and into something considerably more serious. He seized Crayle by the wrist across the table and declared him, at considerable volume, a cheat and a scoundrel. The room fell into a stillness that is peculiar to such places — not absence, but concentration.

Crayle denied it with calm regret and offered himself for examination. The table turned, as tables do in such moments, toward the nearest authority. That authority, by accident of position, became Sir Richmond Holmes.

He rose, unhurriedly, and set down his glass.

"Mr. Peverell's accusation," he said, in a voice that carried without effort, "is not without foundation in perception. However, it has been directed at the wrong man."

The room shifted.

"Mr. Crayle has not touched the cards dishonestly this evening. He has not needed to." He allowed that observation to settle. "The function has been performed by Mr. Gilbey, who has refreshed a prepared deck at intervals of three deals throughout the evening, and who departed this table" — he drew his watch and glanced at it — "four minutes ago."

Heads turned. The chair two places to Crayle's left was empty. I had not noticed Gilbey leave; nor, it was apparent, had anyone else — though I recalled then a slight disturbance at the edge of my attention some minutes earlier, a chair drawn back, a coat brushing the wainscot, which I had taken for a servant and dismissed.

"The departure," said Sir Richmond, "was timely. It was, in fact, precisely timed — which is itself the most durable evidence remaining, since the man is no longer present to be questioned."

There was a long silence. Crayle had gone quite pale. He opened his mouth — I saw the effort of it, the drawing together of something that might have been a denial — and then his gaze moved, involuntarily, to the empty chair. Whatever he had been about to say did not survive the glance. He rose without a word, and no one moved to stop him; there was a quality in Sir Richmond's delivery that did not invite interruption or refusal, and the room seemed to understand that the matter had been concluded in a manner more complete than any quarrel could have achieved.

Peverell sat down. He looked, if anything, deflated — as men sometimes do when the instrument of justice proves less dramatic than anticipated.

Sir Richmond resumed his chair, reclaimed his glass, and drank from it at last.

"The second figure," I said, after a moment.

"Is already in a hackney," he said. "Or near enough. It is of no consequence. The system is destroyed. Another table, another partnership — those are problems for another evening."

I remarked that it seemed to me the system had been permitted to operate for some time before this evening.

"All systems are," he said, without apparent dissatisfaction. "The question is not whether they persist, but whether they are understood when they are finally seen."

He said nothing further on the matter. I recorded it then and there, in the margin of a letter I had in my pocket, while the details were still sharp.

What became of Gilbey is not recorded with certainty, though a man answering his description appears in the passenger rolls of a vessel departing Gravesend the following week. What became of Crayle is more obscure; a later notice suggests departure from England under unexplained circumstances. Sir Richmond read it without comment.

Peverell disappears from record entirely.

The evening itself leaves no official trace. White's does not preserve such matters.

What remains is this account, which exists because the faculty existed — independently of convenience, of intention, and of any invitation to appear. The Yorkshire blood ran thin in him, diluted by London and habit and company. But it ran.

It continued to run.

— S. Carrow, 1708
