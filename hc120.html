SIR RICHMOND HOLMES

~1670 — Yorkshire, England

Sir Richmond Holmes moved to London after his father’s death and discovered, as a number of clever men have discovered before and since, that cleverness in the company of the wits and gamblers of the coffee-houses of St James’s is an insufficient protection against their pleasures. His friendship with men of similar gifts and similar appetites was expensive in every sense of the word, and Sir Richmond pursued it with an enthusiasm that his estate could not sustain. He could read men well enough; he simply chose, with the clarity of someone who knows exactly what they are doing, to read them toward his own entertainment rather than his own advantage.

He possessed, however, a faculty that would not be entirely quieted by indulgence. When a thing appeared that did not fit, he saw it, and once seen, it held him with a persistence that no pleasure could wholly obscure. The gift in him did not assert itself as a profession or a calling; it surfaced as a disturbance — a small, unwelcome pressure against the smoothness of his chosen life.

Among the papers preserved by his descendants there is a written account by a later family scribe, Samuel Carrow, whose task was to gather and preserve these fragments. Carrow wrote with the patience of a man who understood that he was not recording a hero but a tendency — something that surfaced intermittently, imperfectly, and yet unmistakably when it did appear. What follows is taken from his record, though where the account bears marks of omission or hesitation, that too has been preserved, for it reflects the limits under which it was made.

The Card at White’s

Set Down by Samuel Carrow from the Recollections of Those Present

Being a fragment from the collected papers of the Holmes family, transcribed and arranged by Samuel Carrow, gentleman’s clerk, in the year of our Lord 1708, from such testimony as could be recovered. Where memory failed or decency required restraint, this account has noted the fact honestly rather than paper it over with invention.

The night in question — and there were, I am given to understand, a great many nights that might answer to such a description in the life of Sir Richmond Holmes — was a night in November, and November in London is a particular kind of dark. Not the clean dark of the countryside, which Sir Richmond had known as a boy in Yorkshire and which he would speak of occasionally when deep in his cups, with an expression that sat uneasily between contempt and longing. London’s dark is a working dark. It has the smell of coal smoke and river mud and the particular sweetness of the oil in the parish lamps, which are never quite sufficient and which gutter in the river wind that comes up from the Thames and finds its way through every seam in every coat.

Sir Richmond had come to White’s that evening with no particular intention beyond the intention that governed most of his evenings: to be among clever men and to let the night resolve itself into whatever the night chose to become. He had dined indifferently. He had drunk enough to smooth the edges of himself without dimming what lay beneath the edges, which was his perpetual habit of observation. The room was warm with bodies and tobacco and tallow candles, and the basset table was already occupied.

He had not been there long before he began to watch the man across the table.

Mr. Albinus Crayle was a man who understood that appearance was a form of credit. He played badly at first, losing with a well-judged composure that suggested neither theatre nor collapse. The table accepted him as a man whose luck was temporarily impaired.

Sir Richmond accepted something else.

He observed that Crayle’s losses produced no corresponding disorder in the body — no contraction, no shift of weight, no honest physical response to misfortune. The face performed correctly. The body did not confirm it. This, to Sir Richmond, was the beginning of structure.

He had identified, within the first quarter of an hour, that something in the performance did not extend beyond the surface. What remained was to discover where the truth had been relocated.

The accusation came later, from a young man at the table named Peverell, who seized Crayle by the wrist and declared him a cheat. The room fell into a stillness that is peculiar to such places — not absence, but concentration.

Crayle denied it with calm regret and offered himself for examination. The table turned, as tables do, toward the nearest authority. That authority, by accident of position and attention, became Sir Richmond Holmes.

He spoke without haste. The accusation, he said, was not without foundation in perception, but it had been misdirected. The interference with the cards existed, but it did not originate where it was thought to originate. The visible man was not the mechanism.

The mechanism had already left the table.

He did not name the second figure at first. When pressed later, he observed only that the departure had been timely, and that timing of that kind was itself a form of evidence.

Crayle withdrew. The matter dissolved in the ordinary way of such rooms, leaving behind only the residue of money and interpretation.

Peverell left shortly thereafter. Sir Richmond remained, ordered another glass, and did not drink it for some time.

The resolution of such a matter, he later observed, does not conclude interest. It removes it.

The difficulty, he said, is not seeing. The difficulty is what remains after seeing, when the structure of the evening has been exhausted and nothing further is permitted to appear.

The gift, he added, does not arrive when required. It arrives when it chooses.

What became of the secondary figure is not recorded. What became of Crayle is uncertain, though a later notice suggests departure from England under unexplained circumstances. Sir Richmond read it without comment.

Peverell disappears from record entirely.

The evening itself leaves no official trace. White’s does not preserve such matters.

What remains is the account, and the account exists because the faculty existed, and the faculty existed independently of intention.

The Yorkshire blood ran thin in him, diluted by London and habit and company. But it ran.

It continued to run.

— S. Carrow, 1708
