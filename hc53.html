HOLKOS OF MEMPHIS

~490 BC — Memphis, Egypt

Memphis under the Persian Achaemenids was a city in the condition that the family had always found most stimulating and most dangerous: a great centre of civilisation under foreign administration, its ancient institutions intact but its political life constrained, its intellectual culture continuing but its freedom of expression subject to the calculations of imperial preference. Holkos arrived from Athens with his father’s philosophical formation, his family’s commercial connections, and the particular alertness of a man entering an unfamiliar world and needing quickly to understand it.

He served in the Persian administrative apparatus as a translator and legal assessor — the role that the family’s combination of linguistic facility and analytical precision had filled in one form or another since the Assyrian chancelleries of the previous century. He assessed disputes between the Egyptian population and the Persian administration with a scrupulousness that earned him the trust of both, which in an occupied city was both an achievement and a delicate position to maintain. He understood perfectly well that his neutrality was not neutrality at all but a highly calibrated performance of it, and that the performance required constant adjustment as the political situation shifted — an adjustment he made with the ease of a man for whom reading the gap between appearance and reality had never been anything other than habit.

His record was kept by Petros, an Egyptian scribe attached to the temple administration of Ptah, whose own loyalty lay first with the temple and only second with the Persian administration. Petros writes with a careful hand and an awareness that certain conclusions could not be written openly without consequence. His account preserves both what was said and what was left unsaid, and notes where silence itself carried meaning.

The Affair of the Mistranslated Tablet

Copied from the working journals of Petros, Scribe of the Second Order, attached to the Temple of Ptah at Memphis. Let the reader understand that certain passages have been rendered with deliberate imprecision, for reasons that the careful reader will themselves deduce. Some truths belong to the temple. Some belong to the record. Few belong to both.

The smell of the body reached us before we reached the body.

This is not an observation I make for effect. In Memphis in the month of Thoth, when the river has dropped and the mud remembers the flood for weeks afterward, a man left in the shadow of the temple storehouse will announce himself with considerable authority by the second morning. The storehouse of the Temple of Ptah sat in a passage between the main sanctuary precinct and the administrative quarter, a narrow corridor of stone and shadow where the lamp oil from the temple workshops left a permanent resinous film on the air — cedar resin and sycamore gum and the particular bitterness of natron that never quite left the precinct no matter how far you walked from the purification chambers. Into this atmosphere, someone had placed the body of Darius-Pharnaces, junior administrator of the Satrapy of Egypt, assistant keeper of the imperial grain accounts, and — as I would learn over the course of the following days — a man of considerably more importance than his rank suggested.

I had been sent for as a matter of administrative procedure. The body had been found within the temple precinct, which created a jurisdictional question of the kind that Memphis in its current condition generated constantly: who held authority here, the temple or the garrison? The Satrap's office had sent its own man — a Persian officer of middle rank named Bagadata, a solid, honest professional of the kind that every imperial administration produces in abundance and every complex case defeats — and Bagadata had sent for me, because I had been sent for in such situations before, and because the garrison and the temple had reached a working arrangement in which a neutral assessor was less expensive than a constitutional crisis.

Holkos was already there when I arrived.

I say already there as though this were unremarkable. It was not. Holkos had not been summoned, had received no message, had been given no authority to attend. He had been passing through the administrative quarter in the early morning — he claimed — and had observed the direction in which the garrison soldiers were walking with that alert, purposeful step that distinguishes men going toward a dead body from men going anywhere else. He had, in his phrase, found the observation sufficiently stimulating to follow.

He was crouched beside the body with the expression he wore when his mind was fully engaged — which is to say, no expression at all. I have seen him watch a man weep with the same attention he gives to a column of account figures, and it would be wrong to say this indicated coldness, precisely. It indicated, rather, that in those moments the face had been stood down from social duty and the whole of his resources directed elsewhere. His travelling cloak — the wool one, the Athenian weave that he had owned since before I knew him and would not relinquish despite the climate — was folded across his arm with the careful precision of a man who expected to be some time about his business.

"Petros," he said, without looking up. "You are later than I expected. You took the longer route through the market."

"I was buying breakfast," I said, which was true.

"The fig-seller on the corner of the grain-measurers' street. You have patronised him for long enough that he holds particular figs aside for you, which means you had to wait. Next time, Petros, do not patronise a fig-seller with a sense of relationship. It makes you predictable." He looked up at Bagadata. "The officer will have sent for the priest already."

Bagadata's expression shifted in the way that Bagadata's expression always shifted when Holkos said something that was accurate and that Bagadata had not intended to reveal. "The high priest of the sanctuary precinct has been informed—"

"Not the high priest. A specific priest. An Egyptian of perhaps fifty years, temple-born, attached to the ritual calendar. He was seen by one of your soldiers near this storehouse two days ago, in circumstances that your soldier found irregular but did not know how to characterise. Your soldier put it in his report as 'the Egyptian was watching.' Yes?"

A longer pause. "The report used those words."

"And because your bureaucratic procedure demands a suspect and because you have a witness and a proximity, the priest is your suspect, and you have sent for him to be held pending inquiry." Holkos rose from his crouch with the controlled ease of a man whose body he treated as a tool to be maintained. "His name is Harsiese. He is not guilty of this killing. I will explain why shortly. First, however, I need to examine what the dead man was carrying."

The dead man had been carrying a great deal.

Darius-Pharnaces, as I have noted, held a rank that did not account for the complexity of his actual function. His satchel — leather, Persian work, the kind with the pressed cavalry-horse decoration on the flap — contained the documents of a man engaged in something rather more interesting than grain accounting. There were letters in three languages: Persian, which was expected; Aramaic, which was the administrative lingua franca and also expected; and Greek, which was less expected, and which Bagadata could not read.

There was also a clay tablet.

Memphis under Persian administration was a city of papyrus; clay tablets were Mesopotamian practice, survivals from the previous administrative tradition. To see one in the satchel of a junior Persian official in Egypt was the kind of incongruity that most men would note and set aside. Holkos sat down on the storehouse step, held the tablet to the angle of morning light coming through the passage entrance, and was silent for a longer time than I had ever seen him give to any single object.

The hinge of the storehouse gate, in need of oil since the previous season, creaked in the draught from the river. Somewhere in the sanctuary precinct, the morning ritual had begun; I could hear the specific timber of the sacred percussion instruments, the sound that Memphis has made every morning since before any living person was born, travelling through the stone of the precinct wall with a solidity that made the Persian presence feel, for a moment, like weather — something that came and passed while the stone continued.

"Bagadata," Holkos said at last, "is the name Artabanus known to your garrison intelligence?"

The silence that followed this question was a different quality of silence from the ordinary administrative silences I had spent my career navigating. Bagadata had the face of a man who had been given a word in a language he knew he should not recognise.

"It is not a name in my—"

"Do not trouble yourself with the performance," Holkos said, not unkindly. "You are a professional and I am not questioning your integrity. I am asking because the name appears on this tablet and its significance is legible in the document context, and because what I am about to explain will require certain decisions from you that you will find easier to make if you have already made them privately." He set the tablet on his knee. "I will now explain what this document is, and then I will explain what happened to Darius-Pharnaces, and then we will discuss together what the official record of this matter ought to contain."

He had, I observed, placed himself on the storehouse step in such a position that both Bagadata and I were facing him, with the passage wall at his back. He did this without appearing to have arranged it. Holkos was, beneath the analytical apparatus, a man of considerable theatrical instinct, and the instinct had learned over years to operate invisibly.

"This tablet," he began, "is written in Aramaic. Bagadata reads Aramaic. I read Aramaic. Petros reads Aramaic, because Petros reads everything. The body was also carrying a papyrus document — that one, Bagadata, the folded one you placed in your own satchel upon arrival, which you did not intend me to notice — which purports to be a Greek translation of the tablet's contents."

Bagadata's hand moved toward his satchel and then stopped.

"The papyrus translation is in Greek. I read Greek, because I am Athenian and it would be peculiar if I did not. The tablet is in Aramaic. There is a discrepancy between them. A single word, in the middle of the third line, has been rendered differently. In the Aramaic, the word is malka — king, or one of the king's party, a loyalist. In the Greek, the translator has written the word tyrannos — a tyrant, an illegitimate power. These are not the same word. They are not close to the same word. In a legal or diplomatic document, they are opposing words."

He let this settle. The sacred percussion continued from beyond the wall.

"A translator who reads both languages with equal competence does not make this error. Darius-Pharnaces held the position of legal assessor, which requires demonstrated bilingual competence, which means he made this substitution not by mistake but by choice. He rendered a word that meant the Great King's instrument as a word that meant unlawful authority." Holkos held up the tablet. "This document is a set of instructions from a figure called Artabanus — I will not speculate on which Artabanus, as there are several in the imperial court and I have insufficient information to distinguish between them — authorising Darius-Pharnaces to assess certain grain accounts in Memphis not for their stated purpose, which is the supply of the imperial garrison, but for another purpose entirely. The Greek translation was to be the document placed before the Persian governor. The Aramaic original was to be suppressed."

Bagadata was very still.

"Darius-Pharnaces," Holkos continued, "was carrying evidence of a conspiracy within the imperial administration. Whether he was its agent or its discoverer, I cannot say with certainty, though the manner of his death suggests the latter. Someone learned that he had both documents in his possession. Someone who could not afford that particular comparison to be made — by a Greek-reading legal assessor, for instance, of the kind that Memphis currently employs — arranged for Darius-Pharnaces to be unavailable for such assessments." He set the tablet down. "That person is not Harsiese the priest. Harsiese was watching the storehouse because the storehouse contains the grain accounts to which this document refers, and because Harsiese, who has been attached to this precinct for thirty years and knows the smell of administrative irregularity, had noticed that certain quantities were not matching their records. He was doing what priests of Ptah have done for three thousand years. He was keeping the accounts honest."

The lamp oil on the air, the cedar resin and the natron and underneath it all the smell of the river mud, permanent and patient.

"Then who," said Bagadata, and stopped.

Holkos looked at him with the expression that I had by then learned to read: not contempt, but something more complicated — something that recognised competence and its limits simultaneously, and was not unkind about either.

"You have a garrison. I have a name that appears in the administrative appointment records of the past three months and a manner of death that requires both access to the temple precinct after the evening closing and the knowledge that Darius-Pharnaces would be in this particular passage at that particular hour. Those two requirements together reduce the field considerably." He paused. "I will give you the name privately. Not here. Not in a document. In a conversation, in a location of your choosing, with no record made of my involvement. You will then make of it what your judgment and your position require."

"And Harsiese?"

"Harsiese will be released this morning on my assessment that the witness account is insufficient to establish proximity to the act. Which is true. The witness account is insufficient. I have examined it and it is insufficient."

Bagadata looked at the tablet, and at the papyrus in his satchel, and at the body of Darius-Pharnaces, who had known something he should not have known and carried the proof of it one day too long.

"The official record," Bagadata said carefully, "will state that the investigation identified a suspect of Egyptian origin whose guilt was ruled out following assessment. The case will be—" he chose the word with a professional's precision— "referred."

"The case will be referred," Holkos agreed. "That is an accurate description of what will occur."

I walked back with Holkos through the market, past the fig-seller's corner, in the particular morning light of Memphis that is different from the light of anywhere else I have ever been — not softer or harder but simply older, as though it has been filtered through several thousand years of the same transactions and the same dust and the same river smell and arrived at the present moment somewhat worn by the journey.

"You knew before you looked at the tablet," I said.

"I had a hypothesis before I looked at the tablet. The tablet confirmed it."

"How?"

He was quiet for a moment with the quality of quiet that meant he was deciding how much of the working to share. "Darius-Pharnaces had been in Memphis for four months. His sandals showed extensive wear on the inner heel, which indicates a man who walks with the particular tension of someone perpetually aware of being observed. The ink-staining on his right hand was from three different formulations — Persian chancellery ink, Egyptian reed-pen black, and a third that was faded and older, a Babylonian clay-work compound that has no place in his recent administrative duties but every place in the practice of a man trained in multiple documentary traditions. He was not a junior administrator. He had been placed as one."

"He was an intelligence officer."

"He was a man who read documents in multiple languages and noticed things. Whether the imperial apparatus had a specific term for this, I cannot say." A pause. "The family resemblance to my own position was not lost on me."

We passed a water-seller with a cracked clay jug, the water leaving a thin trail in the dust behind him. Holkos watched it for a moment with an expression I could not read.

"The man who gave the name," I said. "You know who he is."

"I know several things about who he is."

"And you gave only the name to Bagadata. Not the others."

He looked at me with what I can only describe as a very old attention — the attention of someone who has decided, over a considerable period, exactly how much of a particular truth serves a particular purpose. "Petros. You are my scribe, and I trust your judgment, and your record is careful and honest. I will tell you this: the name I gave Bagadata is sufficient for Bagadata's purposes, and the purposes it serves are ones that I consider adequate. The other names I have not given to Bagadata because Bagadata's authority does not reach them, and a name given to a man whose authority does not reach it is not information — it is a liability."

"And the conspiracy itself?"

The cedar resin and the river, the morning light through the market's linen awnings, the sound of Memphis conducting its ordinary business around us.

"Memphis is three thousand years old," Holkos said. "It has been administered by Egyptians, Libyans, Nubians, Assyrians, and now Persians. It will be administered by someone else eventually. The conspiracy, whatever its precise architecture, will be disrupted by Bagadata to the degree that Bagadata can disrupt it. Something else will grow in the same soil in time. The grain accounts will be corrected. Harsiese will continue to observe them. This is, in my experience, approximately what justice looks like when it is functioning adequately."

He bought a fig from the corner seller and gave it to me, since I had been delayed earlier by his own insistence on urgency.

"Your breakfast," he said.

Here the formal record concludes. What follows is written by my own hand and kept separate from the official documentation, in the manner that Holkos had by this time established as our custom. The official report of the matter noted that Harsiese, priest, was examined and released; that the death of Darius-Pharnaces was referred to the Satrap's office for further inquiry; and that the legal assessor Holkos of Athens provided consultative services in accordance with his appointment. I am told that a certain officer was reassigned to a remote garrison post within the month, and that a certain set of grain account discrepancies was quietly corrected in the imperial records.

I do not know what was done with the clay tablet.

I do not know, and have not asked, and this restraint is itself a form of record. Some documents are preserved by their existence. Some are preserved by the careful description of the space where they once were.

I am a scribe of the Temple of Ptah. I know which truths belong to which archives.

— Petros, son of Amenhotep, in the service of the temple and of accurate accounting, Memphis, in the year of the reign of the Great King, whom may the gods weigh justly in his time.
