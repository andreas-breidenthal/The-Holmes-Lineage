NABI-ILUM

~1840 BC — Memphis, Egypt

The gods have called — and what they called him toward was south, and west, and the long road through Canaan to the Delta of the Nile. Nabi-ilum left Babylon with a trading expedition organised through one of the Babylonian merchant houses, carrying tin and textiles along the route that ran through the Levantine cities to the Egyptian border, and arrived in Memphis with the intention of returning within two seasons. He did not return. Egypt held him.

Memphis, the great administrative city of Egypt, sat at the heart of the Thirteenth Dynasty, a bureaucracy of established refinement, sustained by a long scribal tradition and marked by careful attention to the recording of administrative reality. Nabi-ilum found in the Egyptian scribal culture something that resonated with the deepest habits of his family — the absolute priority of the accurate account, the reverence for the document that said precisely what was true and nothing else — and he settled among it, married an Egyptian woman of the administrative class, and raised his son in the double tradition of Babylonian analytical rigour and Egyptian scribal discipline. The hybrid would prove, in the generations that followed, exceptionally productive.

The record that follows is preserved in a series of administrative tablets attributed to Paser, a junior translator attached to the palace office. His account is not a formal report but a working record, written as events unfolded and later copied for preservation. Paser does not claim authority over the interpretation of events; he records what he observed, what he was asked, and what he heard without understanding its full consequence at the time.

The Working Record Of Paser

Copied for preservation at the instruction of the Chief Scribe. The events herein occurred in the year of the second inundation of my service. I set down what I observed without embellishment, as my training demands, though I confess the events moved faster than my reed could follow.

The body of Djedhor the merchant was found, or rather announced, on the morning of the third day of the month of Thoth, when his household steward arrived at the office of the Harbour Master weeping with professional conviction and declared that his master had been taken by the river the previous evening while inspecting his mooring.

I say announced because no body was produced. The steward explained, with the fluency of a man who had rehearsed, that the current had taken it south. The Harbour Master, a careful man named Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, noted this in his register and dispatched two river-men to search the eastern bank. He also, as was his habit in matters touching the warehouses of the northern quarter, sent word to the office of the Foreign Assessor.

It was in this capacity that I first encountered Nabi-ilum.

I had been warned. My senior, old Userhat, had described him once as the Babylonian who reads a bill of lading the way a priest reads the future — and is more often correct. I had taken this for the exaggeration of an old man who admired foreigners too easily. I revised this opinion within the first hour.

He arrived at the Harbour Master’s office before I did, though he had been sent for after me. He was already seated when I entered, not at the table but beside the narrow window that overlooked the quay, turned slightly so that he could observe the warehouse district through the opening while appearing to attend to nothing at all. He was a man of perhaps forty years, lean in the manner of someone who forgets to eat when his attention is elsewhere, with the deep-set eyes common to the eastern families and hands that were perpetually ink-stained at the fingertips. His robe was Egyptian linen but cut in a slightly foreign fashion, as though he had adopted the material without fully surrendering the form.

He did not look at me when I entered. He was watching the quayside.

“The steward,” he said, without turning, “came from the direction of the southern warehouses, not the northern. He entered through the servants’ gate rather than the main approach. He has river-mud on his left sandal only, which is consistent with a man who stood briefly at the water’s edge this morning — not with a man who spent the night searching for his drowned master.”

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, the Harbour Master, blinked. “You observed all of this as he arrived?”

“I observed it as you were reading the notation he brought. You were attending to the document. I had nothing else to attend to.”

He said this without pride, simply, as a craftsman might note that he had used the time while the kiln heated to sharpen his tools.

I introduced myself. He turned then and looked at me with the appraising directness that I would come to know well — a look that took in the ink on my fingers, the particular cut of my kilt which marked me as palace-trained, the slight elevation of the left shoulder that comes from years of bending over a low writing surface, and assembled them into a sufficient account of me in approximately the time it takes to draw one breath.

“Paser,” he said. “You translate between the administrative registers — the palace accounts and the foreign merchants’ bills. You have read Djedhor’s shipping documents.”

“I have translated summaries of them, yes.”

“Then you are already more useful than anyone else in this room.” He stood. “Come.”

The warehouse was sealed with the Harbour Master’s cord, the clay stamp unbroken. Nabi-ilum examined the seal without touching it for a long moment, then looked at the ground before the door.

“The seal is intact,” Amenhotep-sa-Hapu said. “Nothing has been removed since we placed it.”

“Nothing has been removed since long before you placed it,” Nabi-ilum said. He crouched and pressed two fingers into the earth just before the threshold. “Stand here. What do you see?”

The Harbour Master stood beside him and looked at the ground. “Earth. The ordinary surface.”

“And what do you not see?”

A pause.

“The wheel-ruts,” I said. I do not know what prompted me to speak — something in the way he had framed the question made me look for the absence rather than the presence. “Cedar comes on heavy carts. There should be ruts from the last delivery.”

Nabi-ilum looked up at me with an expression I could not immediately classify. It was not surprise. It was something more like the satisfaction of a man who has been given, unexpectedly, a good tool.

“The translator observes,” he said. “Yes. The last delivery of cedar to these warehouses, according to the register of the northern quarter, was forty-three days ago. Heavy timber on heavy carts in the soft ground of the post-inundation season. The ruts should be here. They are not. The surface has been worked over.” He stood and turned his attention to the door. “Amenhotep, open it.”

The warehouse contained grain.

Not cedar. Grain — old grain, by the smell, the kind kept as a buffer against the lean years, legitimate and unremarkable. It filled the space completely. There was no evidence that cedar had ever been here, or that it had been hastily removed. The grain had settled into the corners with the patience of things that have been sitting undisturbed for months.

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu stared at it. “The theft was reported three days ago. His steward said twenty talents of cedar, gone between one evening and the next morning. You cannot move twenty talents of cedar and replace it with—”

“No,” Nabi-ilum agreed. “You cannot. Which is why it was never moved.” He turned and walked back into the light. I followed. After a moment, so did the Harbour Master.

Outside, Nabi-ilum sat on the low wall that edged the quayside and pressed the tips of his fingers together in a way I would come to recognise — the posture of a man who was not resting but thinking with his whole body rather than merely his hands.

“Sit,” he said to me. “You have the registers.”

I had brought them as a matter of habit, rolled in my carrying-cloth. I set them between us.

“The Babylonian bill of lading first.”

I unrolled it. He did not read Babylonian cuneiform — or rather, he read it as a native, which meant he read it differently from the way I translated it, attending not to the meaning of the symbols but to the physical impression of the tablet, the pressure of the stylus, the spacing.

“This tablet was written by two different scribes,” he said. “The original notations, here” — he pointed without touching — “and the quantity notation, which was added later by a different hand. The clay has a different surface quality at this point. It was re-wetted and impressed after the original record was set.”

I looked. He was correct. The grain of the clay was different. I had translated this document twice and had not seen it.

“And the Egyptian record?”

I unrolled the second document. He studied it differently — more slowly, his eyes moving along the lines in the manner of a man reading rather than a man reading and simultaneously thinking about something else.

“The Egyptian document records the cedar delivery correctly,” he said. “Forty-three days ago, twenty talents, consigned to Djedhor’s northern warehouse, received and sealed. This record has not been altered.” He set it down. “So the cedar arrived. It was recorded arriving. And now the warehouse contains only grain.”

“Then it was removed after all,” Amenhotep-sa-Hapu said. He had been listening from a few steps away, as men do when they wish to appear to be attending to other matters.

“If it was removed, there would be ruts.” Nabi-ilum looked at the ground again, then at the river. “Paser. The Babylonian merchants who consigned this cedar — do you know their names?”

“The house of Warad-Sin. They operate the northern Levantine route.”

“And do they have a factor in Memphis?”

“They use Djedhor’s agency. He represents them for import and storage.”

A silence settled. Below us, a felucca moved slowly upriver, its sail barely filled. Nabi-ilum watched it.

“The cedar,” he said, “was shipped twice. It arrived here forty-three days ago, was documented, sealed, and within a very short window — before the ground had time to bear the weight of full carts — was loaded again onto a vessel and sent somewhere else. Southward, I would judge, toward Thebes or beyond. The Babylonian bill of lading was then altered to show a smaller quantity, creating a discrepancy between the Egyptian record and the Babylonian record that any translator would struggle to reconcile without examining both documents together. Djedhor then reported the theft of the cedar that was never here, to be compensated by his Babylonian principals for a quantity that he had in fact already sold to someone else. He would collect the compensation from Warad-Sin’s house, and the proceeds of the second sale from wherever the cedar went. He then, to avoid the subsequent accounting, staged his own death.”

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu said, very carefully, “You are saying there is no body.”

“I am saying there is a steward with mud on one sandal who came from the south this morning, and a sealed warehouse that has not seen cedar in forty-three days, and a man who owes a great Babylonian house a very considerable debt that he cannot repay.” He stood. “The question is not where the body is. The question is which of the southern villages is currently sheltering a moderately prosperous Egyptian merchant who arrived recently with no obvious occupation and a tendency to keep indoors.”

I am compelled to record that we found him within four days, which felt like a miracle and which Nabi-ilum treated as arithmetic.

He was in a farming village two days south of Memphis, lodging with a cousin who had, apparently, been paid generously to ask nothing. He had with him documentation in a name I will not record here — a second identity, well-prepared, which suggested this had been contemplated for some time. He was thinner than his household records described him, and had shaved his head in the manner of the farmers rather than the merchants, which made him look, according to Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, like a completely different man.

According to Nabi-ilum, it made him look like a merchant who had shaved his head.

Djedhor was returned to Memphis under escort. He did not speak during the journey, which the guards interpreted as guilt and Nabi-ilum interpreted, I think, as something closer to exhaustion — the particular exhaustion of a man who has been maintaining a false self and has finally been permitted to stop.

What followed is recorded in the official register of the Harbour Master’s office under a summary notation: Resolution of fraudulent loss-claim, northern warehouses, northern quarter. Cedar shipment recovered and returned to principals. Duties remitted and penalties assessed. Matter closed.

The official record does not mention Nabi-ilum. It credits the resolution to the diligence of the Harbour Assessor’s office, which is accurate in the sense that the Harbour Assessor’s office did, ultimately, collect Djedhor and file the appropriate papyri.

I record this discrepancy without complaint. It is the nature of the official record to credit the institution. Nabi-ilum himself did not appear to care. When I mentioned it to him — awkwardly, unsure whether I was offering comfort or making an accusation — he looked at me with that same unclassifiable expression and said: The cedar was found. The fraud was documented. What more needs to be true?

I did not have an answer.

There is one thing I have not yet set down, which I set down now because I believe it belongs to the complete account, even if it does not belong to the official record.

On the evening before Djedhor was handed to the legal office, Nabi-ilum spent an hour with him alone. I do not know what was said. When he emerged, I asked, in the way of a man who has been keeping a working record and finds a gap in it, what had passed between them.

He was quiet for a moment.

“Djedhor’s wife died two years ago,” he said. “Of a fever. The cedar trade had been declining for three seasons before that. He had been covering losses with personal reserves, and when those were exhausted, he borrowed from Warad-Sin’s house against future shipments. When he could not cover the debt, he began to look for another way.” He paused. “He is not a man who was born to fraud. He arrived at it by a series of small steps that each seemed, at the time, like the only available step.”

I waited.

“I told him,” Nabi-ilum said, “that the legal office will assess penalties against the cedar proceeds. But if he provides a full accounting of where the timber went and who received it, the matter of his fraudulent death can be quietly… unrecorded. A man who is dead has already paid a significant social penalty.”

I looked at him. “That is not standard procedure.”

“No,” he agreed. “Standard procedure would pursue the full account, levy the full penalty, and place a man who arrived at crime slowly and by grief into a situation that would ensure he arrived at it again more quickly and by habit. I prefer to prefer a different outcome.”

He said this with no particular feeling — not as a confession, not as a philosophy, but in the same tone in which he had told me the cedar was never moved. It was simply the thing that was true.

I have recorded it here because I believe it is as much a part of the case as the ruts that were not in the ground before the warehouse door.

I am Paser, junior translator, Palace Office of Memphis. I have set down this account in the third month of my second year of service. Should these records be recovered and read by those who come after, I ask only that they be read in the spirit in which they were written — not as the account of great events, but as the record of a particular mind at work, which seemed to me, even then, something worth preserving.
