NABI-ILUM

~1840 BC — Memphis, Egypt

The Ruts of the Northern Quay

Translated from the Hieratic script, as set down by Paser

I.

The document that started everything was on my table for two days before I understood what I had been looking at.

I set this down first, before the events, because the critique will eventually arrive — if not from the Chief Scribe then from my own conscience — and I wish the record to be honest: I had the Babylonian bill of lading in my hands. I translated it. I noticed nothing. Or rather, I noticed something and did not know what I had noticed, which is a more uncomfortable kind of failure because it sits in the memory and does not resolve.

The tablet was cedar-shipment documentation from the house of Warad-Sin, recording a consignment of twenty hundred-deben weights of timber, shipped from the Levantine coast to Memphis, consigned to the merchant Djedhor for storage and sale. I had translated dozens of such documents. I read this one in the usual way — meaning, I attended to what the symbols said rather than to the surface that carried them — and I rendered it into the palace register accurately.

What I did not attend to was the texture of the clay near the quantity notation.

A bill of lading is a flat document, and clay is clay. But this clay, in a band across the lower third of the tablet where the quantity figures sat, had a quality I had not encountered before and did not have a word for. Not rougher than the rest. Not smoother. Something between — a surface that had been worked twice, that had dried once and been re-wetted and worked again, and carried in the grain of it the memory of both states. I noticed this the way one notices a draught without immediately looking for the open window. I set the tablet down. I completed my translation. I went home.

If I had known what I was looking at, matters might have moved faster. But then, as Nabi-ilum would observe later, it is not the function of the translator to know what they are looking at. That is a different profession.

II.

The threat from Babylon arrived on the morning of the third day of the month of Thoth.

Not the indirect kind — not the raised eyebrow across a customs table, not the carefully worded complaint filed through the palace secretariat. A factor of the house of Warad-Sin appeared in person at the office of the Harbour Master, stated that twenty hundred-deben weights of cedar belonging to his principals were unaccounted for, and informed Amenhotep-sa-Hapu that if a satisfactory accounting was not provided before the setting of the sun on the fifth day of the month — two days hence — Warad-Sin's house would petition the palace for a full audit of every import document passing through Memphis for the preceding season.

I will record what that meant, for those who read this in years to come when the politics of it have cooled: Amenhotep-sa-Hapu was not a timid man, but he had a wife, three daughters approaching marriageable age, and a position that depended on the confidence of men above him who were considerably less patient than he was. A full palace audit, finding irregularities in the northern quarter's records — even irregularities he had not caused and could not have prevented — would end his career as certainly as a formal accusation. I watched the colour leave his face.

This was the same morning that the household steward of Djedhor the merchant arrived to declare that his master had drowned.

No body was produced. The steward explained, with the fluency of a man who had rehearsed, that the current had taken it south. Amenhotep noted this, dispatched river-men to search the eastern bank, and sent word to the office of the Foreign Assessor. The factor of Warad-Sin was still in the building, seated in the anteroom, watching the door of Amenhotep's office with the patience of a creditor who has learned that patience is itself a form of pressure.

Two days remaining. A dead merchant, a vanished cedar shipment, and a Babylonian creditor watching from the corridor.

Nabi-ilum arrived before I did, though he had been sent for after me.

III.

I should describe what I found when I entered, because the image has not left me.

The factor of Warad-Sin was still in the anteroom — a compact man from the eastern cities, dressed in merchant's wool despite the heat, who had the specific stillness of a man accustomed to waiting in rooms where decisions were being made without him. He had been waiting there for the better part of the morning.

Nabi-ilum was crouched before him.

Not kneeling, not sitting — crouched, on his heels, at the level of the factor's knees, examining something at the man's feet with the focused attention that I would come to know as his working state. He was perhaps forty years old, lean in the manner of someone who forgets to eat when his attention is elsewhere, with the deep-set eyes common to the eastern families. He had come south from Babylon with a trading caravan some fifteen years prior, married a woman of the Egyptian administrative class, and stayed. His robe was Egyptian linen cut in a slightly foreign fashion — the material of his adopted country, the cut of his birth one. The factor appeared uncertain whether to be offended. Nabi-ilum appeared entirely indifferent to the question. He was looking at the man's sandals.

He stood when I entered — abruptly, that characteristic snapping-to of a man whose thoughts have resolved and who is ready to move — and said: "The factor has come overland from the northern Levantine route in the last six weeks. He arrived in Memphis three days ago — not this morning, which is what his account implies. He has been here long enough to know considerably more about Djedhor's situation than he has told Amenhotep." He looked at the factor. "Shall I tell you which village between here and the eastern border, or would you prefer to add that to your statement voluntarily?"

The factor looked at Nabi-ilum for a long moment. Then he said, in Babylonian-accented Egyptian, "The house of Warad-Sin will have its accounting."

"You will have it by tomorrow's second hour," Nabi-ilum said. "Or you will have nothing, because whatever Djedhor has done with your cedar will have had two more days to vanish further."

He turned to me without waiting for a response. His eyes noted the ink on my fingers, the palace cut of my kilt, the elevation of my left shoulder from years at a low writing surface — assembled, I believe, in the time required to draw one breath.

"Paser. You have the registers."

"I brought them as a matter of—"

"The Babylonian bill of lading. Unroll it now, before we go in."

I unrolled it. He took it from me and held it in the light from the doorway, not reading it but examining the surface, tilting it slowly.

"You translated this," he said.

"Twice."

"Did you notice the texture change here?" He pointed to the lower third without touching. "In the band across the quantity figures."

I looked. And I remembered, then, the draught without the open window — the quality I had noticed and set aside, that I had not had a word for. My face must have shown it, because he said:

"You noticed it. You did not know what you had noticed." He rolled the tablet back into my carrying-cloth. "That is what we are here to identify. Come."

IV.

He was still moving.

That is what I did not fully comprehend at first — that the stillness I had attributed to him, crouching before the factor, was not his natural state but a temporary arrest, the stillness of a hawk between wingbeats. Inside Amenhotep's office, he did not sit. He circled the room once while listening to the Harbour Master's summary of the situation, then positioned himself at the window that overlooked the quay, then moved to examine a shipping register on the shelf, then returned to the window — all while appearing to attend perfectly to what was being said. His fingers tapped a sequence against his thigh throughout: three rapid, two slow, three rapid. A private rhythm, the outward sign of an internal machinery that did not stop.

The steward had already given his account and been dismissed. I watched Nabi-ilum turn from the window to me.

"He came from the south," he said. "The servants' gate, not the main approach. There is river-mud on his left sandal only — not his right, not his kilt hem, not his hands. A man who spent a night searching for a drowned master in the river-mud of the eastern bank would be soaked to the waist. A man who walked to the water's edge this morning, stood briefly, and came north again would be exactly as muddy as that steward. No more." He paused. "He has also recently eaten a meal of considerably better quality than a man in grief typically manages. There was sesame oil on his fingers. Fresh bread, not old."

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu said, carefully: "You observed all of this from the window?"

"I had nothing else to attend to. You were reading the steward's statement." He turned back to the room. "The cedar. Tell me the last entry in the northern quarter warehouse register."

What followed was brisk: the register produced, the entry located — forty-three days ago, twenty hundred-deben weights, received and sealed in Djedhor's northern warehouse, documented by the quarter assessor. No subsequent entry. Seal reportedly intact.

"Take us there," Nabi-ilum said. He was already at the door.

V.

At the warehouse, he stopped.

Not at the door — before it, in the earth before it, crouching with two fingers pressed lightly into the ground. This crouching, I was learning, was his instrument: the posture of a man reading a surface rather than a text.

"Both of you," he said. "Stand here. Look at the ground between the road and the threshold. Tell me what you observe."

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu looked. "Earth. The ordinary ground of the quayside."

"And what do you not observe?"

I looked. I thought of how I had noticed the tablet's texture without knowing what the noticing meant, and this time I looked for the absence rather than the presence. I thought about cedar and what cedar required.

"The wheel-ruts," I said. "Heavy timber on heavy carts. The inundation ended seven weeks ago — the ground was soft for six. If the cedar arrived forty-three days ago, the carts would have sunk deep. Those ruts should still be here."

He looked up at me — not with surprise, but with the expression of a man unexpectedly given a tool of higher quality than he anticipated.

"The ground has been worked over," he said. "Swept and tamped flat after the fact. Which means someone knew those ruts were evidence." He stood. "Now. Before I open this door — Amenhotep, what else does Djedhor store in the northern quarter?"

"Cedar, primarily. Some oil from the western—"

"His grain reserves. Where are those held?"

"He uses the southern compound for—"

"Then what is in this warehouse is not cedar." He looked at the sealed door. "Old grain, I would judge. Buffer stock — the kind held against the lean years, unremarkable in quantity, unremarkable in smell, placed here to fill a space and make the seal meaningful. The cedar was never here long enough for the ground to bear the full weight of its removal." He glanced at Amenhotep. "Open it."

The warehouse contained grain.

Old grain, the kind kept against lean years, settled into the corners with the patience of things undisturbed for months. The smell was the particular dusty sweetness of long storage — not cedar, not recently moved, not anything that had seen the inside of a cart since the previous year. There was no evidence that cedar had ever occupied this space.

Amenhotep-sa-Hapu stood in the doorway and said nothing for a long moment.

"You knew," he said at last to Nabi-ilum. "Before the door was open."

"I knew the cedar had been moved before the ruts could set. I knew the warehouse had to contain something to make the seal plausible. What grain tells me — over and above the fact that it has been here for months — is that Djedhor planned this with some care. He did not improvise. He prepared a substitute in advance." Nabi-ilum turned and walked back into the light. "Which means he also prepared an exit."

VI.

Outside, he sat on the low wall that edged the quayside — briefly, because sitting was not a state he maintained for long — and I spread the documents before him: the Babylonian bill of lading, the Egyptian register record, the northern quarter summaries.

He took the clay tablet and held it again in the direct light.

"Two scribes," he said. "The original notations here, the quantity figures added later by a different hand in re-wetted clay. You can see where the surface texture changes — a band across the lower third, here, where the clay was worked twice." He handed it to Amenhotep, then to me. "You have now both seen it. The quantity was altered after the original record was set. The cedar that arrived and was documented was a larger consignment than the bill now shows — which means the discrepancy between the two registers was manufactured, not accidental. A translator working from only one document would see a plausible number. A translator working both documents together, attending to the physical surface rather than the symbols, would find the seam."

I had the tablet in my hands. The texture change was unmistakable now that I knew what to find — the same thing I had noticed two days ago, had named to myself as an odd quality of the surface, and had set aside because I had not known what it meant.

"He shipped the cedar twice," Nabi-ilum said. He was on his feet again, moving. "The full consignment arrived forty-three days ago, was documented by the Egyptian office, transferred immediately to a vessel — before the ground had time to bear the full impression of loaded carts — and sent south. To Thebes, perhaps, or Abydos. There is a timber market at Abydos that handles northern stock; the quantities will appear in the southern shipping records. Djedhor then reported the cedar stolen, altering the Babylonian bill to show a smaller quantity he could plausibly claim to have received and lost. He would collect compensation from Warad-Sin's house for the theft of timber he had in fact already sold. He had already collected the proceeds of the second sale. And to prevent the subsequent accounting — when both sets of records were eventually compared — he arranged his own death."

A silence.

"Then there is no body," Amenhotep said.

"There is a steward with mud on one sandal who came from the south this morning." He paused, fingers tapping. "And a man who planned this over a period of months would not have planned only a single route of escape."

VII.

This is the part of the account I find most difficult to render accurately, because it moved quickly and I was frightened for most of it, which is not a state conducive to precise observation. I will do my best.

Nabi-ilum's first act was to obtain a boat — not an official vessel, which would require documentation and escort and the better part of an afternoon — but a working depet from a river-man he appeared, improbably, to know by name. While I was absorbing this, he produced from the carrying-case he always had with him a small quantity of natron and a worn-looking piece of linen, with which he proceeded to remove the ink from his fingertips with the methodical efficiency of a man performing a familiar operation. I watched him become, in approximately the time it takes to eat a meal, considerably less identifiable as a palace official. The foreign-cut robe helped.

"Djedhor knows the official apparatus," he said, scrubbing. "He prepared for the official apparatus — false papers, shaved head, a cousin willing to ask nothing. He did not prepare for an assessor and a translator who do not travel in columns and do not send notification ahead. That is the only advantage we have, and it lasts exactly as long as we travel quietly."

I said: "I should note, for the record, that I have not been trained for this kind of—"

"You have been trained to observe accurately and record precisely. Those are exactly the skills required." He looked at me. "And you noticed the wheel-ruts. Most trained officials would not have."

This was, I believe, a mistake. I accepted it in the spirit offered and got into the boat.

VIII.

We found the steward first, which was Nabi-ilum's design.

He had observed the direction of the man's approach that morning — southward, from the servants' gate — and reasoned that a steward who had visited his master once would have established a route he could repeat. We waited at the southern ferry-crossing as the light began to fail on the first day. The steward appeared at the horizon precisely as the sun reached the waterline, carrying a cloth bundle of the kind used to transport provisions.

He saw us at roughly the same moment we saw him.

He did not run immediately. He stood for a moment, looking at Nabi-ilum with the expression of a man recalculating. Then he turned toward the reed-bank, where a small papyrus skiff had been drawn up at the waterline.

Nabi-ilum went after him — not running, moving with the purposeful speed of a man who has calculated the distance and knows he will arrive first. He reached the skiff as the steward was pushing it into the shallows and caught the prow with both hands.

The steward was larger than Nabi-ilum and frightened, which in my experience produces either stillness or violence. This man chose violence. He struck Nabi-ilum across the shoulder with the cloth bundle — which contained, among the provisions, a ceramic oil-lamp of considerable solidity — and the blow put Nabi-ilum on one knee in the shallows.

I am aware that a junior translator, trained in the palace scribal tradition, is not equipped or expected to involve himself physically in the detention of suspects. I note this only so the record reflects that I was clear on the point before I stepped forward anyway. I placed myself between the steward and Nabi-ilum. I had my reed-case in my hand, which is not a weapon. The steward appeared to understand this and looked at me with an expression that suggested he was weighing options.

Nabi-ilum, from the shallows, said: "Your master has already been located. This crossing is closed. If you take that boat, the charge will be obstruction of a Harbour Assessor inquiry rather than accessory to fraud. The penalty difference is considerable. You have one moment to make the better choice."

The steward sat down on the bank.

Good. We had a guide.

But Nabi-ilum, standing, wringing river-water from his sleeve, looked at the bundle the steward had been carrying — now lying half-open on the bank — and then at the provisions spilling from it, and then at the steward with an expression I had not seen before.

"There are two sets of provisions here," he said. "Not one."

He crouched over the bundle, and I crouched beside him. Dates, bread, dried fish — enough for a journey. But divided into two portions, separately wrapped, as though packed for two people travelling independently to the same destination.

"Djedhor is not alone," Nabi-ilum said.

He stood and looked south down the river for a moment, and I watched his face go through something — a rapid interior calculation visible only in the stilling of the finger-tapping, which stopped entirely for the first time since I had met him.

"He has hired company," he said. "Local men, most likely — not from the merchant class, not men who can be traced through the administrative records. Paid for silence and presence. Which means the village at the end of this route is not simply a hiding place. It is a defended position." He looked at me. "The advantage we had — arriving without announcement — has a different quality now. We must think about how to use it."

I said: "I should note, again for the record, that I was not—"

"Trained for this. Yes. Neither was I, formally." He looked at the steward. "How many men?"

The steward was quiet.

"How many men," Nabi-ilum said again, with precisely the same inflection, which somehow made it feel less like a repetition and more like an arrival.

"Two," the steward said. "Nubian mercenaries. He hired them at the market in the south end of the city. He paid them well."

Nabi-ilum was already thinking. I could see it — that particular state of the machine running at full capacity, which manifested in him as an outward calm so complete that it looked, to anyone not watching carefully, like indifference.

"Then we do not go in," he said. "We make him come out."

IX.

This is where I must describe what Nabi-ilum did next, because it is the part of the account that I find most characteristic of him and most difficult to explain to anyone who did not see it.

He composed a letter.

From the steward's provisions, he took the writing equipment the man carried — a scribe's palette, ink, a strip of papyrus — and he sat on the bank of the river in the near-dark and wrote, in Egyptian, a letter from the factor of Warad-Sin's house to Djedhor by his false name, informing him that the investigation had concluded that the cedar had been taken by a second merchant operating out of the northern quarter, that no evidence connected Djedhor to the fraud, and that the Babylonian house therefore considered the matter closed. It went on to note that the house would, in the circumstances, be forwarding a sum to Djedhor's account as compensation for the disruption to his business, and would welcome his return to Memphis at his earliest convenience.

The letter was a work of considerable craft. Nabi-ilum wrote in the formal merchant's register, the precise conventions of which I had spent years learning, and he wrote it correctly — not a parody, not a near-approximation, but a document that, examined by anyone in the merchant trade, would pass without question. He sealed it with wax from the lamp, which he pressed with a Babylonian-style impression using his thumb ring.

"You cannot send that," I said.

He looked up. "Why not?"

"Because it is false. Because if Djedhor acts on it — returns to Memphis, presents himself at the harbour — he will be arrested on the basis of a deception."

"Yes."

"That is—" I stopped. I had been about to say not how it is done, but what I meant was something different, something that required more precision than that phrase provided. "A man who is deceived into walking into a trap has not chosen to be apprehended. He has been manipulated into a false position by an authority that used false information to—"

"To retrieve him from a defended position without bloodshed," Nabi-ilum said, "using only paper and ink rather than soldiers."

"It is still a deception."

"It is." He looked at me steadily. "And the alternative — bringing the Harbour Master's armed escort south to extract one man and two Nubian mercenaries from a farming village, with Warad-Sin's factor watching from the corridor and a palace audit on the horizon — will result in violence, an incident, and the kind of report that ends careers." He held up the letter. "This will result in Djedhor returning to Memphis of his own volition, the fraud documented, and the cedar traced to wherever it went. The Babylonian house gets its accounting. Amenhotep keeps his position. His daughters get their marriages."

I looked at him.

"It is not the procedure I was trained to follow," I said.

"No," he agreed. "The procedure you were trained to follow is designed for ordinary situations. This is not an ordinary situation." He looked at the letter. "I would note, Paser, that I am not asking you to send it. I am asking you to tell me whether the Egyptian merchant's register is correctly rendered."

This was either a genuine question about the accuracy of the document or a very sophisticated way of implicating me in its creation. Possibly both. I examined the letter.

"The seal impression is wrong," I said. "Warad-Sin's house uses a cylinder seal, not a thumb ring. The impression should be longer and more regular."

He handed me the lamp and the wax.

We sealed it properly.

X.

The letter reached Djedhor the following morning, carried by the river-man who had taken us south and who was — I understood now — one of Nabi-ilum's established network of useful people who existed outside the administrative apparatus and therefore outside the things that administrators prepared for.

Nabi-ilum and I waited upstream, out of sight of the village, in a position that allowed us to observe the point where the southern ferry-road met the river-bank. He had predicted, with the calm specificity that I was learning to find simultaneously reassuring and unnerving, that Djedhor would not depart immediately — that a careful man would wait half a day, send someone ahead to verify the route was clear, and move in the late afternoon when the river traffic was heaviest and a single depet would attract least attention.

He was off by approximately one hour.

The two Nubian mercenaries appeared first, moving north along the bank road at a pace that was not quite a march but was too purposeful for a stroll. They were large men in the northern style of dress, carrying the kind of equipment that is not technically a weapon until the moment it is. Nabi-ilum watched them pass from behind the reed-bank without speaking.

"They are going ahead to clear the route," he said. "Which means Djedhor follows in approximately—"

Djedhor came around the bend in the river road at that moment, moving quickly, alone, wearing the clothes of a farmer and the gait of a man who has been briefly convinced that he is safe and is now moving before the conviction fades. He was thinner than his household records suggested, and he had indeed shaved his head, and he was carrying a single bag that contained, I imagined, everything he planned to return to Memphis with.

He had not planned on Nabi-ilum.

Nabi-ilum stepped out of the reeds onto the road. Djedhor stopped.

What followed had none of the dramatics I had been, I confess, half-expecting. There was no sickle. There was no physical resistance. There was a man standing in a road who had been running for three days on borrowed certainty, and another man standing ten feet away who had simply, quietly, placed himself in the only direction that remained.

Djedhor looked at Nabi-ilum for a long moment.

"You wrote the letter," he said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"The seal is wrong. Warad-Sin's house uses a cylinder."

"The impression was an approximation. You knew it was false before you left the village."

A long pause.

"Then why did I come?"

Nabi-ilum said nothing, and I understood — standing there in the heat of the afternoon with the river moving behind us and the mercenaries now well up the road and out of sight — that this was the truest answer he could give, because the answer was not his to provide. Djedhor had come because he was exhausted. Because a man who has been maintaining a false self for months and is offered even the ghost of permission to stop will sometimes take it, even knowing it for what it is.

Djedhor sat down on the bank. He did not speak for a while.

"My wife died two years ago," he said, eventually, to no one in particular. "The cedar trade had been failing for three seasons before that."

"I know," Nabi-ilum said.

XI.

We returned to Memphis by river, Djedhor in the bow of the depet, the steward following under the river-man's watch on a second vessel.

The mercenaries were, as Nabi-ilum had anticipated, a different matter — he had sent word to Amenhotep before we left, and the Harbour Master's office had men waiting at the northern ferry-crossing by the time they arrived. This, too, was Nabi-ilum's design: he had composed a second message at the same time as the first, which I had not known about, and which had been carried north by a second river-man while the first went south. I mention this not to imply he concealed it deliberately — I think he simply did not consider it necessary to explain everything he was doing in real time, any more than a carpenter explains each stroke of the adze while working.

The journey took most of a night. Nabi-ilum sat in the stern and was, for the first time since I had known him, genuinely still. Not the stillness of thought — something closer to its cessation, the temporary quiet of a machine that has completed its work and not yet found the next task. His fingers were not tapping.

Before dawn, without preamble, he said: "The Abydos timber market will show the quantities. Djedhor will provide the full accounting — he is not a man who was born to this, and the relief of an ending will make him cooperative. Warad-Sin's house gets its accounting before the deadline."

"And Djedhor?"

A pause.

"The legal office will assess penalties against the proceeds. If he provides a full accounting — which he will — the matter of his fraudulent death can be quietly unrecorded. A man officially dead has already paid a significant social penalty."

I said: "That is not standard procedure."

"No," he agreed. "Standard procedure would pursue the full account, levy the full penalty, and place a man who arrived at crime slowly and by grief into circumstances that would ensure he arrived at it again, more quickly and by habit." He looked at the river. "I prefer a different outcome."

"And you decide this."

He looked at me.

"Someone has to," he said. "The procedure does not decide. The procedure is a record of what has been decided in situations that resemble this one. When the situation does not resemble the ones the procedure was designed for — when the man is not a criminal by nature and the damage is recoverable — someone must decide. I happen to be the one who is here."

I considered this.

"In Egypt," I said, "the law is the expression of Ma'at. It is not for one man to set aside at his preference."

"In Babylon," he said, "we say that the law was made to prevent injustice. When the law creates the injustice it was made to prevent, a man of good judgment is obliged to think carefully." He paused. "I am not setting the law aside. I am using the law — the provisions of the law — with discretion. There is a difference. You may choose whether you consider it sufficient."

The river moved around us. The first grey of dawn was beginning at the horizon.

I considered it for what remained of the night.

"I consider it sufficient," I said.

He did not respond. But the finger-tapping resumed, slowly — which I had begun to understand was his version of acknowledgment.

XII.

What followed is recorded in the official register of the Harbour Master's office: Resolution of fraudulent loss-claim, northern warehouses. Cedar shipment recovered and returned to principals. Duties remitted and penalties assessed. Matter closed.

The official record does not mention Nabi-ilum. It credits the Harbour Assessor's office, which filed the appropriate papyri. The factor of Warad-Sin received his accounting before the second hour of the fifth day and departed without further complaint. Amenhotep-sa-Hapu's position was secure. His daughters' prospects were unaffected.

When I mentioned the omission of Nabi-ilum's name to him — awkwardly, unsure whether I was offering comfort or registering a protest — he looked at me with the patience he reserved for questions whose answers were, to him, obvious.

"The cedar was found," he said. "The fraud was documented. Warad-Sin's house has its accounting. Djedhor has a second life he did not expect. What more needs to be true?"

I did not have an answer.

I am Paser, junior translator, Palace Office of Memphis. I set down this account without instruction and against convention, in the fourth month of my second year of service, because I believe it is the kind of record that the official apparatus cannot make and therefore will not.

The official register records what was resolved. This record attempts to preserve how — and by what quality of mind, operating under what pressures, making what choices.

I was present. I observed. I was, on at least one occasion, more useful than a junior translator has any right to expect to be, and on at least one other occasion, more frightened. Both seem worth recording.

I noticed the texture of the clay and did not know what I had noticed. Nabi-ilum knew what it was, and knew that I had seen it, and used that — as he used everything available — as a tool.

That, it seems to me, is the whole of the method: to attend carefully to what is in front of you, to know what the absence of a thing means as well as its presence, and to use what you have rather than wait for what you wish you had.

I do not know whether I will encounter such a mind again in this life. I hope I have recorded this one accurately enough that those who come after will feel, in reading it, something of what it was to stand beside it — inadequate, alert, and occasionally, briefly, of some use.

These records are deposited with the palace archive under the responsibility of the translator Paser. Any subsequent reader who finds them irregular in form is referred to the events they describe, which were themselves considerably more irregular than the form.
