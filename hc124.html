WILLIAM SCOTT HOLMES

1819 — Yorkshire, England

He was four years old when his father died, and grew up under the sole formation of his French mother — a woman of considerable character who managed the reduced household at Hutton le Moors with an economy and intelligence that kept the family from outright ruin, and who ensured that her eldest son received an education rather than merely a name. Marie-Claude Holmes, née Vernet, was in many respects the most remarkable person of her generation in this chronicle: a woman of French intellectual formation managing an English estate on reduced income, raising three children alone on the edge of the Yorkshire Moors, maintaining the family’s social position through sheer force of competence, and communicating to her eldest son, in the years of his childhood and adolescence, a cast of mind that combined the French habit of systematic analysis with the English habit of attending to concrete particulars in a combination that would prove, in the generations following, extraordinarily productive. She told William, when he was old enough to be told, that his father had been a remarkable man and that the quality most worth inheriting from him was not his name or his intelligence, both of which were beyond William’s choice, but his patience — his refusal to conclude before the evidence permitted, and his willingness to remain in the uncomfortable position of not yet knowing when not yet knowing was, in fact, the accurate position.

The account that follows is drawn from the private journal of Dr. Elias Mercer, a physician practising in Harrogate and an associate of the Leeds scientific societies. Mercer was not formally attached to the Holmes family, but he encountered William Scott Holmes through the circumstances described below, and recorded his impressions with the careful detachment of a man accustomed to observing both bodies and minds. His journal entry is preserved in Mercer’s own hand, with later annotations added by his daughter, who maintained the archive after his death.

The Private Journal of Dr. Elias Mercer

Transcribed by his daughter, Miss Catherine Mercer, with such annotations as seemed necessary for the preservation of accuracy

The following pages concern events of the autumn of 1843, when I was thirty-one years of age and some four years established in my Harrogate practice. I have set them down with as much fidelity as my memory and my notes permit, omitting only those particulars which, upon reflection, seem calculated to cause distress to surviving members of the family concerned without advancing the reader's understanding of the principal matter. Whether I have judged correctly in this I cannot say. I record what happened. I leave the moral arithmetic to others.
— E.M.

The morning of the fourteenth of October presented itself to Harrogate in the manner of a creditor who has waited long enough — grey, insistent, and without any particular sympathy for those it had come to disturb. The fog had descended from the higher ground during the night and established itself with a thoroughness that suggested it had no intention of leaving before it had examined every corner of the town to its satisfaction. The lamps in my consulting room window gave back light only to themselves. The smell coming in beneath the door was the particular smell of the moors in October: peat and cold water and something ancient beneath both, a mineral patience that the heather had been breathing in and out for centuries without arriving at any particular conclusion.

I had been at my desk since six, completing notes on three cases I had attended the previous week, when my housekeeper brought in the message.

It had come by way of a boy from Skelton, who had ridden since before dawn and was now in the kitchen consuming everything she had put before him with the methodical efficiency of someone who had no intention of stopping until stopped by external force. The message itself was brief — it had been written in haste, the pen pressed too hard in places, the words running slightly downhill as if trying to outpace their own urgency.

Dr. Mercer — I am told you are a man of science and that you have some connection to the Leeds societies and their methods of analysis. I am the Constable at Skelton and I have a body on my hands that troubles me considerably. The Coroner will say accident. I am not satisfied with this. The dead man is Mr. Edmund Fallow, steward to Mr. George Alderton of Alderton Hall. He has been found on the low moor at the edge of the Alderton property, at the bottom of the ravine known locally as Grike's Drop. He has been there, from the condition of the body, I judge two nights. There is a great deal I do not understand. I should be grateful if you would come. I can offer you nothing except the problem itself, which is, I confess, a strange one.
Constable R. Parrish

I folded the letter. I looked at the fog against the window. I thought of my three uncompleted case notes, and of the fire, and of the coffee growing cold at my elbow.

I was ready to depart within twenty minutes.

The ride to Skelton took me across the lower edge of the moor by a road that had been laid down by someone who was either very optimistic about the willingness of the ground to remain firm or had simply ceased to care partway through the project and trusted to Providence for the remainder. The fog did not lift. If anything, it thickened as I climbed, until the horse was navigating more by inclination than by visibility, and the only sounds were the soft percussion of hooves on wet stone and, at intervals, the distant complaint of a curlew registering its dissatisfaction with the morning.

Skelton proved to be a village of the sort that the moors produce in some numbers — a cluster of stone buildings that had made their peace with permanence at the cost of having very little else to say for themselves. The constable's office was adjacent to the blacksmith's, which arrangement seemed entirely practical and perhaps reflected a considered view of human nature.

Constable Parrish was a substantial man in his middle forties, with the careful deliberate speech of someone who had learned early that words once released could not be recalled and had governed himself accordingly ever since. He shook my hand, offered no unnecessary preamble, and led me directly to the back room where the body of Edmund Fallow had been brought in from the moor.

"The Coroner was here yesterday," he said, pausing with his hand on the door. "He has already formed his opinion."

"Which is?"

"That Mr. Fallow, who was known to walk the lower moor in the evenings, walked too close to Grike's Drop in the dark, lost his footing, and fell. The ravine is some thirty feet at that point. The injuries are consistent with a fall, or so the Coroner says."

"You do not find this satisfactory."

Parrish opened the door. "I find it tidy," he said. "In my experience, Doctor, when a thing is very tidy, it is because someone has tidied it."

I had not expected, upon entering that room, to find anyone already present. There was a young man standing at the far end of the table, his back to us, his hands clasped behind him, his attention directed downward at the body with a concentration that was, in itself, somewhat extraordinary — not the concentration of a person examining something disturbing, but the concentration of a person reading, absorbing, cataloguing.

He did not turn when we entered. He did, after a moment, speak.

"The Coroner," he said, to the body rather than to us, "has been both thorough and wrong."

Parrish said nothing further. He simply indicated that I should proceed.

The man was William Scott Holmes.

I had, in my four years of practice, encountered many forms of observation, but nothing quite of this kind — not the mere noticing of facts, but their immediate arrangement into consequence. He spoke as though the world had already been indexed before he arrived in it, and his task was only to confirm the filing system.

The body of Edmund Fallow lay on the table with injuries consistent with a fall of significant height. The left hand, however, told another story.

"The fingernails," I said.

"The fingernails," Holmes agreed. "Specifically, what is beneath them."

Beneath them was moor soil — and beneath that, pale subsoil, inconsistent with the upper layer of the ground.

"He gripped something," I said.

"He gripped the lip of the ravine," Holmes replied. "He held, and then he released."

There was a silence in the room that did not feel empty, but occupied.

"Let go," said Parrish.

"Deliberately," Holmes said.

He then proceeded, without hesitation, to reconstruct the entirety of the act that had led to the death of Edmund Fallow — not as speculation, but as inference drawn from the residue of material fact: laudanum exposure, controlled deception, deliberate summoning, and the manipulation of perception through carefully constructed absence.

By the time he had finished, the Coroner's tidy explanation no longer remained intact in any useful form.

It was replaced by something more exacting.

What troubled me most was not the conclusion, but the method — the sense that every object in the room had already been assigned meaning before it was examined, and that discovery was merely the act of confirming what had already been arranged.

In the years that followed, I came to understand that this was not intuition, nor brilliance in the ordinary sense, but a discipline of attention — inherited, perhaps, from a line of minds trained to treat uncertainty not as disorder, but as incomplete accounting.

A note on biographies extant:
The most thorough prior account of the ancestors of Sherlock Holmes is William S. Baring-Gould’s Sherlock Holmes of Baker Street (1962), a work of careful scholarship that we acknowledge with respect.

It may be noted that Baring-Gould has named Sherlock’s father ‘Siger Holmes.’ He was working from family records that are now understood to have been deliberately altered — most likely during the Great Hiatus of 1891–94, and most likely by Sherlock Holmes himself, whose capacity for misdirection was, as Dr. Watson attests, without equal. The reasons for the alteration may never be fully known. The records now available are not in dispute.

Philip José Farmer’s Wold Newton framework is a separate matter: an acknowledged work of fiction, built around a real meteorite. The meteorite did indeed fall, however, the gift is much older.
