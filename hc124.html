WILLIAM SCOTT HOLMES

1819 — Yorkshire, England

He was four years old when his father died, and grew up under the sole formation of his French mother — a woman of considerable character who managed the reduced household at Hutton le Moors with an economy and intelligence that kept the family from outright ruin, and who ensured that her eldest son received an education rather than merely a name. Marie-Claude Holmes, née Vernet, was in many respects the most remarkable person of her generation in this chronicle: a woman of French intellectual formation managing an English estate on reduced income, raising three children alone on the edge of the Yorkshire Moors, maintaining the family’s social position through sheer force of competence, and communicating to her eldest son, in the years of his childhood and adolescence, a cast of mind that combined the French habit of systematic analysis with the English habit of attending to concrete particulars in a combination that would prove, in the generations following, extraordinarily productive. She told William, when he was old enough to be told, that his father had been a remarkable man and that the quality most worth inheriting from him was not his name or his intelligence, both of which were beyond William’s choice, but his patience — his refusal to conclude before the evidence permitted, and his willingness to remain in the uncomfortable position of not yet knowing when not yet knowing was, in fact, the accurate position.

The account that follows is drawn from the private journal of Dr. Elias Mercer, a physician practising in Harrogate and an associate of the Leeds scientific societies. Mercer was not formally attached to the Holmes family, but he encountered William Scott Holmes through the circumstances described below, and recorded his impressions with the careful detachment of a man accustomed to observing both bodies and minds. His journal entry is preserved in Mercer’s own hand, with later annotations added by his daughter, who maintained the archive after his death.

The Death of Edmund Fallow

An Account by Dr. Elias Mercer

Transcribed by his daughter, Miss Catherine Mercer, with such annotations as seemed necessary for the preservation of accuracy.

Author's Note:

The following pages concern events of the autumn of 1843, when I was thirty-one years of age and some four years established in my Harrogate practice. I have set them down with as much fidelity as my memory and my notes permit, omitting only those particulars which, upon reflection, seem calculated to cause distress to surviving members of the family concerned without advancing the reader's understanding of the principal matter.

Alderton Hall was, at the time of these events, considered among the most stable and long-established estates in the district. Its reputation was, in the precise sense of the word, impeccable. I mention this not as sentiment, but as the first necessary fact: the whole architecture of what followed was erected upon that reputation, and could not have stood without it.

Edmund Fallow, who served as steward to Mr. George Alderton, was a man of whom his neighbours said, with the particular emphasis that distinguishes observation from compliment, that he was incorruptible. I have met few people to whom that word could be applied without qualification. Fallow was one. It was, as I came to understand, the instrument of his destruction.

Whether I have judged correctly in what I have chosen to record I cannot say. I note, for the reader's consideration, that I did not fully understand what I had witnessed at Grike's Drop until Holmes spoke on the road back to Skelton — and that what he said was not, in its essential nature, an explanation. It was an epitaph.

I record what happened. I leave the moral arithmetic to others.

— E.M.

I.

The morning of the fourteenth of October presented itself to Harrogate in the manner of a creditor who has waited long enough — grey, insistent, and without sympathy for those it had come to disturb. The fog had come down from the higher ground during the night. The lamps in my consulting room window gave back light only to themselves.

I had been at my desk since six, completing notes on three cases I had attended the previous week, when my housekeeper brought in the message.

It had come by way of a boy from Skelton, who had ridden since before dawn and was now in the kitchen consuming everything she had put before him with the methodical efficiency of someone who had no intention of stopping until stopped by external force. The message itself was brief — written in haste, the pen pressed too hard in places, the words running slightly downhill as if trying to outpace their own urgency.

Dr. Mercer — I am told you are a man of science and that you have some connection to the Leeds societies and their methods of analysis. I am the Constable at Skelton and I have a body on my hands that troubles me considerably. The Coroner will say accident. I am not satisfied with this.

The dead man is Mr. Edmund Fallow, steward to Mr. George Alderton of Alderton Hall. He has been found on the low moor at the edge of the Alderton property, at the bottom of the ravine known locally as Grike's Drop. He has been there, from the condition of the body, I judge two nights. He was expected elsewhere that evening — a tenant matter at the Beckwith farm, which he did not keep — and no account has been given of what drew him to the moor instead.

There is a great deal I do not understand. I should be grateful if you would come. I can offer you nothing except the problem itself, which is, I confess, a strange one.

Constable R. Parrish

I folded the letter. I thought of my three uncompleted case notes, and of the fire, and of the coffee growing cold at my elbow.

I was ready to depart within twenty minutes.

II.

The ride to Skelton took me across the lower edge of the moor by a road that trusted to Providence for the final quarter-mile. The fog did not lift.

In Skelton itself, waiting for Parrish to conclude a matter with a farmer at his door, I spoke briefly with two or its villagers who had known Fallow. The picture that emerged was consistent. He was methodical. His habits were known because they did not vary without cause. He was not a man who walked the high moor in darkness without reason. If he had gone to Grike's Drop on the night of the twelfth, something had called him there.

What had called him, no one could say.

Constable Parrish was a substantial man in his middle forties, with the careful deliberate speech of someone who had learned early that words once released could not be recalled. He shook my hand, offered no unnecessary preamble, and indicated we should proceed.

"The Coroner was here yesterday," he said, as we walked. "He has already formed his opinion."

"Which is?"

"That Mr. Fallow walked too close to Grike's Drop in the dark, lost his footing, and fell. The ravine is some thirty feet at that point. The injuries are consistent with a fall, or so the Coroner says."

"You do not find this satisfactory."

Parrish opened the door to the back room. "I find it tidy," he said. "In my experience, Doctor, when a thing is very tidy, it is because someone has tidied it."

I had not expected to find anyone already present.

III.

There was a young man standing at the far end of the table, his back to us, his hands clasped behind him, his attention directed downward at the body with a concentration that was, in itself, somewhat extraordinary — not the concentration of a person examining something disturbing, but the concentration of a person reading, absorbing, cataloguing.

He did not turn when we entered. He did, after a moment, speak.

"The Coroner," he said, to the body rather than to us, "has been both thorough and wrong."

Parrish said nothing. He simply indicated that I should proceed.

The man was William Scott Holmes.

I approached the table and began my examination systematically, without preconception, attending first to what was present before considering what was absent. The injuries were consistent with a fall of significant height. The left temporal region showed trauma of a kind produced by impact with stone. The limbs had sustained fractures attributable to the same cause. Nothing, on initial survey, contradicted the Coroner's account.

I said so.

"Continue," said Holmes, without turning.

I continued. It was in examining the hands that I paused.

"The fingernails," I said.

"The fingernails," Holmes agreed.

Beneath them was moor soil — dark, peaty, of the kind found at the surface. Beneath that, worked deeper under the nail of the left index finger, a pale subsoil, of the kind found twelve or more inches below the surface, where the lip of a ravine, eroded and exposed, meets open air.

"He gripped something," I said.

"He gripped the lip of the ravine," Holmes replied. "After the fall began. He caught himself."

I looked at the hands again. The fingers showed no other significant abrasion. The palms were unmarked.

"He held," I said. "And then he fell."

"And then he released," Holmes said. There was a precision to the distinction that I felt, at the time, was perhaps excessive. I came to understand, before the day was out, that it was not.

I continued the examination. The boots showed minimal scuffing — inconsistent with the scrambling of a man who had lost his footing in darkness and panic. The clothing was orderly. There was no evidence of struggle, no tearing, no displacement consistent with violent contact.

I noted also, in the condition of the eyes and the slight dryness of the mucous membranes, something that I hesitated to name, but named nonetheless.

"Laudanum," I said. "Or a preparation containing it. Not immediately before death — the presentation suggests several hours prior."

Holmes turned for the first time and looked at me directly.

"When, Doctor," he said. "That is the question worth asking. Not what, and not how much. When."

He said nothing further for a moment. Then:

"He did not fall by accident. He was not pushed. He did not intend to die when he arrived at that place. We have, therefore, a considerable problem."

He picked up his coat from the chair where he had set it and moved toward the door. "I should like to see the ravine."

IV.

The path to Grike's Drop ran east from the village along a sheep-track that had achieved the dignity of an established route without quite achieving the dignity of a road. The ground rose in a way that concealed the ravine entirely until one was within thirty yards of it. Walking that path in daylight, I understood readily enough how a man might come to the edge without anticipating it — if his judgment were impaired.

Holmes walked ahead of us, his attention on the ground, pausing at intervals in a manner that suggested he was reading something the rest of us could not see.

At the lip of the drop he crouched and examined the earth. Parrish and I waited. Below us the ravine was invisible, its depth implied by the sound of water somewhere in the darkness beneath.

Holmes stood.

"Two sets of footprints," he said. "The first is Fallow's. The stride is consistent with a man walking at a normal pace — not rushing, not stumbling. He came to this spot under his own volition." He indicated a line of impressions leading directly to the edge. "The second set." He moved three feet to the left, where the ground showed the impression of a heavier boot, planted and still. "Someone waited here. Or arrived with him and stood."

"Stood where he could not be seen from the path," Parrish said, quietly.

"Where he would be between Fallow and the way back to the Hall," Holmes replied. "There is no sign of struggle. No disturbed vegetation, no indication that either man moved under physical compulsion." He looked at the edge. "And here — here is where Fallow's hands were. He went over, caught himself, and held."

"And the other man?" I asked.

Holmes looked at the second set of prints. They had not moved.

"The other man stood where he was," he said. "And spoke."

He turned to look back along the path toward the Hall. The second set of prints led back in that direction, distinct and unhurried.

"Conversation, not violence, determined the outcome," Holmes said. "The question is what was said, and what made it sufficient." He began to walk. "I should like to call upon Mr. Alderton."

V.

The Hall presented itself through the fog with the composed solidity of a house that had nothing to apologise for. I had passed it on the road to Harrogate any number of times and had always taken it for exactly what it appeared to be.

George Alderton received us in his study — a well-proportioned room, the desk positioned to command the window, the accounts ledger open upon it with the easy confidence of a man who has nothing to conceal. Holmes noted the ledger. I noted Holmes noting it.

Alderton was a man of perhaps fifty, with the kind of face that composes itself for encounters without apparent effort. He expressed condolence for Fallow's death in terms that were measured and appropriate. He had no explanation, he said, for what had drawn his steward to the moor that evening. Fallow's habits were regular; the appointment at Beckwith farm was genuine. He could only suppose some errand of which he was unaware.

Holmes asked, in the course of a wide-ranging and apparently aimless conversation, about the household medicine chest. Alderton confirmed it was well-stocked. He suffered from sleeplessness — a hereditary complaint — and kept a preparation of his physician's recommending.

Holmes asked whether he might examine the preparation. Alderton, with the composure of a man who sees no reason to refuse a harmless request, produced a small brown bottle from the cabinet beside his desk.

Holmes uncorked it, applied it briefly to the back of his wrist, and handed it to me.

I recognised it at once. It was a compound tincture — laudanum combined with a preparation of belladonna, used in certain cases of nervous sleeplessness. It was not a formulation a man would self-administer in any quantity without awareness: the belladonna element, even in mild dose, produces a characteristic dryness of the mucous membranes and a slight irregularity of the pupils.

Those were precisely the signs I had noted in the body of Edmund Fallow.

The signs I had attributed, in my examination, to laudanum alone.

Holmes watched my face as I made this connection. He said nothing. He recorked the bottle, returned it to Alderton with a word of thanks, and moved on.

Two servants, called separately at Parrish's request, each independently reported that Fallow had seemed in excellent spirits in the days preceding his death. The agreement was precise, the language almost identical. Holmes, listening to the second account, permitted himself the very slight adjustment of expression that, I had already begun to learn, served him in place of other men's raised eyebrows.

It was in Fallow's rooms — adjacent to the Hall, neat to the point of austerity — that the morning changed.

The steward's accounts occupied three shelves and were maintained with the exactitude one would expect of a man whose professional character had been summarised by his neighbours in a single word. Holmes moved along the shelves methodically, drawing down volumes, opening them, replacing them. After some twenty minutes, he set one volume on the table and opened it at a page near the middle.

He said nothing. He simply stood aside so that I could see.

The page contained, in Fallow's careful hand, a column of figures entered under no heading and corresponding to no category that appeared elsewhere in the accounts. The dates ran back fourteen months. The amounts were consistent — regular, as though expected. They were not small.

"He kept a record," I said.

"He was constitutionally incapable of not keeping one," Holmes replied. "It was not in his nature to allow an injustice to go uncatalogued, even when cataloguing it was all he could do."

He replaced the volume and turned his attention to the coat hanging on the back of the door — the one Fallow had worn on the night of his death, returned with his effects from the constable's office. In the inner breast pocket he found a letter.

It was unsealed, unaddressed, and had never been sent. The handwriting was Fallow's.

Holmes read it without expression and handed it to me.

I shall not reproduce it in its entirety. It was not long. In it, Fallow set out, in the same precise language he applied to his accounts, the following: that fourteen months previously he had identified a systematic misappropriation of trust funds held by the estate — funds designated for dependents named in the late Mr. Alderton senior's settlement. That he had confronted George Alderton with this discovery. That Alderton had responded not with denial, but with a counter-proposition: that Fallow's own history contained one matter — minor in itself, sufficient in the wrong hands — which would, if disclosed, end his reputation and his livelihood. That the arrangement was simple: silence in exchange for silence. That Fallow had, for fourteen months, kept his side of an agreement he had never wished to make. That he was writing this because he could not let the account stand uncorrected, though he did not know what he intended to do with it, and suspected he would do nothing.

The letter ended without a conclusion, in the manner of a document whose author had run out of road.

Holmes folded it carefully and placed it in his coat.

"Now we have the arrangement," he said. "What remains is the sequence."

VI.

On the road back to the village, Holmes was silent for a time.

"The message," he said, at last.

"What message?" Parrish asked.

"Precisely," Holmes replied. "Something drew Fallow to the moor on the night of the twelfth. He abandoned a legitimate appointment to go there. The cause was not recorded in his rooms, was not mentioned by any person we have spoken to, and has not presented itself in any material form." He paused. "We are not looking for what was sent. We are looking for what should have been found and was not. A man does not walk to a ravine in October darkness without reason. The reason was given to him, and then removed."

Parrish considered this. "A message fabricated to draw him out. Destroyed after he left."

"Consistent with his duties," Holmes said. "Routine enough to raise no question. Gone before anyone thought to look for it." He glanced at Parrish. "I should like to know whether Mr. Alderton rode out that evening."

Parrish made inquiry of the stable hand before we reached the village. Alderton had ridden out on the evening of the twelfth and returned after dark. He had mentioned no such ride in his account of the evening.

Holmes received this without comment. He did not appear to need it in order to believe what he already knew. He appeared, rather, to have needed it in order to have the right to say it aloud.

VII.

We sat in the constable's office — Parrish behind his desk, myself in the chair beside the window, Holmes standing at the mantelpiece. The fire gave what warmth there was.

Holmes spoke sequentially, without ornament, each statement placed upon the one before it.

"Fourteen months ago, Edmund Fallow discovered that George Alderton had been systematically misappropriating trust funds — amounts significant, the mechanism careful, the practice almost certainly of several years' standing. He confronted Alderton. Alderton applied pressure: Fallow's history contained one vulnerability, minor in itself, sufficient in the wrong hands to end the career of a man whose livelihood was his reputation. The arrangement was silence for silence. Fallow kept it, and recorded it — in the accounts, in the letter. He could not speak, but he could not let the record stand unchallenged.

"The arrangement eventually became insufficient. Alderton fabricated a summons — routine in character, consistent with Fallow's duties — that drew him to the lower moor on the evening of the twelfth. The message was destroyed after Fallow left. Its absence is its own evidence.

"Earlier that day, in food or drink taken at the Hall, Alderton administered a preparation from his medicine chest — the compound tincture of laudanum and belladonna that Dr. Mercer has now examined. The dose was small. Insufficient to incapacitate. Sufficient to soften, by some degree, the judgment of a very precise man at a moment when precision mattered.

"Alderton rode to the moor. The ground records both men at the edge of Grike's Drop. There was no struggle. Fallow went too close, caught himself — the subsoil beneath his fingernails confirms it — and held. Something was said. He released."

Holmes paused for a moment.

"The blackmail is provable. The financial crime is provable. The fabricated summons is provable by its absence and by an unaccounted ride that the stable hand has confirmed. The laudanum-belladonna compound is identified, its effects are consistent with the post-mortem signs, and its source is Alderton's own cabinet. What was said at the edge of that ravine is not provable, and never will be. The law does not require it."

Parrish was already reaching for his coat.

VIII.

We returned to Alderton Hall in the early afternoon. The fog had thinned enough to give the house a sharper outline, and Alderton received us a second time with the composure of a man who has considered his position and determined that he is safe. He had assumed, I judged, that our first visit had exhausted what could be found. He was accustomed to assuming correctly.

Holmes sat. He did not pace, did not stand over the man, did not employ any of the theatrical apparatus that a less disciplined intelligence might have reached for. He addressed the facts as though Alderton's guilt were a matter already settled that required only the formality of being spoken.

"Your steward kept records, Mr. Alderton. It was his nature, and I believe you understood that nature less precisely than you supposed. He recorded the financial irregularities in his accounts and the coercion in a letter found in the coat he wore on the night of his death. Both are now in the possession of Constable Parrish."

Alderton said something — a redirection, a qualification. Holmes did not argue with it.

"The ground at Grike's Drop preserves two sets of footprints. One is Fallow's. One is yours — your boots are not a common pattern, and I confirmed the impression at the ravine this morning. You were there. Your own account of the evening does not include a ride to the moor. Your stable hand's account does."

He set the brown bottle from the medicine cabinet on the desk. Alderton looked at it. His composure did not collapse, but it required visible effort for the first time.

"The compound in this bottle produces specific and identifiable signs in those who receive it — signs that Dr. Mercer observed and recorded during his examination of Edmund Fallow's body. There is no other source for it in this account."

Alderton looked at the bottle for a long moment. Then at Holmes. The expression on his face was not what I had anticipated: not fear, not defiance, but something closer to exhaustion, as though a weight had been placed on him that he had for some time been expecting and had grown very tired of anticipating.

He said, very quietly: "What do you intend?"

"I intend nothing," Holmes replied. "Constable Parrish, however, has both intention and authority."

Parrish stepped forward.

IX.

The proceedings that followed were sufficient. I was not required to give evidence, which told me something about how the matter was characterised in its formal aspect, and something about what the law was and was not, in the autumn of 1843, prepared to name. I have set that consideration aside. The law reached Alderton by the paths available to it, and I am satisfied, on the whole, that it reached him.

What has stayed with me is not the confrontation itself, which was quieter than I had anticipated, nor Alderton's expression when he understood that the bottle had been taken from the cabinet — that expression I have described and will not return to. What has stayed with me is Holmes, afterward, on the road back to Skelton, saying nothing for a considerable distance, and then remarking, apparently to the fog:

"He relied upon the man's integrity. That was his instrument and his miscalculation both. A less scrupulous steward would have taken the money and kept the secret. Fallow could do neither. Alderton chose his victim very carefully, and not carefully enough."

He said nothing further. We rode the remainder of the way in silence.

I have thought about that observation many times in the years since. I have not yet decided whether it constitutes an epitaph for Edmund Fallow, a verdict on George Alderton, or simply a statement of how the world is arranged, offered without particular feeling by a man who had seen too much of its arrangements to be surprised by any of them. Perhaps it is all three.

Fallow went to the edge of Grike's Drop knowing, I think, exactly what he was doing, and why. Whether that constitutes courage or its opposite I have never been able to determine. I leave the moral arithmetic, as I have said, to others. I find, at my present age, that I am no longer certain others have managed it either.

— E.M., 1871

A note on biographies extant:
The most thorough prior account of the ancestors of Sherlock Holmes is William S. Baring-Gould’s Sherlock Holmes of Baker Street (1962), a work of careful scholarship that we acknowledge with respect.

It may be noted that Baring-Gould has named Sherlock’s father ‘Siger Holmes.’ He was working from family records that are now understood to have been deliberately altered — most likely during the Great Hiatus of 1891–94, and most likely by Sherlock Holmes himself, whose capacity for misdirection was, as Dr. Watson attests, without equal. The reasons for the alteration may never be fully known. The records now available are not in dispute.

Philip José Farmer’s Wold Newton framework is a separate matter: an acknowledged work of fiction, built around a real meteorite. The meteorite did indeed fall, however, the gift is much older.
