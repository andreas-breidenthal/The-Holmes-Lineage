HOLKOS OF THE WESTERN SEA

~1090 BC — Sicily

The Affair Of The Poisoned Cup

Being the Account of Ilu-Baal, Son of Shipti-Baal, Scribe to the Merchants' Quarter of Tyre, Written in the Reign of Abibaal, from His Own Observation and Such Testimony as He Was Given Leave to Hear

I.

I had been on the water for two days when I understood, for the first time in my life, that I was going to die there.

Not die in the way that young men imagine when they stand on harbour walls and think grand thoughts about the sea — the heroic kind, with a storm and a prayer and something worth recording. The kind I mean is smaller and colder: the realisation, on a grey morning in the middle of the western sea, that the ship beneath me was not going where its manifest said it was going, and that the man responsible for this understood the sea in ways that I did not, and that I had become, without being consulted, a variable in someone else's calculation.

Holkos noticed that I had arrived at this understanding. He noticed most things. He was standing at the stern, one hand resting on the steering oar with the easy authority of a man who has been doing this for two decades, and he looked at me across the length of the ship with that particular gaze of his — not unkind, precisely, but entirely precise, the way a man looks at rigging when he is deciding whether it will hold — and then he returned his attention to the horizon, which told me that I had been assessed and filed and that whatever he had found in my face had not changed his calculations.

I was, I should explain, not a natural choice for such a voyage. I had been assigned to the Melqart's Threshing Floor by the merchant house of Abibaal as a recording scribe — a position that sounds more consequential than it is, involving clay tablets and cargo counts and the notation of anything worth preserving. I had spent my professional life in the upper rooms of counting houses in Tyre, learning port accounting from Nahar-Iddin, my predecessor, who had died of a fever the previous spring and left me his tablets, his chair, and, it appeared, his voyages. I had never been west of Cyprus. The western sea, Holkos had told me before we left, smelled different from the Great Green — a heaviness of salt and distance, as though the water itself were older and less tolerant of men upon it — and when I had pressed him to explain the difference, he had set down his cup and looked at the middle of my face and said only: The Great Green knows us. The western sea is still deciding. I had thought it was poetry. I was beginning to understand it was not.

He had been sailing these waters for twenty years without the loss of a ship. It was not luck. That was the sustained practice of a man who understood the difference between risk that could be assessed and risk that could not, and who refused, on principle, to accept the second kind. I had been told this by three separate people before we left Tyre, in tones ranging from admiration to envy, and I had recorded it in my tablets as a piece of professional biography and thought no further about what it might mean to be on a ship with such a man when the second kind of risk arrived.

It arrived on the morning of the second day out from Makhon.

II.

The Melqart's Threshing Floor was fifty feet of cedar and pine, twelve oars a side when they were needed, a single mast, and a crew of nine men plus Holkos and the pilot Barak. She carried copper ingots from the Sicilian interior and two sealed amphorae of wheat samples destined for examination by the merchant houses of Tyre. She was two weeks from home. The morning had broken grey, the sail drawing poorly on a wind that kept shifting two points south and two points back, and I was sitting in the bow with my tablets and my complete ignorance of the sea, watching the horizon and trying to look as though I had a function.

It was Yathon who found him — Yathon the oarsman, a compact, dark-faced man who had come aboard the morning of our sailing and who I had noticed because Holkos had spoken briefly to him before we cast off in the tone of a man confirming an arrangement rather than issuing instructions. Yathon found Barak at the stern, slumped against the steering oar, the colour of old wax, his hands trembling in a way that the motion of the ship did not explain.

The cry brought the crew. I followed, because I did not know what else to do, and because Holkos's movement toward a thing was always, I was learning, worth following.

What happened next is the moment I have returned to most often in the years since, and which I record here as precisely as my memory allows, because it was the first time I understood what Holkos actually was.

He did not kneel beside Barak. He did not touch him. He stood at the edge of the stern platform and looked at the pilot for perhaps ten heartbeats — at his hands, at his trunk, at his face, at the position of the goatskin water vessel beside him — and then he turned to the crew assembled behind him, and his expression had already closed into the particular stillness I would come to know, the stillness that meant the surface had been quieted over a great deal of motion underneath, and he said: "Go to your stations. All of you."

Gerashtart, the cargo master, a narrow, sincere man of perhaps fifty, said: "He has taken ill. We should —"

"He has not taken ill," Holkos said. Not loudly. In the tone of a man correcting a navigational error on a chart — precisely, without heat, without the implication that the error reflected on its maker. "He has been given something. Go to your stations."

The silence that followed had a particular quality. I watched the crew's faces and saw the thing that happens when a group of men simultaneously understand that the situation they are in is worse than they had believed. Gerashtart opened his mouth and then closed it. Yathon, who had found Barak and who I had expected to show some reaction, went still in a way that I noticed without yet knowing how to read. The others — the oarsmen, the deck hands — simply turned and went, because Holkos had told them to and because, I think, none of them wanted to be the last one standing there with the question of what had been given and who had given it hanging in the morning air.

Then Holkos turned to me.

"You are the scribe," he said.

I said that I was.

"Then stay." He looked back at Barak. "And observe this, carefully, because I will want it recorded."

He crouched beside the pilot — he had the particular economy of movement of a man who had spent twenty years on moving surfaces, never a gesture wasted — and he looked at Barak's hands and then at his face and then at the set of his trunk, and he said, without looking up: "The trembling is in the extremities only. The frame is still. This is not fever — fever shakes the whole body, because the whole body is at war. This is the trembling of a body that does not know what enemy it faces." He looked at Barak's eyes, briefly, and the pilot's gaze moved toward him with the slow, confused responsiveness of a man whose understanding is working at half its ordinary speed. "Reactive to light. Slow." He looked at the skin around Barak's mouth. "A particular tightness. These are not signs a physician reads from a scroll. They are signs a man reads from twenty years of moving cargo through places where men sometimes wish other men not to arrive."

He straightened. He lifted the goatskin by its neck and held it toward me.

"Tell me what you smell," he said.

I smelled it. Beneath the more immediate smell of water stored in hide, something sharp and bitter, like the kernels of certain stone-fruit that the physicians use carefully and in very small quantities.

"Correct," he said. He set it back in exactly the position in which he had found it. "Bitter almonds. The compound is derived from the eastern method — I encountered it in Byblos, where the physicians keep it under lock because the line between a sleeping dose and a final one is a matter of grains." He stood for a moment looking at the horizon. "Note further that the trembling has not worsened since Yathon found him. A compound designed to kill worsens without plateau. This one has reached its limit." He paused. "Someone wanted the pilot unable to function. Not dead."

"Not yet," I said, before I had thought about it.

He looked at me with something that might, in a more expressive man, have been approval. "Not yet. Though in fact not at all, I think. A dead pilot is a mystery. An ill pilot is a delay. The poisoner wants delay, not mystery. They want the ship without reliable navigation for a specific number of days." He looked at the horizon. "We are four days from a small harbour on the eastern coast of Sardinia, where I sometimes stop."

He said this in a particular tone that I would also come to know — the tone of a man who has just said something that matters more than it appears to.

"You do not intend to go there," I said.

"I have not yet decided," he said, and I would understand later that this was not the same thing as no.

III.

The mechanism by which Holkos had been removed from the stern at the critical hour was a small question about the cargo manifest. Before dawn, Gerashtart had woken him with a concern about the ingot count — a discrepancy he thought he had seen between the loading record and the manifest, a matter that had apparently troubled him since Makhon. Holkos had gone forward to examine the ingots. The question had no genuine answer he did not already know — the count was correct, the discrepancy a phantom — but it had kept him occupied for the better part of two hours, standing over copper in the bow with a man of narrow and entirely honest sincerity who had no idea he was being used.

Three men had had access to the stern platform in the hours before Barak's collapse. Yathon the oarsman, stationed there by Holkos's prior arrangement — for reasons I would understand only later. Gerashtart, whose path from bow to stern in checking the copper took him past the pilot's station. And the Sicilian interpreter the factor Hanno-Melqart had pressed upon Holkos as a condition of the copper trade — a man whose full name the tablets record as Ashtun-something, the second element resisting the Phoenician syllabary — who had spent the voyage sitting at the bow with his knees drawn up, watching the water with an expression that Holkos had described to me, on the second day out from Makhon, as interesting.

I had asked him what he meant by interesting.

"Most men who are planning nothing think about many things," he had said. "That man thinks about one thing. I cannot read its shape. I can read its weight."

I had written this in my tablets as an observation of character and thought nothing further of it. I thought a great deal about it now.

Holkos sent me to help move Barak into the shade of the sail. We gave him water from the common barrels — Holkos had already inspected these, he told me quietly, and found them clean — and wrapped him in sailcloth and left him to his body's slow work. Then Holkos did what I had not expected: he sent the remaining crew to their ordinary duties and sat beside Barak's wrapped form and appeared to do nothing.

I sat nearby and waited, because I had already learned that his stillness was performance and that the performance was worth watching.

After perhaps a quarter of an hour, he said, without looking at me: "Gerashtart asked his question sincerely. I could hear it — he is a man whose sincerity is structural, not ornamental. Someone introduced that concern into his mind during the previous day, in conversation that would have seemed entirely unremarkable. Someone who had been on the Makhon loading dock long enough to understand Gerashtart's character." He was quiet for a moment. "Yathon arrived the morning we sailed. I had arranged for him to spend the previous night in a waterfront establishment where a man I trust kept him occupied and well-supplied — because I had decided that a night watch at the stern required a man whose movements I could account for completely. Yathon found Barak because he was precisely where I had placed him. He administered nothing, because he had not been at the dock."

"Ashtun was at the dock for three days," I said.

"Ashtun was at the dock for three days," he agreed. "He had time to observe our arrangements, to understand which water source served the pilot, and to speak with Gerashtart about cargo in the tone of a man making ordinary conversation." He looked at the horizon. "The goatskin was contaminated before we left Makhon."

He said it all in the same flat, navigational register. I found myself thinking of what he had told me about the western sea — still deciding about men — and understanding that Holkos had been deciding too, since before we left, had been arranging pieces into positions based on something he had read in Ashtun's stillness before any of the rest of us had read anything.

"What will you do?" I said.

"I am going to read the current," he said, "by dropping something light into it. And then —" He paused. "Then I am going to arrange for the current to do my work."

I asked what he meant by that.

He did not answer immediately. He was looking past me, toward the open sea to the south-east, with an expression I had not seen before — not the processing-stillness, but something sharper and more outward. Then he said: "There is a vessel, two points off the starboard bow. It has held that position since mid-morning."

I looked. It was there — low, narrow, moving with the deliberate purpose of something that knew its destination.

"It is not fishing," I said.

"No," he said. "It is an escort. The consortium sent it to ensure delivery." He was quiet for a moment. "Which means the timeline is fixed. The escort expects to see this ship arrive at the eastern harbour in four days. If we do not arrive, they will know something has gone wrong. And when they know something has gone wrong —" He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to.

The cold that settled in me then was not the cold of the morning wind.

"We are between the poisoner and the escort," I said.

"Yes." He looked at the escort vessel for another moment. "But they do not know yet that I know either of them is there. That," he said, "is the only advantage I intend to require."

IV.

What followed, I witnessed directly.

He went to Gerashtart first, while they stood together at the ingots. He mentioned, in the tone of a man sharing a minor change of plan, that he had decided to put in at a different Sardinian harbour than usual — one on the western coast, which he named with easy confidence. He watched Gerashtart's face. I watched also, from a slight distance. It showed mild curiosity and nothing else.

He went to Yathon at midday. He said that Barak was speaking again and would probably be fit for the oar by tomorrow. This was not true. Yathon looked relieved — the loose, shoulder-deep relief that is not easily manufactured.

He went to Ashtun last.

He did not speak to him about harbours. He spoke about the copper — that the Makhon factor had told him the ingots were of unusually high purity, that the Tyrian merchants would pay a premium above the agreed price. He spoke of this in the tone of a man sharing good fortune, inviting the other man into his satisfaction.

Ashtun went still.

Not the comfortable stillness of a man receiving pleasant but irrelevant news. The particular, effortful stillness of a man recalculating beneath a surface he has deliberately quieted. Three heartbeats. Then he smiled and said that was good news.

Holkos thanked him for his help with the heading the previous evening and returned to the stern. When we were out of earshot, he said, without looking at me: "The copper's value matters to him because it determines what the consortium will pay him for the delivery. He had not accounted for a higher price. He needed a moment to recalculate his share." He put his hand on the steering oar and adjusted our heading by some fraction I could not calculate but which I understood was real. "He confirmed everything. Now I need him to act."

"How?" I said.

"Tonight," he said, "I am going to show him the trap he is already in."

V.

He corrected our heading by eleven degrees north before the sun moved another hand's breadth across the sky. He did not announce this. He simply stood at the steering oar and made the correction, with the easy, unhurried manner of a man adjusting for wind.

That night, he did not sleep. I know this because I also did not sleep — I lay in the bow and watched the stars and listened to the breathing of men around me and thought about the escort vessel and whether Holkos's plan was the kind that would still involve me being alive at the end of it. At the second watch, when the sky was clearest and the sea had settled into a dark, cold running, he went to Ashtun.

I positioned myself behind the main cargo chest, in the shadow between two stacked amphorae, where I could see the stern and not be seen from it. I am aware this resembles the behaviour of a frightened man. It was the behaviour of a frightened man. I record it faithfully.

"The stars tonight are clear," Holkos said to Ashtun. "I would welcome your company at the steering oar. You said you knew the Sicilian stars."

Ashtun said that he did.

"Then come. Barak is still unwell. A second opinion on the heading is worth having."

They stood together at the stern in the dark, the sea running cold around the hull, and I watched from the shadows. Holkos showed Ashtun the stars he was using. He described the heading. He spoke about the western sea in the manner of a man who has survived it long enough to love it with precision — not warmly, but with the exact, detailed attention that is its own form of devotion. He spoke for a while in this way.

Then he said, without changing his tone: "Look to the south-east."

Ashtun looked.

On the horizon, perhaps two miles distant, a light had appeared. A fire — not the steady, small light of a cooking fire, but a built-up signal fire, the kind that is intended to be seen across water. The escort vessel.

Signalling.

Not to us.

"They are marking position," Holkos said, "for the harbour. Telling the men on shore where we are, so they know how long to wait." He let Ashtun look at it for a moment. "The difficulty — for them — is that we are not where they believe we are. I corrected our heading eleven degrees north this afternoon. In four days, we will raise a Phoenician anchorage on the northern coast. The men in the eastern harbour will wait, and then they will stop waiting, and then they will wonder what became of their escort's information."

Ashtun said nothing. He was watching the signal fire.

"Barak is recovering," Holkos continued. "The dose was calculated for four days of incapacity. He has perhaps two of those remaining. The copper will reach Tyre. The wheat samples will reach Tyre." He turned, at last, and looked directly at Ashtun — the first time in the conversation he had done so. "You will reach Tyre."

The silence that followed had a different quality from the ones before it. I could see Ashtun's face in the firelight from the horizon. I could see the rapid, private calculation moving beneath it. He looked at the signal fire. He looked at Holkos. He looked, briefly, toward the bow — toward where I was — and in that moment, with a cold clarity that I remember with great precision, I understood what he was calculating.

He was calculating whether there were actions still available to him.

He moved.

Not toward Holkos — which I think Holkos had expected, because he did not move at all, which was itself a kind of movement. Ashtun moved toward the stern rail, and for one heartbeat I thought he meant to go overboard — to swim, to signal the escort in some way — but what he reached for was the goatskin. The contaminated goatskin, which Holkos had carefully replaced in exactly the position it had been found.

He had it in his hands before Holkos's voice came, perfectly level: "Yathon."

Yathon stepped out of the darkness at the port side of the stern — he had been standing there, I understood, since before the conversation began, placed by Holkos in the shadow of the sail with the same quiet, confirmatory instruction I had observed before we left Tyre — and he was not a large man but he moved with the economical speed of someone who had been waiting and who knew precisely what he was waiting for, and his hand closed on Ashtun's wrist, and the goatskin went nowhere.

Ashtun stood for a moment with Yathon's hand on his wrist and the signal fire on the horizon and Holkos in front of him.

He was not, I think, a man who frightened easily. But there is a particular kind of stillness that is the opposite of his calculating stillness — the stillness not of a man organising his options but of a man who has discovered that his options have been organised for him. That is what I saw in him then.

Holkos waited.

Then he said, quietly: "The goatskin is evidence. If it reaches the harbour-master in Tyre, what it tells him is unambiguous. The question is what Ilu-Baal records in the clay, and what the goatskin accompanies, and how those two things relate to each other when the merchant houses examine them." He did not look at Yathon. He looked at Ashtun. "Barak will give his testimony when he is well. He will describe what he remembers of his last cup of water and the smell of it and when he drank it. He will remember that Ashtun was nearby. These are fixed points. What is not yet fixed is the shape of the account that surrounds them."

Ashtun was looking at the signal fire.

"They will conclude," Holkos said, "that something went wrong. The question they cannot answer, without your account, is whether you are responsible for the failure or a victim of it. That ambiguity is the only thing I am offering you."

Yathon released his wrist.

Ashtun looked at the sea for a long time.

And then he spoke.

He spoke for the better part of an hour, there in the dark, with the signal fire slowly dying on the horizon and the crew asleep around us and Barak unconscious in his sailcloth. He named factors and captains. He described the mechanism: the interpreters placed with Phoenician merchants as conditions of trade, the targeting of water sources during loading, the harbours where cargo was transferred and manifests were adjusted. He had been part of this for two years. He had, he said, with the flat affect of a man recounting bookkeeping, been present for two other pilots who had been given the same compound.

"Were they recovered?" Holkos said.

Ashtun looked at the water.

"No," he said.

Holkos stood at the steering oar for a long moment after this, making no response. I watched him from the shadows and saw nothing move in his face, but I understood — having sat beside him now for two days, having watched him perform stillness over great internal motion — that something was moving.

Then he said: "You will take the bow watch. You will stay where Yathon can see you until we raise the northern anchorage. You will not approach the stern platform. If you attempt to communicate with the escort vessel by any means, the goatskin goes to the harbour-master with a full account, and Ilu-Baal's clay reflects what you did, not what you later provided." He paused. "If you do none of those things, Ilu-Baal's clay will reflect a man who assisted with navigation when the pilot was unwell."

Ashtun nodded. He went to the bow. He did not sleep.

Neither did Holkos.

VI.

The signal fire on the horizon burned for perhaps another hour, then went dark. The escort vessel held our course through the rest of the night and into the grey of dawn. Then, as the morning light strengthened and made visible the direction we were making for — north, clearly, and north of the heading that would bring us to the eastern harbour — it altered its course south and was eventually lost in the haze.

I let out a breath I had been holding, I think, since Ashtun's hand had closed on the goatskin.

Barak recovered on the fourth day. He was weak when he finally stood at the steering oar, but he knew the northern stars and he brought us into the Phoenician anchorage without incident. When Holkos told him, on that fourth day, what had happened during his sleep, he was very angry for about an hour. Then he said — and I include this because it is the most intelligent thing Barak ever said, and because Holkos's response to it illuminates something I believe is true — who would bother poisoning a pilot? Kill the navigator and the ship goes where the wind chooses. Poison the pilot and the navigator simply steers himself.

"He was entirely correct," Holkos told me afterward. "Someone who wanted the Threshing Floor did not understand what Barak actually did. They understood that pilots navigate, and they thought removing the pilot removed navigation. They could not see the difference between the function and the man. This is the limitation of people who have never sailed. They see the ship as a single object. It is not. It is a system of dependencies, and every dependency has a redundancy, and understanding those redundancies is the whole practice of keeping a ship afloat on the western sea."

He paused.

"Ashtun was an interpreter. He could make a Sicilian and a Phoenician believe they understood each other. But he had never sailed. He did not understand what he was trying to break."

VII.

The names that Ashtun provided were communicated to the council of the merchant houses in a meeting that I was not permitted to attend. The account had been submitted in advance, in my clay, and my presence was not required for what followed. The door-keeper told me, when I was admitted for the summary, that three of the five men present had emerged appearing to have made decisions they found uncomfortable.

Two Sardinian factors were subsequently dropped by the Tyrian houses without explanation.

One Phoenician captain who had been working the western routes for seven years retired abruptly to an estate above Sidon and was not thereafter seen in the harbour district.

Ashtun arrived in Tyre as a man who had helped with navigation. He was paid what he was owed. He took the earliest ship back toward Sicily. Whether the men he had named found him there is not recorded. Holkos gave no opinion on this, and when I asked, once, imprudently, whether it troubled him, he looked at me for a moment.

"He tried to take my ship," he said, "and rob my cargo, and leave my pilot to wake somewhere he would never have chosen. I gave him a future. What he does with it is his work, not mine."

He set down his cup.

"The sea has enough bodies in it already. I do not feel compelled to add to the number."

VIII.

I set down here, for those who will come after and read these marks in the clay and wonder what manner of man this was, an observation that is my own, which I offer not as fact but as the honest impression of a scribe who was on that ship — who smelled the bitter almonds in the goatskin, who crouched between two amphorae in the dark while a poisoner stood four feet away and calculated his remaining options, who watched Yathon's hand close on a wrist and felt, for the first time in his professional life, that he had witnessed something that no ledger of accounts could adequately contain.

Holkos was not a kind man in the way that kind men are kind. He did not comfort easily, or grieve visibly, or remember the names of the dockworkers' children, or give to the temple funds with any consistency. He would have made a poor priest and a worse husband and an impossible guest.

But he never, in the years I knew him, used a man beyond what that man had offered. He gave Ashtun a future because a future had been bargained and because Holkos kept his bargains the way he kept his cargo manifests — exactly, without sentiment, and without error. He kept Gerashtart from knowing what had been done through him, because Gerashtart's knowledge would have cost him something and gained him nothing. He did not tell Barak until Barak was strong enough for anger, because anger before strength is only suffering.

These were not kindnesses. They were the calculations of a man who understood that the western sea was long and the men who sailed it were few, and that you needed all of them to be as intact as possible for as long as possible, because you did not know yet which of them you would one day need.

He was, in this way, the most useful man I have ever known. And I have recorded him as faithfully as my skill allows, because I believe — though I cannot prove it, and I am a scribe and not a prophet — that the manner of thinking he practised is not particular to navigators, or to merchants, or to this century before the common reckoning. I believe it is particular to a kind of mind, and that such minds appear again, in different waters and different centuries, recognisable by the same qualities: the stillness over the motion, the patience before the moment, the absolute refusal to leave a variable unaccounted for.

If such a mind should read this — if the marks survive long enough to find it — I would say only: you are not alone in the archive.

In the year of his death, Holkos had not lost a ship.

I think that was the only achievement he considered worth recording.

These marks are true, as I understood what I observed.

The gods of the sea know the rest.

—Ilu-Baal, Son of Shipti-Baal, Tyre
