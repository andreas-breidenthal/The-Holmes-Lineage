HOLKOS OF THE WESTERN SEA

~1090 BC — Sicily

He was named for the sea he sailed toward — the western Mediterranean, which the Phoenicians were entering in this generation as merchants and pioneers, feeling their way along routes that were still being established and into waters that were still being learned. Holkos made the voyage to Sicily and back three times in his career, carrying Tyrian manufactured goods west and returning with Sicilian metals and grain, and each voyage was a sustained exercise in the assessment of unfamiliar peoples, unfamiliar currents, and unfamiliar risks.

The Sicilian trading was dangerous in ways that the eastern Mediterranean routes were not — the peoples of the western coast were less predictable, the politics of the island more complex, the distance from any Phoenician support more consequential if something went wrong. Holkos navigated these conditions with a prudence that his colleagues occasionally mistook for timidity and that was in fact the careful calculation of a man who understood the difference between risk that can be assessed and risk that cannot, and who refused, on principle, to accept the second kind. He survived twenty years of western voyaging without the loss of a ship. In the eleventh century BC, on those waters, that was remarkable.

The surviving account was recorded by Nahar-Iddin’s successor, a younger scribe named Ilu-Baal, who had been trained in both port accounting and coastal testimony. His record is more fragmentary than earlier tablets, and at times his interpretations intrude where his observations thin. Still, he preserves the sequence of events with sufficient clarity to reconstruct the reasoning that unfolded aboard Holkos’s vessel and in the Sicilian settlements.

The Affair Of The Poisoned Cup

Recorded by Ilu-Baal, Son of Shipti-Baal, Scribe to the Merchants' Quarter of Tyre, in the Reign of Abibaal, from the Testimony of Holkos the Navigator and Such of His Crew as Survived to Give It

The western sea has a smell that the Great Green does not. Holkos had told me this before I had ever left the sight of our own harbour walls — a heaviness of salt and distance, as though the water itself were older and less tolerant of men upon it. When I pressed him to explain the difference, he set down the cup of Cypriot wine he had been considering, looked at the middle of my face in that way he had, and said only: The Great Green knows us. The western sea is still deciding.

I record this not as poetry but as evidence of method. Holkos observed everything. He could not be made to stop.

It was the second day out from the Sicilian coast — from the Sicilian settlement — the Phoenician traders called it Makhon, the one good anchorage on that stretch of coast, where the factor Hanno-Melqart kept a trading house that smelled of fish oil and old disagreement — that Barak the pilot was found by the oarsman Yathon slumped against the steering oar with the colour of old wax and a trembling in his hands that the motion of the ship did not explain.

I was not there. I give you what Holkos told me, and what Ilu-Baal recorded, and where the two differ I will say so plainly.

The morning had broken grey. The sail was drawing poorly on a wind that kept shifting two points south and then two points back, which Holkos said later he had been watching with the part of his attention he kept permanently in reserve for such things. The galley was the Melqart’s Threshing Floor — fifty feet of cedar and pine, twelve oars a side when they were needed, a single mast, and a crew of nine men plus Holkos and Barak. She was loaded with copper ingots from the Sicilian interior and two sealed amphorae of wheat samples, which the merchants of Tyre wished to examine before committing to a larger purchase. Her home was Tyre. She was two weeks’ sailing from it, assuming the winds co-operated, which they were already indicating they did not intend to do.

Barak was a man of perhaps forty-five years, wide in the chest, narrow in the opinion. He had piloted for Holkos on two of the three Sicilian voyages and knew the western stars well enough to navigate by feel when the sky went dark — which it did, often, in those waters. He was not a man who fell ill. He had sailed through a storm off the Sardinian coast that had driven two other vessels onto rocks and emerged from it sunburned and contemptuous. He did not, in short, have the constitution of a man who succumbed to bad air and weak sleep.

He had every constitution of a man who had been given something.

Holkos saw this within moments of reaching him. I asked later how he knew so quickly, and he turned the question back on me with that particular patience he reserved for things he considered obvious.

“What did I tell you, Ilu-Baal, about the difference between a sick man and a poisoned man?”

“You told me nothing,” I said. “I was not there.”

“Then I will tell you now, and you will record it, and perhaps someone will benefit from it when neither of us is alive to say it again. A sick man’s body fights what is inside it. A poisoned man’s body is confused. It does not know what enemy it faces. The trembling in Barak’s hands was not the trembling of fever — fever shakes the whole frame, because the whole frame is at war. Barak’s hands trembled and his trunk was still. His eyes were reactive to light but slow — slower than fear alone makes them. The skin around his mouth had a particular tightness. These are not signs a physician reads from a scroll. They are signs a man reads from twenty years of carrying cargo through places where men sometimes wish other men not to arrive.”

He paused.

“Also,” he said, “there was the smell of the water in his cup.”

The pilot’s water was kept in a sealed goatskin at the stern platform. This was not unusual. Holkos maintained separate water supplies for different functions — navigational personnel drank from one source, oarsmen from the common barrels, and the cargo manifest was stored in a chest that smelled, permanently and inexplicably, of Sardinian juniper. This was not hygiene as the physicians discuss it. It was what Holkos called the management of variables.

“A sick pilot loses me a voyage,” he had once said to Hanno-Melqart, who had complained about this arrangement as an affectation. “A sick crew costs me a little time. I manage the variables in order of their consequence.”

The goatskin was the variable that mattered.

Three men had access to the stern platform in the hours before Barak’s collapse: Yathon the oarsman, who had been assigned to the stern quarter for the night watch; Gerashtart, the cargo master, whose manifest required him to check the copper ingots that were stowed amidships but whose path from the bow to the stern took him past the pilot’s station; and a man Ilu-Baal’s account names only as the Sicilian, a local interpreter the merchants at Makhon had pressed upon Holkos as a condition of the copper trade. His name was Ashtun-something — Ilu-Baal confesses he could not render the second element in any Phoenician syllabary that made sense — and he had spent the voyage thus far sitting at the bow with his knees drawn up, looking at the water with an expression of concentrated private thought that Holkos had found, from the beginning, interesting.

I asked Holkos what he meant by interesting.

“Most men who are planning nothing think about many things,” he said. “Ashtun thought about one thing. I could not read its shape, but I could read its weight.”

This, I confess, is where Ilu-Baal’s account grows thinner and Holkos’s reconstruction grows correspondingly more prominent. I will mark the boundary clearly and trust the reader to maintain their own judgment.

Holkos did not call all three men together and announce his suspicions. He later expressed something close to contempt for this approach, which he had apparently seen Hanno-Melqart attempt once in a dispute about missing silver and which had produced, in his words, a great deal of protestation and no information whatsoever.

Instead, he sat beside Barak and watched the wind for a while.

The trembling in the pilot’s hands had not worsened, which Holkos said was significant. A progressive poison — the kind derived from certain eastern plants, which he knew from a season spent trading in Byblos — worsened steadily. A poison designed to incapacitate rather than kill often reached a plateau. This was, he said, a distinction worth dwelling on.

Someone had wanted Barak unable to pilot. Not dead.

Not yet.

He sat with this. The wind shifted again. The sail luffed and Gerashtart, without being asked, moved to adjust the sheet, which told Holkos something about where Gerashtart’s attention was — on the ship, on the cargo, on the ordinary work of the voyage. He filed it.

Yathon, who had found Barak, was standing at the bow with his back to the action, which was either the posture of a man who had done something and wished not to see its consequences or the posture of a man who was frightened and did not know where to look. These, Holkos allowed, were genuinely difficult to distinguish.

Ashtun had not moved from his position. He was watching the water.

“You will want to know,” Holkos said to me, some weeks after this voyage, while we sat in the upper room of the merchant’s house off the Street of the Purple-Workers, “how I reasoned through it.”

I said that I wanted to know nothing more urgently.

“Then let us begin with what we can establish beyond argument and work outward from it.” He counted on his fingers, which was unusual — he rarely made such gestures, preferring to leave his reasoning invisible until it was complete, and I understood, watching him, that this was performance; that he was letting me see the machinery because he had decided I should record it, not because he needed the visual aid. “First: Barak’s goatskin was the only source of the compound. I smelled it. An odour of bitter almonds beneath the more prominent smell of water stored in hide — faint, but present. The common water barrels had no such smell. Therefore the administration was deliberate and targeted. Second: the plateau in Barak’s condition argued for a calculated dose. Someone had thought about this, not merely acted in rage. Third: we were two days out from Makhon, and our heading, if we maintained it, would bring us to a small natural harbour on the eastern coast of Sardinia — a stopping point I sometimes use — in perhaps four more days, depending on the wind. Fourth, and here we leave simple observation and enter inference: if the poisoner wanted Barak incapacitated, they wanted the ship without its pilot for some portion of those four days. The question I asked myself was, what changes when a ship has no pilot?”

I said: “The ship cannot be navigated reliably. It drifts, or it runs with the wind wherever the wind chooses.”

“It can be navigated,” he said, “by anyone who knows how. But it cannot be navigated without drawing attention to the fact of its navigator. Normally, Barak stands at the stern and the crew accepts his authority over our heading without question, because that is his function and they have no particular investment in where we point. But if Barak is gone —”

“Then whoever steers is announcing themselves.”

“Yes. And I was not on deck when Barak fell ill, which was also not accidental. I had been drawn forward by a question about the cargo manifest that Gerashtart had raised two hours before dawn — a question that, on examination, required no answer I did not already know, about an ingot-count that had been settled in Makhon. The question was a mechanism for placing me out of sight of the stern platform at the critical hour.” He paused. “Which tells us something about Gerashtart.”

I waited.

“It tells us that he was used,” Holkos said. “The question was planted with him. He asked it sincerely — I could hear that he asked it sincerely, because Gerashtart is a man of narrow sincerity, which is not altogether a bad quality. Someone had introduced the concern about the ingot-count into his mind during the previous day, in casual conversation. Someone who knew that Gerashtart’s sincerity would cause him to raise it at the first convenient opportunity. Someone who had therefore spoken to Gerashtart about cargo, plausibly and unremarkably, during the loading at Makhon.”

“Ashtun,” I said.

“Ashtun,” he said, “was at the Makhon loading dock for three days. Gerashtart was at the Makhon loading dock for three days. Yathon was not. Yathon arrived the morning we sailed. He had been drinking in a waterfront establishment the night before, which I know because I arranged for him to be bought several cups of wine by a man I trust, because I had already decided that a night watch at the stern required a man who was entirely my own and whose movements I could account for. Yathon found Barak because Yathon was meant to find Barak — he was at the stern specifically because I stationed him there — but Yathon administered nothing, because Yathon had not been in a position to arrange anything.”

The logic, laid out in this way, does seem elementary. I confess it did not seem so at the time.

Here is what Ashtun had arranged, as Holkos reconstructed it and as Ilu-Baal’s fragmentary account partly corroborates from what emerged later.

The Sicilian factor Hanno-Melqart was in debt to a consortium of traders from the Sardinian settlements — men whose precise nature I will not detail further than to say they had grown wealthy on the distinction between what manifests declared and what ships actually carried. They had a presence in the small harbour on Sardinia’s eastern coast. They had an expectation, which Ashtun had communicated to them, that the Melqart’s Threshing Floor would arrive without its experienced pilot, perhaps slightly off course, slightly confused — a ship in difficulty, which any reasonable harbour-master would take under his protection while he assessed what assistance was required.

The copper ingots would not have been on the manifest when the ship left Sardinia. Ashtun would have overseen their transfer. The wheat samples, which were irrelevant to the operation but valuable, would also have been retained. Holkos, it is implied in Ilu-Baal’s account, would probably not have been physically harmed. He would simply have found himself in an unfamiliar harbour with no cargo and a story that sounded, to any authority in Tyre, like negligence at best and collusion at worst.

The western sea.

Still deciding about men.

Holkos did not share any of this with his crew. He went to Ashtun at the bow and sat beside him and looked at the water for a while.

“You know the stars well?” he said.

Ashtun said that he knew the Sicilian stars.

“Good,” said Holkos. “Barak is unwell. I will need a second opinion on our heading when the sky clears tonight. Will you help me with this?”

Ashtun said that he would.

Holkos thanked him, and returned to the stern, and changed their heading by eleven degrees north.

This is the part of the account that Ilu-Baal preserves most completely, because three crew members were later able to speak to it, and their accounts are consistent where they overlap.

The wind had been the problem all morning. It worsened in the afternoon — not dangerously, not the Sardinian kind of worsening that pins a sail flat and turns the sea to hills — but steadily unhelpful, the kind of wind that requires constant small corrections and that exhausts a navigator through accumulation rather than crisis. Barak was wrapped in a sailcloth in the stern and given water from the common barrels, which Holkos had inspected personally, and he slept with the particular heaviness of a man whose body is doing work it has not been asked to do.

Holkos did not sleep.

He stood at the steering oar himself and watched the sky and calculated, with that portion of his brain that never entirely stopped, the relationship between their current speed, their revised heading, and the position of the Sardinian harbour relative to where they would now be making for instead. He was not making for Sardinia. He was making for a small Phoenician outpost on the coast north of Sardinia — not a proper settlement, barely more than a seasonal anchorage, but one where the harbour-master owed him a measure of professional respect and where the men waiting in the eastern harbour could not quickly follow.

The question was time.

Barak’s condition was not worsening, which was the calculation’s fixed point. A plateau dose meant the poisoner had expected the incapacitation to last until Sardinia. If Barak began to recover too early, the entire mechanism might be adjusted — but Holkos judged, from the smell of the goatskin and from what he knew of similar compounds encountered in ports he will not name, that recovery would be slow. Perhaps three more days of confusion and weakness. Perhaps four.

He had perhaps four days to manage a poisoner, a changed heading, and a crew who had not been told what was happening, before they reached safety.

On the morning of the second day after the poisoning, Holkos began to talk.

This was his method, he told me, for the cases in which he could not act directly — when he needed a man to reveal himself through small adjustments rather than confrontation. He called it reading the current by dropping something light into it, and I have tried since to understand what he meant and I think I do now: you learn more from how a man reacts to information than you learn from how he reacts to accusation, because accusation hardens the surface of a man and information flows beneath it.

He told Gerashtart, while checking the ingot count together, that he had decided to put into a Sardinian harbour he did not normally use — a different harbour, on the western coast, which he named with the casual confidence of a man who has simply reconsidered his route.

He watched Gerashtart’s face, and Gerashtart’s face showed nothing but mild curiosity about the change.

He told Yathon, while they shared dried fish at midday, that Barak was speaking again and would probably be fit for duty by tomorrow.

This was not true. He watched Yathon’s face. Yathon looked relieved. He filed this also.

He went to Ashtun and said nothing about harbours. He spoke, instead, about the copper. He mentioned that the factor at Makhon had told him the ingots were of unusually high purity and that the merchants in Tyre would pay a premium. He spoke of this in the tone of a man sharing good fortune. He watched Ashtun’s reaction.

Ashtun’s reaction was stillness. Not the comfortable stillness of a man receiving irrelevant information. The particular stillness of a man recalculating.

He did it for perhaps three heartbeats. Then he smiled and said that was good news. By which time Holkos had everything he needed.

I had expected, when Holkos described what happened next, a confrontation. An assembly of the crew. A revelation. Some equivalent of the merchant’s arbitration hall — accusation, defense, the structure of justice.

What happened was this:

Holkos went to Ashtun that evening and told him that the stars that night would be useful for practicing navigation — that he would welcome his company at the steering oar after the second watch, when the sky was clearest. Ashtun agreed. He had, after all, agreed to be useful with the stars. To refuse now would be conspicuous.

They stood together at the stern in the middle of the night with the sea running dark and cold around them and the stars, for once, entirely clear.

Holkos showed him the heading. He described the stars he was using. He spoke about the western sea in the way that a man speaks about something he loves with the particular care of someone who has survived it long enough to understand the terms. And then, without changing his tone, he said:

“The men in the Sardinian harbour will not find us. We are not going there. Barak is recovering. The copper will reach Tyre.”

The silence lasted.

“I would like to know,” Holkos said, “who sent you. Not what they paid you. Not what they promised. Who sent you, and how many ships they are doing this to, and how long it has been their practice.”

Ashtun looked at the water.

“And in exchange?” he said, eventually.

“In exchange, you arrive in Tyre,” said Holkos. “As a man who helped with navigation when the pilot was unwell. That is what Ilu-Baal’s predecessor will record, and that is what the harbour-master will hear. The men in the Sardinian harbour will conclude that something went wrong and that you were unable to communicate it. Whether they believe you is your problem. But it is a survivable problem, which is better than the alternative.”

He waited.

“The alternative being that he finds out what you did,” Ashtun said.

“The alternative,” said Holkos, “being that you try to correct your situation before we dock, and that you discover I have not slept since the morning Barak fell ill and that I do not intend to.”

Another silence, and this one had a different quality. Ilu-Baal’s account notes, at this point, simply that Ashtun spoke at length into the night about the men of the western consortium, and that what he said was of great use to Holkos and of considerable interest to the trading houses of Tyre upon their return. The specific names are absent from the tablet. Whether Ilu-Baal was told not to record them, or chose not to, or whether that portion of the clay was lost, I cannot say.

I suspect he was told.

The pilot recovered. It took four days rather than three, and he was weak at the steering oar when he finally stood there again, but he knew the northern stars and he brought them into the Phoenician anchorage without incident.

Holkos told Barak what had happened on the fourth day, when Barak was well enough to be angry about it.

“He was very angry,” Holkos told me. “For about an hour. Then he said — and I record this because it is the most intelligent thing Barak ever said — who would bother killing a pilot? Kill the navigator and the ship goes where the wind takes it. Kill the pilot and the navigator just steers himself.”

“What did you say?” I asked.

“I said he was entirely correct and I was grateful for the insight and that I intended to adjust my arrangements accordingly.” He picked up his wine cup. “Someone who wanted to take the Threshing Floor did not understand what Barak actually did. They understood that pilots navigate, and they thought removing the pilot removed navigation. They could not see the difference between the function and the man. This is the limitation of people who have never sailed. They see the ship as a single object. It is not. It is a system of dependencies, and every dependency has a redundancy, and understanding those redundancies is the whole practice of keeping a ship afloat on the western sea.”

He drank.

“Ashtun was an interpreter,” he said. “He could tell you what a Sicilian said and what a Phoenician said and make them think they understood each other. But he had never sailed. He did not understand what he was trying to break.”

The names that Ashtun provided — at least, the names that Holkos carried back to Tyre — were communicated to the council of the merchant houses in a meeting that Ilu-Baal was not permitted to attend, about which he records only that it lasted half a day and that three of the five men present appeared, when they emerged, to have made decisions they found uncomfortable.

Two Sardinian factors were subsequently dropped by the Tyrian houses without explanation.

One Phoenician captain who had been working the western routes for seven years retired abruptly to an estate in the hills above Sidon and was not seen thereafter in the harbour district.

Whether this constitutes justice in the sense that the temple priests intend by the word, I cannot say. Holkos did not use that word. He used the word correction, as a navigator uses it — a small adjustment to heading, made before the deviation becomes too large to recover from.

Ashtun, as promised, arrived in Tyre as a man who had helped with navigation. He was paid what he was owed for the interpreting work. He took a ship back toward Sicily at the earliest opportunity. Whether the men he had named found him there, or did not, is not recorded.

Holkos gave no opinion on this. I once, imprudently, asked him whether it troubled him.

He looked at me for a moment.

“He tried to take my ship,” he said, “and rob my cargo, and leave my pilot to wake up somewhere he would never have chosen to go. I gave him a future. What he does with it is his work, not mine.”

He set down the cup.

“The sea has enough bodies in it already,” he said. “I do not feel compelled to add to the number.”

I set down here, for those who will come after and who will read these marks in the clay and wonder what manner of man this was, the following observation, which is my own and which I offer not as fact but as the honest impression of a scribe who knew him across eleven years and three accounts:

Holkos was not a kind man in the way that kind men are kind. He did not comfort easily, or grieve visibly, or remember the names of the dockworkers’ children, or give to the temple funds with any consistency. He would have made a poor priest and a worse husband and an impossible guest.

But he never, in eleven years, used a man beyond what he offered. He gave Ashtun a future because a future had been bargained. He kept Gerashtart from knowing what had been done with him, because Gerashtart’s knowledge would have cost him something and gained him nothing. He did not tell Barak until Barak was strong enough for anger, because anger before strength is only suffering.

He made these calculations the way he made all his calculations: not out of warmth, but out of the understanding that the western sea was long and the men who sailed it were few, and that you needed all of them to be as intact as possible for as long as possible, because you did not know yet which of them you would one day need.

In the year of his death — of which I will write separately, if I am granted the years — he had not lost a ship.

I think this was the only achievement he considered worth recording.

These marks are true, as I understood what I observed. The gods of the sea know the rest.

—Ilu-Baal, Son of Shipti-Baal, Tyre
