FLAVIUS HOLCUS

~115 BC — Rome, Roman Republic

The Caecilii Case

From the private papers of Quintus Fabius Labeo

I

There is a particular quality to Roman silence in the hours before dawn. It is not the silence of peace — Rome is never at peace — but the silence of held breath, of a city listening to itself for the sound of boots on cobblestones. In the autumn of the year Sulla's proscription lists began to circulate, that silence had acquired a new texture: thick, almost physical, like wool pressed against the ears. The torches in the street shrines burned low and sulphurous. The Tiber smelled of things that had been put into it. Even the dogs, which are in ordinary times the vocal conscience of every Roman neighbourhood, had learned to be quiet.

It was in this silence that a man came to the door of the rooms above the fuller's shop.

II

The man was not my father-in-law. I should say at once that it was not Lucius Sertorius Cappa who came — Cappa was, at that moment, two days' ride north of Rome, at his estate near the Via Flaminia, ignorant of the fact that his name had been placed in preliminary circulation among Sulla's administrative council as a candidate for the following month's proscription list. I did not know this either, not yet. What I knew, at the seventh hour of the night, was that a slave had knocked at my lodgings with a message written in a hand I recognised: Come at once. Bring nothing that would embarrass you if searched.

Holcus.

I should explain, for any reader who comes to these papers without prior acquaintance, that Flavius Holcus was, at this period, the most accurate legal mind in Rome and the least prestigious address. He occupied a set of rooms above a fuller's shop near the Aventine — a location which I had always privately considered ill-suited to a man who had spent fifteen years in the eastern provinces watching the rule of law bend beneath the weight of whoever held the army, and who had returned to Rome with the cold attention of a man who understood, better than most of his colleagues, exactly how contingent the Republic's norms had always been. When I pointed out, once, that the fumes from below might discourage clients, he observed that the discouragement of casual clients was worth rather more than a prestigious address, and that the clients worth having would come regardless of the smell.

He was at his table when I arrived, in the focused stillness of a man who has set his mind at a problem and intends to extract its solution by the same method a surgeon employs on an interesting wound. Across from him, seated on the room's second chair with the rigid posture of a man suppressing acute distress, was a woman I did not immediately recognise.

When she turned, I placed her: Fabia Crispa, niece of Lucius Sertorius Cappa. A woman of perhaps five-and-twenty, with her uncle's sharp jaw and a quality of contained alarm that she was managing with rather more composure than the situation perhaps warranted.

"Sit down, Labeo," Holcus said, without looking up. "Signorina Crispa has told me that her uncle's name has been mentioned in preliminary council discussions concerning the coming list. She has also told me that the man who introduced the name to those discussions was one Publius Quinctius Balbus, a senior member of Sulla's administrative council, who had until recently been the beneficiary of your father-in-law's grain storage facilities near the Flaminian milestone. This arrangement has apparently soured. I have asked her to wait while I consider the correct approach."

He looked up then. The face he showed me was the attentive one — the one in which something in his eyes sharpened almost imperceptibly, the way the surface of a pool changes when a fish moves beneath it.

"There is also," he said, "the matter of Marcus Caecilius Varro."

III

I should say at the outset that I did not like Varro. This is a confession the reader may find surprising in what purports to be a record of justice, but Labeo has never seen the utility of omitting relevant facts, and my dislike of Varro is, as Holcus eventually demonstrated, directly relevant to the case. Varro was a man of the equestrian order who had made his fortune in the grain trade with Sicily and spent it purchasing the goodwill of men more powerful than himself. He was not brave. He was not honest. He was, however, exceptionally well-connected, and in Rome in the eighties, well-connected was frequently sufficient to substitute for every other virtue.

He had been found in his atrium before the seventh hour of the preceding night, face-down, a wound between his shoulder blades delivered with sufficient force to crack the fourth rib from above. His household slaves had been secured in the lower rooms. His wife, Caecilia Tertia — a woman of notably better sense than her husband, though she had thought fit not to exercise it when selecting him — had been at her father's house since the previous afternoon, as she subsequently testified before the magistrate, with some forty persons to confirm an alibi that was neither slave nor relation and therefore theoretically credible.

The Sullan functionary assigned to investigate — one Gaius Umbrenus, a man who had transferred his competence from the collection of debts to the collection of intelligence with a smoothness that suggested the disciplines were not, in his estimation, substantially different — had informed me the following morning that the matter was entirely clear. An attempted robbery. Varro had disturbed the intruder. The atrium window on the garden side showed signs of forcing. A slave had heard a sound consistent with running feet in the street outside shortly after the estimated hour of death.

"And Balbus," Holcus said now, picking up the thread, "was Varro's principal patron. Varro held warehouses on Sicily that Balbus used for the transit of certain goods whose precise nature does not appear in any official record I have been able to locate, but whose value to Balbus is suggested by the number of years he maintained the arrangement and the degree of goodwill he extended to Varro in return." He set down his stylus. "Varro is dead. Cappa's name is in circulation. Balbus is the point of intersection. I intend to understand why." He looked at Fabia Crispa with the brief, acknowledging attention of a man who has taken a commission. "You should go. Come back at the fifth hour tomorrow. I expect by then to have something definite to tell you."

She left with the erect composure of a woman who had used the last of her composure to get through the door, and had nothing in reserve for the street.

IV

Holcus sent me to Varro's house at first light with instructions precise enough to constitute an insult to my professional judgment, had I not, in three years of working beside him, come to understand that his instructions were precise because imprecision had cost him things he did not discuss.

"The window," he said. "The garden-side atrium window. Look at the latch. Look at the frame around the latch. Look at the sill on both sides. Tell me exactly what you see, not what you think it means. The thinking comes after the seeing."

The window in question was a narrow affair set into the garden wall of the atrium, hinged on the left, with an iron latch on the inside. The latch had been pushed back — forcibly, I assumed at first, until I looked more carefully. The wood around the latch showed fresh marks, bright against the older patina of the timber, three of them, parallel, consistent with the tip of a knife or a similar implement. This was what Umbrenus had seen, and it was what one was meant to see.

What Umbrenus had not apparently observed — or had not thought significant — was that the marks were on the inside of the frame.

I stood looking at this for rather a long time.

If the window had been forced from outside — as a genuine intruder would have forced it — the marks would be on the outside of the frame, where the implement would have been inserted from the garden. These marks were on the inside. The window had been made to look as though it had been forced from outside. It had, in fact, been opened from within.

There was one further detail. The sill was dusty — the fullish, fine dust of a Roman house in autumn, undisturbed by cleaning slaves who had not yet been released to their ordinary duties. The dust on the outer sill was unmarked. No foot had touched it. No body had come through this window from outside. Whatever had come through this window, if anything, had gone out.

I should note here what I observed next, because it was the detail Holcus later told me was the first competent independent contribution I had made in his presence — by which I understood him to mean that it was a contribution he had not prompted, and had not anticipated, and was therefore of genuine utility.

On the floor below the window, half beneath the edge of a low cedar chest, lay a small clay seal. I knew it by its device — a simplified rendering of a sheaf above a ship's prow, which I recognised as the commercial mark of the Sicilian grain factors who operated through the port at Lilybaeum. I had seen such seals on Varro's documents during the Caecilii matter, three years prior: contract tallies, receipts, letters of credit from the Sicilian operations. The seal had broken, recently — the clay was pale at the fracture, not yet darkened — and the cord through it had been cut, not snapped.

Someone had opened a sealed letter in this room, and the seal had fallen, and they had not stopped to retrieve it.

I wrapped it in a fold of my tunic and brought it to Holcus along with the rest of my observations.

V

He listened without moving. Holcus had a quality I have never seen in another advocate: he listened with his whole body, as though each word were a physical object being handed to him for examination. When I finished, he set down his stylus with the precise care of a man placing a stone in a particular spot for a particular reason.

"The window," he said first. Then, when I described the seal: he stood up — a thing he did rarely enough to be noticeable — and walked to his own window, which looked down onto the fuller's courtyard below, and was very quiet for what felt like an unusually extended time.

"Tell me," he said, "about the Sicilian seal."

I told him what I knew of it: the Lilybaeum factors, the grain operations, the fact that I had seen identical seals on Varro's documents in the Caecilii matter.

"And the Caecilii matter," he said, "involved a property dispute in which Varro's warehousing facilities were central to the claim." He was not asking. "Varro's Sicilian agent — a man named Sosias, I believe, based on the correspondence I reviewed at the time — was the individual whose records would have been most directly illuminating about the precise quantities of grain transiting through those warehouses, and about the identity of the parties on whose behalf certain shipments were made." He turned from the window. The morning light fell across his face at an angle that made his expression difficult to read — though I had learned by then that this was not entirely accidental. "Sosias is still in Lilybaeum. He is also, I suspect, the reason Varro was killed."

I waited.

"Not because Sosias acted," Holcus said. "Because Sosias exists, and possesses records, and can be reached by anyone who knows where to find him — including the enemies of the man whose shipments he has been facilitating for the past four years." He walked back to his table. "The seal that you found is from a letter Varro received recently from Lilybaeum. The letter was read in this room, before the meeting — or at the meeting, which is more suggestive. The visitor read it. The visitor understood what it meant. And the visitor decided that the safest course of action was to ensure Varro never had the opportunity to use it."

"The slave who heard the running feet," I said. "Southward. Toward the Circus."

"Yes. Not the flight of a panicked thief. A man who knew the route home before he arrived. Now. The slaves were secured below. Who gave that instruction?"

VI

The answer — obtained from Varro's steward, a Cappadocian freedman named Phormio who was in a state of agitation that made him considerably more forthcoming than he might otherwise have been — was that the slaves had been secured on Varro's own instruction, delivered approximately two hours before his death. Varro had told Phormio he was expecting a private visitor and did not wish the household present. He had sent Phormio to the lower rooms himself.

He had been expecting someone.

I returned to Holcus with this and found him in the other of his characteristic states — not the focused stillness of the morning but a different mode, in which he was pacing the length of his room with the rapid, uneven energy of a man who has ceased to think in words and is working in something faster. He barely paused when I came in.

"Sit. Talk."

I talked. He paced. When I finished, he stopped.

"The name of the visitor," he said. "Phormio did not know it?"

"Varro did not tell him. Simply instructed him to secure the household."

"Then Varro knew the meeting was not one he wished observed. He was either ashamed of the visitor or afraid of what the visitor might do if seen." He resumed pacing, more slowly. "Not afraid of the visitor himself, or he would not have met with him alone at night with the household removed. He trusted the visitor. He feared the knowledge of the meeting."

"Why?"

He stopped pacing. He turned and looked at me with the expression I had come to recognise as the one that preceded his making something obvious that had not, until that moment, been obvious to me.

"Because of what the meeting was about. Think about Varro specifically. What was his defining characteristic? Not his virtues — his mechanism."

"He purchased protection," I said. "He made himself useful to powerful men — money, trade access, warehousing — in exchange for their names between him and danger."

"Correct. Now: Sulla's proscriptions are coming. Everyone in Rome knows this. The lists will be published soon. What does Varro do?"

I considered. "He goes to one of his patrons — one of the men in Sulla's circle who will know the list's contents before publication — and he offers something. More money. Information. The Sicilian connection. In exchange for the assurance that his name will not appear."

"And his patron agrees. And then something changes." Holcus sat down. "The letter from Lilybaeum. Sosias has written to Varro — perhaps to report that someone has been asking questions about the Sicilian shipments. Perhaps to report that a representative of one of Sulla's opponents has made contact. In either case, the letter informs the patron, when he reads it in this atrium, that Varro has become more dangerous alive than dead. That Sosias exists, that Sosias has records, and that those records can be reached by Varro's enemies as easily as by Varro's friends. The patron has, in a single evening, both the motive and the letter in his hands as evidence of the risk. He makes a decision."

The room was very quiet. Below, someone dropped a heavy ceramic vessel on stone.

"He struck from behind," I said.

"A man Varro trusted sufficiently to stand behind him, at the seventh hour of the night, in a secured house. The wound between the shoulder blades — not the wound of a surprised householder, but a deliberate strike, placed with knowledge and force. He opened the window from inside to suggest exterior entry. He left southward, with composure — a man who had planned the route before he arrived." Holcus looked at me steadily. "The killer is one of Sulla's inner circle. He had access to the lists before publication. I believe he subsequently used that access to add Varro's name — so that Varro's death would appear to be merely the precursor to official condemnation, a man killed ahead of his proper fate rather than murdered to prevent disclosure."

"Then the list entry—"

"Was recorded after the body was found. Or I believe so. Umbrenus's records will show the precise hour of that entry. That verification," he said, "is your task, and it is a task suited to your particular knowledge."

It was the first time he had said so directly.

VII

I will record the significance of this, because Holcus was not a man who used flattery and rarely deployed precision without intention. He was right that the verification required specific knowledge: not merely the ability to read the secretariat's records, but the advocate's understanding of proscription procedure — of when, in the formal sequence, individual names were entered into the register, and which hours of the night that process was staffed, and what it meant for an entry to appear outside those hours.

It was, I confess, a problem I had not framed in those terms until he pointed me toward it. But it was a problem I could solve where he could not — the secretariat's clerks had no particular reason to speak candidly to a senior advocate, and considerably less reason to speak to a man who appeared to be examining their records for discrepancies. They had, however, been accepting professional courtesies from junior advocates for years, and I had three years' worth of courtesies extended that were not yet repaid.

Varro's name had been entered into the proscription register at the third hour of the morning following the discovery of the body. I knew, because I understood the procedure, that formal entries were made during a specific administrative window — the first hour to the second, when the relevant secretariat was staffed and witnessed. An entry at the third hour, unstaffed, unwitnessed, required someone with both authority and after-hours access.

The number of men who possessed both was, as Holcus had said, very limited.

I brought this to him before the second hour of the afternoon, and found Fabia Crispa already returned — earlier than he had instructed, which suggested that composure had its limits. Holcus was at his table, no longer writing, his stylus set down with the care of a man who has finished with a thing. He listened to my report of the secretariat records with his eyes on the middle distance and the stillness of a man who has just confirmed the last stone in an arch.

"Balbus," he said, when I finished. Not as a question.

He told us what he intended to do.

VIII

I should say, before I describe the meeting in Balbus's tablinum, that it was the most frightening hour of my professional life, and that I was frightened not because of what Holcus said, but because of what Balbus might do, and because I was at that point acutely aware of how thin the distinction was between a man conducting a private legal analysis and a man conducting an unofficial accusation against a member of Sulla's administrative council, and how rapidly and permanently the second category of man could find himself on a list.

Holcus had used the morning to write two documents. One was a letter to a legal colleague in Sicily — a man named Tullus who practised in Lilybaeum, and who had, I happened to know, a professional acquaintance with a grain factor's agent by the name of Sosias. The letter, Holcus informed me, contained a request that Sosias preserve and formally certify a specific set of transaction records relating to four years of shipments through Varro's warehouses, pending possible future proceedings. The letter had already been dispatched before I arrived.

The second document was a sealed copy of my account of the secretariat records, the broken Sicilian seal, and the observations from the atrium window. This document was not dispatched. It was entrusted to a man whose name Holcus did not share with me, with instructions I was not given, contingent on certain events.

"If we do not return from Balbus's house," Holcus said, with the pleasant tone of a man discussing arrangements for an outdoor dinner, "the document will reach the person for whom it is intended. If we return, it will be destroyed. In either case, you will find it useful to know it exists, because you will be able to indicate, if the question is raised, that certain records pertaining to the Varro matter are not held exclusively by the two of us."

I found this information useful in precisely the way he described, and alarming in every other respect.

IX

Balbus received us in the tablinum of a house that smelled of expensive Syrian cedar and the particular quality of lamp oil imported from Alexandria — not the common Italian pressing but the lighter, cleaner grade that costs three times as much and is used by men who wish to signal, through the medium of combustion, that they are not the sort of men who count costs. He was a man of late middle age, broad-shouldered, with hands that had once been calloused and were now merely strong. He had, I noticed, a freedman standing just inside the doorway behind us — a large man who had not been there when we entered and whose presence I registered as I would register the sound of a gate being latched.

Balbus looked at Holcus with the assessment of a man evaluating what something would cost him. Then he looked at me.

"You have something to tell me," he said. It was not a question.

"I have an analysis to share," Holcus said, "in the spirit of intellectual collegium, without any implication of official proceedings." His voice was entirely pleasant. "I have been examining the matter of Marcus Caecilius Varro, and I find myself in possession of certain observations — about a window latch whose marks were on the wrong side of the frame, about a broken seal from Lilybaeum found on the floor of the atrium, about the direction a man ran who knew his route home before he arrived, about the hour at which an entry appeared in the proscription register when the secretariat was not staffed." He paused. "I have also this morning written to a colleague in Sicily with instructions regarding the preservation of certain shipping records maintained by a factor's agent who has, I understand, kept meticulous accounts of four years' worth of warehouse transactions."

Balbus was very still.

"The letter," Holcus said, "has been dispatched. It cannot be recalled. Whatever response it receives, Sosias's records will now have been drawn to the formal attention of a legal officer in Lilybaeum, which means their destruction would constitute a separate offence — one that would require a degree of reach that I suspect even you would find inconvenient to exercise at this particular moment." He let this settle. "This is not a threat. I am describing the current state of affairs, which exists independently of anything you and I might discuss."

The freedman at the door had not moved. I was aware of him with the attention one reserves for walls that might not be structural.

Balbus turned his head, very slightly, toward the man at the door.

"If you are considering," Holcus said, in the same pleasant voice, "whether our absence from this house might simplify matters, I should mention that a sealed document recording my analysis of the Varro matter, including the specific observations I have described to you today, is currently in the possession of a third party, with instructions contingent on our return. Our failure to return would not simplify matters. It would complicate them considerably."

The quality of silence in the room changed. I cannot describe it more precisely than that. It was as though a sound that had been present — not quite a sound, more a potential — went out of the air.

Balbus looked at Holcus for a very long time.

"What," he said at last, "do you want?"

"Nothing that is not already owed." Holcus's voice had not changed by a fraction. "I want the Caecilii case — a property dispute I have been retained to argue — to receive the impartial hearing to which my client is entitled under Republican procedure. I want Gaius Umbrenus's investigation to reach its official conclusion — robbery, intruder, unknown — and to proceed no further. And I want the name of Lucius Sertorius Cappa removed from any list, preliminary or formal, on which it currently appears."

Balbus's gaze moved to me. A professional assessment. A man placing a price on a face.

"Cappa is a man of suspect loyalties," he said.

"Cappa is an old man of no loyalties at all, who has not been in Rome in two years and whose inclusion was, I believe, a matter of property convenience rather than political necessity. The holdings near the Flaminian milestone are not worth what this particular negotiation would cost you." Holcus remained entirely still. "You should also consider that a man who keeps scrupulous records of a transaction is a more reliable guarantor of silence than a man who does not. Sosias has kept records for four years. He will continue to keep them. That is, from your perspective, either a permanent liability or a permanent insurance, depending entirely on the nature of your subsequent conduct."

I had not, until that moment, fully understood what Holcus was doing. He was not merely extracting a concession. He was offering Balbus a reason to honour the arrangement beyond the immediate pressure — a future interest in Holcus's silence that would make the agreement self-sustaining. He was not blackmailing a murderer. He was making a murderer his reluctant client.

Balbus said nothing for a very long time. The freedman at the door had become furniture.

"The Caecilii case," Balbus said at last. "The investigation closes as robbery. Cappa's name does not appear."

"I am satisfied," Holcus said, "with the elegance of that arrangement."

X

We walked back through streets that smelled of charcoal smoke and other people's fires, and I did not speak until we had crossed two full blocks and the density of foot traffic had reached the level at which a conversation could not be reliably overheard.

"The letter to Sicily," I said. "You sent it before we knew with certainty that Balbus was responsible."

"I sent it before we went into a room with a man who might be responsible," he said, "and who has demonstrated a capacity for decisive action when faced with inconvenient knowledge."

"You used my father-in-law's name without telling me."

He was quiet for a step or two. "I used it with the intention of returning it to you intact, which I have done. I am aware that the method left something to be desired in terms of prior consultation."

It was as close as he came to an apology. I had, by that point, learned to accept what was offered.

XI

Publius Quinctius Balbus murdered Marcus Caecilius Varro. He murdered him deliberately, from behind, in an atrium that smelled of the better grade of Alexandrian lamp oil, for reasons of self-preservation that were rational if not honourable. Holcus knew this. I knew it. Umbrenus, had he been asked the right questions, might eventually have worked toward it.

Balbus was not tried. He was not condemned. He lived for another twelve years, served in two more administrative posts, and died of a fever in Brundisium during the wars that followed Sulla's own death. His family kept the property near the Via Flaminia.

Varro, who was not a good man, received an official verdict of death by unknown intruder — which was in some technical sense less accurate than what he actually received, which was death by a powerful man who had decided he knew too much.

My father-in-law Lucius Sertorius Cappa was not proscribed. He lived to see his grandchildren, which was more than many men managed in those years, and which was, I have always told myself, the thing that mattered most.

I put this to Holcus once — the moral question, I mean, the question of what we had done and whether it bore a name that a Roman advocate might say aloud in court. It was winter by then, a different year, the fuller's shop below busy with the smell of urine and wool, and Holcus was in one of his languid states, the ones that came between cases like the slack water between tides, in which he sat perfectly still and gazed at the middle distance and appeared to be thinking about something very far away — thinking, perhaps, about the eastern provinces, about the courts of men who held their law on sufferance from men who held the swords, about the particular quality of justice available in places that understood it had always been contingent.

"We obtained," he said, without looking at me, "the best available outcome in conditions that did not permit the ideal one. Varro was dead. Balbus was untouchable. The choice was not between justice and injustice — that choice had already been made, by others, before we arrived. The choice was between a negotiated settlement that saved one innocent life and preserved the function of the courts in two ongoing cases, or a principled accusation that would have destroyed us both, freed Balbus, and done precisely nothing for Varro, who was in any case beyond assistance."

"And that," I said, "is how you sleep."

He was quiet for a moment.

"I sleep," he said, "by not sleeping very much. There are wax tablets in the second chest if you are inclined to study the Aemilii matter. I expect we will have a client before the third hour."

We did. He was, as always, correct.
