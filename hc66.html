FLAVIUS HOLCUS

~115 BC — Rome, Roman Republic

Flavius Holcus returned to Rome from the eastern provinces in time to find the Republic in the early stages of the convulsion that would eventually destroy it. The generation of Marius and Sulla—the first Romans to march their armies against the city itself—was his generation, and he watched the breakdown of Republican norms with the cold attention of a man who had spent his career in places where the rule of law was always understood to be contingent on the willingness of those with power to observe it.

He practised law in Rome during the worst of the Sullan proscriptions, which required a very particular kind of courage—not the courage of the battlefield but the courage of a man who continues to argue in court for principles that the man with the army has declared temporarily suspended. He did not always prevail; no one always prevailed in Rome in the 80s BC. But he was known as a man whose assessment of a legal situation was accurate even when—especially when—that accuracy was unwelcome, and there were clients who valued that quality enough to seek it out even in dangerous times.

The account that survives is drawn from the papers of Quintus Fabius Labeo, a junior advocate who attached himself to Holcus during these years and kept private notes of proceedings he did not trust to the official record. Labeo admired Holcus without fully understanding him, and his record preserves both the method and the distance between them.

The Private Papers of Quintus Fabius Labeo

There is a particular quality to Roman silence in the hours before dawn. It is not the silence of peace—Rome is never at peace—but rather the silence of held breath, of a city listening to itself for the sound of boots on cobblestones. In the autumn of the year Sulla's proscription lists began to circulate, that silence had acquired a new texture: thick, almost physical, like wool pressed against the ears. The torches in the street shrines burned low and sulphurous. The Tiber smelled of things that had been put into it. Even the dogs, which are in ordinary times the vocal conscience of every Roman neighbourhood, had learned to be quiet.

It was in this silence that I first heard of the death of Marcus Caecilius Varro.

I should say at the outset that I did not like Varro. This is a confession the reader may find surprising in what is meant to be a record of justice, but Fabius Labeo has never seen the utility of omitting relevant facts, and my dislike of Varro is, as Holcus eventually demonstrated, directly relevant to the case. Varro was a man of the equestrian order who had made his fortune in the grain trade with Sicily and spent it purchasing the goodwill of men more powerful than himself. He was not brave. He was not honest. He was, however, exceptionally well-connected, and in Rome in the eighties, well-connected was frequently sufficient to substitute for every other virtue.

He was found in his atrium before the seventh hour of the night, face-down, a wound between his shoulder blades delivered with sufficient force to crack the fourth rib from above. His household slaves had been secured in the lower rooms. His wife, Caecilia Tertia—a woman of notably better sense than her husband, though she had thought fit not to exercise it when selecting him—had been at her father's house since the previous afternoon, as she subsequently testified before the magistrate, and had some forty persons to confirm this alibi who were neither slaves nor relations and therefore theoretically credible.

The Sullan functionary assigned to investigate—one Gaius Umbrenus, a man who had transferred his competence from the collection of debts to the collection of intelligence with a smoothness that suggested the disciplines were not, in his estimation, substantially different—informed me the following morning that the matter was entirely clear. An attempted robbery. Varro had disturbed the intruder. The atrium window on the garden side showed signs of forcing. A slave had heard a sound consistent with running feet in the street outside shortly after the estimated hour of death.

Umbrenus said all of this with the confident finality of a man who had completed a task. He was not, I think, a stupid man. But he was a man whose career depended on completing tasks efficiently, and in a city where Sulla's proscription apparatus required every instrument of the state to be pointed in a single direction, the Sullan functionary who consumed himself in puzzles was the Sullan functionary who would not be a functionary much longer.

I went to see Holcus.

Flavius Holcus occupied, at this period, a set of rooms above a fuller's shop near the Aventine—a location which I had always privately considered ill-suited to a man of his reputation, until he pointed out to me, once, that a fuller's shop produces fumes which discourage casual visitors, and that the discouragement of casual visitors was, in his estimation, worth a great deal more than a prestigious address. He was at his table when I arrived, surrounded by documents I did not examine and a quantity of clay tablets I did not touch, and he was writing with the focused stillness of a man who has set his mind at a problem and intends to extract its solution by the same method a surgeon employs on an interesting wound.

He looked up when I entered. I had been his junior advocate for three years by this point, long enough to know his faces. The face he showed me that morning was the attentive one—the one in which something in his eyes sharpened almost imperceptibly, the way the surface of a pool changes when a fish moves beneath it.

"Sit down, Labeo. You have news and you are uncertain whether to believe it."

I sat down. I told him about Varro.

He listened without moving. Holcus had a quality I have never seen in another advocate: he listened with his whole body, as though each word were a physical object being handed to him for examination. When I finished, he set down his stylus with the precise care of a man placing a stone in a particular spot for a particular reason.

"The window," he said.

"The garden-side atrium window. Signs of forcing, Umbrenus said."

"You did not examine it yourself."

"There was no occasion. Umbrenus had concluded his—"

"No. But you are going to examine it now, and you are going to bring me your observations before the second hour of the afternoon. Not Umbrenus's observations. Yours." He picked up his stylus again. "Do not disturb anything. Simply look. That is, I am aware, a harder instruction than it sounds."

He was already writing before I reached the door.

I will record what I found, because Holcus later told me that my record of it was the first competent observation I had made in his presence, and I have decided to accept this as the praise he perhaps intended it to be.

The window in question was a narrow affair set into the garden wall of the atrium, hinged on the left, with an iron latch on the inside. The latch had been pushed back—forcibly, I assumed at first, until I looked more carefully. The wood around the latch showed fresh marks, bright against the older patina of the timber, three of them, parallel, consistent with the tip of a knife or a similar implement. This was what Umbrenus had seen, and it was what one was meant to see.

What Umbrenus had not apparently observed—or had not thought significant—was that the marks were on the inside of the frame.

I stood looking at this for rather a long time.

If the window had been forced from outside—as a genuine intruder would have forced it—the marks would be on the outside of the frame, where the implement would have been inserted from the garden. These marks were on the inside. The window had been made to look as though it had been forced from outside. It had, in fact, been opened from within.

There was one further detail. The sill was dusty—the fullish, fine dust of a Roman house in autumn, undisturbed by the cleaning slaves who had not yet been released to their ordinary duties. The dust on the outer sill was unmarked. No foot had touched it. No body had come through this window from outside. Whatever had come through this window, if anything, had gone out.

I brought my observations to Holcus before the second hour of the afternoon, as instructed.

He listened. He set down his stylus. He stood up—a thing he did rarely enough to be noticeable—and walked to the window of his own room, which looked down onto the fuller's courtyard below, and said nothing for what felt like an unusually extended time.

"The slave who heard the running feet," he said finally. "The street outside. Which direction?"

I told him: toward the Circus, southward.

"And Varro's position—face-down, wound between the shoulder blades."

"Yes."

"Not the wound of a surprised householder struck from behind in a panic. A man struck from behind by someone standing very close, with force and deliberation, who knew exactly where to place the blade." He turned from the window. The morning light fell across his face at an angle that made his expression difficult to read—though I had learned by then that this was not entirely accidental. "The wound, the window, and the direction of the footsteps are individually consistent with Umbrenus's account. Together they are not. The window was opened from inside to suggest entry from outside. The wound was delivered by someone Varro trusted sufficiently to stand behind him. And whoever left that house departed southward with enough composure to run—not to flee in blind panic, but to run with direction. That is not a thief, Labeo. That is someone who had planned the route home before they arrived."

I am a competent advocate. I can follow an argument. "Then it was someone known to Varro."

"More than known. Trusted. In the atrium, behind him, at the seventh hour of the night, when the household slaves were secured below. That last detail is not incidental. The slaves were secured. Who secured them?"

I opened my mouth.

"Do not guess," he said. "Find out."

The answer—which I obtained from Varro's steward, a Cappadocian freedman named Phormio who was in a state of agitation that made him, if anything, more forthcoming than he might otherwise have been—was that the slaves had been secured on Varro's own instruction, delivered approximately two hours before his death. Varro had told Phormio he was expecting a private visitor and did not wish the household to be present. He had sent Phormio to the lower rooms himself.

He had been expecting someone.

I returned to Holcus with this intelligence and found him in the other of his characteristic states—not the focused stillness of the morning but a different mode entirely, in which he was pacing the length of his room with the rapid, uneven energy of a man who has ceased to think in words and is working in something faster. He barely paused when I came in.

"Sit. Talk."

I talked. He paced. When I finished, he stopped.

"The name of the visitor," he said. "Phormio did not know it?"

"Varro did not tell him. He was simply instructed to secure the household."

"Then Varro knew the meeting was not one he wished observed. He was either ashamed of the visitor or afraid of what the visitor might do if observed." He resumed pacing, though more slowly. "Not afraid of the visitor himself, or he would not have met with him alone at night with the household removed. He trusted the visitor. He feared the knowledge of the meeting."

"Why?" I said—and I confess I asked it as a genuine question, not as Holcus sometimes suspected, merely to prompt his explanation, though the distinction may have been less clear than I intended.

He stopped pacing. He turned and looked at me with the expression I had come to recognize as the one that preceded his making something obvious that had not, until that moment, been obvious to me.

"Because of what the meeting was about," he said. "Think about Varro, Labeo. Not abstractly—specifically. What was his defining characteristic? Not his virtues, which were limited. His characteristic."

"He purchased goodwill. He paid for the friendship of powerful men."

"He purchased protection," Holcus said. "That is a more precise description. A man like Varro—moderately wealthy, no military distinction, no great family—survives in Rome through a very particular mechanism. He makes himself useful to men who are powerful enough to protect him, and he does this by giving them things they want: money, information, access to his trade connections, his warehouses, his ships. In return, they place their names—implicitly or explicitly—between him and danger." He walked back to his table and stood behind it, which was as close as he came to a formal posture. "Now. Sulla's proscriptions are coming. Everyone in Rome knows they are coming—the lists will be published soon. What does a man like Varro do?"

I considered. "He tries to ensure his name is not on the list."

"He tries to ensure his name is not on the list. He goes to one of his powerful patrons—one of the men in Sulla's circle, one of the men who will know what the lists contain before they are published—and he offers something. More money. Information. A particular service. In exchange for the assurance that he will be protected." Holcus paused. The fuller's fumes rose from below. "And his patron agrees."

"Then why is he dead?"

Holcus looked at me for a long moment.

"Because," he said carefully, "Varro knew something about his patron. Not something the patron had told him—something Varro had learned independently, through his trade connections, his warehouses, his ships. Something that made Varro useful to his patron while Varro was alive, and dangerous to his patron if Varro were in a position to testify about it before someone whose opinion mattered. Which he would be, if someone else—a rival, an enemy of the patron—found a way to approach Varro and offer him better protection in exchange for that information."

The room was very quiet. Below, someone dropped a heavy ceramic vessel on stone.

"The patron," I said slowly, "decided to call in the debt before Varro could be purchased by someone else."

"The patron decided," Holcus said, "that a dead Varro was more reliably silent than a living one. He arranged the meeting—or accepted Varro's request for a meeting—and came himself, at night, after Varro had been persuaded to secure the household. He struck from behind. He opened the window from inside to suggest exterior entry. He left southward." He sat down. "The killer is one of Sulla's inner circle. He had access to the lists before publication. He knew Varro's name was on them, which means he placed it there—or had it placed—as additional protection after the killing, so that Varro's death would appear to be merely a precursor to the official condemnation."

I stared at him.

"The list," I said. "You're saying Varro was already marked for death on Sulla's list, but—"

"The name was added afterward. Or will be, if it has not been yet. Check Umbrenus's records. If Varro's name appears on the list, note the precise time that entry was recorded. If it was recorded after the body was found—or at a suspicious remove from the time of death—then the list was used to obscure the murder, not to authorize it." He picked up his stylus again. "Which means the killer is someone with sufficient authority to add names to a proscription list. That is a very limited number of men, Labeo."

I will not set down here the process by which Holcus identified the man, because some part of that process involved access to records and sources which it would be neither safe nor kind to document in any permanent form, and Fabius Labeo, whatever his other limitations, understands which truths require a locked box and which require a public court. Let it suffice to say that the name on Varro's entry in the proscription register was recorded at the third hour of the morning following the discovery of the body—a time at which the formal publication of the day's list had not yet occurred, and at which the relevant secretariat should have been unstaffed.

The man who had authority to make additions to the secretariat's records at that hour, and the access to Varro's home that the meeting implied, and the trade connection to Varro's Sicilian grain operations that gave Varro his hold on him—this man was Publius Quinctius Balbus, a senior member of Sulla's administrative council and a person of, in the public estimation, considerable honour.

Holcus requested a meeting.

I was present. I will record what I witnessed.

Balbus received us in the tablinum of a house that smelled of expensive Syrian cedar and the particular quality of lamp oil imported from Alexandria—not the common Italian pressing but the lighter, cleaner grade that costs three times as much and is used by men who wish to signal, through the medium of combustion, that they are not the sort of men who count costs. He was a man of late middle age, broad-shouldered, with hands that had once been calloused and were now merely strong. He looked at Holcus with the assessment of a man accustomed to evaluating what things would cost him.

"You have something to tell me," Balbus said. It was not a question.

"I have an analysis to share," Holcus said, "in the spirit of intellectual collegium, without any implication of official proceedings." His voice was entirely pleasant. "I have been examining the matter of Marcus Caecilius Varro, and I find myself in possession of certain observations about the window latch, the direction of the wound, the timing of the proscription entry, and the nature of Varro's trade connection to a particular Sicilian agent whose testimony before a sufficiently sympathetic magistrate might prove—illuminating on several counts. I share this analysis with you not because I intend to act upon it, but because a man of your administrative responsibility deserves to be aware of gaps in the official account."

Balbus looked at him for a very long time.

"What," Balbus said, "do you want?"

"Nothing that is not already mine," Holcus said. "I want the Caecilii case—a property dispute I have been retained to argue—to receive the impartial hearing to which my client is entitled under Republican procedure. I want Gaius Umbrenus's investigation to reach its official conclusion—robbery, intruder, unknown—and to proceed no further. And I want the name of Lucius Sertorius Cappa to be removed from tomorrow's list."

I looked at him. I had not known about Cappa.

Balbus's expression did not change, but something behind his eyes did—a small recalibration, the kind that happens when a man revises his estimation of what he is dealing with.

"Cappa is a man of suspect loyalties," Balbus said.

"Cappa is an old man of no loyalties at all, who has not been in Rome for two years and whose inclusion on the list was, I think, a personal convenience for someone who wishes to acquire his property holdings near the Via Flaminia." Holcus's voice remained pleasant. "He is also the father-in-law of my assistant Labeo here, which perhaps explains my interest. The holdings near the Via Flaminia are not worth what this particular negotiation would cost you."

Balbus said nothing for a long moment.

"The Caecilii case," he said finally. "The investigation closes as robbery. Cappa's name does not appear."

"I am satisfied," Holcus said, "with the elegance of that arrangement."

I have thought about that meeting for many years. I have thought about it in the way one thinks about a thing that is entirely clear in its outlines and entirely resistant to a final moral verdict.

Publius Quinctius Balbus murdered Marcus Caecilius Varro. He murdered him deliberately, from behind, in an atrium that smelled of the better grade of Alexandrian lamp oil, for reasons of self-preservation that were rational if not honourable. Holcus knew this. I knew it. Umbrenus, had he been asked the right questions, might eventually have worked toward it.

Balbus was not tried. He was not condemned. He lived for another twelve years, served in two more administrative posts, and died of a fever in Brundisium during the wars that followed Sulla's own death. His family kept the property near the Via Flaminia.

Varro, who was not a good man, received an official verdict of death by unknown intruder, which was in some technical sense less accurate than what he received, which was death by a powerful man who had decided he knew too much.

My father-in-law Lucius Sertorius Cappa was not proscribed. He lived to see his grandchildren, which was more than many men managed in those years, and which was, I have always told myself, the thing that mattered most.

I put this to Holcus, once—the moral question, I mean, the question of what we had done and whether it bore a name that a Roman advocate might say aloud in court. It was winter by then, a different year, the fuller's shop below busy with the smell of urine and wool, and Holcus was in one of his languid states, the ones that came between cases like the slack water between tides, in which he sat perfectly still and gazed at the middle distance and appeared to be thinking about something very far away.

"We obtained," he said, without looking at me, "the best available outcome in conditions that did not permit the ideal one. Varro was dead. Balbus was untouchable. The choice was not between justice and injustice—that choice had already been made, by others, before we arrived. The choice was between a negotiated settlement that saved one innocent life and preserved the function of the courts in two ongoing cases, or a principled accusation that would have destroyed us both, freed Balbus, and done precisely nothing for Varro, who was in any case beyond assistance."

"And that," I said, "is how you sleep."

He was quiet for a moment.

"I sleep," he said, "by not sleeping very much. There are wax tablets in the second chest if you are inclined to study the Aemilii matter. I expect we will have a client before the third hour."

We did. He was, as always, correct.

The names of the individuals described herein have in some instances been rendered imprecise for reasons the reader who has followed the account attentively will understand. The author does not apologize for this. A man who has lived through a proscription understands that the precise truth and the recorded truth are different instruments, suited to different purposes, and that the wise scribe knows when to reach for which.
