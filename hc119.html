SIR SYMONDS HOLMES

~1635 — London, England

Sir Symonds Holmes grew up in a Royalist household during the years of the Parliamentary ascendancy — his father’s estate under sequestration, his family’s fortunes dependent on the kind of patient, cautious discretion that the Interregnum demanded of those who had chosen the losing side. He came of age not in the noise of battle but in the quieter and more dangerous work of maintaining connections, preserving correspondence, and reading the political weather with sufficient accuracy to know, well before most of his neighbours, that the Restoration was coming and what it would require of those who wished to benefit from it. He was commended for his contributions to the Royalist cause in the years immediately preceding Charles II’s return — contributions that his commanding officer would later be unable to recall making — in the manner of men who had done useful work in circumstances where the record of that work was itself a liability.

The family’s estates suffered during the Interregnum years — sequestration, fines, the grinding attrition of supporting a losing side — but Sir Symonds did not go into exile, and at the Restoration he was among those who found themselves, after years of patient loyalty, in a position to be rewarded. He used that position with the precision of a man who had been waiting. He applied for and received a modest annuity in recognition of his service, cultivated the right connections at the restored court without becoming dependent on any of them, and retrieved the sequestered portion of his estate through a legal process so carefully documented that the Parliamentary committee’s own records furnished the evidence of the restoration.

The record of his work survives in part through the journals of Elias Cartwright, a clerk in the employ of the Royal Society, who recorded not only experiments but also the conversations that accompanied them. Cartwright’s notes reveal a man attentive not only to phenomena, but to the manner in which phenomena were approached — a distinction that would define the next stage of the family’s work.

The Sign of the Three Crowns

Recorded in the Journals of Elias Cartwright, Clerk

A note upon this record: I have debated, these many years since the Restoration, whether to commit this account to any permanent form. The man at its centre would counsel against it — he has always understood that the record of certain work is itself a kind of danger. But I am old now, and I have watched Sir Symonds Holmes receive no credit for what was, in my reckoning, the most singular act of reasoning I have witnessed in a life spent among men who pride themselves upon their reasoning. The official account, such as it is, credits Captain Thresh's diligence. I leave it to posterity to judge between them.

— E. Cartwright, London, 1672

The body was found in the stable yard of the Three Crowns at half past four in the morning, which I know because I was awake to hear the ostler's cry — a sound that carried across the yard and through the shuttered window of the room I shared with Symonds Holmes, and which caused him to sit upright in the darkness with the immediate clarity of a man who had not, I now suspect, been sleeping at all.

I had been travelling with Holmes for three days by that point, accompanying him north on what he described as a matter of correspondence that required personal delivery. I was then a junior clerk in the employ of a natural philosopher of modest reputation, and Holmes had made it worth my while to serve as his secretary for the journey. I understood, in the way one understands things in 1657 without committing them to explicit statement, that the correspondence was Royalist in nature, and that Holmes's reasons for requiring a witness to his movements were connected to his need, should any question arise, to be demonstrably occupied with legitimate philosophical business.

The inn sat on the Great North Road at the edge of Doncaster, a sprawling stone building that smelled of horse-sweat and tallow and the particular damp of Yorkshire in November — a cold that came not from the air alone but seemed to rise from the earth itself, as though the ground had drawn all warmth downward and locked it away. The yard, when we descended into it, was lit by two lanterns that threw more shadow than light, and the body lay between them in a posture that suggested the man had not fallen but had been arranged — or rather, had arranged himself — in the manner of one who had sat down for a rest and found the rest permanent.

He was a young man, perhaps five-and-twenty, dressed for travel in a buff coat that had seen hard use. His satchel remained buckled at his side.

Captain Thresh of the local militia was there before us — a broad, deliberate man with the air of someone who had learned that appearing unhurried was indistinguishable, to most observers, from appearing competent. He had already formed a conclusion. I could see it in the satisfaction of his stance. He was accompanied by a younger officer whom he introduced briefly as Lieutenant Reath, who was posted at the eastern lane in case anyone should attempt to use the back road before the constable arrived. Reath was a trim, dark-haired man who nodded without speaking and returned to his post before I had occasion to study him further.

"Footpad," Thresh said, when Holmes knelt beside the body. "The Great North Road draws them. Man was likely taken for his horse."

"His horse," Holmes said, without looking up, "is in the third stall, fed and watered and entirely unharmed. The ostler confirmed this before you arrived."

Thresh looked at the ostler, who nodded with the misery of a man caught between authorities.

"Then robbery of his person."

"His purse," Holmes said, and here he rose and held it out — I had not seen him take it — "contains four shillings and a sixpence. If this man was robbed, his killer was either very thorough or very poor at arithmetic." He let the purse hang from his finger for a moment, then replaced it with the careful precision of a man who wished it known that he had replaced it. "Neither possibility suggests a footpad."

He did not stand immediately. He remained kneeling, and I watched his eyes move across the body with the systematic patience of a man reading a page he has not seen before but suspects he already understands. He examined the hands first — turning them palm-upward, studying the knuckles in the lantern light. Then the boots. Then, with a care that struck me as peculiar, the collar of the buff coat, which he raised and inspected along the inner edge before allowing it to fall back into place. Finally he pressed two fingers to the cobblestones immediately beside the body, held them there a moment, and rose.

"The body has been here no more than two hours," he said. "The stones beneath him retain warmth his coat has not yet fully surrendered. He did not die at half past four — he was found at half past four. He died closer to three."

Thresh opened his mouth and closed it again.

"Furthermore," Holmes continued, moving without ceremony toward the stable door, "he did not die where he lies."

This produced a silence of the useful kind — the kind in which a man of Captain Thresh's temperament has no recourse but to follow.

The stable was warm with the breath of horses, and Holmes moved along the stalls with his lantern held low, examining the floor with the absorption of a scholar among manuscripts. I watched him stop at the far end of the building, near a low door that opened onto a secondary lane running behind the inn. He crouched, touched the ground, and held up his fingers: they came away dark.

"Here," he said simply.

The blood was not copious — a smear rather than a pool, but unmistakable in the lamplight. Beside it, half-obscured by straw, was a small bright object that Holmes retrieved and held to the light. It was a button, military in character, bearing an impression I could not make out at the distance.

Holmes studied it for a long moment, then placed it in his coat pocket with the air of a librarian shelving a volume he intends to return to shortly.

"He was struck here," Holmes said, "near this door, which opens onto the back lane. He was then carried to the yard and arranged. Some trouble was taken to make him appear as a man who had simply sat down and died of cold or misadventure." He straightened. "The trouble was competent but not expert. An expert would have ensured the body lay where the cold of the cobblestones could work upon it uniformly; instead our man placed him near the southern wall, where the stones are sheltered and retain their temperature longer. A detail that matters only if one knows to look for it."

The dead man's name, established through the contents of his satchel, was William Forde, and he had been a courier — though for whom remained, in that first hour, a matter of some delicacy. The satchel contained despatches, sealed with a cipher I recognised as Royalist work, and their presence on a man found dead on the Great North Road in the middle of Cromwell's England was, to phrase it temperately, a situation requiring management.

Holmes managed it with the efficiency he applied to all things inconvenient. He spoke quietly to Thresh about the natural philosopher's business, produced a letter of introduction that I was surprised to discover I had apparently signed, and secured for himself ten minutes alone with the body and the satchel while Thresh attended to the formality of sending for the constable.

I stood watch. This was my function. The yard had grown quieter in the intervening quarter-hour, and the cold had settled more completely into the cobblestones and the timber frames of the outbuildings. From somewhere along the east wing — the direction of the back lane, where the secondary chimneys served the upper-floor hearths — there drifted a faint, acrid trace in the night air, thin and sharp beneath the heavier smells of tallow and horse, the unmistakable ghost of something recently burned. Paper, I thought vaguely, or oilskin of some kind. But the constable was coming through the gate with his lantern, and I turned my attention to him, and thought no more about it until later.

When Thresh returned, the satchel was buckled exactly as it had been, and Holmes was examining the dead man's left hand once more, this time with the thoroughness of a physician reconstructing an event from its aftermath.

"Robbery," Holmes said, pleasantly, "seems the likeliest explanation. I defer entirely to your experience of the road, Captain."

Thresh was satisfied. I was not, and I said so, quietly, as we climbed back to our room.

"You read the despatches," I said.

"I read everything," Holmes said. He moved to the window and stood looking down into the yard where the body still lay, attended now by the constable and a boy with a torch. The November wind pressed against the glass, and the tallow candle on our table threw his shadow long across the floor. "The despatches are intact. Every seal unbroken. Forde was not killed for the despatches."

"Then for what?"

He turned from the window. In the candlelight, his face had the quality I had come to recognise over three days of travel — not cold, precisely, but resolved, in the manner of a mathematical expression that has been reduced to its simplest form and requires no further commentary.

"Sit down, Cartwright. I am going to explain something to you, and I would prefer you to be seated when I do, because your instinct when surprised is to stand straighter, and that becomes, after a time, rather wearing to observe."

I sat.

Holmes remained standing, which I had come to understand was his habit when thinking required the appearance of stillness.

"There is," he said, "a great deal of correspondence in this world that never reaches its destination, and most of it is lost to carelessness, weather, or the simple entropy of a country at war with itself. But some correspondence is lost by design — removed not because the remover wished to read it, but because the remover could not permit anyone else to do so." He moved to the table and sat, finally, with the abruptness of a man who has resolved the question of movement and considers it now closed. "Forde carried despatches, yes. But he also carried private letters — the kind of private letters that couriers carry as a matter of professional courtesy and modest supplementary income. Those letters are gone."

"He might have delivered them before he died."

"He arrived at this inn at eight o'clock in the evening. I have confirmed this with the ostler and with the kitchen girl, who remembers him because he ordered the better ale and paid without complaint, which she notes as unusual. He had not left the inn between his arrival and his death. He delivered nothing."

I considered this. "Someone took the private letters from his person."

"Took them with sufficient knowledge of their existence to know where to look, and with sufficient urgency to kill for them rather than steal them by subtler means. A pickpocket would have taken the purse. Our man took only the letters. He knew they existed. He knew Forde had them. And he knew that they contained something he could not allow to be read." Holmes rose and took the button from his coat pocket, setting it on the table between us. "Militia issue," he said. "The impression is a Parliamentary crowned portcullis — standard to the Doncaster garrison. Our man is not merely someone who passed through this inn tonight. He is someone who has reason to be in the vicinity of the Great North Road, who has access to the militia's intercept intelligence, and who knew Forde's route."

I leaned forward to look at the button. It was small and cold, and it seemed to me extraordinary that so much consequence could attach itself to so ordinary an object. "He lost it in the struggle."

"In the struggle at the stable door, yes. Forde did not go quietly — the position of the bloodstain suggests he turned before the blow fell, which means he saw or heard something that alerted him. He had time to resist but not to prevail. The button was torn from the killer's coat in that brief interval." Holmes tapped it once with his forefinger. "More than this: I examined the collar of Forde's buff coat when I knelt beside him. There was a thread caught in the stitching — a thread of dark blue wool, not Forde's colour; his coat is buff throughout. Dark blue is the militia's winter weight. Our man's coat made contact with Forde's collar during the struggle, or during the subsequent carrying of the body."

"That narrows it considerably," I said.

"It narrows it to the garrison," Holmes agreed, "and to a man of sufficient rank and freedom of movement to leave his post at three in the morning without accounting for his whereabouts. A private must answer for his hours. An officer need not." He looked at me with the patience of a man who has not yet reached the portion of the explanation he considers interesting. "But I have not yet told you what the letter contained."

"You said you didn't read the private correspondence."

"I did not read it. It is ash — I could smell it from the yard, faintly, beneath the tallow and the horse-dung. The killer burned it within the hour. But I did not need to read it." Holmes pressed his fingertips together. "Forde carried his private letters in a separate oilskin wallet, inside the satchel's false bottom — a common courier's concealment. That wallet is gone. But Forde was a methodical man, as methodical men are; I found, tucked within the lining of the satchel itself, a slip of paper on which he had noted his private commissions in a simple clerk's shorthand. Four entries. Three remain — amounts, destinations, initials. The fourth has been rubbed out, but not thoroughly. Underneath the rubbing, in a good light, one can make out a name."

He placed the slip of paper beside the button. I looked at it. The fourth entry, though heavily obscured, bore the ghost of a word: a surname of five letters, the first of which was unmistakably a capital R. Five letters. Beginning with R.

The name came to me before Holmes spoke it.

"The recipient," Holmes said. "A man who was to receive a letter identifying an informant — a man within the Royalist network who had been carrying information to the Parliamentary authorities. The letter named him. Whoever arranged its writing intended it to reach the network's leadership so that they would act against him. Forde was merely the mechanism of delivery. He was used and then removed because a mechanism that can speak is a liability."

"Then the killer is whoever wrote the letter."

"Or arranged its writing. Yes." Holmes rose, which meant the thinking portion of the evening was either complete or had moved to a phase that required movement. "A man currently in this inn, in a room whose position I can deduce. The back lane onto which that stable door opens runs parallel to the inn's east wing. A man carrying a body from that door to the yard would emerge from the east end of the building. The guest rooms on the east side are numbers three, four, and five. Three is occupied by a drover — a large man, strongly built, who arrived this afternoon and paid in advance, which is the habit of a man accustomed to leaving without ceremony." He paused, and I confess I had already begun to form a picture of the drover as our man, sufficiently large to carry a body, sufficiently anonymous to go unremarked. "However," Holmes continued, "I observed his boots when he came through the common room. They were caked with field mud — not road dust, not the particular grey clay of the Great North Road, but the dark loam of worked farmland. He has come from the west, across fields, not along the road. He could not have had foreknowledge of Forde's route, which means he could not have arranged the meeting, which means he could not have committed this particular crime. A drover who happens to be large and to pay in advance is simply a drover who happens to be large and to pay in advance. Five is occupied by a wool merchant's wife who has not left her room since supper, on account of a cough that the entire inn has had occasion to notice. That leaves room four."

I stared at him. "You deduced the room from boot mud and a cough."

"I deduced the room from the elimination of alternatives," Holmes said, with the mild patience of a man accustomed to this response. "Which is not the same thing, though I grant it sounds similar."

He was already at the door.

"Where are you going?" I asked.

"To have a word with the gentleman in room four," Holmes said. "You will remain here. If I am not returned within twenty minutes, you will go directly to Captain Thresh — not to help me, you understand, but to ensure that someone of official capacity is made aware of the militia button and the rubbed entry on Forde's commission slip. I leave both items on the table for that purpose."

Before I could object, he was gone.

I counted the minutes with the candle as my only company. It burned lower. The yard below was quiet. The boy with the torch had long since gone to his bed, and the constable with him, and the night settled over the Three Crowns with the specific silence of a place that has decided to have no further events before morning.

Holmes returned in fourteen minutes. He was unhurried, his coat unmarked, his expression carrying the quality I can only describe as a particular kind of satisfaction — not triumph, which is loud, but completion, which is quiet.

He sat. He picked up the militia button and turned it in his fingers once, then set it down.

"Well?" I said.

"The gentleman in room four," Holmes said, "is a man of intelligence and nerve, which I had already surmised from the competence of his work. He is also a man who, when confronted in the small hours of a November morning by someone who can account for his precise movements from nine o'clock to half past three, and who holds in his hand a button that matches the gap on his coat and a commission slip bearing a rubbed-out name that any careful reader can reconstruct, does not waste time in protestations of innocence."

"He confessed?"

"He confirmed," Holmes said, making the distinction with care. "There is a difference. A man who confesses is broken. A man who confirms has simply recognised that the game has moved to a different board, and is deciding how best to play upon it." He was quiet for a moment. "He was, I think, more unsettled by the fact of my knowing than by the knowing itself. He had believed himself careful. He is not accustomed to the experience of having been seen."

"And you simply spoke with him and came away."

"I came away in possession of certain additional information that would not have been freely given in any other circumstance, and he came away in the knowledge that a sealed letter — a copy of the commission slip, with my interpretation appended — is to be delivered to a party I declined to name, should I not personally countermand the arrangement by noon tomorrow. He understood, without my needing to elaborate, that the party in question would not be Captain Thresh, and that its interest in the matter would be considerably less procedural." Holmes looked at the candle. "Which I shall countermand, provided that the gentleman in room four departs at first light, does not return to his militia posting, and makes no further use of the intelligence networks to which his position has given him access."

I absorbed this. "You are letting him go."

"I am conducting a negotiation," Holmes said, "the terms of which serve several interests that I am not at liberty to enumerate." He rose and moved to the window once more. Below, the yard was dark and empty, the cobblestones pale in the moonlight. "Thresh will record a robbery by persons unknown. Forde will be buried as a traveller of no particular occupation. The man in room four will be gone before the constable's morning rounds, and within the week he will be gone from the county entirely, which is a more satisfactory outcome than the alternatives."

"And the informant whose name was in the letter?"

Holmes said nothing for a moment. Then: "The informant made his choices. He was not forced into them."

It was the only hard thing I heard him say in three days of travel, and he said it without inflection, which made it harder.

We had breakfast at seven. We continued north. The correspondence was delivered. I do not know by what other hands or by what other roads the consequences of that November yard were eventually carried to their destinations. Holmes, if he tracked them, did not say.

The official record of the Three Crowns affair, such as it is, appears in the Doncaster constabulary notes for November 1657, where it is listed as an unsolved robbery and attributed by Captain Thresh to persons unknown. William Forde is recorded as a traveller. His occupation is not specified.

What I know is what I have recorded here: that a man was found dead with his official correspondence intact and his private correspondence missing, and that another man read the nature of that absence — and the warmth of a cobblestone, and a thread caught in a collar, and the ghost of a rubbed-out name, and the bright cold weight of a button no larger than a shilling — and knew it for what it was. The law saw a robbery. Holmes saw a signature.

I have done my best to record it faithfully. The reader will judge whether I have succeeded. The subject of the record, were he to read it, would note three errors of sequence, one error of meteorological detail, and would observe that I have somewhat understated the quality of the tallow at the Three Crowns, which was, in fact, particularly offensive.

He would be correct on all counts.

— E. Cartwright
