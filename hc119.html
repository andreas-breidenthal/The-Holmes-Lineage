SIR SYMONDS HOLMES

~1635 — London, England

Sir Symonds Holmes grew up in a Royalist household during the years of the Parliamentary ascendancy — his father’s estate under sequestration, his family’s fortunes dependent on the kind of patient, cautious discretion that the Interregnum demanded of those who had chosen the losing side. He came of age not in the noise of battle but in the quieter and more dangerous work of maintaining connections, preserving correspondence, and reading the political weather with sufficient accuracy to know, well before most of his neighbours, that the Restoration was coming and what it would require of those who wished to benefit from it. He was commended for his contributions to the Royalist cause in the years immediately preceding Charles II’s return — contributions that his commanding officer would later be unable to recall making — in the manner of men who had done useful work in circumstances where the record of that work was itself a liability.

The family’s estates suffered during the Interregnum years — sequestration, fines, the grinding attrition of supporting a losing side — but Sir Symonds did not go into exile, and at the Restoration he was among those who found themselves, after years of patient loyalty, in a position to be rewarded. He used that position with the precision of a man who had been waiting. He applied for and received a modest annuity in recognition of his service, cultivated the right connections at the restored court without becoming dependent on any of them, and retrieved the sequestered portion of his estate through a legal process so carefully documented that the Parliamentary committee’s own records furnished the evidence of the restoration.

The record of his work survives in part through the journals of Elias Cartwright, a clerk in the employ of the Royal Society, who recorded not only experiments but also the conversations that accompanied them. Cartwright’s notes reveal a man attentive not only to phenomena, but to the manner in which phenomena were approached — a distinction that would define the next stage of the family’s work.

The Sign of the Three Crowns

Recorded in the Journals of Elias Cartwright, Clerk

A note upon this record: I have debated, these many years since the Restoration, whether to commit this account to any permanent form. The man at its centre would counsel against it — he has always understood that the record of certain work is itself a kind of danger. But I am old now, and I have watched Sir Symonds Holmes receive no credit for what was, in my reckoning, the most singular act of reasoning I have witnessed in a life spent among men who pride themselves upon their reasoning. The official account, such as it is, credits Captain Thresh’s diligence. I leave it to posterity to judge between them.
— E. Cartwright, London, 1672

The body was found in the stable yard of the Three Crowns at half past four in the morning, which I know because I was awake to hear the ostler’s cry — a sound that carried across the yard and through the shuttered window of the room I shared with Symonds Holmes, and which caused him to sit upright in the darkness with the immediate clarity of a man who had not, I now suspect, been sleeping at all.

I had been travelling with Holmes for three days by that point, accompanying him north on what he described as a matter of correspondence that required personal delivery. I was then a junior clerk in the employ of a natural philosopher of modest reputation, and Holmes had made it worth my while to serve as his secretary for the journey. I understood, in the way one understands things in 1657 without committing them to explicit statement, that the correspondence was Royalist in nature, and that Holmes’s reasons for requiring a witness to his movements were connected to his need, should any question arise, to be demonstrably occupied with legitimate philosophical business.

The inn sat on the Great North Road at the edge of Doncaster, a sprawling stone building that smelled of horse-sweat and tallow and the particular damp of Yorkshire in November — a cold that came not from the air alone but seemed to rise from the earth itself, as though the ground had drawn all warmth downward and locked it away. The yard, when we descended into it, was lit by two lanterns that threw more shadow than light, and the body lay between them in a posture that suggested the man had not fallen but had been arranged — or rather, had arranged himself — in the manner of one who had sat down for a rest and found the rest permanent.

He was a young man, perhaps five-and-twenty, dressed for travel in a buff coat that had seen hard use. His satchel remained buckled at his side.

Captain Thresh of the local militia was there before us — a broad, deliberate man with the air of someone who had learned that appearing unhurried was indistinguishable, to most observers, from appearing competent. He had already formed a conclusion. I could see it in the satisfaction of his stance.

“Footpad,” he said, when Holmes knelt beside the body. “The Great North Road draws them. Man was likely taken for his horse.”

“His horse,” Holmes said, without looking up, “is in the third stall, fed and watered and entirely unharmed. The ostler confirmed this before you arrived.”

Thresh looked at the ostler, who nodded with the misery of a man caught between authorities.

“Then robbery of his person.”

“His purse,” Holmes said, and here he rose and held it out — I had not seen him take it — “contains four shillings and a sixpence. If this man was robbed, his killer was either very thorough or very poor at arithmetic.” He let the purse hang from his finger for a moment, then replaced it with the careful precision of a man who wished it known that he had replaced it. “Neither possibility suggests a footpad.”

The dead man’s name, established through the contents of his satchel, was William Forde, and he had been a courier — though for whom remained, in that first hour, a matter of some delicacy. The satchel contained despatches, sealed with a cipher I recognised as Royalist work, and their presence on a man found dead on the Great North Road in the middle of Cromwell’s England was, to phrase it temperately, a situation requiring management.

Holmes managed it with the efficiency he applied to all things inconvenient. He spoke quietly to Thresh about the natural philosopher’s business, produced a letter of introduction that I was surprised to discover I had apparently signed, and secured for himself ten minutes alone with the body and the satchel while Thresh attended to the formality of sending for the constable.

I stood watch. This was my function.

When Thresh returned, the satchel was buckled exactly as it had been, and Holmes was examining the dead man’s left hand with the attention of a physician, though I knew him to be nothing of the sort.

“Robbery,” Holmes said, pleasantly, “seems the likeliest explanation. I defer entirely to your experience of the road, Captain.”

Thresh was satisfied. I was not, and I said so, quietly, as we climbed back to our room.

“You read the despatches,” I said.

“I read everything,” Holmes said. He moved to the window and stood looking down into the yard where the body still lay, attended now by the constable and a boy with a torch. The November wind pressed against the glass, and the tallow candle on our table threw his shadow long across the floor. “The despatches are intact. Every seal unbroken. Forde was not killed for the despatches.”

“Then for what?”

He turned from the window. In the candlelight, his face had the quality I had come to recognise over three days of travel — not cold, precisely, but resolved, in the manner of a mathematical expression that has been reduced to its simplest form and requires no further commentary.

“Sit down, Cartwright. I am going to explain something to you, and I would prefer you to be seated when I do, because your instinct when surprised is to stand straighter, and that becomes, after a time, rather wearing to observe.”

I sat.

Holmes remained standing, which I had come to understand was his habit when thinking required the appearance of stillness.

“There is,” he said, “a great deal of correspondence in this world that never reaches its destination, and most of it is lost to carelessness, weather, or the simple entropy of a country at war with itself. But some correspondence is lost by design — removed not because the remover wished to read it, but because the remover could not permit anyone else to do so.” He moved to the table and sat, finally, with the abruptness of a man who has resolved the question of movement and considers it now closed. “Forde carried despatches, yes. But he also carried private letters — the kind of private letters that couriers carry as a matter of professional courtesy and modest supplementary income. Those letters are gone.”

“He might have delivered them before he died.”

“He arrived at this inn at eight o’clock in the evening. I have confirmed this with the ostler and with the kitchen girl, who remembers him because he ordered the better ale and paid without complaint, which she notes as unusual. He had not left the inn between his arrival and his death. He delivered nothing.”

I considered this. “Someone took the private letters from his person.”

“Took them,” Holmes said, “with sufficient knowledge of their existence to know where to look, and with sufficient urgency to kill for them rather than steal them by subtler means. A pickpocket would have taken the purse. Our man took only the letters. He knew they existed. He knew Forde had them. And he knew that they contained something he could not allow to be read.”

“But you don’t know what they contained.”

Holmes looked at me with the expression I had learned to interpret as patience being exercised.

“I know precisely what they contained,” he said. “One of them named an informant.”

The official record of the Three Crowns affair, such as it is, appears in the Doncaster constabulary notes for November 1657, where it is listed as an unsolved robbery and attributed by Captain Thresh to persons unknown. William Forde is recorded as a traveller. His occupation is not specified. The despatches he carried were, presumably, destroyed or delivered by other hands in the days that followed; I do not know which, and Holmes, if he knew, did not say.

What I know is what I have recorded here: that a man was found dead with his official correspondence intact and his private correspondence missing, and that another man read the nature of that absence and knew it for what it was. The law saw a robbery. Holmes saw a signature.

I have done my best to record it faithfully. The reader will judge whether I have succeeded. The subject of the record, were he to read it, would note three errors of sequence, one error of meteorological detail, and would observe that I have somewhat understated the quality of the tallow at the Three Crowns, which was, in fact, particularly offensive.

He would be correct on all counts.

— E. Cartwright
