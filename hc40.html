AKAROS OF DELPHI

~880 BC — Delphi, Greece

A Record Kept By Melanthios

I.

The oil lamps in the treasury burned olive pressed from the sanctuary's own grove, and they gave off a smell distinct from any other — slightly bitter, almost medicinal, as though the trees themselves had absorbed something from the stone of the mountain. I mention this because a room that should have been empty was not, on the night I am about to describe, and it was the lamps that said so.

I did not know that yet. Sostias knew it, or half-knew it, which is the condition most productive of terror.

He came to my door in the hour before dawn on the third day before the feast — the morning after the Koryneia delegation's arrival — moving with the particular quality of haste that belongs to a person who wishes urgently to move and is simultaneously terrified of being seen moving. He was seventeen, possibly eighteen; he was not certain himself. He had been in the treasury's service for two years, assigned to the archive room as a copying scribe, which meant he spent his days among the consultation records of the administration — copying, cross-referencing, learning the procedures of the sanctuary's bureaucratic life with the thoroughness that only the very young and very attentive bring to work they have been given because no one else wanted it.

I knew him well enough to know he was not given to alarms. I sat him down and gave him water.

He said: I made an error. Or I was made to make an error. I am not certain which it is.

He paused. He said: And I think I may be in it, whatever it is. If it comes out wrong, I think I may be in it.

I understood then that his stakes were not merely a guilty conscience. He was a boy with no patron, no family connection to the sanctuary, no standing that would survive even the suspicion of involvement in a treasury irregularity. If the matter surfaced through channels other than his own disclosure, his two years of careful and anonymous service would count for nothing. He would be the scribe who had been present and said nothing. He knew this. It was in his face.

I told him: then let us ensure it comes out right. Tell me exactly what you heard.

The delegation from Koryneia had arrived the previous afternoon — nine of them, six attendants bearing the sealed chest, two administrative men whose names had been provided in writing as Polydoros and Euarchos, and one man who had given his name as Therimander. I had received them formally. Therimander was perhaps fifty years of age, broad through the chest, with the kind of weathered stillness that belongs to men who have made decisions for a long time and have grown accustomed to the consequences not appearing immediately. The chest was sealed with three separate fastenings, each marked with the city's civic symbol, and had been taken into the inner treasury to await the ceremony of opening, two days hence.

Sostias had been working late in the archive room. The lamps had been burning for several hours. He had been copying a record of the previous year's consultations — precisely the records, I noted, of the consultation administration — when he became aware that the lamps were burning differently. That a door had opened and closed elsewhere in the treasury complex. The smoke had shifted.

He had gone to look. The inner treasury door was not merely unlocked. It was slightly ajar.

He had stood in the corridor long enough to hear, from within, the sound of something being moved across stone — a dragging, deliberate sound — and then he had retreated to the archive room and sat with his lamp and his records until morning.

He said: I should have woken the night watch. I chose not to, and I cannot stop knowing that I chose not to, and I do not know whether that makes me part of whatever was done in there.

I told him he had done right to come to me. I told him that his account was valuable and that I intended to ensure it remained so. I told him to go to the archive room, to his desk, and to remain there — visibly working — until I sent for him.

He went. I went to find Akaros.

II.

I should say something of Akaros, since the events that follow are otherwise incomprehensible. He had come to the sanctuary in the service of supply — accounting for the movement of animals, grain, timber, and bronze — with what I can only describe as a gift for the discrepancy: not for numbers alone, which any competent man could manage, but for the gap between what the numbers said and what the numbers meant. He noticed when a surplus in one column produced no corresponding change in another. Over the years, his responsibilities had expanded: he reviewed dedications, attended preliminary consultations when delegations submitted questions to the oracle's process, examined the precise wording of a question — not the answer, but the question, its hidden conditions, the thing being asked that was not the thing being stated. He was, in his middle years, a lean man with an asymmetry to his face that gave him the appearance of perpetual appraisal, as though one eye were always weighing what the other merely saw. He ate very little during the working day, and occasionally nothing at all, and then consumed quantities of figs and olives in the evenings with the focused efficiency of a man making up lost time. The stomach, when occupied, he said, makes claims on the attention.

I have known administrators, priests, and soldiers in my years at this sanctuary. I have known men of shrewdness, cunning, and courage. I have known only one man who was all three simultaneously, and who treated that combination as though it were the most natural thing in the world.

I found him in the archive room, at a different desk from Sostias's — Sostias had not yet arrived, but Akaros had evidently sent for the boy's lamp and relocated it without explanation, which I would understand shortly. He was standing over the open record of the previous year's gift dedications with his wax tablet in his hand and the expression he wore when something had offered him an aperture he intended to enlarge.

I said: The inner treasury was accessed in the night.

He said, still looking at the record: I believe so, yes.

I said: You believe so? You don't know?

He set the tablet down and turned to me. He said: I know that this delegation's chest arrived lighter than it was dressed to appear. I observed the six attendants set it down at the reception. They did so in perfect unison, which is not instinct — it is rehearsal. Men rehearse precise movements when the thing being moved is differently weighted than it appears. The chest moved as a chest of the second rank should move, but with a quality I can best describe as too easy — as though what was inside had been arranged to simulate a weight rather than constitute one. I did not go into the treasury last night. I was here, in the archive room, since the second hour of the night, working through these records. But I have been expecting this conversation since the delegation arrived, and I believe there is more to find. He straightened. I intend to go in now, in the early light, before the morning ceremony. I would be glad of your company. Your authority removes any procedural difficulty.

It was the first time he had asked me to accompany him to the scene, rather than presenting me with his conclusions afterward. I recognised, even then, that this was deliberate — that he wanted a witness, and that he wanted that witness to see what he saw, in the order he saw it. I did not yet understand why.

We went.

III.

The inner treasury in the early light had a quality of stopped time. The lamps had not been relit. The grey came through a high latticed aperture in the eastern wall and fell on the chest where it sat on its stone plinth, and on the two large storage amphorae beside it, and on the bare floor, in which our footfalls raised the faintest disturbance of dust that had otherwise been settling undisturbed since the previous evening.

Almost undisturbed.

Akaros crouched at the entrance before we reached the chest and pointed to the floor without speaking. I crouched beside him. In the dust, barely legible but unmistakable once seen, were the impressions of feet — not sandal prints, but the edge-weighted marks of a man moving carefully, in soft soles, attempting and largely succeeding at leaving no trace. They went from the door to the chest and back. A second set of impressions, heavier, partially overlaying the first.

Two men.

He straightened and went to the chest. He examined the fastenings without touching them for a long moment, then looked at the floor immediately beneath and around the plinth. He said: The plinth has been used as a working surface. There are small score marks here, along the edge — consistent with the fastenings being laid flat during the opening, then replaced. The dust displacement around the base is not uniform. The chest was moved several inches toward the examiner and returned.

He opened the chest.

I do not know what I had expected. What I found was silver vessels of adequate quality, and a quantity of gold leaf rolled onto wooden formers, and — filling the back third of the interior — folded linen cloths, smooth and anonymous-looking, packed with apparent care to maintain the silhouette of a full chest.

Akaros took each cloth out methodically and set them in a row on the plinth beside the chest. There were seven. He examined each one along the selvage — the woven edge — running his thumb along the inner border in the grey light, angling the fabric toward the aperture.

At the fourth cloth he stopped.

He held it up. He said, without emphasis: Come and look at this.

I came and looked. Along the selvage, worked into the weave at intervals of roughly two fingers' width, was a sequence of coloured thread — not decorative, nothing that would catch the eye of a casual examiner, but deliberate. A pattern. The sanctuary marked its own stores in this manner: each precinct maintained an inventory cycle identified by a particular thread sequence worked into the border of its linen goods at the weaving stage. I knew this because I had approved the procedure some years before, on the recommendation of the man who had designed it.

I said: Which precinct?

He said: I believe I know. I want Sostias to confirm it.

I said: Why Sostias?

He said: Because for the past two years, Sostias has been copying the consultation administration's records. He has been looking at these thread sequences in the inventory annexes without knowing that is what he was looking at. He will recognise it. And his recognition — a copying scribe who had no reason to study the system, but who absorbed it in the course of ordinary work — will be a cleaner identification than mine, which could be contested.

I looked at him. He was already refolding the cloth.

I said: You want a witness who cannot be accused of having manufactured what he found.

He said: I want several things. A witness is one of them.

He took the cloth. He returned the others to the chest in the order he had removed them and closed the fastenings. The chest, to any subsequent examiner, was as we had found it.

We went to the archive room.

IV.

Sostias identified the thread sequence without hesitation, and without knowing that his hesitation would have told a different story. He looked at the selvage, looked up at me, looked at Akaros, and said: That is the consultation administration's mark. I have been copying their inventory annexes for eight months. He paused. He said: That cloth did not come from Koryneia.

Akaros said: No. It did not.

He set the cloth aside and reached for the dedication record he had been examining when I found him. He said, to me: Sit down. I want you to read the inscription.

He had already transcribed it from the bronze plaque submitted by the delegation. He set the tablet before me and said nothing. I read it.

The city of Koryneia, having received the counsel of far-shooting Apollo in its hour of necessity, dedicates this gift in recognition of the arrangement made between the god's sanctuary and the city's representative, in accordance with the terms agreed, as full settlement of the understanding reached in the matter of the boundary stones of the Lykian field, whose removal the god's voice sanctioned, for which the city offers thanks and this treasury offers acknowledgment.

I read it again.

Then he said: Read it as a legal instrument rather than as a dedication.

The arrangement made between the god's sanctuary and the city's representative. The terms agreed. Full settlement of the understanding reached. The treasury offers acknowledgment.

I said: This is not a dedication.

He said: No. It is a receipt.

He explained it with the patience he reserved for moments when the thing was entirely obvious to him and he had decided to make it so to others before acting on it. The city of Koryneia was in dispute with its eastern neighbours over boundary stones — the Lykian field, a hillside of considerable agricultural and strategic value. A delegation had come to Delphi privately, some months before, and received a consultation. The oracle's answer had been ambiguous, in the way of all such answers, but someone had shaped the phrasing of how that answer was recorded so that it supported the interpretation most convenient to Koryneia. That someone worked in the consultation administration. He knew the archive's procedures because he maintained them. He had drafted an inscription that would read as a dedication to any ordinary eye, and as a binding contract to any eye that knew what to look for. If the arrangement was disputed, the inscription was proof. If it was never disputed, it sat in the archive as an ordinary gift.

He said: Whoever drafted it understood that the inscription would be transcribed by this office. He knew it would enter the permanent record. He wrote something that functions in either condition — disputed or undisputed.

He paused. He said: It is, structurally, not entirely unlike an oracle's answer.

Then he said: The cloth tells us who. The inscription tells us why. What Sostias heard last night tells us when the final payment was transferred — not in Koryneia, but here, in this sanctuary, in the small hours, when the chest was opened and its remaining contents reduced and the space restuffed with linen from the consultation administration's own stores. The payment was made in two parts. The larger portion was delivered separately, in transit. The remainder was verified and transferred here, in person, last night, by Therimander.

I said: The man inside the sanctuary — the one who received it —

He said: Is senior enough to have designed the archive marking system for the consultation administration's stores. Is trusted enough to have managed the phrasing of consultation records without oversight. Has been, for a considerable time, invisible precisely because he is known to be scrupulous.

He paused. He looked at the cloth.

He said: I need to add a notation to the archive before we speak with Therimander.

V.

He wrote it himself, in his characteristic hand, at the foot of the transcription of Koryneia's inscription. He wrote a cross-reference to the procedural record governing dedications made in settlement of prior agreements with treasury officials.

I said: There is no such procedural record.

He said: No.

I said: Then what is the cross-reference for?

He set down the stylus. He said: Anyone who reads this inscription and follows the cross-reference will find that the referenced procedure does not exist. This will not appear to be a clerical error. It will appear to be a deliberate notation pointing toward a gap — a thing that should be there and is not. This is how an archivist of this office signals, without stating, that a document is not what it presents itself to be.

He looked at me steadily.

He said: No one who benefits from this inscription being read as an ordinary dedication will want any reader to follow that reference. Therimander does not know the archive notation system. But the man inside this sanctuary who helped draft the inscription — he knows it precisely. When I tell Therimander that the cross-reference is in the archive, I am also telling him that his man here will understand what it means. And his man will understand that he cannot remove the notation without drawing attention to the removal. The trap is in the gap, not in the reference.

Sostias had been listening from his desk. He said, quietly: If the cross-reference is followed, and the procedure is not there — and if someone then looks at which dedications were handled by which officials in the relevant period — the gap leads directly to the person who should have created the procedure and did not.

Akaros looked at him. He said: Exactly so.

He said it with a quality I had not heard in his voice before — something between approval and satisfaction, the tone of a man whose estimation of someone has moved upward without warning. Sostias absorbed this without expression, which told me the boy was more composed than his earlier terror had suggested.

Akaros said, to Sostias: I want you to come with us to the gate.

VI.

We found Therimander at the outer gate of the sanctuary complex in the early grey light, two hours before the delegation had formally announced for their departure. He had his travelling cloak on. Polydoros and Euarchos were with him. The six attendants were already loading the carts.

He saw us coming — three of us now, which he had not expected — and something in the arrangement registered before he could prevent it. A flicker, only. Then the weathered stillness returned.

He said: Melanthios. A courtesy visit before we depart? You are kind.

I said, because Akaros had asked me to begin: we have a small administrative matter to discuss before you leave.

Therimander looked at Akaros for the first time directly, with the quality of attention men employ when determining whether a thing requires management or dismissal. His gaze moved to Sostias. Lingered there for a moment. The boy met it steadily, which I noted and which I think Therimander also noted, because something shifted in the quality of his attention.

He said: I do not know this man. He meant Akaros.

Akaros said: You looked for me among the reception party yesterday. I was not there. I was in the archive room. He paused. And this morning I was in the inner treasury. He allowed that to settle. I have transcribed your inscription into the permanent record, as is customary. It is a remarkable document. The draftsmanship is of a very high order.

Therimander said: I am pleased the dedication meets the sanctuary's standards.

Akaros said: It exceeds them in several respects. Particularly in the phrasing of the second clause — the arrangement made between the god's sanctuary and the city's representative, in accordance with the terms agreed. I have added a cross-reference in the archive, to the procedural record governing dedications made in settlement of prior agreements with treasury officials.

Something moved behind Therimander's eyes. The recalculation of a man who has planned for several outcomes and is now determining which one he faces.

He said, very quietly: There is no such procedural record.

Akaros said: No.

The word fell into the silence between them like a stone into water.

Therimander said: A cross-reference to nothing is a clerical error. If pressed, you would appear confused.

Akaros said: I would appear precise. The inscription is in the archive. The cross-reference is in the archive. The cities to the east of Koryneia — those most interested in the Lykian field dispute — have representatives who visit this sanctuary. Those representatives read archives. They follow references. They notice when a reference points toward an absence.

He said: Therimander. Consider who next reads the archive. Consider what they find when they follow the reference to the procedure that is not there, and then consider which official of this sanctuary ought to have created that procedure, and did not.

The weathered stillness had become something harder. Therimander looked at Polydoros, and a signal passed between them that required neither words nor any movement obvious enough to name — and then two of the six attendants at the carts turned from their work and came toward us. They were large men, and they came with the deliberate, unhurried pace of men who do not need to hurry. They stopped perhaps four paces from us, one to each side, and stood.

The courtyard was empty of guards at that hour. The nearest precinct was a full terrace away. There was nothing between us and whatever Therimander had decided to make of the next few minutes except the open morning air and the sound of the sacred spring somewhere below.

I will tell you plainly: I was afraid. I have been afraid before — in political disputes, in moments of personal danger — and I have learned that the chief value of fear is the precision it brings to one's attention. I was very attentive.

Akaros did not look at the attendants. He appeared to be examining the middle distance, in the manner of a man reviewing a calculation. Then he reached into the fold of his cloak and took out the linen cloth and held it where Therimander could see it.

He said: Look at the selvage.

Therimander looked at the cloth. He looked at it for a long time. He did not move.

Akaros said: The linen that padded your chest when it was repacked in the inner treasury last night. Not what padded it when it left Koryneia — that is long dispersed, and I do not care about it. This cloth came from the stores of the consultation administration of this sanctuary. The thread sequence in the border is the inventory mark of that precinct's supply cycle. It was placed in your chest here, last night, by the man who met you in the treasury — the man who completed the transfer you had come to complete. He repacked the chest with the linen available to him, which was this sanctuary's own linen, because he had not thought to prepare any other.

He said: This cloth is the record of a transaction that took place inside these walls, conducted by a senior official of this sanctuary using materials drawn from stores he administered. It is, in its way, as precise a record as an inscription.

He said: Sostias.

Sostias stepped forward. He said, directly to Therimander, with a steadiness I found remarkable in a boy of his age: I can confirm the thread sequence. I have spent eight months copying this precinct's inventory annexes. That mark belongs to the consultation administration. I would confirm this before any council this sanctuary could convene.

Therimander looked at the boy. He looked at the cloth. He looked at me.

I held his gaze. I did not speak, because my function in that moment was not speech but presence — the weight of the sanctuary itself, standing there in the grey morning, watching.

Therimander said to the attendants, very quietly: Stand down.

They moved back to the carts without ceremony.

He said to Akaros: You are asking for the name.

Akaros said: Written on a wax tablet, delivered to me before your carts reach the lower road. In exchange, your delegation departs without further record of this conversation. The archive notation stands — I cannot remove it without drawing more attention to it than leaving it produces — but I will ensure it is read by no one who would use it as a weapon against Koryneia before your city has had opportunity to address the boundary dispute by other means.

He said: I make no promises about the official. That is entirely separate and not yours to negotiate.

Therimander said: And if I decline?

Akaros said: Then I carry this cloth to the consultation administration's storeroom and ask the inventory keeper to account for it. The answer will take less than an hour. I will have the name by that route also, and the trail from the cloth to its owner will be considerably more legible to any external inquiry than a name quietly delivered this morning. He paused. Your man is already exposed. The only question is how thoroughly, and in whose hands the record ends up.

Therimander stood for a long moment. The pine trees on the slope above us had caught the full morning light, and the sacred spring, somewhere below the terrace, was making its steady noise, as it had made for centuries and would make long after every arrangement any man had negotiated on these slopes had been either honoured or broken or forgotten.

He said, finally: You will have the name within the hour.

He turned and walked back toward the guest quarters. Polydoros and Euarchos followed. The attendants returned to their carts. The two large men did not look at us as they worked.

Akaros said, to Sostias: You spoke well.

Sostias said: I told the truth.

Akaros said: In my experience, the two are not always identical in effect. This morning they were. He paused. I would like you to be present when we speak with the official. What you know may be required again.

VII.

The name arrived written on a wax tablet in Therimander's own hand, delivered by Polydoros without ceremony. It was Eudaimôn.

I want to say something about what it meant to read that name. Eudaimôn had served the sanctuary's consultation administration for twenty-two years. He was perhaps fifty-five, compact and high-browed, with the deep-set watchful eyes of a man accustomed to reading documents more carefully than other people read them. He had been, for all those years, known as a scrupulous keeper of the very records he had been corrupting, which was not coincidence — he had understood, from the beginning of the arrangement, that his protection lay in reputation, and had maintained that reputation as carefully as any man I have known. He had received, across three separate delegations over two years, gifts of gold that appeared in no inventory, because he was the man who maintained the inventory.

He was at work when we entered his chamber. Of course at work. It was his armour.

Akaros closed the door. He set the linen cloth on the writing table, directly on top of the consultation records spread there, and he set the wax tablet — Therimander's tablet, with the name — beside it.

He did not speak.

Eudaimôn looked at the cloth. He looked at the tablet. He read the name on the tablet as though verifying that it was his own. He looked at Sostias, standing just inside the doorway.

I watched Eudaimôn understand that Sostias had not been placed there by accident. The boy who spent his days copying consultation administration inventory annexes — who had been doing so for eight months — who stood there with the precise quality of a person who has already been asked what he knows and has answered. Eudaimôn was not a man who needed things explained to him.

He said, to Akaros, after a silence that lasted long enough to become its own statement: How much of the archive have you been through?

Akaros said: Enough.

Another silence. Eudaimôn reached out and touched the selvage of the cloth, running his thumb along the thread sequence in the unconscious gesture of a man verifying something he already knows to be true.

He said: The other two delegations. You found the discrepancies.

Akaros said: The gift records for the relevant periods have certain characteristics in common. A practised eye, given sufficient time and the relevant inventory annexes, finds them.

Eudaimôn withdrew his hand from the cloth. He looked at the lamp burning on the table between them. He said, quietly — and this is the question I have thought about since, the question that told me more about him than anything he might have confessed: The answers. The original phrasing — before I altered it. Was it genuinely ambiguous? Or did I—

He stopped. He could not finish it.

Akaros said: The original phrasing was ambiguous. You tightened it. Toward the required interpretation.

Eudaimôn said: I told myself it was a small distinction.

Akaros said: I know. It generally is, at the beginning.

The lamp burned between them. The consultation records lay beneath the cloth and the tablet, twenty-two years of careful, scrupulous, accurate work surrounding the small precise corruptions he had introduced, as a man might hide three stones in a long and otherwise honest wall.

Eudaimôn looked at Sostias one more time. He said to the boy, with a deliberateness that I understood was the only apology available to him: You should not have been put in the position of witnessing what you witnessed.

Sostias said: No. But I was.

Eudaimôn said nothing further. He turned to me and said: I will not compel the council to remove me. He said it with the dignity of a man who has one choice remaining and intends to take it cleanly.

I said: The council will convene this afternoon.

He said: I will be ready.

We left him there, with his lamp and his records and the cloth and the tablet laid among them, and he did not flee, and he did not protest, and the stillness in which he sat was the stillness of a man who had understood, in a single morning, that the thing he had spent twenty-two years making invisible had been made visible, and that the making-visible could not be undone, and that there was therefore nothing left to do but be still and let the consequences find him.

VIII.

The senior council convened that afternoon. The cloth was presented. Therimander's tablet was presented. The archive notation was read aloud, and the cross-reference was followed in the council's presence, and the procedure that was not there produced in the assembled faces the cold recognition of a gap — not confusion, but the specific understanding of a thing deliberately left open.

Eudaimôn appeared before the council. He spoke carefully and at length. He did not deny what the cloth and the tablet established. He resigned his position before the council was required to remove him, and he was escorted from the sanctuary before the end of the afternoon.

The three consultation records he had altered were reviewed and corrected and the affected cities notified. The Lykian field matter was reopened, to Koryneia's considerable difficulty for the better part of the following year. The chest, opened in formal ceremony the following morning, was received with appropriate solemnity, and the inscription was read aloud from the bronze plaque, and to those assembled it was a dedication. Sostias stood at the edge of the colonnade and listened to it with the expression of a person hearing words that mean one thing to everyone present and another thing entirely to himself.

He was, in the following months, given a more senior position in the archive's permanent staff. This was not coincidental. He was also, I think, the only person present at the ceremony who understood completely what the cross-reference in the archive meant, and what it would continue to mean to anyone who followed it in the years to come. He had, after all, helped design the trap. He had simply not known that was what he was doing, until Akaros showed him.

IX.

Afterward, I fell into step beside Akaros as he walked back toward the archive room.

I said: Eudaimôn.

He said: Yes.

I said: What do you make of him?

He walked for a moment without speaking. He said: He was thorough. He had constructed something that would have survived any ordinary scrutiny. He paused. He was also a man who had, I think, genuinely believed at the beginning that the distinction between an ambiguous answer and a slightly less ambiguous one was small enough to be negotiated with. He was not wrong that it was small. He was wrong about the direction in which small things travel.

I said: Do you feel anything for him?

He considered this seriously, which was the only way he considered anything.

He said: I feel that a man who can design a marking system subtle enough to survive twenty years of routine handling, and who can draft a legal instrument inside a religious dedication, and who understands the archive well enough to corrupt it without detection — that man, given different choices, or different circumstances, or perhaps simply a different moment of temptation — would have been the person who found the next Eudaimôn. He paused. That is not a comfortable thing to feel. I am aware of it.

I said: No. It is not comfortable.

We walked in silence. The pine trees on the slope held the late light. The sacred spring ran in its channel, as it had run since before the sanctuary existed and would run long after every record kept within it had crumbled to dust or been deliberately unmade.

He said: The archive is wrong in three places that I have found so far. I intend to correct them before the end of the week.

I said: The council has already—

He said: The council addressed the consequences. I intend to address the record. They are different things.

He returned to the archive room. The lamp was burning when I passed the door an hour later. It was burning when the precinct fell quiet for the evening. For all I know it burned the whole night through — he would not have considered the cost of oil a relevant consideration, set against the cost of inaccuracy.

I have written down what I witnessed. I have added no notation of my own. What notation is required has already been added, in the archive, in the hand of Akaros — cross-referenced to a procedure that does not exist and therefore asks, in perpetuity, a question that requires no one to answer it, only to find it, read it, and understand what it means that the answer is not there.

Sostias will find it, I think, if no one else does. He knows what to look for now.

It will be found. These things generally are.
