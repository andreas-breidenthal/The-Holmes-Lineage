A note on the record:
The chronicler who has followed this line from its beginning — from the scribe of Ur who first bent his gift toward the hidden discrepancy in the grain tally — arrives now at the period closest to his own time, and finds the record both more abundant and more difficult than any that preceded it. More abundant because the modern world documents everything; more difficult because the modern world also obscures everything, and the members of this line, from the second Sherlock onward, appear to have understood this and acted accordingly. What follows is assembled from private memoranda, legal archives, a handful of unpublished intelligence summaries, and one annotated copy of a family document that passed through several hands before reaching this account. Where the record is thin, the chronicle says so. Where it is absent entirely, the chronicle says that too.

The organisation that the great detective knew as Moriarty's empire did not die with its Napoleon. It had not died with any of its previous commanders, across the four thousand years this chronicle spans. It reorganised. It found new instruments, new languages, new legitimate fronts behind which the same essential structure — hierarchical, durable, insulated from prosecution by layers of apparent respectability — continued its operations. The Whitechapel counting house that appears in the records of the second Sherlock's time bore a different name in each subsequent generation. The address did not change. Addresses, unlike names, are harder to fabricate from nothing.

SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅱ

born 1893 — London, England

He was born in the autumn of 1893, during the Great Hiatus — the years when London mourned a detective who was not, in fact, dead. The boy's father was somewhere in the world, very much alive, though entirely unavailable, and known to the public only as a man who had gone over a waterfall in Switzerland with the most dangerous criminal mind in Europe. His mother, Miss Falkland, was a woman of considerable intelligence and deliberate social invisibility, who had managed, in the months preceding the birth and in the years following it, to keep her son's parentage a matter of inference rather than record. She raised him in Holborn, in respectable obscurity, among books and the careful habits of a woman who understood that knowledge, properly ordered, was the only reliable protection a person of limited means could acquire. The boy grew up knowing, in the way that such things are known — not told but observed, deduced, confirmed by the particular quality of his own mind when set against the minds of those around him — exactly who his father was. When his father returned from the Great Hiatus in 1894, the acquaintance was made; and when the Great Detective retired to Sussex in 1903, it continued — quietly, discreetly, and on terms that suited both men's instinct for operating without advertisement.

Watson encountered Holmes Ⅱ once in that same autumn of 1903, when the boy was ten years old and already possessed of that quality of watchful attention that the old doctor, who had spent twenty years in its company, could not mistake. He noted, in a private memorandum that survived among his papers, that the boy's eyes were his father's exactly — and that when introduced to a stranger, he had spent the first thirty seconds in a silence that was not shyness but assessment. Watson did not record what conclusion the boy had reached. He recorded that the stranger, a solicitor of his acquaintance, had been visibly unsettled by it. What Watson also noted, though he did not dwell on it, was that the boy had apparently just come from the Sussex Downs, and that he spoke of his father with the matter-of-fact ease of someone who had recently been in his company.

The record that follows is drawn from the surviving notebooks of John H. Watson II, son of Dr John Watson, who had been raised in the shadow of his father’s long association with the elder Holmes and who, by temperament and training, inherited both a habit of careful observation and a willingness to set down what he saw without embellishment. It is through his hand that the account of the following inquiry has been preserved, and his voice, where it intrudes, reflects the perspective of one who stood close enough to the work to witness its method, though not always close enough to grasp its full structure until after the fact.

Holmes Ⅱ was making his early way in the years before the Great War as an inquiry agent in the Holborn district, building a practice quietly and without publicity — operating under his own name without publicly connecting it to his father, who was alive and keeping bees on the Sussex Downs and whose name, attached to a son's shingle, would have invited expectations Holmes Ⅱ had no interest in managing. The work, in his experience, went better when the subject of an inquiry did not know they were being observed; and the subject tended to know very quickly if they understood they were dealing with the son of Sherlock Holmes. He was not his father in method, in any case; the flamboyant reconstruction, the dramatic disclosure, the audience arranged for maximum effect — these were not his ways. He was quieter, more patient, content to allow the truth to arrive at its own pace so long as it arrived. He was young, and his reputation was still forming; his clients in those early years were merchants, solicitors, and insurance companies willing to try an unknown quantity, and occasionally individuals who had found themselves in circumstances they could not account for and did not know where else to turn. When a case touched on something he could not place, he knew where to go. The old man on the Downs did not discourage these visits.

It was the insurance work that brought him, in the spring of 1912, to the case that would occupy the next two years of his professional life and confirm, beyond any further personal doubt, that the organisation his father had faced at Reichenbach had not ended there.

The matter began with a routine referral from a marine underwriter at Lloyd's — a firm of Holborn shipping factors, T. Hargreave & Sons, had filed three substantial claims in eighteen months for cargo lost in transit between the Port of London and Rotterdam. The losses were well documented, the manifests in order, the captain's depositions consistent and detailed. The underwriter had paid the first claim without suspicion, made enquiries before paying the second, and declined the third pending investigation. The enquiry that followed was, on its face, unremarkable: goods did vanish in transit, captains were not always honest, port officials were occasionally compromised. Holmes Ⅱ was asked to review the documentation and offer an opinion.

He began, as he always began, not with the claims themselves but with the pattern of the claims. He noted also that all three vessels had been loaded at the same Thames-side wharf, by the same gang of stevedores, under the supervision of the same wharfinger — a man named Crale, twenty-two years in the trade, unremarkable record, lodgings in Bermondsey. He noted that the manifest for each lost cargo had been countersigned, at the factor's office, by the same clerk. He noted that the clerk had resigned his position three weeks after the third claim was filed, giving no forwarding address.

Before following the clerk, he spent an afternoon at the wharf. Crale was a broad, settled man with rope-calloused hands and a particular habit, Holmes Ⅱ noticed, of keeping his eyes above the level of a conversant's face when speaking — not evasion, which tends inward, but the watchfulness of a man accustomed to monitoring something over another man's shoulder. Holmes Ⅱ asked him no questions about the lost cargoes. He asked him, instead, about the tidal conditions on the particular autumn evening when the second vessel had loaded: whether the water had been high enough to permit a lighter to come alongside the starboard bow without a pilot, and whether the dockmaster on that shift was the regular man or a relief. Crale answered both questions with a fluency and specificity that confirmed he had been present and attending carefully to details that a wharfinger with nothing to conceal would not have needed to retain. He also, in answering the second question, named a relief dockmaster whose name did not appear on any of the official logs for that evening. Holmes Ⅱ wrote down the name and thanked him and left, and Crale watched him go with the particular stillness of a man revising his assessment of what he had just disclosed.

The resigned clerk was, ordinarily, where the matter would have ended — a dishonest employee, a crooked wharfinger, a straightforward fraud. But Holmes Ⅱ was his father's son, and his father's son had not merely inherited a document: he had gone, in person, to the Sussex Downs, and had sat with the original Sherlock Holmes himself, and had been told directly, by the only man who had faced the organisation at its full height, what to look for and how to read it. Among what his father had told him was this: the organisation never operated through a single corruptible employee. It always operated through a corruptible employee, a legitimate commercial front, and a directing intelligence at a third remove from the actual fraud — insulated by two layers of apparent innocence from any consequence that might follow discovery. The clerk was layer one. Crale the wharfinger, Holmes Ⅱ suspected, was layer two. Layer three was elsewhere.

The relief dockmaster's name led him to a lodging house in Wapping, from which it led to a bill-discounting house in a court off Bishopsgate. It was in reading the bill-discounting house's incorporation documents — dry as dust, a solicitor's filing of forty lines — that the pieces settled. The ink on the solicitor's address stamp was a particular blue-black that Holmes Ⅱ had seen once before, on a letter his father had shown him in Sussex: a London commercial letter from the 1880s, preserved in the old man's files as an example of what the organisation's correspondence looked like at its respectable surface. The stamp was not the same firm — the name was different. But the distinctive formulation of the address, the specific abbreviation of the street name, the typographic habit of placing a full stop after each element — these were not coincidences. The counting house had changed its sign. It had not changed its compositor.

That led in turn to a counting house in Whitechapel, the senior partner of which was a man named Gruner — not the Baron Gruner his father had dealt with in 1902, but a cousin, it emerged, a man of careful commercial reputation and no criminal record, whose name did not appear in any public connection with the Hargreave frauds or with any of the eleven other insurance claims that Holmes Ⅱ identified, over the following eight months, as sharing the same structural signature. The Whitechapel address he brought directly to his father — who was on the Sussex Downs, retired but entirely alert — and the old man identified it without hesitation as the same counting house that had appeared in the background of two Moriarty-adjacent cases from the 1880s. Different names both times. Same address. His father had not forgotten it. He did not forget addresses.

He brought his analysis to the underwriter at Lloyd's, who brought it to a King's Counsel, who brought it before a special examiner appointed by the Board of Trade. Holmes Ⅱ did not appear at the examination. His name does not appear in any of the proceedings. Gruner's firm was wound up in December 1913; Gruner himself left for the Continent and did not return; Crale the wharfinger was convicted of fraud at the Old Bailey in February 1914 and sentenced to seven years. The network, as always, contracted but did not cease. The address in Whitechapel stood empty for six months, then acquired new tenants and a new name.

He lived quietly, worked methodically, and died in 1946, having seen the world he had been born into become something he could not entirely have imagined — and having had, throughout all of it, the singular circumstance of a father who outlived him. He predeceased the Great Detective by some eleven years. He never sought public acknowledgement of his parentage. That was, one imagines, exactly as both of them intended.

From a notebook found among his effects, undated, in a hand that tightened in the later pages:

I have been patient where he was quick, methodical where he was inspired, and I have occasionally been wrong in circumstances where he, I think, would not have been. I do not regard this as a deficiency. The work requires what it requires. What I notice is that the work does not leave off when I leave the office. Last night I was reading Gruner's correspondence at the dinner table and my wife said my name three times before I heard her. This is, I understand, the family condition. It is not entirely comfortable to inherit.

SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅲ

born 1914 — Sussex, England

He was born in 1914 in Sussex — the same county where his grandfather had retired the previous year to keep bees on the Downs, and where, accordingly, his earliest memories included a tall, spare old man who moved through the garden with the unhurried precision of someone accustomed to reading things at a distance. His first year of life coincided with the outbreak of a war that would kill a generation, and his childhood was shaped by the long aftermath of that killing: the silence in households where the men had not come back, the particular English habit of not speaking about things that had happened, the sense, pervasive in the twenties and thirties, that something fundamental had been broken and not yet repaired. He grew up in Holborn, in his father's household, a spare and watchful boy who read everything and spoke selectively. His father, Holmes Ⅱ, had himself grown up without the Great Detective's direct presence; he had come to know his own father late, and at a careful distance, and understood the value of that acquaintance precisely because it had been so hard to come by. He was not going to allow his son to be similarly deprived. The boy grew up knowing his grandfather — visiting the Sussex cottage, sitting with the old man on the Downs — and when the gift declared itself early, as it did, the explanation of what it was and where it came from came from its proper source, directly and without intermediary. The Great Detective, by then in his seventies and eighties, was entirely willing to provide it. He was not, as his grandson later observed, a man who found it easy to be retired.

He did not attend university. He was recruited instead, in the spring of 1936, by a department of the Foreign Office whose precise functions were not described in his letter of appointment, which was brief and which he was asked to destroy. He spent the next three years learning languages — German, principally, and enough French and Italian to be useful — and the related disciplines of close observation, documentary analysis, and the careful management of what other people believed about you. He was good at all of these. He was particularly good at the last.

In the years between his recruitment in 1936 and the outbreak of war, Holmes Ⅲ undertook, under the direction of the Foreign Office, a piece of misdirection so audacious that only a man of the family's particular temperament could have conceived it. He took the screen name Basil Rathbone and appeared, in two films produced in 1939 — The Hound of the Baskervilles and The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — as his own grandfather, rendering Watson's Victorian record with a fidelity that drew on something rather more than research: the man himself was eighty-five years old and living in Sussex, and had been consulted, in his characteristically oblique fashion, on whether the enterprise was worth undertaking. He had indicated, with the economy of expression that his grandson had grown up reading, that it was. When war came, the Universal Pictures series that followed updated the setting to the present conflict, and Holmes Ⅲ continued in the role — now, in effect, playing himself, a consulting detective operating against German agents in wartime Britain, under a name the public associated with fiction and the Intelligence Inner Council knew to be rather more than that. It was, by any measure, the most sustained and elaborate piece of cover that any member of the line had yet constructed. The original, still alive on the Downs, is not recorded as having commented on it. That, too, was characteristic.

One further acknowledgement is warranted. Nigel Bruce played Dr. Watson alongside Holmes Ⅲ in all fourteen films — a loyal, bumbling, good-hearted Watson who bore little resemblance to the precise and formidable man of the original record, but who brought to the role an affection for his subject that was entirely genuine. Bruce never knew that the man beside him on screen was the grandson of the very detective he was portraying — a detective who was, at the time of filming, alive and in his mid-eighties in Sussex — nor that the actual grandson of John H. Watson was working alongside Holmes Ⅲ in the field when the cameras were not rolling. Had anyone told him, it is difficult to imagine he would have believed it. It is equally difficult to imagine a more perfect illustration of the family's gift for operating in plain sight.

From this point the account that follows is preserved in the hand of Dr. James Watson, grandson of the original, whose record I have received and transcribed without alteration beyond the necessary preservation of form. He writes in the first person, and the events are as he observed and understood them at the time.

I had long been accustomed to the presence of Holmes Ⅲ in a professional capacity by the time of the matter I am about to record, though I had not yet learned to regard my own observations as anything other than provisional. He would often allow me to speak first, not from politeness, but because he wished to see what I would attend to without guidance. In this I sometimes failed, and sometimes succeeded, and he made no distinction in his manner, though I was not unaware of the distinction in his conclusions.

It was in the winter of 1942 that I first became aware that the matter at hand extended beyond the ordinary course of intelligence work. A series of intercepted communications had been placed before us, the paper still bearing the faint crease of the original folding and the slight roughness at the edge where it had been opened. I recall the texture clearly, for I held one sheet longer than the others: the ink had spread minutely into the fibres in a manner suggesting damp storage before dispatch. Holmes Ⅲ noted this without remark, though he turned the sheet once between his fingers as if confirming something not yet expressed.

“You see the direction,” he said, though I had not spoken. “Not where the money is going, but where it is allowed to be.”

I examined the papers again. The transactions did not align with the usual patterns we had come to expect. I asked him whether this suggested a diversion or a cover. He did not answer at once, but instead asked me whether I had ever seen a ledger where entries were balanced not against profit, but against exposure. I had not, and said so. He nodded once, as if confirming a small point.

We went to Whitechapel in January of the following year. The counting house occupied two rooms above a chandler’s shop, and the stair beneath us bore the marks of many repairs. I remember placing my hand on the banister: the wood beneath my palm changed from smooth, worn oak to something rougher and newer within the span of a single step. The smell of tallow rose from below, mingled with paper and a close, stale air that had no passage for circulation. The clerk who received us did so without hesitation. His manner was correct, his answers immediate.

Holmes Ⅲ asked for information regarding a bond certificate. The clerk replied without delay. Then Holmes Ⅲ asked whether their arrangements with their Geneva correspondent had altered in recent months, noting that certain restrictions had been reported in November.

The clerk paused. The pause was slight, but absolute in its quality. He then stated that the firm had no Geneva correspondent.

Holmes Ⅲ inclined his head and thanked him. We departed without further enquiry.

“You observed his certainty,” I said as we walked.

“No,” he replied. “I observed his instruction.”

“And the distinction?” I asked.

He did not answer immediately. Instead, he asked me whether a man could be certain of something he had not himself verified. I said that he could, if he trusted the source. Holmes Ⅲ then asked whether a man instructed to answer in a certain way could distinguish between a question he had been prepared for and one he had not. I conceded that he could not.

Only then did he explain that the clerk’s answer had contained an inconsistency: a denial of a connection that required prior knowledge to deny. The presence of that knowledge implied instruction, and instruction implied awareness within the organisation. The matter was therefore not merely financial.

Watson, I should record, questioned him further that evening, pressing him to clarify whether this implied a single source or a distributed arrangement. It was in this exchange that I realised the value of his persistence, for it compelled Holmes Ⅲ to distinguish between what was possible and what was probable. It was not the first time I had seen such a distinction drawn, but it was the first time I understood its necessity in practice.

In the weeks that followed, the pattern emerged in full. What began as isolated transactions resolved into a structure that had persisted across years, its parts changing while its form remained. I recall the moment when this became clear: not in a single revelation, but in the quiet alignment of records across different offices. The same names did not appear, yet the relationships between them repeated with an exactness that could not be coincidental.

Holmes Ⅲ made no declaration at that point. He simply set aside one sheet and drew another closer. The action itself was the conclusion: the matter had passed from uncertainty to structure. I understood then that the work was complete in its essential form, though not yet in its consequence.

The memorandum that followed was delivered without ceremony. Its effect was immediate, though its origin was not attributed. I record here only what I observed, and not what I was told of its reception.

The grandfather said, when I was twelve and had asked him how he knew when he had enough: you never have enough. You stop when the evidence permits a conclusion that is actionable, and not before. Theorising beyond the evidence is the one luxury the work cannot afford. — I have found this reliable. I have also found that knowing when the evidence permits the conclusion requires a quality of judgment that the instruction alone does not transmit. One acquires it, if one acquires it, by doing the work badly enough times to learn the difference. The grandfather did not mention this part. Perhaps it was implied.

SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅳ

born 1945 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was born in September 1945, six weeks after the Japanese surrender, into a household where the war's end had produced not relief but a particular quality of English silence that his American relatives found difficult to interpret and his mother had long since learned not to attempt to explain. His father's silences were not unhappy; they were the silences of a man who had spent a professional lifetime reading what other people concealed, and who had made, on arriving in America, a private decision to direct that faculty inward rather than outward — to observe his own life, for once, with the same attention he had previously given to the lives of others. He was, by his son's account, an exceptional father: present, attentive, and constitutionally incapable of pretending not to know things he knew, which meant that the usual parental fictions were unavailable to him and that the boy grew up in a household of considerable, occasionally startling, transparency.

He inherited the gift fully and expressed it in the language of his time and place, which was the language of the law. He read at Harvard, was admitted to the Massachusetts bar in 1969, and spent the early years of his career in a Boston firm of commercial solicitors before moving to the federal practice that would occupy the remainder of his professional life. His particular facility was financial fraud — cases built not on witness testimony or physical evidence but on documentation: the ten thousand pages of ledgers, correspondence, incorporation filings, and banking records that told, to a reader with the patience and the precision to follow them, a story entirely different from the one their authors had intended. He was not, his colleagues noted, a brilliant advocate; he did not perform in court with any of the theatrical authority that juries respond to. He was something rarer and, in the federal context, considerably more useful: a man who could read a fraudulent financial structure from its documentary residue the way his great-great-grandfather had read a criminal from his boots and his cuffs, and who could explain what he had found in terms precise enough to survive cross-examination by the best commercial barristers in New York.

There is, attached to his papers, a private record — compiled across several decades, in the spare and precise prose he brought to everything — of his childhood visits to the Sussex cottage. His father had taken him to England several times during the first decade of his life, and Holmes Ⅳ had understood, even as a small boy, that these journeys were not ordinary ones. The record he kept is not a diary; it has no dates and no sequence of entries. It is instead a set of recollections, written down at intervals as they became clear to him in retrospect: the smell of the cottage; the quality of the light over the Downs in different weathers; the old man's hands, which he remembered as precise and still; the bees, which were everywhere in summer and which his great-grandfather discussed with an earnestness that a child could follow, and that Holmes Ⅳ came to understand, much later, was not a simplification for the child's benefit but the genuine thing — the same complete attention brought to the same subject that had once been brought to criminal networks and ciphers and the unrecorded history of four thousand years.

What the record preserves most precisely is the quality of conversation. The Great Detective, in his final decade, did not perform for visitors, not even for the smallest ones. He asked questions. He listened to the answers. He asked more questions. Holmes Ⅳ, who was three or four or five years old, answered them with the directness of a child who had not yet learned the adult habit of managing what he disclosed. The old man noted what the boy said with the concentration of a man still in practice — not indulging a great-grandchild, but attending to an intelligence he had taken the measure of and found, in the characteristic manner of the line, worth attending to. Holmes Ⅳ could not have articulated this at the time. He articulated it, with considerable precision, in the private record he kept, across the long years of his career in the law. It was, he wrote, the most complete attention he had ever been given. He did not think he had ever been given it again, by anyone, in quite the same way.

In later years, Holmes Ⅳ's records note visits from Dr. Amelia Watson, a contemporary of his own generation and a direct descendant of John H. Watson. She had, for some months, been keeping a personal chronicle of Boston medical and legal affairs, which she shared with Holmes Ⅳ during discussions that ranged from clinical ethics to forensic procedure. It was in these sessions, often held over the clatter of a distant hospital ward or the rustle of financial files, that she first began to record the remarkable acuity with which he approached complex human behaviors and financial structures. The account she maintained — her own voice — would become a primary repository for the observation of Holmes Ⅳ in action, capturing his methods and temperament from a witness uniquely positioned to note both the gift and its occasional blind spots.

He married in 1972, late by his generation's standards, and said very little about it then or afterward. His wife, a woman of considerable private force, died young; and Holmes Ⅳ, who had not been demonstrative in his happiness, was similarly unrevealing in his grief, which I observed closely, noting the subtle shifts in his manner that a less practiced eye would have missed.

The case that defined the middle period of his career came to him indirectly, in the winter of 1977, through a referral from a colleague at the Securities and Exchange Commission who had noticed, in the wreckage of a collapsed Boston pension fund, something he could not quite account for — a structural anomaly in the fund's investment allocations that suggested, to his colleague's less developed but genuinely alert instinct, that the collapse had not been accidental.

Holmes Ⅳ took the files home over a weekend. I observed him as he handled the manila folders: the slight unevenness of the paper stacks, the faint acrid scent of ink, the snap of treasury tape as it was peeled away. He sorted the contents into piles with a methodical precision. Each folder was opened, touched, and examined before a conclusion was drawn. He paused, fingers lingering on one document, eyes narrowing, inhaling the faint tang of wax seal and paper dust — a pause I later realized marked the exact instant when the deductive chain began to coalesce in his mind. He asked me, unprompted: “Do you know who visited the Meridian offices in ’42?” It seemed peripheral, yet the answer unlocked the pattern: a single counsel linked through wartime correspondences to Aldgate Capital Partners.

Over three months, he compared files from five collapsed pension funds. Each time, he confirmed details physically — the texture of stationery, the embossing on seals, the precise ink flow — before mapping relationships among firms and directors. I could follow each step, seeing him eliminate alternatives, resisting premature conclusions until evidence allowed only one coherent narrative. When he finally wrote same counsel, 1942–1958: continuous in the margin, I felt the moment of clarity: pieces I had been watching fall into place simultaneously in his mind and on the table, the human calculation of greed and the protective maneuvers of Aldgate’s principals fully revealed.

He delivered his forty-seven-page memorandum with twelve supporting files; seventeen individuals were indicted, eleven convicted, and the firm dissolved. Holmes Ⅳ moved on immediately to other work, neither seeking acknowledgment nor deflecting it. The gift, exercised with singular temperament, had done its work — and I, Amelia Watson, had recorded it with the fidelity that only long acquaintance and professional attentiveness allow.

From a set of recollections compiled across several decades, found among his papers, written without dates or sequence:

The smell of the cottage: coal smoke and beeswax and, in summer, always the bees themselves, which were everywhere and which he discussed with the same attention he gave everything else — not simplified for a child, simply explained as if a child were a reasonable person who might find the subject interesting. I did find it interesting. I found everything he talked about interesting. I understand now that this was itself an aspect of the gift: to attend so completely to a thing that the thing becomes interesting to whoever is being attended to. My clients, in later years, have occasionally noted that I have a similar effect on documents. I am not sure this is a compliment, but I have not discouraged it.

Dr. Amelia Watson, who has kept her own account of these observations, adds in her margin notes: “He pauses before a single word in the ledgers, reads it aloud, then looks up as if testing my awareness — a seemingly trivial question, yet it always draws the truth out. He does not need dramatics; the clarity is in his attention.”

SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅴ

born 1970 — Boston, Massachusetts

He was his father's son in the way that the line had always produced its sons: not by any particular intention on either side, but by the operation of something in the blood that expressed itself, in each generation, as the same fundamental faculty in a new set of circumstances. His father had explained it to him, as his great-great-grandfather had explained it to Holmes Ⅲ, as a thing to be understood rather than celebrated — a tool with specific purposes and specific limitations, not a general superiority but a particular kind of sight that came with the obligation to use it for something other than personal advantage. Holmes Ⅴ absorbed this instruction and, by his own account, failed to live up to it in approximately the ways that his father had predicted he would: he was impatient with people who did not follow his reasoning; he was poor at concealing what he had concluded; and he had, as his daughter Charlotte observed in her less guarded moments, the intermittent quality of being entirely present and the more frequent quality of being present in body while absent in everything else, which was difficult to live with and impossible to argue with because he was, when challenged about it, always able to explain precisely where he had been and why.

He was educated at Harvard, like his father, read law, and then did not practice it — turning instead to the adjacent but less constrained world of policy analysis and congressional consultation, where the analytical faculty he had inherited could be applied to the large, complex, poorly documented systems of power and money that his generation had inherited from the Cold War and were dismantling, or believed they were dismantling, with varying degrees of success. He worked as a consultant to several Senate committees through the nineties and into the first years of the new century, with a reputation for finding the thing in a document collection that everyone else had looked at and not seen.

Among the papers and diaries that survive from this period, there appears a voice distinct from Holmes Ⅴ, that of Edward Watson, a contemporary and great-great-grandson of Dr. John Watson. Edward recorded his impressions and the actions of Holmes Ⅴ with meticulous care, providing his own perspective — at once observant and occasionally constrained by personal concern — and offering the account that follows.

In the autumn of 2001, in the weeks immediately following the attacks of September 11th, Holmes Ⅴ was engaged by the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence to assist in a review of pre-attack intelligence failures. I, Edward Watson, was assigned to observe and maintain a contemporaneous record of his methods and discoveries. He accepted the engagement with the private reservation that intelligence failures of that scale were rarely simple failures, and that the more productive question was usually not what had been missed but what had been noticed and not acted upon, and why.

He was given a basement room in a Senate annex building and seven hundred and forty-two boxes of interagency communications, most printed on thermal paper already beginning to fade. The faint acrid scent of aged thermal paper filled the air. Each document, when lifted, crinkled softly, a subtle auditory cue he seemed to use instinctively. He tapped edges, rubbed corners between thumb and forefinger, and held sheets to the light, noting slight differences in opacity and fiber texture. He explained once, obliquely, that he was observing the paper stock to detect anomalies. I watched him, recording both the process and my own impressions of how minute physical variations guided his thought.

Weeks passed. He compared documents, marking those with irregular stock or inconsistent annotations. One Tuesday, while barely touching his lunch, he paused mid-bite and regarded a cluster of delayed assessments as a set rather than a sequence. He asked, suddenly, “Did these three contractors ever brief the coordination unit directly?” The retired director of the unit answered without hesitation, “No,” adding unnecessarily that there would have been no reason to do so. That answer, unguarded, allowed Holmes Ⅴ to perceive a pattern: administrative delays had systematically protected specific contractors from scrutiny. The calculation was human, deliberate, and instrumental — greed and self-interest embedded in bureaucratic procedure.

He traced the contractors through nominee directorships, shared counsel, and offshore holdings until he linked them to a dissolved New York firm, substantiated by an SEC filing obtained via FOIA. Every inference — from paper characteristics to interview responses to filings — followed directly from observed facts. No assumption filled a gap. The moment of clarity arrived abruptly, the set revealing its hidden shape, and I recorded each realization as it unfolded, aware that my own understanding lagged behind his intuitions.

Holmes Ⅴ submitted a memorandum; the implicated contractors lost security clearances, though the public record omitted his role. He acknowledged the result quietly, turning attention to the next task, and privately recognized the gift in Charlotte before she herself realized it. I noted the precision, the human patience behind each calculation, and the temperament of a Holmes combining impatience with methodical care. My own entries, colored by concern for his endurance, preserve both his genius and his human limits.

SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅵ

born 1999 — United States

Of all the members of this line since the first Sherlock, he is the most difficult to write about. The difficulty is not a function of the record's scarcity, though the record is scarce; it is a function of a quality in the man himself that the record, where it exists, consistently reflects. He moves through documentation as he apparently moves through rooms: leaving the minimum necessary trace, attending to everything, committing to nothing that would fix his position for the benefit of anyone who might, at a later point, wish to know where he had been.

His father observed the gift in him early — earlier, perhaps, than in any generation since the first Sherlock — and transmitted to him, as his own father had transmitted it to him, the instruction that had accompanied it through the line: what it was, where it came from, what it was for. Holmes Ⅵ received this instruction and drew from it a conclusion that no previous member of the line had quite reached, or had reached and not acted on so completely: that the gift, properly exercised, operated most effectively in advance of the event rather than in response to it. His predecessors had been detectives in the fullest sense — readers of what had happened, tracers of what was hidden, resolvers of crises already in progress. Holmes Ⅵ was something different. He was a preventer.

The record also notes a contemporary presence: Olivia Watson, a great-great-granddaughter of Dr. John Watson, who maintained a parallel account of Holmes Ⅵ’s work. She observed his methods with the rigor and perspective of a trained chronicler, noting both his brilliance and the edges of his endurance. Her detailed records, which form the basis for the following account, reveal the gift in action, the surprises he provokes, and the human calculations he uncovers.

In the spring of 2021, a series of coordinated intrusions into the settlement infrastructure of several major financial institutions came to Holmes Ⅵ’s attention. I was present to observe and record. He approached the materials with his usual meticulousness. At his standing desk, he examined printed logs and digital feeds, noting timing, anomaly, and deviation. The faint warmth of the monitor screens on his hands and the crispness of printed reports under his fingertips were the physical starting points for observation.

On the fourth day, he asked a question his contact had not anticipated: whether the command-and-control infrastructure had been stood up before or after public reporting on the alleged state actor's tooling. The answer — eleven days after — was unguarded and immediate. Holmes Ⅵ’s gaze did not shift; the significance registered in a pause so slight I almost missed it. From this, he inferred that the infrastructure was a mimic, not an original: a calculated deception by human actors with access to open information, not a state-sponsored espionage. Observation, deduction, and logic aligned through sensory grounding and temporal data.

He triangulated the origin of the intrusion using three convergent threads: timing inconsistent with a state actor yet consistent with multi-time-zone commercial operation; the nature of extracted data, which revealed settlement exposure of a network he had mapped through historical filings; and a cryptographic signature embedded in the infrastructure, echoing a classified annex produced by his father years earlier. Each step built on precise, physically experienced evidence, resisting speculation until the data permitted a clear chain of reasoning.

He communicated his findings through a trusted channel with documentation sufficient for action, leaving no further explanation. Within six weeks, the operation’s true origin was corrected, financial disruption prevented, and public misattribution quietly amended. His reply to a note of thanks was brief, characteristic, and impersonal — three words without “you.”

The cost of this method was singular. Holmes Ⅵ did not sleep well, and had not since his mid-twenties. He kept notepads beside his bed, using the early morning hours when conclusions crystallized naturally. I observed him at two in the morning, pen in hand, reviewing digital logs and printed sheets, aligning the sensory evidence with emerging patterns. He managed this condition as a feature, not a flaw, allowing observation, deduction, and logical inference to function uninterrupted. My record captures both the method and its physical, temporal, and human context.

SHERLOCK HOLMES Ⅶ

born 2021 — United States

Son of Sherlock Holmes Ⅵ. The seventh to bear the name; the latest in a line that stretches back, through England and Rome and Greece and Egypt and Mesopotamia, to a scribe in the temple of Nanna at Ur who could read discrepancy in a grain tally as other men read weather, and who understood, in whatever language available to him across four thousand years of distance, that the gift was not his own — that it had come to him from somewhere behind him and was going somewhere ahead of him, and that his obligation was to exercise it faithfully and pass it on intact.

He is, at the time of this writing, a child.

The record will resume when there is something to record.

Children:
- Not yet recorded.

The chronicle ends here, for now.
The organisation whose operations this record has traced across four millennia — from the temple fraud at Ur to the financial networks of the present century — has not been destroyed. It has not been destroyed in any generation. It has contracted, reorganised, changed its name, moved its address, found new instruments, and continued. It will continue. The line that has opposed it has also continued, generation by generation, without public acknowledgement and without any formal appointment, sustained by nothing more and nothing less than the inheritance of a particular quality of mind and the understanding, transmitted from each member of the line to the next, of what that quality was for.

The scribe of Ur did not know that his grandson would carry what he had discovered. The seventh Sherlock does not yet know what he will discover, or what his children will carry forward from it.

The record waits.
