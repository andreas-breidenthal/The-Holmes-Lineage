MARCUS HOLCUS PRIMUS

~170 BC — Rome / Judea

Marcus Holcus Primus followed the Roman administration into Judea at the point when Rome’s involvement there was shifting from the indirect—treaty relationships, the occasional arbitration of dynastic disputes—toward the more direct engagement that the Maccabean revolt and its aftermath made inevitable. He served as a commercial representative and factor attached to Rome’s treaty relationship with the Hasmonean dynasty, responsible for facilitating commercial and legal arrangements between Roman merchants and the Hasmonean administration.

He was, inevitably, a student of the Jewish law—not from religious sympathy, but because a man who administers a jurisdiction without understanding its foundational legal system is an administrator working blind, and Holcus Primus was not a man who worked blind. He learned enough Aramaic to read the documents, enough of the Mosaic code to understand the disputes brought before him, and enough of the deep history of the Jewish people to appreciate that he was operating in a place where the past was not background but active force. He found Judea the most intellectually demanding posting of his career, and said so in the only letter of his that survives.

These pages are preserved from the journal of Titus Varro, attached to Holcus Primus as a clerk and scribe during that period. Varro was not trained in law but in record-keeping and measure; his duty was to observe, to note, and to preserve. His account is imperfect in places, for he often records what he understood rather than what he witnessed in full, yet his notes remain the closest surviving record of the matter that follows.

Certain Events Occurring in the Second Year of Our Factor's Assignment to the Hasmonean Administration

The valley road from Jerusalem to the settlement they called Kefar Adon smells, in the month of Adar, of dust and dead olive wood. The rains that should have come had not come for the third year running, and the trees along that road stood in postures of supplication, their grey branches reaching at angles that put me in mind of beggars with upturned hands. Marcus Holcus Primus walked ahead of me at his customary pace, which was to say a pace no man of normal disposition could sustain for long without complaint, his dark cloak gathering the yellow dust of the valley as though he were collecting evidence of the road itself.

I had not yet understood, in those early months of my attachment to him, that his pace always quickened when his mind was working.

The matter had come to us by an unusual channel.

It was not the Hasmonean administration that brought it. It was not the Temple authorities, though they had their own interests. It came, as I have since learned so many of the more consequential matters come, through a crack in the wall—a single document, misfiled, that arrived among a consignment of commercial records requiring Holcus Primus's countersignature as factor.

He had been working through the consignment at the long table in our Jerusalem quarters, the oil lamps arranged with the exactitude he always demanded—three positioned to his left to illuminate the script, one behind his shoulder for the seals—when he set down his stylus and said, without looking up, "Varro."

"Yes."

"Come here."

I came. He held the document flat with two fingers, a gesture of a man touching something he does not wish to disturb.

"Read this aloud to me. Slowly."

The document was a temple contribution record from a district administration. A quarterly accounting, in Aramaic, of the half-shekel contributions from the farming settlements of a valley region north of Jerusalem. I read it aloud with what care I could manage, for my Aramaic was serviceable but not elegant.

"Again," he said, when I had finished. "The second column."

I read it again.

"Stop." He touched the document. "The second entry. What does it say the harvest yield was, for the settlement of Kefar Adon, in the third quarter?"

I looked. "Three hundred and forty measures of barley."

"Now look at the fifth entry. The contribution assessed."

"Eleven shekels."

He sat back in his chair, which he rarely did. When Marcus Holcus Primus sat back in a chair, I had learned, it meant he had encountered something that required stillness before motion.

"Varro," he said, "I am going to explain something to you about Mosaic tax law, because you will not understand what I am about to do unless you do."

I sat. This was another thing I had learned: when he said he was going to explain something, there was no shorter course than to sit and receive the explanation in full.

"The Mosaic code," he said, "is not a Roman system. It does not operate on fixed assessment. The half-shekel is a religious obligation—each person, each household—but the agricultural contributions to the Temple treasury, the first-fruits, the tithes of the produce, these are calculated against yield. Against what the land actually gave. A prosperous harvest produces a larger contribution. A failed harvest produces a smaller one." He paused. "This is, from a purely administrative standpoint, a more sophisticated system than most Romans credit. It builds adjustment directly into the mechanism."

"Yes," I said.

"The contribution recorded here—eleven shekels—is the contribution appropriate for a harvest of three hundred and forty measures of barley."

"Yes. So recorded."

"The problem," he said, "is that I know what the harvest of Kefar Adon was in the third quarter. I know it because I have read every commercial record from this valley district for the past eighteen months, as part of my treaty assessment work. No Roman factor in Judea has read those records as carefully as I have." He looked at me with the expression I would come to know well: not pride, but the cool satisfaction of a man whose preparation has met its occasion. "The harvest of Kefar Adon in the third quarter was not three hundred and forty measures. It was one hundred and twelve."

I stared at him.

"The yield recorded in that document," he continued, "is false. Someone has tripled it, very nearly, on the official Temple record. Which means the contribution collected from the settlement was calculated against a yield the land never produced. Which means, Varro, that the farmers of Kefar Adon paid a Temple contribution of eleven shekels in a year when the law—the very Mosaic law the Temple administration exists to uphold—entitled them to pay perhaps three."

The smell of the oil lamps was very strong in that room. I remember it precisely: the particular heaviness of the Judean oil, darker than what we used in Rome, with a bitterness underneath the smoke.

"Who recorded these figures?" I asked.

"That," he said, picking up his stylus, "is what we must determine."

I will not set down in full the three weeks that followed, because much of it was work of the kind that makes for poor reading—the examination of records, the cross-referencing of district accounts, the patient comparison of figures against figures. Holcus Primus did not sleep well during those weeks. He ate little. He paced the floor of our quarters at hours when the night was absolute, and once, when I woke at some dark interval to find him still at the table, I asked if I should bring him wine.

"I don't drink wine when I am thinking," he said. "Bring me that other document. The second quarter, the western settlements."

This was his vice and his stimulant both: the problem itself. I have known men who required poppy, or the games, or the company of certain establishments to manage the tedium between their moments of purpose. Holcus Primus required only the next problem. The absence of one was the only true deprivation he suffered.

The fraud, when he had assembled it fully, was this:

A district tax official—a man named Yehoshua bar Eleazar, who administered the collection of contributions from eleven settlements across the valley region—had been, for a period of no less than four years, systematically recording false harvest yields. Always upward. Always in the same settlements. The effect was to inflate the assessed contributions, which were then collected from the farmers in full.

The question of where the difference went—between what the farmers paid and what the Temple records showed as received—was answered by the records themselves, once Holcus Primus had laid enough of them side by side. The official Temple receipts showed the full falsified amount as collected and received. But the actual deposits, reconstructed from the treasury records, were smaller. The gap was consistent. The gap was, in every quarter over four years, almost exactly the amount that Yehoshua bar Eleazar had drawn in administrative expenses against his district accounts.

"It is elegant," Holcus Primus said, not with admiration, but with the dispassionate recognition a craftsman gives to another's technique. "He inflated the assessed contribution. He collected the inflated sum from the farmers. He then under-deposited at the treasury, recording the correct—that is, the falsified—figure on the receipt, and drew the difference as administrative expense. Both sets of records look internally consistent. He could only be caught by a man reading both sets against a third source he didn't control."

"The commercial yields," I said. "Which you had."

"Which I had." He set the documents aside. "And which no Temple administrator would have had reason to consult, since the commercial records of Roman trading activity are not within their remit."

We were in a wineshop near the lower market, because Holcus Primus occasionally conducted his thinking in public places, finding, as he once explained to me, that the noise of human activity served as a useful counterweight to excessive abstraction. Around us the city moved in its ancient rhythms—the calls of vendors, the argument of merchants, the bell of a passing cart.

"What do we do with it?" I asked.

He looked at me with the expression of a man who has been waiting for precisely that question.

"First," he said, "we go to Kefar Adon."

The settlement was smaller than I had imagined. I do not know what I expected from a place that had appeared so often in the documents, but I had perhaps given it more weight in my mind than it possessed in stone and timber. There were perhaps forty households, arranged on the floor of a valley where the hillsides showed the terracing of ancient agriculture—the cut marks of generations of labour in the rock. The olive trees on the terraces were old and gnarled and, in this third dry year, showing the brittle grey of trees that are considering whether to die.

The village elder who received us was a man past sixty, with the burnt and compressed quality of someone who has spent his life outside, a white beard and hands that looked as though they had been shaped by the tools they'd held. His name was Shimon. He spoke some Greek, which eased matters, and he looked at us—a Roman factor and his clerk, arriving unannounced in a drought year—with the wariness of a man who has learned to expect that official visits bring complications.

Holcus Primus did not begin with the fraud. This was another of his qualities: he gathered before he disclosed, always. He asked about the settlement, about the valley, about the state of the harvest over recent years. Shimon answered with the precision of a man accounting for disaster—the yields falling across three seasons, the water table dropping, the eastern terraces already given up as unusable.

"And the Temple contributions?" Holcus Primus asked, as though it were an afterthought. "The assessments—are they current?"

Shimon was still for a moment.

"They are paid," he said.

"I know they are paid. I am asking whether the community can sustain them."

A longer silence. The sound of children somewhere behind one of the houses. The creak of a wooden water-frame in the wind.

"We sustain them," Shimon said.

Holcus Primus looked at him for a long moment. "Tell me about the administrator. Bar Eleazar."

Something crossed Shimon's face—not fear, exactly, but the near-relative of it.

"He is the district administrator."

"Yes. And what else?"

The old man looked away, at the dry terracing on the hillside. Then, with the manner of a man making a calculated decision, he said: "He has been lenient, with us. In the hard years."

Holcus Primus said nothing.

"The assessment, by law, should have been higher. In a good year. He recorded... he wrote the figures carefully."

"He recorded a lower yield than you produced," Holcus Primus said. "In the good years. So that when the law calculated your contribution, the calculation was more favourable to your community than it should have been."

Shimon turned back to look at him. His expression was now very careful.

"And in the bad years," Holcus Primus continued, his voice carrying no judgment whatsoever—he might have been discussing the theory of the thing in an abstract—"he recorded a higher yield than you produced. So that the contribution assessed against you would be what you had already established you could pay, rather than the very small amount the law would have calculated against your actual failed harvest."

The old man said nothing.

"He was," Holcus Primus said, "in effect, smoothing your contribution over time. He borrowed from your good years against your bad ones. He extracted the excess from the good years through his expense accounts, keeping the balance he had effectively advanced to you during the failures." He paused. "It is not, from a certain perspective, entirely dissimilar to how a competent Roman banking factor manages accounts across a fluctuating season."

Shimon bar-whatever-his-name-was—I confess I did not catch his full name—looked at the Roman factor before him with an expression I had not seen directed at Holcus Primus before, and have thought about since.

"If the Temple administrators discover this," the old man said quietly, "it is not only bar Eleazar who is ruined."

"I know," said Holcus Primus.

We stayed that night at the settlement. They gave us what hospitality they had, which was not nothing, because the people of this country understand hospitality as an obligation older than their other laws, though it clearly cost them. Holcus Primus ate the food set before him and asked questions about the valley and the farming techniques and the old water-cisterns cut into the hillside that were no longer sufficient, and to watch him you would have thought he was a man on a visit of agricultural curiosity, with nothing more pressing on his mind.

After the meal he walked with me along the terrace road above the settlement, in the dark, where the city lights of Jerusalem were just visible as a pale glow to the south.

"You have established the fraud completely," I said, because by then I knew my role in these conversations. I was his Varro, which is to say I was the man whose function was to say the obvious thing, so that he could then say the true thing.

"Completely," he agreed.

"And Yehoshua bar Eleazar has been defrauding the Temple treasury for four years. The evidence is irrefutable."

"It is."

"And yet."

He stopped walking. Below us the settlement was quiet, its small fires guttering in the wind that came up the valley after dark.

"The law," he said, "is a blunt instrument, Varro. Every lawyer who has ever lived has known this. The Mosaic code knows it too—it is built into the structure of the thing, if you read it carefully enough. There is provision upon provision for the widow, the orphan, the foreigner, the poor. The architects of that code understood that a law applied with perfect uniformity against a world that is imperfect in its distribution of disaster is not justice. It is mathematics."

"He stole," I said.

"He administered," Holcus Primus said. "He administered a system that would otherwise have driven this community off their land in the second year of the drought. And he extracted a fee for doing so, which is—from a Roman commercial standpoint—entirely normal." He paused. "He stole from an institution that is rather well-resourced. He stole in amounts that never drew attention precisely because they were calibrated not to draw attention. And he used what he took to sustain his own household while doing so. He is not a good man. He is, however, a useful one, and his arrangement has kept forty households on land their families have farmed for three hundred years."

"And if we report it?"

"If I report it," he said, "bar Eleazar faces the full weight of the Temple administration's judgment, which will be condign and severe. The fraudulent contribution records will be corrected. The back contributions owed—the gap between what this community paid and what they should have paid in the good years—will be assessed and collected. Against a community that has nothing." He looked at the pale glow to the south. "They will not survive it."

I stood beside him in the cold Judean dark and felt the weight of what he was carrying with what I can only describe as something like respect.

"What will you report?" I asked.

"I will report," he said, "that I have completed my review of the valley district commercial records and find no irregularities that fall within the scope of the Roman treaty administration's oversight." He turned back toward the settlement. "Which is technically true. The fraud is a matter entirely between the Temple treasury and the Temple's own district administrators. It is not a Roman matter. My remit is commercial treaty compliance, and the treaty's commercial provisions are satisfied."

"And bar Eleazar?"

Holcus Primus was quiet for a moment.

"I will speak with him. Tomorrow, before we return to Jerusalem. I will explain to him precisely what I found, and precisely how I found it, and I will make clear to him that the mechanism he constructed is visible to any man who reads the right three sets of records in combination." He paused. "He is not a foolish man. He will understand the implication."

"Which is?"

"That the next administrator who reviews those records may not have my reasons for discretion."

I met Yehoshua bar Eleazar once, briefly, at the district office where Holcus Primus arranged their meeting. He was a man of middle age, with the professional smoothness of someone who has spent years navigating the space between institutions, and when Holcus Primus concluded his account of what he had found, bar Eleazar went very still, in the manner of a man who has just understood that the ground beneath him is not what he thought it was.

I did not hear all of what passed between them, because Holcus Primus had sent me to wait outside, which was his way of telling me that the conversation was not for the record.

What I can say is that when Holcus Primus emerged, perhaps half an hour later, his pace was the slow and even one—not the quick pace of a working mind, but the steady pace of a matter concluded.

On the road back to Jerusalem he said, without preface: "You will note in your record that the review of the valley district accounts was completed and found to be in order with respect to all matters within our administrative jurisdiction."

"Yes," I said.

He walked for a time in silence. The dust rose around us in the afternoon wind, and the olive trees stood in their postures of endurance on the dry hillside.

"There is a form of intelligence," he said, as though continuing a conversation he had been having privately, "that mistakes the law for the purpose of the law. It is the most common error of trained minds. The law is a mechanism, Varro. A mechanism built to serve an end. When the mechanism and the end diverge—when following the mechanism precisely would destroy the end it was designed to serve—a man of genuine reason is obligated to recognize the divergence."

"Even when the mechanism is the law?"

"Especially then," he said. "Because the law is the mechanism most likely to be mistaken for the principle itself."

I wrote that down. I write everything down, as any clerk must. But I wrote that with more care than most things, because I thought I understood, then, why I had been attached to this particular man.

The matter was not spoken of again between us. The official record of that assignment, filed with the Hasmonean administration and with the Roman commercial office in Rome, notes that the valley district accounts were reviewed and found in compliance. The full account—the documents, the comparison of yields, the journey to Kefar Adon, the conversation I waited outside of—exists only here, in these pages, which I do not expect any official eye to read.

I note, for my own satisfaction as a recorder, the following, which seems to me significant:

In the year following our visit, Yehoshua bar Eleazar resigned his position as district administrator, citing reasons of health. His replacement, a young man from a Pharisaic family, administered the valley district by the letter of the law with strict and impartial precision.

The settlement of Kefar Adon was assessed, in the following year's contribution, correctly against its actual yield, which was again poor.

I do not know what became of the settlement after that. I was reassigned with Holcus Primus to a posting further north, and the valley district passed out of our records and therefore out of my knowledge.

I hope, for what that is worth in a document no one official will read, that they managed.

Here the account of Titus Varro ends, as it pertains to this matter. A subsequent notation in the margin, in a different hand and apparently a later date, reads only: the valley settlement is not recorded in the municipal rolls of the following census. This notation is unsigned.
