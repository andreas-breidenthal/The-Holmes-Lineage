HOLKOS THE ELDER

~1330 BC — Athens, Greece

He was called the Elder to distinguish him from the son who would bear his name and surpass him — a distinction that pleased him, he is said to have remarked, since a father whose son exceeds him has succeeded in the most important project of his life. Holkos the Elder managed the family’s trading connections through the reign of the Mycenaean palace system’s last confident generation, before the pressures that would eventually destroy it had become visible in their full severity.

He was an assessor of men as much as of goods — the two being, in the palace economy of Mycenaean Greece, largely the same problem. The wanax’s administration required constant evaluation of the reliability of dependent producers, the honesty of factors, the genuine versus the stated capacity of craftsmen and farmers to deliver what they had promised. Holkos the Elder conducted these assessments with a speed that surprised those being assessed, because long practice had given him a precision of reading that most men did not know was possible and that he could not, if asked, have easily explained.

The record that follows is preserved by the scribe Phaedimos, whose role in the chamberlain’s office was to maintain accounts of negotiations and private meetings. His notes are unusually detailed in description, though he often remarks that he did not understand the full implications of what he recorded until after the events had concluded and Holkos explained them to him in private.

The Account Of Phaedimos

Set down from my own notes and from what Holkos the Elder later chose to explain to me — though he was careful, as always, to explain only as much as served his purposes.

The morning it began, the oil lamps in the scribal hall were burning the cheap stuff — the pressed lees of the second pressing, not the clean oil kept for the wanax’s own chambers — and the smell of it sat in the back of the throat like a complaint. I had been at my tablet since before the light came properly through the slit windows, copying the season’s grain assessments from the preliminary records onto the master ledger, when Drosos came in.

Drosos was the kind of man the palace produced in abundance: capable, presentable, possessed of sufficient intelligence to execute instructions without requiring them to be repeated, and deeply untroubled by questions that exceeded his instructions. He served as collection officer for the smith-bronze quota in the district of the lower valley, which meant he was responsible for ensuring that the dependent farmers who had been allocated bronze ingots for agricultural tools returned the expected portion of finished implements to the palace stores. It was a position that required the counting of things and the filing of accounts. Drosos was very good at counting things.

He dropped a tablet on the table beside me with the particular firmness that means a man believes he is doing something important.

“There is an irregularity,” he said. “In the account of Kerkyon of the River Farm.”

I looked at the tablet. It was a secondary accounting — a field officer’s personal record, not a master document — and it showed Kerkyon’s registered bronze obligation, the ingots issued, and the shortfall in returned implements: eighteen bronze points for ox-goads, undelivered.

“Substantial,” I said, because it was.

“I have filed the accusation with the chamberlain’s office. Kerkyon will be summoned.” Drosos permitted himself the smallest possible expression of satisfaction. “I thought you should know, since you will be preparing the formal notation.”

After he left, I sat for a moment with the smell of bad oil and the sound of the building settling in the morning heat — the particular groan of the storage corridor’s wooden beam that I had learned to anticipate every summer, regular as a gate-hinge suffering from the same seasonal complaint. I noted the tablet’s contents in my daily record, as my function required. I did not yet think there was anything unusual about the matter.

I would not have brought it to Holkos the Elder at all, except that I happened to encounter him that same afternoon in the corridor outside the chamberlain’s office, where he had come to review a separate matter concerning the reliability of a factor in the eastern olive district. He had the look he sometimes wore when his mind was occupied but his body was bored with standing still — a kind of coiled stillness, like rope before the tension is applied. He asked me what I was carrying.

I told him about Kerkyon. It was the sort of thing one mentioned in passing.

He asked to see my notes.

He read them in the way he read everything — not scanning but consuming, the way a smith looks at metal rather than the way an ordinary man looks at metal. Then he handed them back.

“Where is the registered obligation written?” he said.

“In Drosos’s field tablet, which—”

“Not Drosos’s field tablet. I mean the master register. The palace record of what Kerkyon was assessed to owe this season.”

I thought about it. “That would be in the preliminary assessment rolls. Which I was copying this morning.”

Something in his expression changed in a way I could not then have named, though I understand it now as the difference between a man who is merely interested and a man who has seen something that the rest of the world has not yet seen. “The preliminary assessment rolls,” he said, “that you were copying onto the master ledger this morning. Which is to say, the preliminary rolls have not yet been superseded.”

“Not until I finish the master transfer. They remain the governing document until then.”

“Then,” Holkos the Elder said, with the mildness of a man stating something he considers obvious, “I think we should look at them.”

He required two things of me that I found, at the time, peculiar.

The first was that we visit Kerkyon’s farm before we looked at the rolls. I pointed out that the farm was below the lower valley road and that the afternoon was already advanced. Holkos observed that the afternoon was indeed advanced, which was precisely why we should go now, before Drosos’s official summons arrived in the morning and Kerkyon spent the night believing himself a criminal. I did not follow the reasoning but I had learned, by then, that failure to follow Holkos’s reasoning was not evidence that the reasoning was wrong.

The farm sat in a crook of the river where the bank had built up good alluvial soil over generations. The kind of farm that had clearly been managed carefully for a long time — the field boundaries straight, the drainage channels maintained, the trees at the edge of the cultivation line properly pruned. Kerkyon himself was a man of perhaps forty-five years, built with the particular solidity of men who have done physical work since childhood and never quite stopped. He came out of the barn when he heard us on the path and stood with his hands at his sides in the posture of a man who does not yet know whether he should be afraid.

Holkos the Elder looked at him for perhaps the space of four heartbeats.

Then he walked past him into the barn.

I followed, because I did not know what else to do. The barn smelled of straw and animals and the particular sharp mineral smell of worked bronze, because in one corner there was Kerkyon’s smithing area — a small operation, the kind a marginal farmer maintains to produce the implements his allocation requires — with the tools, the fuel store, the water basin, and the finished work waiting for collection. I counted the waiting implements. I counted them again. I was not a specialist in bronze-work quotas but I had been copying assessment numbers all morning and the figures were fresh.

Holkos the Elder was doing the same thing, but he was also doing something else: he was examining the actual condition of the implements, the finish of the work, the length of time the metal had been sitting in that corner. He picked up one of the ox-goad points and looked at it in the light from the doorway. He set it down. He looked at the fuel store — the quantity remaining, the marks of what had been used. He looked at Kerkyon’s hands.

“You finished early this season,” Holkos said to the farmer. Not accusatory. Simply observational, the way one notes that a cloud has moved.

Kerkyon blinked. “The bronze worked well. Good pour. I had it done before the feast of the second month.”

“And when was the collection officer last here?”

“Drosos? He came in the first month, at the start. To check the ingots were received.” A pause. “He wrote something. He always writes something.”

On the walk back up to the palace, as the light went orange over the hills and the air began to carry the first suggestion of evening cool, I asked Holkos what he had seen.

He was quiet for long enough that I thought he would not answer. Then:

“Tell me what you observed in that barn.”

“The implements were present. In sufficient quantity.”

“In sufficient quantity,” Holkos agreed. “More than sufficient quantity, in fact. Kerkyon completed his quota and then continued working because the conditions were favorable. He is that kind of man — the farm is that kind of farm. When work goes well, he does not stop. He has three additional points beyond his required number sitting in that corner.”

I considered this. “Then the accusation is simply wrong. Drosos miscounted.”

“Drosos,” Holkos said, “did not miscount.” He said it with the particular precision he used when he wanted a sentence to carry weight. “A man who miscounts leaves the error in the numbers. A man who is wrong in a different way leaves the error somewhere else.”

I did not understand him then. I have since learned that when Holkos the Elder said something I did not understand, the correct response was to hold it and wait.

We returned to the scribal hall in the early evening. The lamps were lit — the good oil now, for the evening work, cedar-clean and steady — and I retrieved the preliminary assessment rolls from the shelf where I had left them. Holkos sat across from me and I read him the relevant figures: Kerkyon’s name, his farm designation, the ingots issued, the registered obligation.

Holkos asked me to read the obligation figure again.

I read it.

“Now,” he said, “look at your own morning notes. What figure did Drosos’s field tablet show for the registered obligation?”

I looked. I looked again.

The figure in Drosos’s field tablet was larger. Not enormously — not a number that would announce itself as a fraud to a casual eye. But larger. Eighteen points, where the preliminary assessment roll showed twelve.

I said: “That is not the same number.”

“No,” Holkos agreed. “It is not.”

“Drosos’s tablet shows—”

“Drosos’s tablet shows what Drosos chose to write on Drosos’s tablet.” Holkos leaned back. In the lamp-light his face was calm with the specific calm of a man who has solved an equation and is waiting for the other person to check the working. “The master register has not yet been completed — you told me so yourself. The preliminary rolls are the governing document until the transfer is finished. Which means the registered obligation exists in two places at the moment: the palace preliminary roll, and Drosos’s own field record. And the numbers are different.”

“Drosos inflated the obligation,” I said. Slowly, because I wanted to be certain I was not constructing something that was not there. “He wrote a higher number in his own record than the assessment actually requires. And then he waited until he could file an accusation of shortfall — a shortfall that only exists against his number, not the real one.”

“Kerkyon,” Holkos said, “would have been summoned. He would have protested his innocence. Drosos would have produced his field tablet, which is the document of record as far as the collection officer’s office is concerned. Kerkyon would not have known which version of the number was the true assessment — farmers are not generally invited to inspect the palace rolls. In the confusion, the palace would have taken the implements Kerkyon had made beyond his quota — those three additional points — and Drosos would have...”

He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to.

“Sold them,” I said. “Or traded them. Bronze is bronze.”

“Bronze is bronze,” Holkos agreed. “It has been, I would estimate, a reliable arrangement. Kerkyon is almost certainly not the first.”

The lamp flame moved in a draft from the corridor, throwing brief shadows across the tablet in my hands. Outside, somewhere in the storage buildings, that wooden beam made its evening complaint — the same sound it made every night, faithful as a bad conscience.

The question of what to do with the information occupied me considerably less than it seemed to occupy Holkos the Elder.

I assumed, in my straightforward scribal way, that we would take the discrepancy to the chamberlain. There was documentary evidence. There was a clear comparison between the preliminary roll and the field tablet. The conclusion was not difficult to reach.

Holkos said nothing for a time. He had the ennui on him now — the other version of himself, the one that set in after the problem was solved, when the world became temporarily uninteresting. He sat with one hand flat on the table and looked at the lamp as if it had said something disappointing.

“The chamberlain,” he said at last, “will want to know how I arrived at the discrepancy.”

“You observed it from my notes, and from our visit to the farm, and from—”

“From an informal visit to a farm that no official process authorized, conducted at an hour when the relevant official had already concluded his own inquiry.” He looked at me. “The chamberlain will also want to know who else has been affected. Which means an audit of Drosos’s records across the district. Which means, if Drosos has been careful — and I think he has been careful — a period during which the irregularities may be obscured before the auditors arrive.”

I had not thought of that.

“And Drosos,” Holkos continued, in the same level tone, “is not working alone. A collection officer who has been running this arrangement for — I estimate, from the condition of Kerkyon’s barn and the pattern of the seasonal notes — at least three seasons, has accommodation from someone in the assessment office who is preparing the preliminary rolls in the first place. The discrepancy has to be introduced somewhere. Drosos could not have done it alone.”

The lamp. The smell of good cedar oil. The beam in the storage corridor. The whole familiar weight of the palace settling around us in the evening.

“So we cannot simply report it to the chamberlain,” I said.

“We can report it to the chamberlain,” Holkos said. “But the way in which we report it determines whether the grain is saved or scattered.”

What he did next I have reconstructed partly from my own observation and partly from what he explained afterward, on the condition that I would not record it in any form that would reach official eyes.

He spent the following morning doing nothing that appeared to be anything. He sat in the outer scribal hall and read the current season’s assessment rolls — all of them, not merely the bronze quotas but the grain, the linen, the livestock — with the patience of a man who is not in a hurry because he already knows approximately what he is going to find. He ate the bread that was brought for the scribal staff, which he normally did not bother with. He was very still.

In the early afternoon he asked me, in passing, which scribe in the assessment office was responsible for the bronze quota rolls.

I told him. The name was Alektruon.

He asked me, as if idly, how long Alektruon had held that position.

Four seasons, I said.

He nodded as if this confirmed something he had already decided. He went back to his reading.

By evening he had what he needed, though he did not tell me then what it was. He said only that he would speak with the chamberlain in the morning, and that I should be present.

I was present.

I will not set down the full substance of what passed in that room, partly because some of it pertained to individuals whose situation has since been resolved in ways the palace has chosen not to publicize, and partly because Holkos was careful — as he was always careful — to give the chamberlain the conclusions without all of the working.

What I can record is this:

He presented the tablet comparison — the preliminary roll figure against Drosos’s field tablet figure — without drama, simply as a documentary fact. The chamberlain was a careful man and he saw immediately what it meant.

Holkos then presented three additional discrepancies he had identified in the morning’s reading, from previous seasons, in the records of two other farmers in Drosos’s district. Smaller variations, handled more delicately, but following the same pattern.

He then observed that the preliminary rolls containing the relevant assessments had all passed through the same scribal position in the assessment office, and that cross-referencing those assessment decisions against the external ledger — the ledger that recorded what was actually delivered to the factor at the point of collection, before Drosos’s own records were consulted — revealed a consistent shape of erasure.

The chamberlain asked how this had not been detected in the standard review process.

Holkos said, with the courtesy he used when pointing out that someone had asked a question whose answer was uncomfortable: the standard review process compared the collection officer’s records against the assessment rolls. If both documents were prepared by parties working in concert, the comparison would confirm rather than reveal the arrangement. The external ledger — the factor’s record at the point of final collection — was the only document neither of them had controlled, and it was not part of the standard comparison.

The chamberlain was quiet for a moment.

Then he asked what Holkos recommended.

This was the part I have thought about most in the years since.

Holkos said that Kerkyon should be told immediately that the accusation against him had been found to be without foundation and was withdrawn. He said this with a firmness that left no room for procedural equivocation.

He said that the audit of Drosos’s records should be conducted by officers from the grain quota administration, who had no connection to the bronze quota system, and should be framed as a routine seasonal cross-check rather than an investigation — because an announced investigation would accelerate the destruction of records.

He said that Alektruon’s position should be quietly transferred to a different scribal function before the audit concluded, on administrative grounds, without accusation.

And then he said: the individuals involved should face the chamberlain’s judgment in private, without the matter reaching the wanax’s formal court.

The chamberlain raised an eyebrow at this. “You advocate for leniency?”

“I advocate for containment,” Holkos said. “A public proceeding names names. Named names invite questions about how long the arrangement operated and who knew. Those questions, pursued thoroughly, will reach walls you would prefer not to breach.” He paused. “The arrangement was not large enough to justify the walls it might damage. Adjust it quietly. The farmers will know justice was done; they do not need the proceedings published.”

I thought, at the time, that I disagreed with this. I was the moral element in that room — I felt the weight of Kerkyon’s night, the sleeplessness of a man who does not know if he will be condemned for something he did not do. I wanted a public accounting.

I have since come to understand that Holkos had made a calculation I was not equipped to make. The walls were real. The chamberlain knew it. The quiet resolution protected something that mattered more, and distributed a justice that was, if not visible, at least actual.

Kerkyon received a formal notification, delivered by palace messenger, that an administrative review had determined his quota obligations had been fulfilled and that no further proceeding would occur. It was written in the dry language of the scribal office and said nothing about why. But it said what it needed to say.

I asked Holkos, afterward, when we were alone and the cedar oil was burning and the storage-corridor beam had made its evening noise, why he had wanted to visit the farm first. What he had learned from Kerkyon’s hands, from the fuel store, from the condition of the implements that he could not have learned from the documents.

He was in the languid mode — the problem was finished, the world was flat and uninteresting, and he was looking at the wall with the expression of a man for whom the next few days promised nothing stimulating.

“I needed to know,” he said, “whether Kerkyon had anything to hide.”

“Because if he had—”

“Because if he had, the arithmetic would have had two explanations instead of one. A man who is genuinely withholding does not keep his surplus visible in an unlocked barn.” He picked up a small piece of clay from the table, turned it in his fingers, and set it down. “The farm told me the accusation was false before the tablet confirmed it. The tablet only told me who had made it false.”

I noted that the preliminary rolls had been the instrument — that if I had completed the master transfer before the accusation was filed, the preliminary document would have been superseded and the comparison would have been impossible to make.

Holkos said: “Yes.”

He said it in the way that meant he had considered this already.

I asked whether he thought the timing had been deliberate.

He looked at me then, and there was something in his face that was not quite amusement and not quite sadness — a quality I have seen in very few men, which is the expression of someone who understands exactly how much the world is arranged against simple honesty, and who has made a kind of settled peace with that understanding without being destroyed by it.

“Phaedimos,” he said, “everything in that arrangement was deliberate. The only thing that was not deliberate was you.”

He meant that I had been in the hall when Drosos filed the preliminary report. He meant that I had been copying the rolls that morning, so that the figures were fresh. He meant that I had mentioned it to him in a corridor, because I happened to meet him there, and he had happened to ask what I was carrying.

He meant, I think, that it had been a near thing.

I have set this account down as faithfully as I am able, recording what I observed and what was later explained to me. I have omitted those elements that the chamberlain asked should not be written, and I have preserved those elements that seem to me to illuminate something worth illuminating — which is that the detection of an untruth requires, before it requires anything else, a correct understanding of what the truth should look like.

Holkos the Elder would have said this was obvious.

Most things, when he was finished with them, seemed obvious.

That was the nature of the man.

—Phaedimos, scribe of the chamberlain’s office
Second season of the current administration
