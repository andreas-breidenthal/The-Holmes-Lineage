HOLKOS OF SUSA

~790 BC — Susa, Elam

Susa stood among the oldest centres of administration in the ancient world, where the organisation of goods, people, and records had long been formalised into systems that outlasted individual rulers. In this environment, Holkos arrived as a merchant of established standing, carrying forward the practices of observation and comparison that had defined his predecessors. The eastern routes demanded different competencies than the western ones: greater attention to distance, to the condition of goods after long travel, and to the reliability of the records that accompanied them.

He remained in Susa for many years, working between the Elamite merchant houses and the wider network of trade that connected the Iranian plateau to Mesopotamia and beyond. His work required him to move not only goods but also assessments of quality, origin, and consistency. He learned the Elamite language in order to reduce uncertainty in his dealings, and found in that reduction the particular advantage that falls to the man who can follow a conversation all the way to its end.

The account of his work was preserved by a scribe named Eshmun-iddin, who served within one of the merchant houses that relied upon Holkos’s assessments. Eshmun-iddin was trained in record-keeping and familiar with the flow of goods through Susa, but his role did not extend to interpretation beyond the immediate context of transactions. He recorded the events as they occurred, and his account preserves the sequence of observation and action without embellishment. It is from this record that the following account is drawn.

The Account Of Holkos Of Susa

Susa, in the reign of Shutur-Nahhunte, approximately the four hundred and twelfth year of the Elamite reckoning

The smell of a thing gone wrong is different from the smell of a thing merely lost.

I have worked with manifests long enough to know the difference, though I could not have named it before the morning Ribat came to stand in the doorway of the counting room with his face the colour of river clay and his hands working at the hem of his robe as though he meant to unravel it. He smelled of river clay too — of sweat and the particular sourness of a man who has not slept because sleep would mean accepting that the morning will bring what it has brought.

Behind him, the city of Susa was waking. The oil lamps in the courtyard of the House of Khumban-nikkash had not yet been extinguished, and their smoke drifted through the door-gap in thin blue threads that smelled of sesame and the residue of the night's accounting. In the distance, the great temple platform cast its early shadow across the lower quarters. The gate-hinge of the outer wall had not yet been oiled that season, and every entry into the compound was announced by it — a long, arthritic protest of bronze against stone that I had ceased to notice, until I noticed it now, because it had been this same sound, three days prior, that had marked the arrival of the Babylonian delegation's mules and their handlers, and the departure of a sealed consignment that was, as of this morning, nowhere.

Ribat was not yet weeping. He was past the point where weeping would have released anything.

I set down my reed and looked at him.

My name is Eshmun-iddin, and I am a scribe of the third rank within the House of Khumban-nikkash, which stands among the senior merchant houses of Susa and maintains accounts with no fewer than eleven corresponding houses in Babylon, Nippur, and the cities of the northern routes. I record what passes through this house — the weight of copper, the count of vessels, the movement of silver by talent and shekel — and I record it in the Elamite script that my master's house uses for internal correspondence, and in the Akkadian that our Babylonian partners require. I am competent at both. I make this observation not in vanity but because it bears on what follows: I could read every document in the affair, and I did not see what Holkos saw in them. This continues to trouble me, though less than it once did.

Holkos had been resident in Susa for eleven years at the time of these events. He arrived as a merchant of the western routes — his family's lineage ran to Ugarit, and before that to places I have not visited and cannot picture — but he had adapted himself to the eastern trade with a thoroughness that I had not expected when he first engaged a desk in our counting room for his assessments. He was lean in the manner of men who forget to eat when a problem has hold of them, and he had a quality to his attention that I can only describe as total: when Holkos looked at a manifest, he appeared to be looking through it, as though the clay tablet were a window rather than a surface, and the thing he was examining existed somewhere behind and beneath the marks.

He had learned Elamite. This alone would have distinguished him among the western merchants. But he had learned it, he once told me, because the gap between what a man says in his own tongue and what he consents to have rendered in another is where the interesting things live. I wrote this down. I was not certain at the time that I believed him. I believe him now.

The consignment in question was lapis lazuli.

Lapis lazuli is not the most valuable thing that passes through the House of Khumban-nikkash, but it is among the most traceable. Its quality is determined at the point of origin — the mines of the eastern mountains, far up the routes that run north and east beyond the Median territories — and assessed again at Susa, which serves as the primary weighing station for the westward trade. A stone that has been assessed at Susa carries with it a sealed clay record: its weight by talent and shekel, its grade according to the colour-depth system that the Elamite houses have used for three generations, and the mark of the assessor who examined it. This record travels with the consignment and is matched against the receiving manifest when the goods arrive at their destination.

The consignment that had vanished was fourteen talents of lapis lazuli, grade two by Elamite reckoning, assessed and sealed, destined for the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina in Babylon. It had been placed in the charge of a junior factor named Kubatum, who was twenty-three years of age and had been with the house for two years and who was, by every account I had collected before Ribat's appearance in my doorway, both diligent and honest.

Kubatum was, as of that morning, sitting in the small room adjacent to the courtyard where grain samples were stored, under the observation of two of the house guards. He had been placed there by Ribat's senior partner, a man named Adad-apla-iddina, who had returned from Babylon four days ago and who had identified, upon reviewing the records, that the consignment had been signed out by Kubatum and had not arrived. Adad-apla-iddina had delivered this finding to Ribat with the confidence of a man who considers the matter settled and requires only the formality of a formal accusation before the city magistrate.

I had been recording the relevant tablets when Holkos arrived, as he sometimes did in the mornings, to use the counting room's light for his own work. He looked at me. He looked at Ribat. He looked at the tablets I had been recording.

He sat down.

"You have a missing consignment," Holkos said. He was already reaching for the nearest tablet.

"Fourteen talents of lapis lazuli," Ribat said. "Grade two. Sealed under my mark and the mark of the assessor Khumban-nikkash employed last season."

"And a suspect."

"Kubatum. The factor who signed it out. He cannot account for three days of his itinerary."

Holkos turned the tablet. He turned it again. He set it down and took up the next one. His face expressed nothing beyond the mild attention of a man reading a list of grain prices.

"Eshmun-iddin," he said, without looking up. "Bring me the dispatch records for the past thirty days. Not the summaries. The originals."

I brought them. There were fourteen tablets.

"And the Babylonian side of the correspondence. Whatever the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina sent regarding this consignment."

There were three tablets. One was the initial order. One was the confirmation of terms. One was the arrival manifest, which was how we had discovered the discrepancy — the goods had not arrived, the manifest noted their absence, and the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina had sent a politely worded but entirely inflexible query regarding the location of goods for which they had already partially paid.

Holkos arranged all seventeen tablets in a line across the counting room floor. He lay down beside them, which I had seen him do before and which continued to unsettle the junior scribes who witnessed it. He read from left to right, then from right to left, then in some order I could not follow.

Ribat watched this. Ribat was a man who had built his house on the reliable operation of systems he understood, and what he was watching was not a system he understood.

"Is he —" Ribat began.

"He is working," I said.

"It does not look like working."

"No," I agreed.

Holkos was still for a long time. The lamps continued to burn. The gate-hinge complained once, admitting a delivery of wool from the northern quarter, and then was quiet. Somewhere in the grain-room, Kubatum sat and waited.

Then Holkos rose, with the abruptness of a man who has been lying in wait rather than lying in rest, and he stood over the tablets and he spoke without preamble.

"The factor is innocent," he said. "He could not have done this. Not alone, and not at all, given the structure of the records."

Ribat opened his mouth.

"Listen to me carefully," Holkos said, "and I will show you why the records themselves have told us this, if we read them in the language in which they were written."

He gestured at the line of tablets.

"There are two languages in this room. Not Elamite and Akkadian — though those are present, and their differences matter enormously. I mean the language of dispatch and the language of receipt. A dispatch record and a receiving manifest are not simply the same information written twice. They are written by different hands, from different positions, at different moments. The gap between them — the time and space and circumstance that separates them — is where a crime, if one has been committed, must leave its mark."

He crouched and touched the dispatch record.

"Kubatum signed out the consignment on the third day before the month's end. This is recorded here, in his hand, in Elamite. The weight is correct. The grade marks are correct. The seal impression of the house assessor is present. There is nothing irregular in this document. It is the work of a careful man."

He touched the receiving manifest.

"The House of Nabu-shuma-iddina notes in this document — in Akkadian, written by their own scribe — that the consignment did not arrive. They note the date on which it was expected. They note that the Babylonian factor who was to receive it waited three days before sending word."

He paused.

"Which Babylonian factor?"

Ribat blinked. "Their man in Susa. The one who was to escort the goods back on the return journey."

"And that factor's name is recorded in their correspondence?"

"It is. Nabu-zer-kitti-lishir. He has handled three transfers before this one."

"Good. Now." Holkos moved to the middle of the tablet line. "Between these two points — Kubatum's dispatch and the Babylonian manifest's notation of non-arrival — there is a period of eleven days. The standard journey from Susa to Babylon, with a mule-train of this weight, is eight days in good conditions. The dispatch was made in the month when the roads are dry. Eleven days, therefore, contains a three-day anomaly. An unexplained interval."

"Kubatum cannot account for three days," Ribat said, and I could hear the relief in his voice — the relief of a man who believes he is being given confirmation of what he already suspects.

"Neither can Adad-apla-iddina," Holkos said.

The relief stopped.

He walked me through it slowly, in a manner I recognised as performed — not for my benefit, for I could see he had already arrived at his destination, but for Ribat's, who needed to travel the road rather than simply be shown the destination. This is a quality in Holkos that I have tried to record faithfully, because it is not kindness exactly, but it produces the effect of kindness: he gives you the steps because he understands that arriving without steps is not understanding but only belief, and he has a low regard for belief.

"The dispatch records show Kubatum's movements precisely," he said. "The house records his daily accountability by habit — the weights he handled, the seals he applied, the measurements he made. He was present in this compound on the fourth day after the dispatch, assisting with the copper assessment. He was present on the sixth day, recording a grain delivery. He was present, Eshmun-iddin, on which days precisely?"

I checked my own records. "The fourth, the sixth, and the eighth."

"Eight days. The entire transit window. Kubatum never left Susa. He dispatched the consignment to the care of the Babylonian factor — which was his assignment — and then returned to his ordinary duties. The three-day gap that cannot be accounted for is not in Kubatum's itinerary. It is in the transit itself, after Kubatum's responsibility ended."

He turned to the final set of tablets. These were the ones I had not initially included, because they seemed peripheral: the correspondence logs for Adad-apla-iddina's Babylonian journey, which had overlapped with the transit period.

"Adad-apla-iddina departed for Babylon on the same day as the consignment," Holkos said. "His stated purpose was the renegotiation of terms with the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina for next season's cedar contracts. His journey is recorded here. He arrived in Babylon — and this is the element that I needed you to bring me, Eshmun-iddin, because it is written in Akkadian and concerns Babylonian logistics rather than Elamite accounting — on the twelfth day."

"Twelve days," I said slowly.

"Not eight. Twelve. A man travelling quickly, without a laden mule-train, should make the journey in seven. With ordinary provisions, eight. Twelve days suggests either an illness or a diversion."

Ribat had gone very still.

"Furthermore," Holkos said, and here his voice did not change in tone but changed in something I cannot easily name — a quality of inevitability entered it, like a river discovering its own gradient — "the receiving manifest from the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina notes that their factor, Nabu-zer-kitti-lishir, waited at the appointed waystation for three days before reporting non-arrival. Three days in which a man travelling from Susa might reasonably detour, if he knew when and where the consignment was to pass through a particular waystation, and if he had established, in advance, a relationship with someone who could receive diverted goods discreetly."

He stood upright.

"I would suggest that the governor's men make enquiries at the waystation at the river crossing on the Susa-Babylon road, six days east of Babylon. I would further suggest they enquire after a warehouse in the lower merchants' quarter of Babylon, one associated not with the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina but with one of the smaller independent dealers in uncut stone. I have not been to Babylon in four years, but the infrastructure of this kind of diversion does not change quickly. And I would suggest," he added, almost as an afterthought, though I knew by then that his afterthoughts were never afterthoughts, "that they conduct these enquiries before Adad-apla-iddina has reason to send a letter."

What followed is less mine to record than the governor's men's to explain, and they have explained it in their own documents, in which the House of Khumban-nikkash does not appear and neither does Holkos.

I will say only that the records proved accurate. I will say that Adad-apla-iddina, when confronted with the weight of documentation that Holkos had assembled — the transit time, the itinerary gap, the receiving correspondence read in its original Akkadian rather than in the Elamite summary he himself had prepared — found that his explanations became shorter and shorter until they stopped entirely. I will say that Kubatum was released from the grain-room before the midday meal, and that he sat in the courtyard for a long time afterwards without speaking, which I understood.

Holkos did not stay for this. He had returned to the counting room, where he sat with the blank expression of a man whose brain has been working very hard and has now stopped, and is finding the cessation unpleasant. He had brought from somewhere a collection of small stone weights of varying origins — they were not his, and I did not ask whose they were — and he was turning them in his fingers in a pattern that was almost but not quite regular.

I sat with him.

"You knew from the dispatch records," I said. "Before you asked for the Babylonian correspondence."

"I suspected from the dispatch records," he said, without looking up from the weights. "The dispatch records told me where to look. The Babylonian correspondence told me what I would find there."

"But the three-day gap. You said it was not in Kubatum's itinerary. Ribat said Kubatum could not account for —"

"Ribat said Kubatum could not account for three days. Ribat meant that Kubatum could not explain the three-day transit anomaly, which is not the same as saying that Kubatum was present during it. Adad-apla-iddina framed the question very carefully. He asked whether Kubatum could account for the three days. A man who dispatched a consignment and then returned to his desk cannot account for what happened to that consignment afterwards. No one expects him to. Adad-apla-iddina was relying on Ribat to make the obvious inference rather than the accurate one."

"He was relying on him to hear 'cannot account for' and understand it as absence or evasion."

"Yes."

I thought about this. "He nearly succeeded."

"He succeeded entirely, for three days. He would have continued to succeed. The magistrate's proceeding would have been brief and damaging, and Kubatum would have been a ruined man by the month's end, and the lapis lazuli would have moved through its new channel quietly and the House of Nabu-shuma-iddina would have been compensated from the house accounts and the loss absorbed." Holkos set down one of the weights and picked it up again. "He is not an unintelligent man. He understood the systems he was manipulating. He simply believed that no one would read both the Elamite and the Akkadian records together and notice that they described two different travel times."

"Because ordinarily —"

"Because ordinarily Ribat's house reads the Elamite records and the Babylonian house reads the Akkadian records and neither house reads the other's documents in the original. The gap lives in the space between the languages."

I sat with this for a moment.

"You said to move before he could send a letter."

"He has partners in Babylon. Not many, and not the kind he would speak of openly, but the infrastructure that allows a man to divert and store fourteen talents of lapis lazuli without immediate detection does not belong to one man. When he understood the direction of the enquiry, he would have sent word. The governor's men were quick enough." He paused. "Or they were not, and some portion of the network has folded itself away and will surface elsewhere. These things do not end cleanly."

The weights continued their almost-regular movement through his fingers.

"Will you eat?" I asked.

"Later."

He did not eat until the following morning, at which point he ate a great deal very quickly and then went back to his work, and I returned to mine, and the counting room settled into the sounds that counting rooms make when everything is as it should be — the scratch of reed on clay, the movement of tablets, the gate-hinge marking the ordinary commerce of a city that did not know, and would not be told, what had nearly passed through it unobserved.

I have recorded it here instead. Someone should.

— Eshmun-iddin, scribe, in the service of the House of Khumban-nikkash, Susa.

Here the account ends.
