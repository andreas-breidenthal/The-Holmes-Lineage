HOLKOS OF SARDIS

~670 BC — Sardis, Lydia

The Matter Of The Death Of Phrasilas The Assayer

Being the record of Lysanias, clerk to the market authority of Sardis, in the reign of Ardys, second year of the new weighing standards

I.

The morning fog comes off the Pactolus before the sun has cleared the eastern ridge, and on the days it is thick, Sardis smells like wet stone and the cold breath of the river — a mineral smell, clean and slightly alien, as if the earth itself were exhaling something it has held for too long. That was the smell on the morning they found Phrasilas, and it was still in the air when the boy appeared at my door before the second hour, gripping the doorframe with both hands, and said: "Come now. Holkos is already there, and he says to bring your tablets."

The boy was Damon — Phrasilas's apprentice, fourteen years old, and frightened in a way that had passed beyond the ordinary expression of fright into something more controlled and therefore more alarming. I had seen him before, in the market authority's dealings with the assay district. He had always been a careful, quiet boy — attentive in the way of apprentices who understand that attention is the principal thing their position requires.

He was not quiet now, and what he said as we moved through the warehouse passage toward the workshop is the thing I must record first, before I record anything else, because it is the thing on which everything that follows depends.

"Ondres has been asking questions about me," he said, in the rapid tight-jawed manner of a person rehearsing an argument against an opponent who has not yet arrived. "Where I was last night. Whether I have a key to the workshop. Whether I was the last to see Phrasilas before — before." He stopped. Drew a breath. "I was the last to see him. I do have a key. I was alone in my room in the apprentice-house after the second hour, and there is no one who can say so because it is the nature of being alone in a room that no one else is there." He looked at me with a steadiness that cost him something. "I did not kill him. He was — he was the man who trained me. He was —" He stopped again and this time did not continue that particular thought. "Whatever they say about his reputation by nightfall, I need someone who will look at the truth of this before Ondres files his report. Holkos said you were the one to ask. So I am asking."

I will say plainly: this is not the standard form of an appeal to the market authority, and Damon knew it. He was not filing a dispute or requesting a certification. He was a boy of fourteen with no master, no income, no witness to his whereabouts, and the procedurally tidy shape of a murder accusation forming around him like water finding the lowest available channel — and he was asking me, a clerk, to carry the weight of his situation in the official record before the channel set.

I bore it. It is what I do.

Then he said the other thing — the thing Phrasilas had told him three days before he died — and this I recorded in my tablets immediately, before we had reached the workshop, because it seemed to me the kind of statement that acquires significance faster than the person who made it can anticipate.

"He told me there was a consignment coming that he would assess honestly regardless of the consequence. He said: Damon, when a man who has compromised himself begins to correct that compromise, the danger is not that he will fail — the danger is that he will succeed, and that someone will not want him to. I did not understand what he meant."

He looked at me.

"I understand it now," he said.

II.

Holkos of Sardis was already inside the workshop when we arrived — standing in the doorway with his arms loose at his sides, his eyes moving across the room in the slow, almost incurious sweep I had come to understand was not incuriosity at all but its precise opposite. He is a merchant of the middling rank who trades between the Ionian Greek cities of the coast and the suppliers of the Lydian court, building from this commerce the kind of reputation that draws the market authority to him rather than the reverse whenever a case arises that procedure alone cannot answer. He had the angular, economical face of the western Anatolian trading families — a face built for the assessment of things presented to it — and he was not a tall man, but he occupied doorways strangely, as if they had been constructed to his dimensions.

He had asked Damon to send for me. This is how these investigations begin: with Holkos already inside a room, and with me arriving to find the room has told him something it has not yet told anyone else.

Ondres, the officer of the market guard, was at the room's centre with the expression of a man whose conclusions have arrived before his evidence. He is careful and thorough — I want that recorded clearly — and he had touched nothing. He told us what Damon had found: the lamps still burning, burned almost to nothing, the oil in the lower reservoir dry, the wick beginning to scorch the clay. Damon had seen the lamps before the body, because the lamps faced the door and Phrasilas did not.

The assayer had died at his bench. He was seated, or had been seated, and had fallen forward — left arm extended, right arm folded beneath him. The balance still stood. The weights were still set. The small terracotta cup that held the electrum for measurement was still there, with a quantity of loose metal in it. The wax tablet on which he recorded his certifications lay to his right, the stylus beside it, the wax showing three lines of text — two complete, one begun and not finished.

"The slave quarter at the north of the district," Ondres said, with the careful neutrality of a man who knows his list is not complete, "contains a Carian laborer named Bogas, who had a certification challenged six months ago and was fined substantially. He has no witness to his position after the third hour last night." A pause, deliberate, weighted. "I will also note that the apprentice — Damon — has a key to the workshop. He was the last to see Phrasilas alive, by his own account. He has no witness to his whereabouts after the second hour."

Damon, behind me, said nothing. I did not look at him. I recorded Ondres's words exactly as he spoke them, and I felt, as I wrote, the channel deepening.

"Lysanias," Holkos said, from the doorway — not addressing Ondres, not acknowledging the summary, in the manner of a man who has heard it, assigned it its proper weight, and moved on. "Come here and do not step in the oil near the right-hand lamp-stand."

I came. I stood beside him at the balance.

"What do you see?"

"A balance," I said. "Set for a measurement."

"Yes. What measurement?"

The balance was a standard two-pan instrument. The right pan held three weights — bronze, market-authority issue. The left pan held a small piece of electrum. The beam was not horizontal; the right pan was slightly lower, indicating the weights exceeded the metal.

"The electrum is lighter than the weights," I said.

"Lighter than the weights as currently set. But look at the weights themselves. What do they add to?"

I identified them: a quarter-shekel, a half-shekel, and the largest — I reached for it, and Holkos said, quietly and immediately, "Put it down exactly as it was," and I drew back my hand.

"One and three-quarter shekels in total," I said.

"Which is not a standard certification unit. An assayer certifying a consignment certifies to round denominations — one shekel, two shekels, five shekels. He does not complete a session with his weights set to one and three-quarter shekels unless he was in the middle of a measurement when he died — or unless someone else set those weights after he died."

He moved to the wax tablet.

The three lines of text were in standard certification notation. The first recorded a consignment — eleven shekels gross weight, submitted by a name I will return to. The second recorded the first testing piece: two shekels certified. The third — the unfinished one — began the notation for a second testing piece, with the weight mark started and not completed: one shekel and —

"He was measuring the second piece," I said. "He had certified two shekels of the first, begun the second measurement, and was killed before he could finish."

"Look again," Holkos said. He was watching me.

The weights: one and three-quarter shekels. The electrum: lighter. Slightly. My eyes moved between them, and the pan, and back, and then I understood — not gradually, but at once, in the manner of a thing that has been true for some time before you see it.

"The metal on the scale," I said slowly, "is not two shekels. It is less than one and three-quarter shekels."

"By approximately a quarter-shekel. Which represents — for a piece presented as a full shekel — a dilution of one part in four."

The room went very quiet. Ondres had stopped breathing in his steady official way.

I looked at the pan. I looked at the unfinished line of text on the tablet — one shekel and — — and I thought about the hand that had been writing it. The hand that had set these weights, placed this piece of metal on this pan, watched the beam fail to level, and understood in that moment not merely that the metal was wrong, but what it meant that the metal was wrong — whose metal it was, what arrangement had been made, and what it would cost him to finish the sentence he had begun.

He had picked up the stylus regardless.

"He was discovering," I said. The word came out differently than I intended. Less procedural.

"He had discovered it." Holkos's voice was even, but his eyes, when he glanced at me, held something that was not quite approval and not quite sympathy but was somewhere between the two. "The weights were set for one and three-quarter shekels. The piece on the pan failed to balance. He knew. He was about to write it down." He lifted the stylus from beside the tablet — not touching the wax, only the instrument — and held it in the lamplight. "He was writing when the blow came. The killer placed the stylus on the tablet afterward, thinking it more natural than leaving it in a dead man's hand. But a man at work holds his stylus above the wax. A man at rest sets it beside. It was placed, not dropped. This was calculation, not passion."

He set the stylus down with the precision of a man returning evidence to its position and turned toward the door.

"We need the passage," he said. "And we need it before the market opens."

III.

The shutter on the workshop's single side-window opened onto the narrow passage between the warehouses. Holkos examined it with the attention he brings to everything — fingers along the frame, the latch, the pin — and then stood for a moment looking down the passage toward the lower warehouse district. He said nothing. He was reading something I could not see.

"Walk with me," he said.

We went the length of the passage, which brought us to the rear approach of the larger warehouse complex at the district's southern end. Holkos did not stop at the approach but went to the loading entrance — a wide double door, standing open at this hour for the morning's first deliveries — and stood in the frame, not entering, simply observing the interior in his particular manner.

I stood beside him and looked.

The warehouse was orderly in the way of establishments that handle volume: amphorae racked along the left wall, grain sacks palletised along the right, and at the far end, bales of packing material awaiting use — cloth, straw, wool wadding bundled in the Phrygian manner, which is to say wound with a selvedge band of orange-red thread at each end as a packer's mark, indicating the bundle had been inspected and measured. The orange-red thread is a specific convention of the Phrygian packing trade that I knew from market-authority records, because disputes about short-measure packing had come before the office twice in the previous season. The bales nearest the loading door had the selvedge bands clearly visible: undyed coarse wool, wrapped with the orange-red thread at top and bottom.

The warehouse was Timodoros's. I knew this because the loading entrance bore his mark — the merchant's seal in clay above the doorframe — and because Holkos was already looking at the seal with the expression of a man who has found the last weight for his pan.

He did not explain. He turned and walked back up the passage, and I followed.

At the shutter-latch of the assay workshop, he stopped, and crouched, and looked at the iron pin of the latch with the lamp held close.

"Lysanias," he said.

I crouched beside him. On the pin — caught between the iron and the wooden frame, pulled free by something pressed close against it in passing — were several threads of cloth. Undyed, coarse-woven.

And wound among them, a single thread of orange-red.

I looked at Holkos. He was looking at the threads with an expression of complete, settled composure — the expression of a man for whom the balance has just levelled.

"The selvedge thread," I said.

"From the packing bales in Timodoros's lower warehouse," he said. "A man pressed close to this frame in darkness, moving fast, with cloth about him or in his hands — and left his employer's mark on the latch-pin." He straightened. "Not intuition. Not knowledge of suppliers. A thread seen in a warehouse and a thread found on a pin, and the observation that they are the same thread, seen by the same eyes, an hour apart."

I took the threads carefully from the pin and placed them in my record-pouch. I understood now what he had been doing at the loading entrance: not looking for Timodoros's mark on the door — he had known that already — but ensuring that I had seen the bales, the cloth, the selvedge, with my own eyes, before we found what the passage had left behind. He had been building the chain so that I could testify to every link of it, not merely the conclusion.

This is the kind of thing Holkos does that I have never seen him explain, and which I have recorded here as plainly as I can, because I believe it is the most important thing about the way he works: he does not trust his own observation alone. He arranges for a second set of eyes, so that the truth has a witness.

Outside, the fog was thinning. Damon was at the passage mouth where I had left him. He looked at my face and then at Holkos, and the question he could not ask was visible in the set of his jaw.

Holkos looked at him for a long moment. "You are not accused," he said. "Not by the evidence. You will remain available to the market authority today." He looked at me. "The consignment."

The name on the tablet was Timodoros of the Mermnad quarter — not the royal Mermnads, but a family that had taken the district name by long residence and comfortable association. A silver merchant of the upper middle rank, trading regularly with the court-adjacent suppliers of the palace workshops, whose name was large enough in the market that speaking it aloud in a dead man's workshop felt like an act requiring some nerve.

Holkos already knew before I confirmed it. The name was simply the last weight on the pan.

"Submitted voluntarily for certification," he said. "A man who submits debased metal does not do so in confusion. He does so because he has arranged for the certification to clear."

"Arranged —"

"Phrasilas had a reputation that certain merchants found valuable. A man who had, over some years, developed a relationship with particular clients — consignments processed with speed and without undue scrutiny, gratitude expressed through channels that do not appear in our records. Last night, it failed. Phrasilas reached the second piece of Timodoros's consignment and found what any honest assayer would have found immediately — copper-dressed silver presented as electrum, one part in four adulterated. He had been paid to not find this. And he found it, and he set his weights, and the pan did not balance, and he began to write it down."

"And was killed for it," I said.

"He was killed for it. By a man sent to ensure the certification cleared. A man with access to Timodoros's warehouse, with the build to deliver a blow of that force, and who passed close enough to the shutter-latch in his hurry to leave a thread of his employer's packing cloth on the iron pin." He was looking down the passage toward Timodoros's loading entrance. "Kreos — Timodoros's senior warehouse man — has been seen in this passage before. Three occasions in the past two months, that I have observed personally. He is a large man and moves like one who knows the district's back routes. The selvedge thread is his employer's. The passage is his habitual route. The build is consistent with the force required."

"And the murder instrument?"

"Not in the workshop, because he carried it away. A heavy balance weight — two-shekel denomination, most likely — which will be in Timodoros's warehouse at this moment, because a man does not carry a murder instrument through a waking city. He conceals it near his place of employment and retrieves it when it is safe to dispose of it properly."

He was moving already, back toward the main street, with the decisive stride of a man who has finished reading one document and is moving to the next.

"The market opens at the fourth hour," he said, without turning. "It is past the second. If Timodoros hears before then that the consignment is under scrutiny, the weight disappears. The selvedge thread is suggestive. The weight is conclusive. We need it in hand before he knows what we are doing."

IV.

He spent less than an hour at his own workshop. While he wrote the message — sealed, for delivery to Timodoros's receiving clerk — I sat and watched him run river-sand through his fingers in the shallow tray he keeps on his working bench. I did not ask him what he was doing. I have asked before, and the answer (thinking about what the sand knows that the metal has forgotten) is one that tells me everything about the man and nothing I can record usefully in a procedural document, and so I have settled for recording the image itself and leaving the interpretation to whoever reads this after me.

He rose before I had framed a second thought on the matter, and told me exactly where Kreos would go, exactly what we would find in Timodoros's lower storeroom, and — this was the part that settled in my chest with more weight than the rest — exactly what I should do if Kreos found us before we found him.

"He is a large man," Holkos said, "and he has killed once this week for this consignment. Do not place yourself between him and a door. Place yourself to his right side, where his reach is shortest, and if he moves toward me, move first and move low."

I will record that I did not find this instruction comfortable. I am a clerk. I record disputes and certifications. I had not, until that morning, been given specific tactical advice about my positioning relative to a man who might choose to do me violence.

"Are you expecting violence?" I said.

"I am expecting a man who is frightened," Holkos said, "and who has already demonstrated his preferred response to situations that threaten him." He picked up the sealed message and handed it to the porter. "Frightened men who have killed once find the arithmetic of a second occasion much simpler. It is best to be honest about this in advance."

The message, as he described it to me, said the following: that the market authority had completed its initial examination of the Phrasilas workshop and found the certification records in order; that the consignment of eleven shekels submitted by Timodoros had been cleared posthumously, pending a procedural second examination scheduled for the following morning; and that the consignment weight had been certified and sealed in the lower record-room of the authority's warehouse for safekeeping.

None of it was true.

But a man who has arranged for a certification — and arranged for the removal of the one assayer likely to refuse him — will, on reading that his consignment has been cleared, have one immediate concern: the murder instrument, which he placed in his warehouse, is now in the vicinity of an imminent market authority examination. He will want it gone before morning.

I also record, because it is part of the chain, that Holkos sent a second, shorter message — this one genuine, and carried by a different route — to Ondres. It contained a single instruction: bring two guards to the lower entrance of Timodoros's warehouse complex one hour before the middle of the night, and wait on the signal of a lamp raised and lowered twice. Do not enter before the signal. Whatever you hear inside, do not enter before the signal.

He then told me the third thing — the thing he had not included in either message — which was that Damon was to be positioned in the street outside Timodoros's counting-house at the ninth hour the following morning, with Ondres and two additional guards, to enter on the sound of Holkos's voice raised above the register of ordinary conversation.

"You are planning the confrontation with Timodoros before we have taken Kreos," I said.

"I am planning everything before we have taken Kreos," Holkos said, "because the plan requires that everything occur in the correct sequence and that nothing be improvised."

He said this with perfect equanimity, which is either the mark of a man who is genuinely unafraid or the mark of a man who has learned to perform equanimity so thoroughly that the distinction no longer has practical relevance.

V.

We took our positions an hour before the middle of the night, in the storeroom passage on the lower floor of Timodoros's warehouse complex — Holkos and I alone, with Ondres and two guards twenty paces back at the lower entrance under instruction not to move before the signal.

The passage smelled of grain oil and undyed wool and the cold specific to stone that has not seen sun since the autumn equinox. I had dressed for waiting and had not dressed for what waiting, in those circumstances, actually consists of — which is fear, administered in the slow, measured doses of a long darkness, with no work to do and no sound to attend to except the sounds one cannot help imagining.

Holkos was not still. In the workshop he can stand without moving for long stretches, reading a room. In the dark he was in constant small motion — shifting weight, turning his head, a hand moving briefly near a gap in the storeroom planking. Once, without explanation, he moved two paces to his left and put his back to the wall, and I followed him, and I think this instinct was correct.

We heard Kreos before we saw him. The sound of a large man attempting quietness is not silence — it is a sequence of careful sounds that are, in their very carefulness, more audible than ordinary movement, because a man who is thinking about each footfall is a man who is thinking about something other than where he is placing his feet. Kreos came through the lower entrance with a lamp shuttered to a slit, and he moved without hesitation toward the grain sacks at the far end of the passage — directly to one specific sack, without searching, as a man goes to a thing he has placed rather than a thing he is finding.

He found the sack. He unwrapped the cloth. As he turned the weight in his hands, I saw it: the smooth face where the two-shekel denomination mark had been ground off, carefully, by someone who understood which detail mattered most. He held it up to the lamp-slit and examined it — satisfied himself it was as he had left it — and began to re-wrap it in the cloth.

At which point the lower entrance, behind him, opened.

Not Ondres — I saw that immediately, because the figure was too slight and moved too carefully, and then I understood: Kreos had barred the entrance behind him with the drop-pin, which a man who knew this warehouse would know to do, and which Ondres could not lift from the exterior without the scrape of iron on iron, which would have been audible in the passage. Ondres had done the only thing available to him — waited for Kreos's attention to be elsewhere, and forced it.

The noise was enough.

Kreos turned fast. He had the weight in his right hand, raised, and he was three paces from Holkos, and there was, in that moment, nothing between them.

Holkos did not retreat. He stepped forward and to the side — inside the arc of Kreos's right arm, where the swing had least force — and said, in a voice of complete conversational level: "The selvedge thread on the shutter-pin. We have it. The weight confirms what the thread suggested. You are holding the evidence of your own act."

The check — one beat, one single beat of hesitation — was all I had.

I moved to Kreos's right, as I had been told, and brought the clay lamp down on his extended forearm with considerably more force than I had known myself capable of producing. The lamp shattered. The arm recoiled. Kreos turned toward me, and what was in his face was not pain and not anger precisely but the rapid recalculation of a man who has just revised his assessment of what the evening is going to require.

He swung the weight at the height of my head.

I dropped — not by decision but by the body's own older reasoning, the reasoning that predates thought — and the weight passed through the air where I had been, and Kreos overextended, and Holkos took his right wrist from behind and drove it up and back in a manner that suggested he had done this before, or had thought very carefully about how it should be done, which in his case amounts to the same thing.

Ondres came through the entrance. The two guards followed. From that point it was brief.

When Kreos was on the ground and the guards had him secured, he lay for a moment in the way of a man reassembling himself after a plan has reached its final, unfavorable term. Then he looked at Holkos — not at Ondres, not at me — and said: "The weight was not enough. He would have buried the notation as he always did. He had buried it before."

"Yes," Holkos said. "But on those previous occasions, I suspect the debasing was done with more care. A quarter-shekel's difference cannot be attributed to measurement error. Phrasilas knew it the moment the pan failed to level. And he wrote it down regardless."

Kreos looked at the smooth face of the weight in the guard's hand. He said nothing further.

I record, because the record should contain it: the cut on my palm from the shattered lamp had begun to bleed at some point during the foregoing and had not stopped by the time we left the warehouse. It was not a serious wound. It is now healed. I mention it because I think a record that accounts for every thread of orange-red wool and omits the blood of the person who carried those threads in his record-pouch is a record that has made itself slightly tidier than the events it describes.

VI.

We went to Timodoros the following morning — Holkos and I, through the front door of the counting-house, while Ondres and two guards and Damon waited in the street outside.

Timodoros received us in his front room. He was a compact man of perhaps fifty years, dressed in the Lydian court style, with the practiced pleasantness of a man who has composed his face for incoming visitors so many thousands of times that the composition has become the face — not a mask over something else, but the very surface of him, trained smooth as a worked weight.

"Holkos," he said, with the warmth of a man who has not yet determined whether he is glad to see you. "And the clerk from the authority. A terrible business, Phrasilas. A good assayer."

"An honest one," Holkos said. "In the end."

The pleasantness remained. Underneath it, in the eyes, something recalculated with the rapid invisible speed of an expert assessor running figures.

"I'm told the records were found to be in order," he said carefully.

"You were told what it was useful for you to believe," Holkos said, "so that you would send Kreos to retrieve the weight from your lower storeroom before the authority's examination reached it. He was taken there last night, with the weight in his hand and the cloth still in his arms. The cloth, as your examiner will confirm, matches the packing bales in your warehouse. The selvedge thread — the orange-red packer's mark specific to your Phrygian supplier — was found on the shutter-latch of Phrasilas's workshop. It was there because your warehouse man pressed close to that frame in the dark and left his employer's mark behind him. These are not inferences. They are observed facts."

Timodoros spread his hands — the open, reasonable gesture of a man demonstrating his own transparency. "Kreos conducts his own affairs. I cannot account for everything a warehouse man does in his own time."

"No," Holkos said. "But the certification tablet in Phrasilas's workshop accounts for you. Eleven shekels, gross weight, submitted by Timodoros of the Mermnad quarter. The second testing piece, measured at one and three-quarter shekels with a quarter-shekel's shortfall. The notation begun and not completed, because the man writing it was struck from behind." He paused. "The stylus placed afterward by a man who knew that a stylus in a dead hand looks wrong — but did not know that a man at work holds his stylus above the wax, not beside it. The two-shekel weight with its mark ground smooth, found in your lower storeroom, wrapped in your cloth, retrieved by your man." He looked at Timodoros without expression. "The chain does not require your presence in the workshop. It requires only your name on the tablet, your metal on the pan, your cloth on the latch, and your man on the floor of your warehouse with the murder instrument in his hands."

The pleasantness remained on Timodoros's face. He was, I will record, a man of genuine capability — the pleasantness was not stupidity, not blindness, not the obstinacy of a person who cannot read his situation. He could read it perfectly. He was making a different kind of calculation.

"You are a merchant of the middling rank," he said, and his voice had gone very level. "Trading between the Ionian factors and the court suppliers. A useful position. A position that rests, as all positions in this market rest, on the goodwill of men who hold the larger accounts." He let this settle in the air between us, as a merchant rests a price on a counter, giving the other party time to feel its weight. "There are questions one might raise about any merchant's assessments. The market authority is an institution whose records, if one were minded to examine them carefully, will always yield something of interest to a man who knows what he is looking at."

It was, I recognised, a threat. Stated in the grammar of commerce, but a threat.

Holkos did not respond immediately. He looked at Timodoros for the length of time it takes to let a man hear his own words echo back to him. Then he looked at me. A slight incline of his head — barely visible, the agreed signal — and I crossed to the interior door and opened it.

Damon came through first, with Ondres behind him and the two guards behind Ondres. Damon looked at Timodoros with the particular expression of a boy who has been frightened for many hours and has, in the last few of them, become something slightly different from frightened. He said nothing. He did not need to. He was the figure Timodoros had not expected to see, and the fact of him in the room — the apprentice of the murdered assayer, standing between the market guard and the man who had arranged his master's death — changed the geometry of the situation in a way that no argument could have managed.

Timodoros looked at Damon. Then he looked at the guards. Then, with the slow, involuntary precision of a man watching a balance settle into its final position, he looked at the interior door — the door that led, I noted, to a passage and a rear exit, through which I suspect he had briefly calculated the arithmetic of departure. One of the guards had already moved to stand beside it.

The calculation, visibly, did not resolve in his favour.

Holkos allowed the silence to run for a moment longer than comfort permitted. Then, in precisely the same register as before — as if the threat had been a minor procedural point, now noted and set aside: "The market authority's examiner will assess the consignment honestly. Six shekels and a fraction of pure electrum content, I estimate. The remainder is copper and Phrygian silver. The difference between the value submitted and the value certified represents, at current rates, the price of a house in the upper merchant quarter of Sardis." He moved toward the door. "This office will not detain you further. The authority's process will."

The pleasantness was gone from Timodoros's face. What remained was older and more precise, and looked, without the surface over it, exactly like what it was: the face of a man who has run a great number of successful transactions and has finally encountered one he cannot balance.

He did not speak again.

VII.

Ondres took the formal credit before the market authority, as is proper. The record of the investigation names the market guard as the discovering instrument. I do not contest this, and Holkos does not contest it — he told me once that credit, like debased electrum, is most dangerous to the man who carries it in his pocket and believes it to be something other than what it is.

Timodoros was brought before the authority on the charge of conspiracy in the production and submission of adulterated metal. He was not charged in the death of Phrasilas — the law of our authority does not reach there — but the magistrate who heard the metal charges was not an incurious man, and the sentence, when it came, was of a severity that suggested he had drawn his own conclusions about the cost of the consignment and what concealing it had required. Kreos was bound over to the criminal jurisdiction, which is a slower process and a less forgiving one.

A week after the proceedings closed I went back to the assay district, and Damon was in the workshop, cleaning it. He had received an offer of employment from one of the larger certification houses — arranged, I later understood, through Holkos's intervention with the head of that establishment, who owed Holkos a professional consideration from a previous matter. This is the kind of thing Holkos does not mention and does not record. I record it here because I believe it belongs in this document alongside the weights and the threads and the wax tablets, and because Damon, when I saw him, looked like a young man who had discovered that the ground beneath his feet was still solid — which is not a small thing to discover in the week your master has been murdered and the city has been composing the story it intends to tell about him.

The consignment of eleven shekels submitted by Timodoros was ultimately assayed by a second examiner. The certified weight of pure electrum content was found to be six shekels and one quarter. The remainder was copper-dressed silver of Phrygian extraction.

Phrasilas died for a middling house. The official record does not contain this.

I contain it here. I contain also this: that Phrasilas, in the last minutes of his life, did the work he had agreed not to do. He set the weights. He placed the metal on the pan. He watched the beam fail to level and understood, with the immediate comprehension of a man who has been an assayer for twenty years, exactly what he was looking at and exactly what it would cost him to write it down.

He wrote it down anyway.

He told his apprentice, three days before he died, that the danger of correcting a compromise is not failure but success — and that success, in such cases, produces its own specific danger. He was a man who knew this. He wrote the sentence regardless.

What I make of this I have set down and crossed out and set down again several times, and each time it comes to the same thing: there is a kind of honesty that is neither virtue nor courage, exactly, but something more habitual and therefore more difficult — the honesty of a man whose hands simply know their work too well to do it falsely, even when every other part of him has agreed to do otherwise. I do not know whether to call this admirable. I think it may be the most human thing in this account.

It is certainly the truest.

Lysanias, clerk, market authority of Sardis, in the reign of Ardys, second year of the new weighing standards.
