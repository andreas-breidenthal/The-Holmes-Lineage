# BOOK VI: THE ELIZABETHAN HOLMES
## *1461 – 1687*
### Yorkshire · London, England

---

### SIR WALTER HOLMES of Kirkbymoorside
**fl. 1461 — Yorkshire, England**

Walter Holmes of Kirkbymoorside, a market town eight miles from Pickering in the North Riding, was a man who understood, with a clarity unusual in his age, that battles are decided before they are fought — by the reading of allegiances, the assessment of resources, and the cold calculation of which side, on a given morning, was more likely to prevail. He fought with the Yorkist forces of Edward IV at the Battle of Towton on the twenty-ninth of March 1461, Palm Sunday, in a snowstorm, in what became the bloodiest engagement ever fought on English soil. He chose correctly and survived. He was knighted by Edward in the years following, the family ascending another rung on the ladder they had been climbing, in one form or another, since the wool merchants of the fourteenth century.

What distinguished Walter Holmes was not his courage, which was unremarkable for the age, but his political intelligence — the same faculty that had kept his ancestors alive through plague, war, and dynastic upheaval, now applied to the most dangerous game available. When the Yorkist sun began to set and Henry Tudor's star to rise, Walter read the shift early and repositioned himself accordingly, supporting Henry before Bosworth in 1485 with sufficient conviction to preserve the family's standing through the transition. He died with his name intact and his estate unforfeited, which in the England of the 1480s was an achievement requiring both intelligence and nerve.

### SIR EDWARD HOLMES
**~1480 — Yorkshire, England**

Sir Edward Holmes inherited from his father a knighthood, a Yorkshire estate, and a political position of careful neutrality carefully maintained. He was a consolidator rather than an adventurer — a man who understood that the Tudor settlement, whatever its costs, represented stability, and that stability rewarded those who did not press their claims too vigorously or attract the attention of those above them. He managed the estate through the reigns of Henry VII and the early years of Henry VIII with the methodical competence that had characterised the family's best generations, keeping his accounts in order, his tenants in reasonable contentment, and his opinions largely to himself.

His particular gift was the reading of men in authority — the assessment, from posture and precedent and the small signals of royal favour, of who stood well at court and who did not. In an age when such misjudgements could be fatal, Edward Holmes made none.

### SIR RALPH HOLMES
**fl. ~1530s — Yorkshire, England**

Sir Ralph Holmes came of age in the decade when the world turned. Henry VIII's break with Rome, and the dissolution of the monasteries that followed, redistributed more land and wealth in a shorter time than any event in English history since the Conquest. It required only a clear eye and a willingness to act to profit considerably from the upheaval — and Sir Ralph, surveying the situation with the analytical detachment that ran in his blood, saw exactly what was available, converted to the new religion at a moment precisely calculated to be neither suspiciously early nor dangerously late, and acquired monastic lands in the North Riding at prices that reflected the Crown's eagerness to sell rather than the property's actual worth.

He was not a man of principle in the theological sense; he was a man of observation in the practical one. The Reformation, to Sir Ralph Holmes, was a set of facts to be read and responded to, not a spiritual crisis to be resolved. He read them correctly and died considerably wealthier than he had been born, the family's position stronger than at any point since Walter's knighthood at Towton.

### SIR STAMFORD HOLMES
**fl. Elizabethan / Jacobean — Yorkshire / London, England**

Sir Stamford Holmes sat in successive Elizabethan and Jacobean parliaments and left almost no mark upon their proceedings, which was, one suspects, entirely deliberate. His two recorded contributions were these: in the first, he suggested during a debate on the transportation of convicts that the colonies of New England might serve as a destination for lawbreakers, and was informed by a colleague that felons were already being sent there as indentured labourers; in the second, he requested that the doors of St Stephen's Chapel be closed against the draught. He was a man who attended, observed, and kept his own counsel — the family's analytical habit turned, in this generation, toward the preservation of a comfortable anonymity in dangerous times.

His more consequential act was the marriage of his daughter into the Mycroft family of Yorkshire, connecting the Holmes line to George Riley Mycroft, naturalist and author of *The Beauties of Creation*, a man of considerable local reputation whose surname the family adopted as a given name and carried forward through every subsequent generation. It was a small act with a long echo.

### SIR HENRY HOLMES
**~1615 — Yorkshire, England**

Sir Henry Holmes was born in the reign of James I and came of age watching the country slide, with gathering speed, toward catastrophe. He was not a political man by temperament — the family had cultivated a careful distance from controversy since Sir Stamford's parliamentary career of studied insignificance — but the 1640s did not permit neutrality, and Henry Holmes, when forced to declare himself, declared for the King. He raised his son Symonds in a Royalist household, instilled in him the obligations of loyalty and the habits of careful observation, and appears to have died during the Interregnum years, leaving his son to reclaim what he could when the monarchy was restored.

He is a figure known almost entirely through his son — the generation that prepares rather than acts, whose significance lies in what it transmits rather than what it accomplishes. In a lineage as long as this one, such men are not the least important.

### SIR SYMONDS HOLMES
**fl. 1640s–1660s — Yorkshire, England**

Sir Symonds Holmes rode with Prince Rupert's cavalry at Marston Moor on the second of July 1644 and was present at one of the decisive defeats of the Royalist cause, surviving the rout that followed with his life and his convictions intact. The family's estates suffered during the Interregnum years — sequestration, fines, the grinding attrition of supporting a losing side — but Sir Symonds did not go into exile, and at the Restoration he was among those who found themselves, after years of patient loyalty, in a position to be rewarded.

What he did with the restored peace was characteristic of the best of his line. In the 1660s he became an early subscriber to Robert Hooke's *Micrographia* and a fellow of the newly chartered Royal Society — the first Holmes to apply the family's gift of close observation explicitly to the natural world, to set it within a framework of experiment and verification, to understand that the careful examination of minute particulars was not merely a practical skill but a method capable of unlocking the deepest secrets of nature. His grandson Sherlock would say that he believed in the science of deduction; Sir Symonds Holmes, two centuries earlier, had understood something very similar, and had found the institution that would one day give it a name.

### SIR RICHMOND HOLMES
**fl. 1670s — died 1687 — London / Yorkshire, England**

Sir Richmond Holmes moved to London after his father's death and discovered, as a number of clever men have discovered before and since, that cleverness in the company of the dissolute court of Charles II is an insufficient protection against its pleasures. His friendship with the Earl of Rochester — the most brilliant and most ruinous companion available to a young man of means in the 1670s — was expensive in every sense of the word, and Sir Richmond pursued it with an enthusiasm that his estate could not sustain. He could read men well enough; he simply chose, with the clarity of someone who knows exactly what they are doing, to read them toward his own entertainment rather than his own advantage.

He died in 1687 owing large sums to a considerable number of people, the estate at Hutton le Moors mortgaged against debts that would take three generations to fully account for. He left his son Selwyn the name, the house, and the problem.
