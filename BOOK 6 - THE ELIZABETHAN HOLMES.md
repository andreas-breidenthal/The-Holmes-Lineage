### ✦ WALTER HOLMES of Kirkbymoorside
**fl. 1461 — Yorkshire, England** · [REN]

Son of Edward. Walter Holmes, from Kirkbymoorside eight miles from Pickering, fought with the Yorkist forces of Edward IV at the Battle of Towton on 29 March 1461 — the bloodiest battle ever fought on English soil, ten hours of slaughter in a Palm Sunday snowstorm. He chose the right side. Several years after the battle he was knighted by Edward; the family went up another rung on the social ladder. He survived the subsequent transition from Yorkist to Tudor monarchy with his status intact — supporting Henry VII before Bosworth in 1485. A man of political intelligence as much as military loyalty. The gift, here, as the reading of which way the wind was turning. Holmes noted in a later pastiche that one of his distant relatives married the Commander in Chief of the Naval Forces of His Holiness the Pope — a connection that may originate in this generation's elevation.

*Children:*
- **[unnamed son]** — direct line; grandfather of Sir Ralph; see next entry.
- Sir Charles Holmes — knighted by Queen Elizabeth; documented in Bailey's *The Strange Adventures of Charlotte Holmes*.

### ◇ SIR EDWARD HOLMES
**~1480 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Sir Walter Holmes of Kirkbymoorside; father of Sir Ralph. He held the estate through the turbulent transition from Yorkist to Tudor rule — his father had backed Henry VII before Bosworth, and Edward inherited that political goodwill. He consolidated the family's position through the reigns of Henry VII and the early years of Henry VIII, a period of relative stability for those with the wit to avoid the king's displeasure. He managed it.

*Children:*
- **Sir Ralph Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

### ✦ SIR RALPH HOLMES
**fl. ~1530s — Yorkshire, England** · [REN]

Son of Walter's unnamed son; grandson of Sir Walter. In the mid-1530s, Sir Ralph — one of the century's more opportunist converts to Protestantism — was in a position to benefit substantially from the dissolution of the monasteries. The family's analytical gift here expressed as political timing: he read the Reformation correctly and profited from it while others hesitated. He raised the Holmes profile considerably.

*Children:*
- **Sir Stamford Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

### ✦ SIR STAMFORD HOLMES
**fl. Elizabethan / Jacobean — Yorkshire / London, England** · [REN]

Son of Sir Ralph. A member of successive Elizabethan and Jacobean parliaments, though an undistinguished one. Rennison records only two contributions to their proceedings: in one, he intervened in a debate on shipping convicts to Barbados to suggest that the colonies in New England might also be a good destination for lawbreakers — and was reminded by a fellow MP that felons were already being sent there as indentured labourers. In the other, he asked the Speaker whether the doors of St Stephen's Chapel, Westminster, could be closed since he and other members were feeling the draught. Under the later Tudors and Stuarts, the family made a point of avoiding the religious and political controversies of the time. His daughter married into the Mycroft family, connecting the line to George Riley Mycroft — Yorkshire naturalist and author of *The Beauties of Creation* — whose name the family would carry forward as their most distinctive given name.

*Children:*
- **Sir Henry Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.
- A daughter who married into the Mycroft family; her great-grandfather George Riley Mycroft was the Yorkshire naturalist whose surname became the Holmes family's most distinctive given name.

### ◇ SIR HENRY HOLMES
**~1615 — Yorkshire, England**

Son of Sir Stamford. Born in the reign of James I, came of age under Charles I as the country slid toward civil war. By the time of the confrontation between king and Parliament in the 1630s and 1640s, even the most lackadaisical of MPs and landowners were forced to choose sides. He raised Sir Symonds in a Royalist household, instilling the loyalty to the Crown that would send his son to fight with Prince Rupert at Marston Moor. He may not have lived to see the Restoration — dying in the Interregnum years, leaving his son to reclaim the estate when Charles II returned.

*Children:*
- **Sir Symonds Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

### ✦ SIR SYMONDS HOLMES
**fl. 1640s–1660s — Yorkshire, England** · [REN]

Son of Sir Henry Holmes. His mother was a granddaughter of George Riley Mycroft, the Yorkshire naturalist — through whom the name "Mycroft" entered the family's tradition of given names. He fought with Prince Rupert's cavalry at the Battle of Marston Moor on 2 July 1644 — a Royalist defeat, one of the turning points of the Civil War. The family suffered for its loyalty during Cromwell's Interregnum but was not driven into exile. At the Restoration he stood to prosper, as all who had kept the Royalist faith now did. In the 1660s he became one of the first subscribers to Robert Hooke's ground-breaking *Micrographia* (1665) and an early member of the Royal Society — the founding institution of English empirical science. Here, clearly and for the first time in the documented record, is the Holmes mind applied to science: the close observation of minute particulars, the delight in taxonomy and experiment, the belief that the world's secrets yield to careful looking. Sherlock Holmes's own methods are, in one sense, Sir Symonds Holmes's methods applied to crime.

*Children:*
- **Sir Richmond Holmes** — direct line; see next entry.

### ✦ SIR RICHMOND HOLMES
**fl. 1670s — died 1687 — London / Yorkshire, England** · [REN]

Son of Sir Symonds Holmes. After his father's death he moved south to London in the 1670s and spent more time on the fringes of Charles II's court than he did on his Yorkshire properties. Friendship with the dissolute Earl of Rochester — poet and philanderer — was an expensive indulgence. By the time of his death in 1687 Sir Richmond owed large sums to half the moneylenders in the capital. He died in debt, beginning the slow slide into indebtedness that would plague the family for generations to come.

*Children:*
- **Sir Selwyn Holmes** — direct line; see Book VII.
