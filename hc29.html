HOLKOS THE WANDERER

~1210 BC — The Levant

The Late Bronze Age Collapse arrived not as a single blow but as a cascade — each falling palace taking with it the trade networks and the administrative systems that the next palace depended on, until within a generation the entire interconnected world of the eastern Mediterranean had ceased to exist. The Hittite empire fell. Mycenae fell. Ugarit was burned and not rebuilt. The Egyptian new kingdom contracted to its core. The palace scribes and their Linear B tablets and their careful records of oil and wool and bronze disappeared as completely as if they had never been, and for centuries the world that replaced them could not write.

Holkos the Wanderer moved through this dissolution for twenty years — south from Anatolia through the Levantine coast, reading the direction of the catastrophe and staying one step ahead of it. The ground underfoot varied from packed coastal sand to broken stone paths scorched by fire, and the air often carried the sharp smell of ash or the sour tang of spoiled grain. He was not untouched by it. He lost brothers, trading partners, the commercial network of a lifetime. Yet he carried what remained: the knowledge of routes still open, the memory of who could be trusted, and the recognition of which communities were rebuilding rather than merely surviving.

The record of this period is preserved by Naram, a caravan scribe who attached himself to Holkos after witnessing his handling of a failing grain market. Naram was not formally trained in the palace tradition of record-keeping, but he had a disciplined habit of noting quantities, exchanges, and spoken agreements, and he carried a wax tablet and stylus at all times. His account reflects both what he observed and what he inferred, though his own limitations sometimes led him to misinterpret the significance of what he saw until events clarified it.

The Account Of Naram

Written on wax, to be transferred to clay when clay can be found. The account is set down as faithfully as memory and the condition of the road allow.

We came down out of the hills above Alalakh in the dry heat of late summer, when the dust rose in curtains from the track and settled into every crease of cloth and skin. The smell of that road I still remember — charred timber carried on the wind from somewhere inland, and beneath it the sweeter rot of grain that had been left too long in inadequate storage. The air tasted of endings. It had tasted of endings for three years.

Holkos had been silent for the better part of the morning. This was not unusual. There were two kinds of silence that I had learned to distinguish in the months since I had attached myself to him, and they were as different as still water and still stone. The first was the silence of emptiness, when a case had been resolved and his mind found nothing worthy of its machinery, and he would walk with his eyes unfocused and his jaw set, and I would catch him staring at the embers of a fire long after there was anything to see. During those periods he chewed the dried leaves of a certain bitter herb that the Hittite traders had carried — the ones that were no longer trading — and the chewing seemed to sharpen him, though he denied it was anything more than habit when I remarked on it.

The second silence was the silence of a trap about to spring.

I recognized the second silence by his hands. When his mind was fully engaged, he walked with his hands clasped behind him, and every few paces the fingers moved, calculating. They were moving now.

“You are counting something,” I said.

“I am counting discrepancies,” he said. “Which is to say, I am counting everything.”

I asked him what he meant by that, and he did not answer, which I had also learned to accept.

The settlement at the river crossing had not been a proper town for two seasons. What remained was a cluster of reed-roofed structures that had been warehouses, a well that still functioned, and the administrative compound of a man named Iarim-Adad, who had called himself a grain factor in the days when grain factoring was a profession that the region’s palace networks would recognize and regulate. Those networks were gone. Iarim-Adad remained. He had adapted, as certain men do, by becoming the fixed point around which other, less adaptive men orbited. He bought and stored and extended credit and collected on it, and in the absence of palace authority, this made him something between a merchant and a magistrate.

His compound gate was closed when we arrived. A guard — a hired man, I judged, by the way his spear-grip was too tight for someone who had carried one long — told us that Iarim-Adad was receiving no visitors. There had been a catastrophe, the guard said. A raid. The granary had been struck three nights past, the stock destroyed or scattered, and the factor himself was the only man in his employ to have survived the attack.

Holkos received this information with his head slightly tilted, as though he were listening to music at a great distance.

“The only survivor,” he said pleasantly.

“The others were killed,” said the guard.

“I see. And the grain — was it burned, or taken?”

The guard hesitated. “Scattered,” he said. “Spoiled. The raiders turned it out.”

“Turned it out,” Holkos repeated, and nodded with the air of a man making a note of something amusing. “We are traders from the north, looking to purchase. We will wait.”

The guard looked as though he wished to argue, found something in Holkos’s expression that made argument seem inadvisable, and retreated behind the gate to confer with someone. We waited in the compound’s shade.

I asked Holkos whether he intended to purchase grain at a settlement that had just lost its entire stock.

“I intend to speak with Iarim-Adad,” he said. “What I purchase will depend on what I find.”

The factor received us in his inner room, which smelled of old oil — the cheap rendered kind, not the pressed olive oil of the western coast — and the particular mustiness of administrative papyrus that has been stored in river damp. He was a heavyset man of perhaps fifty years, with the careful stillness of someone who has recently experienced a shock and is managing it with great deliberateness. His face was composed. His hands were folded on the table between us.

He told us his story with the smoothness of a man who had told it several times. Three nights past, raiders from the inland — he was vague about their origin, gesturing eastward in a way that encompassed a considerable area — had come over the compound wall after nightfall. His grain-workers had been killed before they could raise alarm. The granary seals had been broken. The stock — two thousand jars of emmer wheat, four hundred of barley, accumulated over two full trading seasons and representing the entirety of his current operating capital — had been destroyed. Contaminated with river water, or scattered on the ground, or taken; he had not been able to assess the full damage because the shock of it had left him incapacitated for a day, and by the time he had been able to examine the stores, the evidence had been confused by the subsequent attention of birds and vermin.

He was very sorry, but he had nothing to sell. He was very sorry, but he could extend no credit; his own obligations were now uncertain. He was, in summary, very sorry for everything, and perhaps we might find what we needed further south along the coast.

I found his account coherent and his grief believable, and I was formulating the polite language of departure when I observed that Holkos had not moved.

He was examining the room. Not looking around it — examining it, the way I had seen him examine a cargo hold, systematically, in sections, as though taking inventory. His eyes moved from the shelf of jars to the administrative papyrus rolls to the oil lamp to the table to the hands of Iarim-Adad, which remained folded.

“A great loss,” said Holkos. “Two trading seasons of accumulated stock. I imagine the records were also damaged in the confusion.”

“The records were kept in the compound archive, which was not entered,” said Iarim-Adad carefully.

“Then you will have the intake manifests. The weights and origins of everything stored.”

A pause of perhaps two heartbeats. “Of course.”

“Then you can tell me,” said Holkos, with great pleasantness, “the manifest number assigned to the shipment from the factor at Ras Shamra, which arrived, I believe, approximately twelve days before the raid.”

Iarim-Adad’s hands, which had been so carefully folded, moved. Only slightly. Only for a moment. But they moved.

“The shipment from Ras Shamra,” he said.

“The barley. Approximately ninety jars. It would have been the most recent significant intake before your losses.” Holkos leaned forward, and now his tone was the tone of a man genuinely curious about a puzzle. “I ask because we passed, on our road south, the caravan of a man named Shipti-Baal, who is moving north from the coast. He was carrying barley. Ninety jars. I recognized the storage vessels as coastal manufacture — the glazing technique is specific to the river-mouth kilns — and when I remarked on this to Shipti-Baal, he was kind enough to tell me he had acquired them, sealed, from a transport agent he met on the inland track, three days north of here. Two days before your raid.”

The silence in the room was of a different quality than the silences I had noted on the road.

“He will have noted the transaction,” Holkos continued. “Shipti-Baal is a careful man, as all surviving traders must now be. He will have the weight-marks on the jars, the agent’s name, the day of purchase. He shared all of this with me, and I, being a man of habit, wrote it down.” He glanced at me.

I opened my wax tablet. I had indeed written it down, though at the time I had thought it was simply the kind of conversation Holkos conducted with everyone on the road — the relentless cataloguing of goods and routes and transactions that had initially exhausted me and which I had come to understand was how he read the world.

“The agent’s name as given was Kirri,” I said. “The jars were described as emmer and barley, mixed, approximately eighty to ninety in number, sealed with a mark that Shipti-Baal described as a running ox over two horizontal lines.”

“Your seal?” Holkos said to Iarim-Adad.

The factor said nothing.

“We also passed,” Holkos continued, and his voice had taken on the quality of a man building a structure one stone at a time, certain of the final shape, “a family group traveling with a hired cart. They had recently purchased, from a man they met at a river crossing two days north, three months of grain for their household. The transaction was paid in copper, they told me, which surprised them, as copper is increasingly rare as currency. The storage jars were river-mouth manufacture. Their quantity was consistent with an off-loading of approximately forty jars from a larger stock.”

He paused.

“And on the very road from which we descended this morning, I observed, in the softer dust where the track bends near the dry streambed, the wheel-ruts of heavily laden carts — four of them, I think, moving south, which is consistent with the transport of a large quantity of sealed grain jars. The ruts are between six and eight days old. The cart wheels were broad, which is the manufacture of your district, not the coastal style. And the depth of impression is consistent with full jars, not empty ones.”

He folded his own hands on the table, a precise echo of the gesture Iarim-Adad had used, and looked at the factor with something that was not warmth but was, I thought, a kind of professional respect.

“You moved your grain before the raid,” Holkos said. “Two days before, at minimum, and likely three — the timing required to coordinate the dispersal through multiple agents and leave no single transaction large enough to attract notice. You retained enough of your workers’ trust to keep silence until they were paid off and gone, and then you staged the raid with hired men. Your grain was not destroyed. It was sold, in fragments, south and north along the road, for immediate copper, which does not leave papyrus traces. You are now the sole survivor of a catastrophe that has erased two seasons of debt and obligation at a stroke, and if your creditors believe the stock is gone, you are free.”

I will record what I observed next, because I believe it to be important to understanding the man I travel with.

Iarim-Adad did not deny it. He was a man who understood when a position was untenable, and he abandoned it with a merchant’s pragmatism. He confirmed the essential facts — the debts, the calculation, the movement of stock. He had owed obligations to three palace administrators, and all three of those palaces were gone or contracting, and their agents were traveling the roads collecting debts from men who had nothing to repay them with. The grain would have been seized. So he had dispersed it into untraceable small transactions, paid off his workers, and presented the world with a corpse of a business from which no further flesh could be stripped.

He then asked, with the directness of a man who has moved past shame into calculation, what Holkos intended to do with this knowledge.

Holkos was quiet for a long moment. I had expected him to summon whatever local authority existed — the settlement’s headman, some representative of the diminished Egyptian administration that still claimed nominal oversight of the coastal trade. I had begun composing, in my head, the form of the statement I would be asked to write.

“I intend to do nothing with it,” said Holkos.

I looked up from my tablet.

“The men you owed,” he continued, “are agents of palaces that no longer exist in any functioning sense. The obligations were legal under a system that has ceased. The grain, dispersed as it is, is already feeding people along two roads who would otherwise be hungry. The workers you paid are traveling with copper in their pockets, which is more than most men traveling these roads can say.” He stood, straightening his cloak. “The world you built your fraud against is already gone, Iarim-Adad. You committed a crime against a corpse.”

He walked toward the door, and then stopped.

“There is, however, the matter of Shipti-Baal, who purchased ninety jars of your grain at a price I suspect was considerably below their value, because your agent needed a fast transaction. Shipti-Baal was not one of your original creditors. He simply found a bargain on a road, and the bargain was made with your property. He will sell those jars at his usual margin. I would like you to send a man north to meet him before he reaches the next market, with enough copper to close the difference between what he paid and what the grain is worth.” He turned. “This is not negotiable.”

Iarim-Adad considered this for a moment. “And if I do?”

“Then I will have nothing further to write in my scribe’s tablet about the affairs of this compound,” said Holkos, “and we will continue south.”

He left. I scrambled to collect my stylus and follow.

On the road south, in the late afternoon when the dust had gone golden and the smell of the sea was beginning to cut through the ash-smell that had followed us for days, I asked him why.

He walked several paces before answering.

“The law that Iarim-Adad defrauded,” he said, “is a law that does not exist anymore. The administrators who are collecting its debts are parasites on a dead system, stripping the last flesh from its bones and calling it legitimate commerce.” He paused. “I am not interested in serving a legal structure. I am interested in the truth of what happened, and in ensuring that no innocent party is left worse than they would otherwise be.”

“Shipti-Baal,” I said.

“Shipti-Baal paid an honest price for what he believed was honestly offered. He should receive an honest return. That is the extent of justice I can construct on this road, with these tools, in this year.” He glanced at the western sky, where the light was failing. “It is not a satisfying quantity of justice. But it is real, which makes it more valuable than the other kind.”

I wrote this down, as I write everything down. He did not ask me to. He has never asked me either to record or to omit anything, which I take as a form of trust, or possibly as indifference. I have not yet determined which.

The road south continued. The smell of ash diminished, and the smell of the sea increased. Holkos walked with his hands clasped behind him, and after a time the fingers were still, and I knew the case was closed in whatever interior chamber he kept them.

The bitter herb, I noticed, remained untouched in his pack for the remainder of that day.

Here ends this account. Further events are recorded on the tablet that follows, if the wax has held.
