HOLKOS THE WANDERER

~1210 BC — The Levant

THE ACCOUNT OF NARAM

Written on wax, to be transferred to clay when clay can be found. The account is set down as faithfully as memory and the condition of the road allow.

I.

She was sitting on a milestone at the side of the track when we came around the bend above the river valley, and she did not look up as we approached. This is worth noting, because in the third year of the collapse — when the roads between Alalakh and the coast carried as many desperate men as goods — a woman sitting alone on a milestone without watching the road was either beyond fear or performing an approximation of it. Holkos slowed his pace without breaking stride, a compression of movement so subtle I nearly missed it, and I understood that we would be stopping.

She was perhaps thirty-five years of age, dark-haired, with the calloused hands of a household that had worked its own grain. Her travelling cloak was good wool, but travel-worn. At her feet was a bundle that contained, I judged by its shape, a sleeping mat and perhaps three days of provisions. She had come some distance and intended to go no further until she had spoken.

"You are a trader," she said to Holkos, without looking up. It was not a question.

"I am."

"There is a man at the river settlement who deals in grain."

"Iarim-Adad," said Holkos. He said it the way a man names a road he has already decided to take.

She looked up then. Her eyes were the specific kind of red that comes not from recent weeping but from several nights of it, with dry periods between. "You know him."

"I know of him. My scribe has noted his operations in the records." He glanced at me, and I opened my tablet, though I had written nothing about Iarim-Adad in it yet. I have learned that the tablet is sometimes required as a prop before it is required as an instrument. "You have come from the settlement."

"Three days ago." She sat back down on the milestone. "My husband worked for Iarim-Adad. Grain-work — intake, sealing, manifest. Seven years."

Holkos clasped his hands behind his back. "And your husband is dead."

She looked at him with the stillness of a woman who has already weathered the moment of being told, and for whom the fact has become simply the fixed coordinate around which everything else must now be arranged. "Six days ago. Found on the track between the compound and the river. A wound to the head. They recorded it as a fall in the dark."

"He walked that track for seven years."

"Every morning." Her voice was flat, but it was a flatness that had been pressed down hard over something not flat at all. "He knew every stone."

Holkos said nothing for a moment. He was examining her with the particular quality of attention I had come to recognise — not a social look, not a sympathetic look, but an inventory. His eyes moved from her hands to her bundle to the wear-pattern on her sandals to the specific set of her jaw.

"Tell me about the days before he died," he said. "What he noticed. What troubled him."

She was quiet for a moment, and the wind came down the valley carrying the smell I had grown accustomed to in those years — charred timber from somewhere inland, and beneath it the sweeter rot of grain left too long without proper storage. The smell of a world dismantling itself at the joints.

"He was not a man who troubled easily," she said. "That was the first thing — that he was troubled at all. He came home four days before he died and sat at the table without eating, which he never did. When I asked him he said there was a thing at the compound he didn't understand."

"Describe it precisely," said Holkos. "As he described it to you."

"The manifests," she said. "The intake records. He said quantities had been altered — figures crossed out and rewritten, in a different hand than the original entries. Not large changes. Incremental. But consistent, over many months, and always in one direction." She paused. "He said it was as though someone had been recording less than arrived."

Holkos's fingers, behind his back, began to move.

"And there were sounds," she continued. "He heard cart-wheels on the compound road after the third watch. Not once — several nights in succession. He thought at first it was early delivery from the coastal routes, which sometimes arrived at odd hours to avoid the heat. But the direction was wrong. The sound came from inside the compound wall, moving out."

"He heard this but did not see it."

"He was not asked to work night hours. He was a manifests man, not a loader." She looked at her hands. "He raised it with the factor. Iarim-Adad told him it was a transfer arrangement — stock being moved to a secondary storage location across the river, nothing unusual, and nothing that concerned the manifests function." Her jaw tightened. "Four days later he was dead."

I had been writing. I set down my stylus and looked at Holkos. He was gazing at a fixed point somewhere above Shatiya's head with the expression of a man working a calculation he has not yet reached the end of.

She then asked, with the directness of a woman who has moved past the stage of hoping for comfortable outcomes, what Holkos intended to do with what she had told him.

"I intend to understand it," he said. He looked at her. "Which is not the same as being able to remedy it. I want you to hold that distinction before you invest hope in it."

"I have not come here for hope," she said. "I have come because no one at the settlement will write it down. I want what happened to Kunni written by someone who is not in Iarim-Adad's compound." She glanced at me. "You have a scribe."

"I do." Holkos turned to me. "Write her name, and the names of all the dead, and the dates."

I wrote them: Shatiya, wife of Kunni, grain-manifest worker, found on the compound track, six days past.

"Good," said Holkos. "Now we will go to the settlement, and you will wait at the well. There will be a portion of our provisions in your bundle before we leave." He said this with the practicality of a man resolving a logistical problem, and then he turned south on the road without further ceremony, and I fell into step beside him.

II.

He did not speak for some time. I had learned to read his silences as precisely as I read tablet entries, and this was the second kind — not the silence of emptiness, when a case had been resolved and his mind found nothing worthy of its machinery, and he walked with unfocused eyes and reached, eventually, for the dried leaves of the bitter Hittite herb he carried in his pack. This was the silence of a trap in the early stages of construction, when the shape of the thing was not yet visible but the materials were being assessed.

I should record, since it bears on what followed, what I had come to know of him by that point. Holkos was lean in the way that men become lean when they have been walking for years, and dark from the same cause, and hawk-nosed, sharp-jawed, with the stillness of something conserving itself between exertions. His face was never fully at rest — there was always something occurring behind the eyes, some rapid internal transaction that his expression did not quite conceal. When he looked at a thing he looked at all of it: his eyes moved in sections, left to right, top to bottom, the way a scribe reads a tablet. He was not a large man, but he had the quality of occupying a precise amount of space and being entirely within it, which I have observed makes people uncertain about how to approach him.

He walked with his hands clasped behind his back, and when his mind was fully engaged, the fingers moved.

They were moving now.

"You have a theory," I said.

"I have the beginning of one," he said. "Tell me what you make of it."

I considered. "Someone has been skimming from intake. Under-recording what arrives, selling the difference outside the manifest system. The cart sounds at night are the movement of the skimmed stock."

"That is the available inference from the evidence as presented," he said. "What troubles you about it?"

I thought about this. "The quantities. If he has been doing it for months and the manifests show only incremental changes, the total taken would be — significant. But the compound's reputation is for accumulated stock. Two full seasons."

"Precisely. A man skimming from intake would have a thinner stockpile than his manifests showed — not a larger one." He glanced at me. "What does the night movement of carts suggest to you, if the traffic is outbound?"

I opened my mouth and closed it again. "That the grain is not being stolen in but out."

"The manifests were altered not to conceal an incoming theft but to conceal an outgoing one," he said. "Someone has been writing down arrivals that were real, and then arranging for the stock to quietly leave, and the manifest alterations are the effort to make the departure look like a recording error rather than a removal." He paused. "This is, you will observe, a different crime than the one Shatiya has described. It is a more elaborate one. It requires organisation and time." Another pause. "The question now is what the grain departed to fund, and what event was arranged to explain the empty granary after the fact."

I wrote none of this down, because I was thinking. The thinking produced an image: a man who has spent months quietly liquidating his stock, who needs the stock's disappearance to appear catastrophic rather than deliberate.

"A raid," I said.

He said nothing, which I had learned to interpret as confirmation.

"But we don't know this," I said. "We know the manifests were altered and we know carts moved at night. We know nothing yet about where the grain went."

"Which is why," he said, "we will observe the road before we observe the compound."

III.

We had company on the road south for a portion of the way: a man travelling alone and north, who gave his name as Mattan and his occupation as a grain-worker lately employed at the river settlement. He was eager to talk, which I noted, since men with things to hide are often eager to talk in order to establish a version of events before questions arise. He described himself as one of the lucky ones — he had been at the river filling water jars when the raid came, heard the sounds of it, and run rather than returned, a decision he framed with practised self-deprecation as cowardice.

The raiders, he said, had been Sea Peoples. He was certain of it. The crested helmets, he said — a distinctive silhouette, the tall horsehair plumes of the kind he had seen on the Egyptian temple reliefs at the coastal city. Moving in a war-band, he said, thirty at minimum, well-coordinated, not the disorganised opportunism of a starving mob. An organised military action, clearly, against a known commercial target.

I wrote it down. The detail was vivid and specific and internally consistent, and I said as much to Holkos after Mattan had continued north and we had continued south.

"Too specific," said Holkos.

"How so?"

"The crested helmets. The horsehair plumes." He did not look at me. "A man who witnessed a night raid at sufficient distance to escape it, in the dark, describes the precise iconographic detail of the raiders' headdress." He paused. "That is the description of a man who has seen the reliefs, Naram. Not a man who has seen the raiders. The plumes are a detail from stone. In the dark, in fear, at distance, you see shapes and hear sounds. You do not see the composition of a headdress."

I considered this. He was right. I had written it down without examining it, because it was vivid and it was what I expected — the roads were full of accounts of Sea Peoples, some true, some borrowed, some simply the era's available explanation for catastrophe.

"He was paid to say it," I said.

"Or he was present at the raid and is providing cover for its true nature. Either way, he is someone's instrument." He glanced back along the road. "Write that down as well, and note his direction of travel and his description."

I wrote it.

IV.

The bend near the dry streambed was where Holkos stopped, and the stopping was so sudden that I nearly walked into him. He crouched at the edge of the track where the dust had accumulated in the shelter of a rock outcrop — the one place on this stretch of road where the surface was soft enough to hold an impression — and examined the wheel-ruts with the focused attention of a man reading a text he has been waiting some time to find.

Four sets of ruts. Deep. Broad-wheeled — the manufacture of the compound's district, not the narrower coastal style. He pressed two fingers into the dust beside the deepest impression and held them there, reading the resistance.

"Full jars," he said. "Not empty. Moving south." He stood. "Six to eight days old. Four carts, heavily laden, moving south along the track that leads away from the compound." He looked at me. "If someone has been stealing grain into the compound under cover of darkness, the ruts would be deeper on the inbound track and shallower on the outbound. These ruts go south. Outbound. Full."

The initial theory — intake skimming, a thinner stockpile than the manifests showed — collapsed against this evidence like a wall against a river. The grain had not been stolen in. It had been moved out, in quantity, in the days before the raid.

"How many jars?" I asked. "To fill four heavily laden carts."

"A significant fraction of two seasons' stock," he said. "Enough to matter. Not necessarily all of it — you would leave enough behind to make the granary appear full to a casual observer, and destroy or scatter the remainder when the raid was staged, so that anyone examining the aftermath would find genuine wreckage and assume it accounted for everything." He began walking south again. "The raid did not destroy the stock. It provided the explanation for stock that had already been dispersed."

I understood then, fully, what Kunni had heard: not the sound of incoming theft but of systematic outgoing liquidation. What he had seen in the manifests was not evidence of skimming but of the careful administration of a deliberate dismantlement.

And Iarim-Adad had told him it was nothing, and four days later he was dead.

I wrote nothing, because my hands were occupied with the act of understanding, which sometimes requires them to be still.

V.

We had passed, in the hours before reaching the dry streambed, two parties worth recording.

The first was the caravan of a man named Shipti-Baal, moving north along the coastal track, whose mule-train carried sealed grain jars I had examined at Holkos's direction while Holkos and Shipti-Baal discussed the northern routes with the elaborate courtesy of two men who are each assessing the other's trustworthiness. The glazing on the jars was specific: a technique from the river-mouth kilns on the coast, identifiable by the blue-grey of the slip and the double-cord pattern at the neck. A consistent manufacture, not mixed origin. Shipti-Baal had acquired them sealed from a transport agent named Kirri, on the inland track, three days north of the settlement, two days before the raid. Ninety jars. Payment in copper, which Kirri had required. The seal-mark on the jars: a running ox over two horizontal lines. I had written all of it, including the manifest number that Shipti-Baal had noted for his own records — he was, as Holkos observed quietly as we walked away, a careful man.

The second party was a family group with a hired cart, met at a river crossing, who had purchased three months of household grain from a man at the crossing the previous week. River-mouth jars, copper payment, the seller in evident haste. Approximately forty jars' worth.

Two separate dispersal transactions. Different agents, different roads, different buyers. No single transaction large enough to attract notice. All copper, leaving no papyrus trace.

I set this beside the wheel-ruts in my mind, and the shape that emerged was the shape Holkos had already named on the road.

VI.

The settlement at the river crossing had not been a proper town for two seasons. The warehouses were reed-roofed shells; the administrative compound's walls showed the patchwork of deferred maintenance. But it had the tenacity of a place where one man had made himself the fixed point around which less adaptive men orbited. The compound gate was closed. A guard in the gatehouse shade watched us approach with the over-invested attention of a man who has been given instructions he does not fully understand.

Holkos stopped six paces from the gate.

"I need you to take a message inside," he said to me, without looking at the guard. He spoke at a volume calibrated, I realised, for both of us to hear. "Tell the guard that you are a caravan scribe travelling with a grain trader from the north, and that you carry urgent information: that the caravan of a merchant named Shipti-Baal, currently moving north along the coastal track, has been detained by Egyptian administrative officers demanding provenance documentation for ninety sealed grain jars in his possession. The officers are a half-day's march north and will reach this settlement by nightfall." He paused. "Tell them Shipti-Baal has named Iarim-Adad as the factor of origin, and that the officers have requested that a representative of this compound present the corresponding intake manifest at first light tomorrow."

He held my gaze for a moment.

"Produce your tablet," he said, "and show them the entry regarding Shipti-Baal — names, dates, jar count, the seal-mark. Present it as a copy of the administrative citation." He glanced at the guard and then back at me, and his voice dropped fractionally. "A man who has nothing to hide will produce the manifest immediately or invite us in to examine the archive. A man who has something to hide will do one of two other things. Either he will tell you the archive was destroyed in the raid — which will be a lie, because a moment ago he told a client the archive was undamaged. Or he will attempt to send a man north to intercept Shipti-Baal before the story holds up to examination."

"And you will be watching," I said.

"I will be standing where I can see the gate and the road north." He moved three steps to his left, into the shadow of the warehouse wall. He was not invisible. A man looking would see him. But he stood with the quality of something that does not draw the eye, and I had learned that this was not accident.

He looked at me once more. "The guard's spear is not shouldered," he said. "It will not be shouldered when you approach. Do not step within its arc."

I looked at the guard. The spear was held at port — diagonally across the body, not grounded. He was right.

I will record honestly that I was frightened. The roads of the collapse had taught me many things, and one of them was that frightened men with hired weapons make decisions in fractions of moments. The guard at the gate was frightened. I could see it in the quality of his stillness, which was the stillness of a coiled thing rather than a resting one.

I walked to the gate and kept to the right of the spear's arc and delivered the message.

The guard's face underwent several rapid changes. He told me to wait. He went inside.

There was a pause of perhaps a hundred heartbeats. I counted them, because counting is what I do when I need to occupy the part of my mind that would otherwise concern itself with the proximity of armed men.

The gate opened.

Two men came out first. Not administrative men. Not merchants. They had the flat, assessing gaze of men employed for a purpose that does not require them to speak, and they moved to either side of the gate with the practised positioning of people who have done this before. The nearer one stopped close enough to me that I could smell the oil on his leather — close enough, I registered, that moving away from him would require me to move toward the other one.

I did not move.

Behind them, Iarim-Adad emerged into the afternoon light. He was heavyset, perhaps fifty, with the careful stillness of a man who has recently made a decision and is now committed to it. He looked at me and then, very deliberately, at the warehouse wall to his left.

Holkos was already moving.

He stepped out of the shadow with the ease of a man concluding a stroll, and he placed himself between me and the nearer of the two hired men with a geometry so precise it could only have been calculated in advance. He was not large. He was simply entirely present, in the exact location the situation required, at the exact moment it required him there.

"Iarim-Adad," he said pleasantly. "The shade inside will be preferable to this heat, I think. My scribe and I are grateful for the invitation."

The factor looked at him for a long moment — the kind of look that makes several assessments simultaneously. Then he said, pleasantly enough: "Of course. Please come in."

VII.

The inner room smelled of old oil and the mustiness of administrative papyrus stored in river damp. Iarim-Adad sat across the table from us with his hands folded, the composed stillness of a man who has switched strategies mid-approach and is now selecting another. One of the hired men had followed us to the door. I was aware of him in the way one is aware of a dog of uncertain temperament at the edge of vision.

"You have had a difficult few days," said Holkos, with genuine sympathy.

"A devastating loss," said the factor. "Two full trading seasons. I am fortunate to be alive."

"Fortunate indeed." Holkos nodded. "The Egyptian officers on the northern road will require the intake manifest for the Ras Shamra shipment — the barley, approximately ninety jars. The most recent significant intake before your losses." He glanced at me.

I opened my tablet and read aloud: "Manifest record as noted by the merchant Shipti-Baal: ninety jars, emmer and barley, sealed with a running ox over two horizontal lines. Purchased from an agent named Kirri on the inland track, eight days past, for copper payment. Shipti-Baal has the original transaction weight-marks."

Iarim-Adad's hands, so carefully folded, did not move. He was good. "A most unfortunate situation for Shipti-Baal," he said. "However, I am afraid I cannot assist. The shock of the raid left me unable to examine the archive for some days, and when I did, I found that the confusion of the aftermath had — damaged certain of the records."

Holkos tilted his head. "I see. Damaged."

"Water, most likely. Or the smoke."

"You told the administration post at the coast, in a message sent four days ago, that the archive was unaffected." Holkos said this with perfect pleasantness. "The message is recorded. My scribe has noted it."

I had not noted it. I produced my stylus and made a mark on the tablet in a position that suggested I was finding the relevant entry. Naram the prop, performing again.

A very brief pause. "I may have been — imprecise, in the shock of the moment."

"Of course." Holkos leaned back with the air of a man entirely comfortable with the pace of the conversation. "Then let us approach it differently. You told us, just now, that the losses represent two full trading seasons of accumulated stock. That is a considerable sum. I imagine your creditors will need to understand the scale of it."

"It is a matter of great grief."

"I imagine it is. Two seasons of accumulated stock." He paused. "And yet — I spoke this morning with a man on the road north of here. A man named Mattan, who described himself as your former grain-worker. He was fortunate enough to be at the river when the raid occurred."

Iarim-Adad's expression did not change. It was the non-change of a practised man.

"He described the raiders in considerable detail," Holkos continued. "Sea Peoples. Crested helmets, horsehair plumes, thirty strong, coordinated. A very vivid account."

"A terrifying experience."

"Quite. He described the plumes with particular precision. The specific horsehair composition. The exact silhouette of the crest against the sky." Holkos regarded the factor with an expression of mild curiosity. "A night raid. At distance. In the dark." He let the words settle. "One does not see the composition of a headdress in the dark, Iarim-Adad, at a distance sufficient to allow escape. One sees shapes. One hears sounds. The precision of that account is the precision of a man who has examined the temple reliefs at Megiddo — not a man who witnessed a raid."

Iarim-Adad said nothing.

"Mattan is your man," Holkos said. "Moving north to be clear of the roads when the Egyptian officers arrive. Providing a credible account of the raiders in case anyone on the road thought to ask." He folded his own hands on the table, a precise echo of the factor's gesture. "But there are no Egyptian officers. You know that, I think. You knew it the moment I walked through your gate."

The factor looked at him for a long moment. Then, with the smooth recalibration of a man who has been outmanoeuvred and is selecting his next position, he smiled. It was not a warm smile.

"You are sharp," he said. "And sharp men on this road have found it useful to be — practical." He reached to the shelf beside him and produced a small leather pouch. He set it on the table between us. The weight of it, from its sound, was copper. "There are enough uncertainties on the roads between here and the coast that a scribe's tablet might be lost, or damaged, or simply fail to find its way to the relevant authority. I am in a position to make certain inconveniences disappear, for men who understand the value of a flexible memory."

I looked at the pouch. I looked at Holkos.

Holkos looked at the pouch with an expression I had not previously seen on him — not anger, not contempt, but a kind of precise interest, as though he were observing the confirmation of an expected result.

"The road south is not without its hazards," added Iarim-Adad, pleasantly. "Particularly for men travelling without local knowledge. I employ men who know these roads well." He did not look toward the door. He did not need to.

The silence in the room was of a quality I can only describe as load-bearing. It was holding something up.

"You are attempting two things simultaneously," said Holkos, "and they contradict each other. A bribe suggests I have leverage worth purchasing. A threat suggests you have force sufficient to negate that leverage without purchase." He tilted his head. "Both cannot be true. If your men at the door were sufficient to the problem, you would not have produced the copper. And if the copper were sufficient, you would not have mentioned the road."

He let this sit for a moment.

"I will tell you what I have," he said. His voice had changed — not in volume, not in pitch, but in the particular quality that I can only describe as the closing of a door. "I have the manifest details from Shipti-Baal, including the seal-mark, the agent's name, and the transaction record. I have the family group at the river crossing, their purchase, their description of the seller. I have the wheel-ruts on the southern track — broad-wheeled, four carts, full load, six to eight days old, moving south. I have Mattan's statement and the specific impossibility of its detail." He reached forward and picked up the leather pouch without opening it and set it back down in front of Iarim-Adad. "And I have the manifest entry you just told me was damaged by water and smoke, which I suspect is undamaged and would show me precisely what I already know."

He paused.

"I also have Kunni."

The name fell into the room differently than the others.

"Kunni noticed the manifests," Holkos said. "He heard the carts at night. He asked about it and was told it was a transfer arrangement. Four days later he was found on the track he had walked for seven years, with a wound to his head." He looked at the factor with the cold, flat attention of a man who has ceased to find the puzzle interesting and is now simply recording its dimensions. "You may not have intended his death. Intention is, however, a question for whatever authority survives in this region. My concern is the evidence."

Iarim-Adad was very still.

"The copper," he said at last, quietly.

"Is insufficient," said Holkos. "And the men at the door are a calculation I made before I sent Naram to your gate. If you had intended to use them, you would have done so before I was inside this room, where any sound of violence becomes a narrative that travels south faster than your agents." He held the factor's gaze. "You are a pragmatic man. You built a complex operation under considerable pressure, timed it precisely, and you almost succeeded. The one thing you did not account for was a manifest worker who knew every stone on his road."

He let that settle.

"Now," said Holkos. "Here is what will happen."

VIII.

He spoke for some time. I wrote it all, because it was important and because he did not tell me not to.

Shatiya would leave the compound with a sum sufficient for passage south and the establishment of a modest household — drawn from the copper Iarim-Adad had attempted to use for a bribe, which was, Holkos observed, a suitable reassignment of funds. A man would go north before nightfall to meet Shipti-Baal on the road, carrying enough copper to close the difference between what he had paid for the jars and what they were worth. The archive would be produced, intact, and the manifest for the Ras Shamra shipment would be noted in my tablet.

Iarim-Adad said, very quietly: "And the rest of it."

"The men you owed are agents of palaces that no longer function," said Holkos. "The grain, dispersed as it is, is already feeding people along two roads who would otherwise go hungry. The workers you paid are travelling with copper in their pockets, which is more than most men on these roads can say." He stood, and straightened his cloak with the precise movement of a man concluding an appointment. "You committed a fraud against a legal structure that has ceased to exist. I am not a magistrate of a dead system." He paused. "But Kunni walked a road he knew for seven years, and he will not walk it again, and his wife sat on a milestone for three days because there was no one in this settlement who would write down what had been done to him." He looked at the factor. "I cannot build a court on this road. I can build a record. If these terms are not met by morning, the record travels south with the next caravan, and whatever authority remains at the coastal post can make of it what they will."

He walked to the door. The man at the door moved aside.

"There is one further thing," said Holkos, without turning. "Mattan. Call him back south. He is to give no further account of the raid to anyone. If I encounter him again on this road, telling his story about horsehair plumes in the dark, I will add his name and his description to the record, and the record will note his direction of travel and the date." He turned then, and his expression was the one I had no name for — not cold, not warm, but precise. "He has told his story once too often as it is."

He left. I scrambled to collect my stylus and follow.

IX.

We brought Shatiya to the compound before the light failed. She walked with us without speaking, and when Iarim-Adad produced the copper — counted out, pressed into her hands with the mechanical courtesy of a man discharging an obligation he has decided not to resist — she held it and looked at it for a long moment. I do not know what she saw in it. Something, I think, that was adjacent to what she had come for without being the thing itself.

On the road south, when the dust had gone golden and the smell of the sea was beginning to cut through the ash-smell that had followed us for days, I fell into step beside Holkos and asked him what he made of it. The whole of it.

He walked several paces before answering.

"The law Iarim-Adad defrauded," he said, "is a law that no longer exists. The administrators collecting its debts are parasites stripping the last flesh from a dead system and calling it commerce." He paused. "I am not interested in serving legal structures. I am interested in the truth of what happened, and in ensuring that no innocent party is left worse than they would otherwise be."

"Shatiya," I said.

"Will travel south with means. Which is not justice, but it is real, which makes it more valuable than the theoretical kind." He looked at the road ahead. "Shipti-Baal will be compensated for what he paid in good faith. Which is also real."

"And Kunni," I said.

He was quiet for a long moment. "Kunni noticed a thing that did not fit, and he asked about it, which is what an honest man does. He was killed for it, which is what a frightened man does to an honest one." He paused. "The record has his name in it. That is all I can do on this road with these tools in this year. It is not a satisfying quantity of justice." He glanced at the western sky, where the light was failing in long horizontal bands the colour of fired clay. "But he will not be unwritten. That is something."

I had not previously heard him use that construction. I wrote it down.

"The gate," I said, after a while. "When you sent me to the gate. You knew what the two men would do."

"I knew what a frightened man protecting a secret does when the secret is threatened. They would emerge. And outside the wall is a different geometry than inside it." He looked at me. "I apologise for placing you in that position. It was necessary."

"The spear," I said. "You told me which side to stand on."

"Yes."

"You had calculated it."

"I calculate everything," he said. "That is what the fingers are for."

I considered this, and then I said: "You knew he would try to bribe us before he threatened us."

"A pragmatic man always tries to purchase an obstacle before he attempts to break it. It is more efficient, and efficiency is the governing principle of the pragmatic mind." He paused. "When he threatened us after the bribe failed, I knew we had him. A man with genuine force behind him does not need to name it. He names it because he is hoping you will imagine it larger than it is."

"And were they? Larger than they were?"

He was quiet for a moment. "Probably not," he said. "But the geometry was still preferable to finding out."

I wrote this down. He did not ask me to. He has never asked me either to record or to omit anything, which I take as a form of trust, or possibly as indifference, or possibly as something more precise than either word can hold, which I have not yet found the language for. I suspect I will require more road before I do.

Shatiya walked ahead of us on the track, her bundle at her shoulder, the leather pouch at her belt. The sea-smell was stronger now than the ash-smell. The bitter herb, I noticed, remained untouched in Holkos's pack as the light failed.

After a time, the fingers stilled.

Here ends this account. Further events are recorded on the tablet that follows, if the wax has held.
