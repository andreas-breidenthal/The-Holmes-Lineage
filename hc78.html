HOLKOS-THEODOROS

~325 AD — Constantinople / Gothic territory

Theodoros — gift of god in Greek — was the name added to mark the family's Christian accommodation, grafted onto the old name-element as a declaration of faith and a practical adaptation to a world in which such declarations were increasingly necessary. Holkos-Theodoros served in the frontier administration of the eastern empire, posted to the territories where Roman authority met Gothic power along the lower Danube, in the generation before the great migrations would sweep that frontier away entirely.

He was an intelligence officer in all but name — assessing Gothic military capacity, political organisation, and strategic intention from the evidence of traders, deserters, and his own careful observation of the movements and dispositions of the tribes beyond the river. His reports to Constantinople described, with a precision that his superiors found impressive and his successors found prophetic, the nature of the Gothic threat: not a raiding culture but a migrating one, driven by pressures from the east that would not be relieved by treaty or tribute, and that would eventually produce a collision with Roman power of a kind that Roman power might not survive. He was right in every particular, and retired from the frontier service before the catastrophe he had predicted arrived.

The account that follows is preserved in the records of Flavius Marcellinus, a notary attached to the Danubian command, who served as both clerk and observer in the proceedings that unfolded at Noviodunum. Marcellinus did not possess the same acuity of inference as Holkos-Theodoros, but he possessed diligence and an honest eye for detail, and his record reflects both the limits and the clarity of his position. He questioned what he did not understand, and in doing so preserved the questions that revealed the path of reasoning as much as the answers themselves.

The Matter of Alaric the Trader

Resolved at Noviodunum in the Year of Our Lord Three Hundred and Twenty-Five, in the Consulship of Paulinus and Iulianus

What follows is set down by my own hand, Flavius Marcellinus, notary attached to the headquarters of the Danubian command, in the garrison town of Noviodunum. I have written it not for the archive — which will record what it has been told to record — but for a single reader, to whom it will be sent under seal. I have changed no material fact. I have softened no conclusion. If what I describe reflects poorly on men of rank, that is the fault of those men, and not of my pen. I write it because I was there, and because Holkos-Theodoros has asked me to write it, and because I have learned, in the short time I have known him, that when he asks for something he has already calculated why it is necessary.

The Danube in autumn smells of iron and rotting reed. This is not a poetic observation. It is a practical one, which I offer because it explains why the body of Alaric the trader was not discovered until the third morning after his death, by which time the river had done its slow work on him, and the garrison soldiers who pulled him from the shallows near the eastern landing were required to stand well upwind to make their report.

The morning was the colour of old lead. A river mist had come in during the night and settled into every corner of Noviodunum — into the grain stores, into the colonnade of the principia, into the gap beneath my own door — so that the town felt submerged, as though the Danube had decided to take it back. The torches in the gateway burned with flat, orange halos that illuminated nothing. The sound of the water was everywhere and the sound of the frontier, which is the sound of horses and iron and men speaking languages that are not Latin and not quite Greek, was swallowed up entirely.

I had been in Noviodunum for eleven months. I had learned to read the river mist as a harbinger of difficult days, and so when the duty officer hammered at my door before the morning meal to tell me that a body had been found at the eastern landing, I was not surprised. I was only cold, and I pulled my cloak around me and followed him down through the fog to where the body lay on the planking in the grey light, and I began to write in my tablet what I saw.

Alaric — so he was identified by the freight master, who knew his face from a dozen crossings — was a Gothic trader of perhaps forty years, broad through the chest and shoulders, with the fair beard that marked him as a man of the northern tribes and the calloused hands that marked him as a man who worked. He had no visible wounds that the soldiers could find in that light and that cold. No blade had entered him. No cord had marked his neck. He lay in the posture of a man who had sat down to rest and found the rest permanent: slumped against the grain of the planking, one arm extended, as though pointing at something across the river.

Tribune Gallus, who commanded the garrison, arrived as I was completing my notes. He was a compact man with a soldier's efficiency of movement and a bureaucrat's love of obvious conclusions.

"Drunk," he said, looking at the body. "Fell in. The Gothic traders drink through the night when they make crossing. It is a custom of theirs."

I wrote this down.

"Or," said Gallus, as a second thought arrived to support the first, "there was a quarrel. The Gothic traders also quarrel. One of the other traders. Pushed him in. We will question them."

I wrote this down also.

It was at this point that I became aware of a third presence at the landing — a man who had arrived so quietly, and who stood so still in the mist, that I had taken him for one of the mooring posts. He was lean, perhaps fifty years of age, with close-cropped grey hair and eyes that even in that flat morning light appeared to be doing something more than merely looking. He wore the plain wool cloak of a frontier administrator, without ornament or insignia. He was studying the body with an expression I had not seen before on a man's face at the sight of a corpse: not horror, not sympathy, not the brisk professional distance of the soldier, but something that resembled nothing so much as the expression of a mathematician encountering an elegant problem.

"Holkos-Theodoros," said Gallus, without warmth. "I was not informed you were in Noviodunum."

"I am rarely announced," said the man with the grey eyes. He did not look at Gallus. He was still looking at the body. "Who identified him?"

"The freight master."

"By face, or by documentation?"

Gallus paused. "By face."

"Then we know who the river left us. We do not yet know who the freight master's documentation will confirm." He crouched beside the body with the ease of a man half his age and examined the hand — the extended arm, pointing across the water. "Your notary," he said, without turning. "What does he observe?"

I realised he was speaking to me. "A trader," I said. "Gothic. Dead, I would estimate, two to three days. No visible wound."

"Good. And the hand?"

I looked at the hand. It was extended, as I had noted, fingers partially curled. "It points toward the far bank," I said. "The Gothic shore."

"It does," said Holkos-Theodoros. "Note also that the palm faces upward. Note also the discolouration beneath the nails of the first two fingers — not river mud, which would be uniform, but something dried before his entry into the water. The river has softened it but not removed it. And note — " he stood, and turned, and looked not at the body but at the planking around it, " — that the body was placed here."

"Placed," repeated Gallus.

"The eastern landing is down-current from the garrison. A man who fell from the garrison wharf would have been carried past this point, not deposited at it. A man who fell from the Gothic shore would not reach this bank at all without assistance from the current that does not presently run in that direction. The body was not carried here by the river. It was left here by a person, or persons, who wished it to be found by the garrison, in a posture that would invite the conclusion that he fell or was pushed from the far bank." He looked at Gallus with an expression that was entirely without accusation and therefore somehow more pointed than accusation. "Your two theories are, I think, exactly what was intended."

Tribune Gallus looked at the body, then at Holkos-Theodoros, and I saw the particular expression of a competent professional man who has been told, in the mildest possible terms, that someone is considerably cleverer than he is.

"I will want to see his documentation," said Holkos-Theodoros. "His passage records, his freight manifests, his letters of introduction if he carried any. All of it, back to his first crossing."

"The freight master will have records going back perhaps a year," I said.

"Then we will begin with a year." He looked at me directly for the first time. I had the impression of being measured and filed away. "You are Marcellinus. You write quickly and you write what you see, not what you are told. I observed your tablet. Good. I will need a clerk."

It was not a question.

The freight master's records were kept in a low building beside the customs post, smelling of damp papyrus and the particular mustiness of official documentation that has been consulted rarely and sheltered inadequately. His name was Sosias, a thin Alexandrian Greek of bureaucratic temperament who had survived three garrison commanders by the simple method of keeping his records in perfect order and expressing no opinions about anything that occurred above his administrative station.

He provided what he had. I copied.

What emerged from Alaric's crossing records was this: he had been coming to Noviodunum for six years. He traded in dressed leather and occasionally in horses, both of which were items of genuine commercial value. His crossings averaged four times per year in the warm months. His freight manifests balanced — goods declared on entry, goods declared on departure, duties paid without dispute, no irregularities that Sosias had found reason to flag.

And yet.

Holkos-Theodoros sat with the records spread before him on the customs table and was quiet for a long interval. This, I came to understand, was not a pause for thought but a method of it — a deliberate stillness in which he processed what he was reading the way a millstone processes grain, silently and with great interior force. He read not quickly but completely. He read, I realised, not each record in isolation but all of them in relation to each other, looking for what a single document could not show.

"His first crossing," he said at last, "was in the consulship of Crispus. His last was eleven days ago. How many crossings total?"

I counted from my copies. "Twenty-three."

"In twenty-three crossings, how many times did he cross eastward — returning to the Gothic shore — within three days of his westward arrival?"

I counted again. This took longer. "Seven times."

"Seven times in six years he cut short a trading visit that should, by any commercial logic, have lasted at minimum a week — long enough to sell his leather, arrange his next purchase, rest his animals. The other sixteen crossings ran between eight days and three weeks. Standard commercial rhythm. But seven times, something called him back early." He turned a record toward me. "Look at the dates of those seven short returns. Do they suggest anything?"

I looked. I am a notary. I am trained to observe dates. "They are not evenly distributed," I said slowly. "They cluster. Three of the seven fall within a month of the major garrison rotations."

"Yes," said Holkos-Theodoros, and there was in his voice the particular quiet satisfaction of a man who has found that a student has answered correctly. "And of the remaining four?"

I studied them. Two fell in autumn. Two in spring. "The seasons of campaign," I said. "When Gothic movements would be most likely, and most worth reporting."

"When information about Gothic movements would be most valuable," he said. "To someone paying for it."

He stood and walked to the small window that looked out onto the grey river, and for a moment his stillness had a different quality to it — not the millstone-stillness of processing, but something older and more tired.

"Alaric was not a trader," he said. "Or rather, he was a trader in the way that a mask is a face. He traded in leather because leather provides a reason to cross, a reason to return, a reason to carry sealed document rolls that are declared as freight manifests and are not freight manifests. He was an asset. He was run from this command. He brought information about Gothic political organisation, troop dispositions, and the pressures building from the east — pressures which I have been reporting on for fifteen years and which I know to be real, and which certain officers of this command find it convenient to characterise as exaggerated, because the alternative is to admit that what is coming cannot be stopped by the means presently at their disposal."

He turned from the window.

"His reports were going to Constantinople. Someone in this command discovered this. Someone in this command needed them to stop."

The room was very quiet. Outside, the river moved past with its iron smell, carrying whatever it carried.

"Who was his handler?" I asked.

"That," said Holkos-Theodoros, "is what the documents under his nails will tell us, once we have identified what the substance is." He was already moving toward the door. "Come, Marcellinus. We have a body to examine more carefully, and I have sent for the garrison physician, who will not understand what he is looking for but who can be directed."

The garrison physician was a competent Antiochene named Paulos who had served enough frontier postings to have lost his squeamishness and retained his precision. He examined the body under lamplight in the garrison's small medical room, with Holkos-Theodoros at his shoulder asking questions that Paulos clearly found unusual.

"The substance," said Holkos-Theodoros. "Is it consistent with ink?"

Paulos examined the discolouration beneath the nails of the first two fingers of the right hand. "With a particular kind of ink," he said slowly. "I have seen something similar in the eastern trade — not Roman manufacture. Certain Gothic scribes use a preparation of oak-gall and metallic salts in different proportions than we do. It produces a darker residue and it sets more slowly." He looked up. "A man who had been writing with such an ink, writing quickly and at length, might have it under his nails if he had not washed carefully afterward."

"He was writing on the night of his death," said Holkos-Theodoros. "Writing in Gothic preparation. Writing, therefore, either in the Gothic script or in a cipher that used Gothic materials — which suggests either that his document was intended to be read on the Gothic shore, or that it was encrypted in a form that only someone familiar with Gothic scribal practice could decode." He paused. "Was there a document found with the body?"

"Nothing," said Paulos.

"No. There would not be. It was taken." He looked at the extended hand, still in its posture of pointing, and something shifted in his expression — a very slight tightening around the eyes, which I understood, watching him, was the closest he came to visible feeling. "He was composing his final report when they came for him. He had time to hide it. He did not have time to hide himself."

"Where?" I asked.

"If I were a man who knew he might not survive the night, and who needed his information to reach the right person, I would put it somewhere that the person who killed me would not think to look — because the person who killed me would know my habits and my resources, but would not know what I knew they did not know." He straightened. "We will want to examine his freight. Whatever he declared at customs on this final crossing."

The freight manifest for Alaric's last crossing listed sixteen dressed hides, three bolts of undyed wool, and a sealed ceramic jar of tallow declared for purposes of leather treatment. This last item had been impounded at customs upon the discovery of the body — standard procedure for goods whose owner had died before completion of his business — and sat in the customs store.

The tallow, when the jar was opened, was tallow. But the jar had a false bottom, fitted with such care that even Sosias, when shown it, admitted he would not have found it without being directed. Beneath the false bottom, wrapped in oiled skin and sealed with wax, was a roll of thin papyrus covered in writing.

Holkos-Theodoros broke the seal and read it without expression. He read it twice. Then he rolled it carefully and held it in both hands for a moment, as though weighing it.

"What does it say?" I asked.

"It says what I expected it to say," he said. "It names his handler — the officer through whom his reports passed before they reached Constantinople. It notes that in the last four dispatches, the handler had altered his reports before forwarding them — suppressing the most significant findings about Gothic military organisation, changing his estimates of the timeline of pressure from the east, softening his conclusions about the likelihood of large-scale westward migration." He looked at me steadily. "And it names who the handler reported to."

I waited.

"Tribune Gallus," he said, "is not the officer who altered the reports. He is not subtle enough. The name in this document is a name above Gallus. A name that appears in the official record as a vigorous defender of the frontier and a firm advocate of the position that the Gothic threat is manageable by conventional means — because if the Gothic threat is not manageable by conventional means, then the conventional means, and the men who command them, have failed."

He set the roll on the table.

"Alaric understood that his reports were being altered. He prepared this document as an insurance — a full account of his findings, uncorrupted, with evidence of the alterations. He intended it to reach Constantinople directly, bypassing his handler. Someone learned that he had prepared it, or that he intended to send it. And so he was killed, and the document he intended to send was taken from him — except that he had already hidden the original, which is what we are holding."

The lamp between us made a small sound, the wick shifting in its oil. Outside, the Danube moved past in the dark.

"What will you do with it?" I asked.

He was quiet for a long interval.

"The man who ordered his death is a senior officer of the Danubian command," he said at last. "If I present this document to the appropriate authorities, the appropriate authorities are, in significant part, subordinate to or dependent upon the man named in it. A formal accusation would be disputed, the evidence questioned, the document's provenance challenged. Alaric's handler — the intermediate officer — would likely be sacrificed as sufficient explanation. The man above him would survive. The reports to Constantinople would continue to be shaped by the same hands."

He stood and moved to the window. The fog had thinned enough to show the river's surface, black and moving.

"And the frontier?" I said.

"The frontier," he said, "will be swept away regardless of what this document reveals or conceals. I have spent fifteen years calculating what is coming. A prosecution that fails and produces no change in policy delays the catastrophe by weeks at most." He turned. "But there is one man in Constantinople who has read my reports — my actual reports, before they were altered — and who has used them to prepare what preparations can be made. Not many. But some. He needs to know that the information reaching him has been corrupted, and for how long, and by whom, so that he can calibrate what he was told against what was true."

"You will send him the document."

"I will send him this document, and I will send him my own account of this matter, and I will seal both under my own authority, and I will entrust them to a courier whose loyalties I know, because there are still one or two such couriers on this frontier." He picked up the roll. "The man who ordered Alaric's death will not be arrested. He will not be publicly accused. He will continue to serve, and to be promoted, and perhaps to believe that he has resolved his problem."

"And has he?"

"He has resolved it in the way that a man who blocks a river with his hand resolves the problem of the river," said Holkos-Theodoros. "Alaric is dead. The information Alaric carried is not. The man in Constantinople who needed it has it. What he does with it is beyond my calculation or my responsibility." He looked at me. "You have been recording this, Marcellinus."

"I have."

"Continue to do so. Everything I have described to you, every step of the reasoning, every finding, set it down completely and accurately. Not for the official archive — the official archive will record that Alaric the trader was found drowned and that the cause was consistent with accident or tribal dispute, and that the matter was resolved. That is what the archive will record because that is what I will tell Tribune Gallus, who will be relieved, and who will ensure that the record reflects his relief."

"Then for whom do I write it?"

He was already at the door.

"For the same reader who receives the document," he said. "He collects such accounts. He has been collecting them for twenty years. He says they are useful for understanding the shape of things — not the official shape, which is always the shape that power needs them to have, but the actual shape, which is usually considerably less convenient." He paused in the doorway, the lamp catching the grey of his eyes. "He is also, I should tell you, the only man I have met whose reasoning I respect without reservation. It is a short list. You will understand why when you meet him."

He left. I sat with my tablet and my lamp and the sound of the river, and I wrote.

Three days after the events described above, Holkos-Theodoros departed Noviodunum without formal announcement of his departure, which was consistent with his arrival. Tribune Gallus closed the matter of Alaric the trader as death by misadventure, possibly attendant upon intoxication, possibly involving persons unknown on the Gothic shore. This finding was transmitted to the relevant offices and produced no further inquiry.

The officer whose name appeared in Alaric's hidden document continued in his post for two more years, was elevated to a command position in the eastern Balkans, and died of a fever in the third year thereafter. Whether the man in Constantinople to whom Holkos-Theodoros dispatched his sealed account made any use of it, I do not know. I was not in a position to know.

What I know is this: the things Holkos-Theodoros described to me — the pressures from the east, the nature of Gothic movement, the inadequacy of conventional response — proved, in every particular, to be true. The frontier did not hold. I was not present when it broke. I had been transferred, by then, to administrative duties in the west.

I have set down this account because he asked me to, and because I believe he was right that such accounts should exist — not in the official archive, where the shape of things is always the shape that serves the present moment, but somewhere else, in some less official record, where the actual shape can be recovered by anyone who knows where to look and how to read.

He told me, on the morning before he left, that the reasoning was always simpler than it appeared, once the extraneous information had been set aside. "The mind," he said, "is like a well-kept storeroom. Everything in it must earn its place. What Alaric's nails told us, what the crossing records told us, what the posture of the body told us — these were not obscure. They were merely unread. The river had left its deposition plain enough for anyone who looked at it as a document rather than a misfortune."

I asked him if he had ever been wrong.

He considered this for longer than I expected.

"Once," he said. "I calculated everything correctly and arrived too late regardless. The calculation was sound. The variable I had not included was that the man I intended to warn had decided, before I reached him, that the warning was unnecessary." He paused. "I have not forgotten the lesson, but I confess I have not fully determined what the lesson was. Whether it is that human beings are incalculable, or simply that I had not yet learned to calculate them."

He did not explain further. He went to the river, and he took his sealed documents with him, and the fog came in again from the water, and after a little while I could not see him anymore.

I think about the body on the planking — the arm extended, pointing across the water to the Gothic shore, as though Alaric even in death was trying to send his information to where it needed to go. I think about what Holkos-Theodoros saw in that posture that Tribune Gallus did not. I think about the distance between looking and observing, which he spoke of as though it were a simple thing, though I have tried to practice it since and find it less simple than he made it appear.

The river, at any rate, still smells of iron and rotting reed. Some things, at least, can be relied upon.

Here ends the Account of Flavius Marcellinus, Notary of the Danubian Command, sealed in the consulship of Paulinus and Iulianus.
