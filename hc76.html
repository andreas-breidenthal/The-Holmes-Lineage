BYZANTOS HOLKOS

~260 AD — Byzantium / Constantinople

The name shifted back toward Greek in his generation — “Holkos” rather than “Holcus,” the Latin veneer thinning as the eastern empire recovered its Greek identity with increasing confidence. Byzantos Holkos was born in Byzantium and came into his career precisely as Constantine was transforming the city into Constantinople — the new Rome of the east, built with the speed and ambition that only an emperor’s will could command. He served in the imperial household administration during the great building programme of the 330s, managing the documentation of land acquisition, construction contracts, and the complex resettlement of populations that the creation of a new capital from an existing city required. He was a man of the transition — present when the city changed its name, its religion, and its destiny within the space of a single decade.

He worked as a notarios — a legal secretary and drafter of official documents — in the imperial chancery, a position that placed him at the centre of the documentary life of the eastern empire. His particular value was a facility for detecting the falsified document — the forged seal, the altered date, the interpolated clause — that made him the court’s first resort in cases of suspected fraud in legal instruments. It was a gift with very old roots, and he exercised it with the quiet, unhurried confidence of a man who had inherited it rather than acquired it.

The account that follows is drawn from the preserved notes of one Nikodemos, a site supervisor in the imperial building works, whose role as both observer and questioner placed him in proximity to Byzantos Holkos at the moment the matter unfolded. His record is fragmentary in places, but consistent in the parts that concern the inquiry itself, and it is from his notes, with minor corroboration from administrative copies, that the narrative is reconstructed.

The Matter of the Death of Phokas the Overseer

Reconstructed from His Notes and Certain Administrative Copies Preserved in the Chancery Archive

The body was found at the third hour, when the morning mist still lay heavy on the Golden Horn and the smell of fresh lime-mortar hung over the whole hill like a second fog. Byzantium had always smelled of salt and fish and the particular sweetness of rotting wood from the old wharves; Constantinople, as we were learning to call it, smelled of ambition — of cut stone and wet plaster and the burning pitch of torches worked through the night by men who could not afford to stop. It was into this changed air that Phokas fell, or was placed, or was carried — the question being precisely the one that required answering.

I found him myself, which is why the account falls to me to write. He was lying beside the south foundation wall of the new basilica, his head resting against a stone block as though he had sat down to watch the work and simply never risen. A man who did not know the building trade might have thought him sleeping. I knew better the moment I saw the angle of his neck.

Phokas had been overseer of the eastern contracts — the section of the great programme that managed the acquisition of private land along the Mese, the old central street that the Emperor’s architects were widening and re-colonnading. It was thankless work. The land had owners. The owners had documents. The documents, when examined carefully, had a troubling habit of saying whatever was most convenient for whoever held them.

He had a leather satchel still looped over one arm. Inside it was a contract of sale — a substantial parcel of land near the Forum of Constantine, dated some three years past, carrying the seal of the urban prefecture. The seal was perfect. The parchment was old. The ink had the proper brownish fade of documents left in a dry archive. Everything about it announced authenticity, and it was this that made me uneasy, because Phokas had told me two days before that he had found a document he could not quite trust.

I sent for Byzantos Holkos that same morning, before the site foremen had assembled the work gangs. This required some negotiation, because Holkos served in the imperial chancery and his time was not freely distributed, but I had dealt with him twice before in matters of questionable land titles, and I knew that he would come quickly if the word seal appeared in my message. It did, and he came.

He arrived at the sixth hour, walking through the site with the particular gait he always had on such occasions — not the abstracted drift of a man taking in scenery, but a kind of focused stillness, as though he were listening to something the rest of us could not hear. He was perhaps forty-five years of age at this time, lean in the way of men who forget to eat when their minds are occupied, with the deep-set dark eyes common to his family and a habit of wearing his notarial robes loosely, as though he had dressed in some haste and then immediately forgotten he had dressed at all.

He looked at Phokas for a long time without speaking. Then he crouched beside the body, examined the neck, the hands, the sandals, and finally the satchel, all without touching anything he had not first indicated he intended to touch.

“The satchel was on his arm when he was found?” he said.

“As I see it now.”

“Then whoever killed him either did not know what was in it or did not care. Which is interesting.” He rose. “May I take the document?”

I gave it to him. He held it close to his face, which was not, as I had initially supposed, a sign of poor eyesight, but simply the way he worked — bringing his whole attention to bear on a small area, excluding everything peripheral. He tilted it toward the morning light. He pressed the seal gently with one thumb. He turned the parchment over and examined its reverse. He held it briefly to his nose.

Then he said: “Nikodemos. When did the urban prefecture change its seal die?”

I did not know. This was not my particular area of knowledge.

“Four years ago,” he said. “A new emperor requires new instruments of administrative identity, as a rule. This seal is an impression of the current die. The document is dated three years past.” He paused. “You see the difficulty.”

I did. A document made with a tool that did not yet exist when it was supposedly made.

“But—” I said.

“Yes. The parchment is genuinely old. The ink is correctly aged. The hand is consistent throughout, with no signs of interpolation. Someone made this document very carefully, Nikodemos. Not carelessly, not in haste, not for crude profit. They made it well. And they made it with access to a copy of the current prefectural die that I would very much like to locate.” He folded the document with what I was coming to recognise as a kind of professional reverence — the care one takes with fine work even when one is about to prove it fraudulent. “Was Phokas investigating this particular contract?”

“He told me he had doubts about it. He had not yet brought it formally to the chancery.”

“He told you. He told no one else?”

“I believe not.”

Holkos was quiet for a moment. The construction noise surrounded us — the rhythmic crack of stone-dressing, the shouts of gang-masters, the low grinding of the cranes — and he stood in it with his eyes half-closed, as though the sound helped him think rather than hindering him.

“He told you,” he repeated, “and the next morning he is dead. And the document is still on his person.” He opened his eyes. “We will need to find who submitted this contract for registration, and we will need to do it quietly, before the owner of the land in question learns that his document has come to my attention.”

There was, in the matter of Holkos’s working methods, a quality I found difficult to name for some time, and which I eventually settled on calling directed patience. He did not rush. He did not, in the early stages of an inquiry, share his suspicions. He asked questions with the neutrality of a man who has no particular investment in any answer, which meant that people answered him fully and without caution, because they did not feel they were being guided toward anything.

He spent the remainder of that day in the administrative offices of the land acquisition section, reading contracts. He was not searching, as far as I could tell. He was reading. He read seven contracts in the course of the afternoon, making occasional small marks in the wax of his personal tablet. When he was finished, he sat for a while with the tablet face-down on the table, looking at nothing.

I waited. I had learned, in our previous dealings, that this was what was required of me.

“There are four,” he said at last. “Four contracts in this section bearing what appears to be a copy of the current seal. All four concern land parcels previously occupied by families who were displaced in the initial resettlement — the Greeks who had been here when the city was still Byzantium, who had property rights under the old regime that were neither confirmed nor formally extinguished when the building programme began. The official land claims against them were registered by a single landowner.” He consulted his tablet. “A man named Kalliphon. A substantial property owner who had been acquiring land aggressively since the Emperor announced the expansion, on the argument that the displaced families had no valid title.” He paused. “The forged contracts, each of which predates the alleged acquisition by several years, record prior sale to the displaced families themselves — at prices suggesting genuine transactions. They could not have been evicted under them. They were protected by them.”

I worked through this slowly. “Someone was forging documents to protect the families?”

“Yes. Using a copy of the prefectural die to manufacture papers that looked more legitimate than the legitimate claims against them.” Holkos looked at his hands. “It is, considered purely as a technical matter, extremely accomplished work. The parchment was sourced correctly. The ink was properly compounded. The die was copied from the actual current instrument — which means either the forger had access to the chancery itself, or to someone who did.” He was quiet for a moment. “And Phokas found one of these contracts in the course of his acquisition work. And now Phokas is dead.”

“Then Kalliphon—”

“Kalliphon discovered someone was undermining his claims. He could not identify who. He identified what — a forged contract protecting a family whose land he intended to take. He could not bring the forgery formally to the chancery without drawing attention to all four contracts and to his own acquisitions under the legitimate claims that the forgeries were designed to defeat. He is not a stupid man.” Holkos rose. “He dealt with the problem the way men deal with problems when they cannot use the law. He found the forger and he killed him. The document remained on the body because removing it would have drawn attention to the fact that someone knew of it.”

“The forger,” I said. “You know who it was.”

He did not answer immediately. He walked to the window of the administrative office, which looked out over a section of the new construction — scaffolding, blocks, the organised chaos of an empire building itself. The late afternoon light made the limestone almost golden, and the smell of the kilns drifted up from the lower ground.

“I have a strong suspicion,” he said. “I would like to confirm it before I say the name aloud.”

The confirmation required two more days, during which Holkos moved through the chancery with an air of such complete normality that I began to wonder whether his suspicion had simply dissolved. He processed his usual documents. He attended the morning briefing of the notarial staff. He ate a meal of bread and oil and dried fish that one of the junior scribes brought him and which he consumed with the mechanical inattention of a man who regards eating as an administrative task.

On the evening of the second day, he came to find me at the site and said: “Walk with me.”

We walked along the old Byzantium street that would soon become the extended Mese, stepping around piles of dressed stone and coils of rope. The torches were being lit along the scaffolding for the night work, and the smell of burning pitch mixed with the salt air from the Horn.

“The die,” Holkos said. “A copy of a seal die is not made in an afternoon. It requires skill, time, and access to the original — ideally, more than one impression of it, to account for any variation in the stamping. There are perhaps four men in Constantinople who could produce a copy of sufficient quality to deceive a standard examination, and only one of them had any connection to the families along the Mese.”

“Who?”

“A man named Eudoxios. He is a copyist and document restorer — he works under contract to the chancery, restoring damaged legal instruments. He had legitimate access to prefectural seal impressions as part of his restoration work. He lived for twenty years in one of the quarters now being absorbed by the building programme. His sister’s family was among the displaced.” Holkos paused. “I found, in the chancery restoration records, four instruments assigned to Eudoxios for work in the year before the contracts in question were dated. The instruments happened to carry prefectural seals. The restoration notes are thorough — Eudoxios was a meticulous man. Too meticulous, perhaps. His records of the restoration work are more detailed than any comparable entry in the same ledgers.”

“Detailed enough to explain the time he spent with the originals.”

“Yes. He had an alibi for the making of the copies, built into the official record before the copies were ever needed. A careful man.” Holkos stopped walking. We were standing near the south foundation wall, near the place where Phokas had been found. The torchlight threw long shadows across the fresh stonework. “He is dead, Nikodemos. Eudoxios. I confirmed it this morning — he died four nights ago, the night before Phokas, of an apparent fall from the steps of his house. He lived alone. No witnesses.”

I stood with this for a moment.

“Kalliphon found him first.”

“Yes. He found the forger, established how the forgeries had been made, killed him, and then went looking for the documents themselves — which is when he encountered Phokas, who happened to be carrying one. He killed Phokas and left the document, perhaps calculating that a single suspicious contract, unconnected to the restoration records and with its maker dead, would lead nowhere. He may have been correct, had Phokas not told me about it.”

“Told me,” I said.

“Yes. You. And you sent for me.” He was quiet for a moment. “Kalliphon is a man of property and connection. The urban prefect has received at least two letters of commendation on his behalf from senior members of the court in the past year. He will not be touched by a direct accusation without evidence that would withstand examination by men who wish not to find it convincing.”

I heard something in his voice that I had not heard before. Not quite hesitation. Something more like deliberation.

“Then what do we do?” I said.

What he did, in the end, was not what I had expected.

He went to the families.

There were four households — eleven people in total, in the cramped lodgings near the old harbour where the displaced had been settled while their status was argued. He visited each of them over the course of a morning, arriving without announcement and speaking quietly. I went with him. He explained, with the same neutral precision he brought to everything, that the documents protecting them were forgeries, that the man who had made them was dead, and that the documents would not survive formal scrutiny if Kalliphon chose to challenge them in the courts.

Then he told them what to do.

The method was not dramatic. It required only that the families apply for the confirmation of their title through a specific administrative channel — one that Holkos knew to be managed by a notarial superior of impeccable integrity and no particular friendship with Kalliphon. The applications, supported by witness testimony to long-standing occupation and two legitimate instruments that Holkos identified from the actual archive, were sufficient to grant provisional title pending full imperial review. This review, he estimated, would take between three and seven years, during which the families could not be displaced.

By the time Kalliphon’s lawyers moved to challenge the confirmations, the matter had risen, through a sequence of bureaucratic escalations that Holkos had quietly anticipated, to a level of the administration at which Kalliphon’s influence was considerably less effective.

I asked him, on the evening after the last family visit, about Kalliphon himself. About what would happen to him. About Phokas, and Eudoxios, and the justice they had not received.

Holkos was in one of his quiet periods — the long, grey intervals between cases when he sat in his office in the chancery with old documents he had already read and a clay cup of dark wine he rarely drank, and looked at the wall. He had a particular quality of stillness in these times, like a lamp turned low. I had been told by colleagues who had known him longer that it sometimes lasted for days, and that it was better not to disturb it. But I asked.

He was quiet for a while. Then he said: “Kalliphon will face a land dispute that will consume his legal resources for the better part of a decade. He has, in the course of his acquisitions, made enemies of men more strategically positioned than I am, and those men will use the disruption to the title chain — which I have ensured is thoroughly documented — to advance their own claims. He will not be comfortable.”

“But he will not be tried for Phokas.”

“No.”

“Or for Eudoxios.”

“No.”

I said nothing. He looked at the wall for a moment longer, then at the cup of wine, which he turned in his hands without drinking.

“Eudoxios was a forger,” he said. “What he did was illegal, and the legal system would have punished him for it while acquitting the man who ordered his death, because Kalliphon’s lawyers are better than his. Phokas was a careful professional who noticed something he should perhaps have brought to me more quickly, and who died because he had not quite decided what he had found. The law, as it currently operates in this city, is not equipped to address either of these men’s deaths in a way that would result in justice rather than paperwork.”

He set down the cup.

“The families are in their houses,” he said. “Eudoxios’s work stands — not as law, but as fact on the ground, which is more durable than law in a city being rebuilt from the foundation. Kalliphon will spend the next ten years fighting a bureaucratic war he did not choose on ground I have selected. This is what I can do. It is not what the law can do, and it is not what a court would do, but it is what will actually occur.” He looked at me for a moment with those deep-set eyes that always seemed to be measuring something several layers beneath the surface of whatever conversation was nominally in progress. “You are disturbed by this.”

“I think I am,” I said. “Yes.”

“That is correct,” he said. “You should be. Write it down exactly as you are disturbed by it. The discomfort is the honest part of the account.”

He was right, as he generally was. I have written it down.

The official records of the deaths of Phokas the overseer and Eudoxios the copyist list them as accidental. The land titles of four families along the old Mese are on record in the chancery archive as confirmed pending imperial review, supported by legitimate instrument and witness testimony. The name Byzantos Holkos does not appear in any of these documents.

He would have considered this correct.

I record it here because someone should, and because the discomfort, as he said, is the honest part.

— Nikodemos, site supervisor, in the service of the building programme of Constantinople, in the consulship of Optatus and Paulinus
