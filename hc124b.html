A note on this entry and the following:
Having followed the generations from the temple of Nanna at Ur — through Egypt and Canaan, Greece and Rome, through Frankish and Norse lands, the Danelaw and the United Kingdom — we arrive now at a generation for which the record is inexhaustible.

In every preceding entry, we have been able to source records enough to demonstrate the gift that has been evident throughout the generations. This entry, however, and the one following are different. For the generation at the apex of the line, the record has already been made complete by the man who understood that what he was witnessing was worth preserving: Dr. John Hamish Watson, late of the Army Medical Corps, of 221b Baker Street, and of all the places to which his friend's extraordinary temperament eventually led him.

Dr. Watson was not a simple man. He had been to war, had been wounded, had lost a wife and survived the loss, had watched his closest friend apparently die and had mourned him genuinely and then had watched him return. A man of medicine and of letters, of loyalty and of judgment : the human sympathy that could recognise what the pure analytical faculty could not, the steady decency that kept the work grounded in something larger than the problem to be solved.

We are sure that even the Great Detective himself would agree to the propriety of including the following summary of the chronicles of Dr. Watson before proceeding with the Holmes family.


DR. JOHN H. WATSON

1852 — London, England

ON THE CHRONICLES OF DR. JOHN H. WATSON

Being a survey of the sixty accounts preserved by Dr. Watson, with observations on what those accounts reveal, to the reader of this chronicle, about the inheritance they embody

Ⅰ. The Chronicler

John Hamish Watson was born in 1852, took his degree of Doctor of Medicine from the University of London in 1878, and was attached, as assistant surgeon, to the Fifth Northumberland Fusiliers when that regiment was ordered to India. He was wounded at the Battle of Maiwand in July 1880, returned to England in a constitution shaken by wound and enteric fever, and was introduced to Sherlock Holmes in the chemical laboratory of St Bartholomew's Hospital in the early months of 1881 by a mutual acquaintance named Stamford who could not, on reflection, have had the least understanding of what he was arranging.

Dr. Watson's qualifications for the role he was about to assume were not obvious. He was not analytical in the fashion of his companion. He did not read a stranger's history from their cuffs and their bootlaces. He could not identify, at a glance, the soil of forty-seven different London parishes, or distinguish between a hundred and forty varieties of tobacco ash, or tell a Stradivarius from its neck alone. These were not his gifts.

His gifts were different, and they were essential. He was observant in the ordinary human sense — he noticed what people looked like, how they moved, what they said and what they chose not to say. He was loyal without servility, which is the rarest kind of loyalty. He was brave in the uncomplicated, physical manner of a man who has been under fire and has established, empirically, that he does not panic. He was honest — not in the ostentatious way of a man who makes a virtue of his honesty, but in the quiet way of a man who finds dishonesty effortful and has long since decided that the effort is not worth making. And he could write: which was the gift that, in the end, mattered most to us. Because across four thousand years, we have depended on the scribes, and Dr. Watson was, without doubt, the greatest of them all.

He began writing up the cases, as he later explained, partly because he found them extraordinary and could not resist, and partly because Holmes, who was indifferent to his own celebrity and rather contemptuous of the official police's appetite for it, appeared unlikely to document them himself. The first account — A Study in Scarlet, published in 1887 — was Dr. Watson's experiment in a form he had not previously attempted; the uncertainty of a first effort is visible in it, and is part of its charm. By the time he came to write The Final Problem in 1893, he was one of the most accomplished narrative writers of his generation, and he knew it, though he would not have said so.

He wrote sixty accounts in all: four novel-length books and fifty-six short accounts, published across four decades, spanning cases from 1881 to 1914. He continued to write after Holmes's retirement, publishing the last collection in 1927 when both men were in their seventies, long past the period the accounts describe. He chose, in the final years, what to preserve and what to withhold; and the student of these chronicles who attends closely to what Dr. Watson chose not to write will find there a record as illuminating, in its silences, as the record he chose to make.

What Dr. Watson could not know — what this chronicle, reading him from a distance of more than a century, is obliged to know on his behalf — is that he was not merely documenting the career of one remarkable man. He was documenting the apex of a line. Every quality he recorded in Holmes — the methodical observation, the precise inference, the discreet dismantling of organised criminal structures, the compulsion to serve that coexisted with the constitutional inability to serve in any conventional capacity — had been exercised, in forms appropriate to each era, by every member of the line from the first scribe of Ur. Dr. Watson saw Holmes for who he was, but he could not have seen what stood behind Holmes. We, who can, regards Dr. Watson's achievement with an admiration that is, if anything, increased by the understanding of how much more he had witnessed than he could have known.

Ⅱ. The Novels

Dr. Watson's four extended accounts — the novels, as they came to be called — are the fullest canvases on which the gift was displayed, and each illuminates a different facet of the inheritance.

A Study in Scarlet (1887) is the origin account, and as such it serves our purposes in a particular way: it shows the gift in the moment of its first public recognition. Holmes, encountering Dr. Watson at Barts, reads him in thirty seconds: soldier, Afghanistan, wounded — the whole history assembled from the man's tan, his bearing, his arm. For the reader of this chronicle, that moment is not merely a debut; it is the clearest possible demonstration that what the line had carried, quietly and often obscurely, for forty centuries, had arrived in this generation at a new pitch of refinement. The case itself — the murders in Lauriston Gardens, the cipher, the revenge from Utah — establishes the method: observation, inference, the refusal to theorise ahead of data, the willingness to remain in the uncomfortable position of not yet knowing. Lestrade and Gregson receive the credit. Holmes, who arranged this, does not trouble to explain why to Dr. Watson, who has not yet been told.

The Sign of the Four (1890) deepens the picture in a direction Dr. Watson did not entirely intend to go. It introduced Mary Morstan, who became Dr. Watson's first wife, and in this respect it is Dr. Watson's most personally consequential account. But it is also, for us, the account in which the gift operates most purely in its characteristic mode: the reconstruction of a hidden history — the Agra Treasure, the pact between Sholto and Morstan, the loyalty and treachery compacted in Tonga's blow dart — from a chain of physical evidence that begins with a pearl and ends in a boat chase on the Thames. The case is, at its root, about what inheritance means: the treasure that was never properly anyone's, the loyalty that persisted beyond any rational claim, the old crime that emerged into the present because crimes of that quality always do. For Dr. Watson, who was falling in love, it was also a lesson in what Holmes could not do — could not feel, or would not, or had decided not to, for reasons he kept to himself and Dr. Watson, loyally, did not press.

The Hound of the Baskervilles (1902) is Dr. Watson's masterwork, and we acknowledge it as such without reservation. It is the account that demonstrated, definitively, that Dr. Watson could sustain a narrative of novel length without his subject present for the central portion of it — could hold the reader's attention with his own observations, his own judgments, his own growing unease on Dartmoor — and that the partnership was not, as it might superficially have appeared, one of a brilliant man and his useful assistant. It was a partnership of two different kinds of intelligence that were, in combination, greater than either was alone. Holmes, absent from the moor, was thinking; Dr. Watson, present on it, was seeing. The solution required both. The criminal — Stapleton, the vanished Baskerville, the patient planner who had laid his trap and waited — is one of the chronicle's more illuminating villains, because his patience and his organisation and his layers of concealment are, in miniature, the structural characteristics that the great network had always displayed. He was not connected to that network; he was simply shaped by the same conditions that had always produced such men. Holmes read him from his distance, as the scribe of Ur read the grain tally: by attending to what was missing as much as what was present.

The Valley of Fear (1915) is, of the four, the most explicit about the great network and what it represented. Published after The Final Problem and The Empty House had established the outline of the Moriarty story, it reaches back to an earlier encounter and places the Professor in his proper context: not as a singular villain but as the apex of a structure, the Napoleon of a criminal empire that would survive him because empires of that kind always survive their Napoleons. The case moves between an English country house and the coalfields of Pennsylvania, between an English murder and an American labour conspiracy, and in doing so performs, perhaps more clearly than any other of the accounts, the characteristic operation of the line: showing that what appears to be a local problem is the local expression of a system, and that the system must be read before the problem can be resolved. The Scowrers, the secret society that controlled the Valley with terror and blood — these were not unique; they were a provincial chapter of something much older. Holmes knew this. He told Dr. Watson rather less than he knew, which was his consistent habit, and which Dr. Watson recorded faithfully without always understanding it.

Ⅲ. The Short Accounts: A Survey by Theme

Dr. Watson's fifty-six shorter accounts resist simple summary, and we will not attempt it; each deserves its own reading, and the reader who has come this far will not be deterred from that reading by any précis offered here. What can usefully be done is to trace through the collection the threads that concern this chronicle most directly: the gift in operation; the network in shadow; the question of credit; and the recurring presence, in Dr. Watson's writing, of something he could not quite name but consistently recognised — the thing that made Holmes not merely clever but necessary.

The Gift in Operation
The method Dr. Watson documented, case by case, was always the same in its fundamentals: look first, conclude last; attend to what is missing as much as what is present; read the particular and infer the general; never form a theory in advance of the data because the mind bends its facts to suit its theories, and a bent fact is worse than no fact at all. What varied was the material to which the method was applied — the speckled band and the five orange pips, the dancing men and the crooked man, the blue carbuncle and the golden pince-nez — and the infinite variety of that material is what gives the collection its inexhaustible quality. Holmes did not repeat himself because the world did not repeat itself; but the method was always there, the same tool applied to the new problem, wearing down toward its conclusion with the patience that the line had always brought to its work.

Dr. Watson was not always certain what he was watching. He noted the conclusions; he recorded the steps; he occasionally failed to follow the reasoning, as he honestly admitted, and was obliged to wait for Holmes to explain it. But he was attentive enough, and honest enough, to record it accurately even when he did not fully understand it. The result is that the accounts constitute, in aggregate, the most thorough documentary record of the analytical method in action that exists in any language — and the reader who approaches them looking not for entertainment, which they also amply provide, but for the operational logic of the gift, will find it there, case by case, as fully displayed as the gift was in any generation of the line.

We would like to draw particular attention to The Adventure of Silver Blaze, in which Holmes solved a case from the fact that a dog did not bark in the night — not from what was present but from what was conspicuously absent. This operation — the reading of the absence, the inference from the silence — is the signature of the gift at its most refined, and it appears in the chronicles of the line wherever the records are rich enough to show it: in the scribe of Ur who read the gap in the grain tally; in the Byzantine notary who read the clause that was not in the contract; in the Elizabethan intelligencer who read the blank space where a seal should have been. Holmes, reading the silent dog at King's Pyland, was exercising a faculty that had been exercised, in analogous forms, across forty centuries. Dr. Watson recorded it as a brilliant deduction. We read it as a homecoming.

The Network in Shadow
The great network — the organisation that this chronicle has tracked from the temple frauds of Mesopotamia through the Fellowship of the Broken Seal and its various incarnations — appears in Dr. Watson's accounts in the person of Professor James Moriarty, and in the person of Colonel Sebastian Moran who survived him, and in the background of perhaps a dozen other cases where the structure, if not the name, is recognisable to the reader who has read what preceded this book.

Moriarty himself appears in very few of the accounts directly, which is precisely correct: a directing intelligence at that level of an organisation does not appear at the surface. He appears in The Final Problem, in The Valley of Fear, and in Holmes's references to him elsewhere — and in each appearance he is defined not by what he does himself but by what he causes others to do. He is the spider at the centre of the web, to use Holmes's own description; and Holmes's private papers, of which Dr. Watson had partial knowledge, suggested that the web had been accumulating since before either of them was born. Holmes knew, by 1891, that he was not dealing with a man but with a structure; that killing Moriarty would not kill the structure; and that the structure, reorganised under new management, would be his successors' problem as it had been his predecessors'. He went over the Reichenbach Falls knowing this, which is perhaps why Dr. Watson, who did not know it, found the loss so much more absolute than Holmes had intended him to.

The network surfaces in other accounts in forms Dr. Watson identified accurately but could not fully contextualise. The cases of The Red Circle, The Illustrious Client, and The Adventure of the Three Garridebs all involve, at their margins, the kind of organised criminal infrastructure — international, hierarchical, insulated from prosecution by layers of respectability — that defines the network's operations in each era. Dr. Watson noted the connections where they were visible and did not speculate beyond them. Holmes, in each case, removed the local manifestation and allowed the official police their credit. The deeper structure, as always, was addressed in private.

The Question of Credit
In every generation of this line, the credit for the resolution of the cases — the arrests, the prosecutions, the public recognition — has been given to the constituted authorities. This was not modesty, though it was sometimes expressed in the language of modesty; it was operational wisdom. The gift worked best from the shadows, and the work itself — the actual thinking, the actual reading of the evidence — required no audience. The member of the line who sought credit for their work would, by definition, be distorted by the seeking, because the seeking would shape the work toward the demonstrable and the public rather than the true and the necessary.

Holmes's indifference to official recognition is one of Dr. Watson's most consistently noted characteristics of his companion, and it has been read as a personal eccentricity — a component of the general difficult temperament, the irregular hours and the tobacco and the violin at three in the morning. It was not an eccentricity. It was the inheritance. Lestrade and Gregson and Hopkins and MacDonald and all the others received the credit; Holmes received, at most, a private acknowledgement that the police valued more than they publicly admitted; and Dr. Watson received the satisfaction of having been present at something extraordinary, which he then gave to the reading public, who received it with the enthusiasm it deserved. The arrangement suited everyone, and it was, the chronicle notes, the same arrangement that had governed the line's operations in every preceding generation where the record is rich enough to confirm it.

Dr. Watson wrote about this arrangement with a dry affection that is one of his most appealing literary qualities. He did not moralize about it; he did not explain it as self-abnegation or as principled refusal of celebrity. He simply noted, repeatedly, that this was how Holmes operated, and allowed the reader to draw their own conclusions. The reader of this chronicle, equipped with four thousand years of context, will draw rather more specific conclusions than Dr. Watson intended — but they are conclusions that Dr. Watson's own scrupulous fidelity to the facts has made possible.

The Thing Dr. Watson Could Not Name
There is a quality in Dr. Watson's accounts, most visible in the moments when he steps back from the immediate narrative and reflects on his companion at some distance, that suggests he understood something about Holmes that he could not quite articulate. He described it variously as genius, as the analytical faculty, as a power that was beyond his own capacity to follow. He acknowledged it as the reason he stayed, case after case, year after year, despite the inconvenience and the danger and the irregular hours and the violin. He acknowledged it, in The Final Problem, as the reason his grief at the Reichenbach Falls had the quality of a bereavement so total that he could not immediately continue with the accounts at all.

What Dr. Watson could not name was the weight of the inheritance. He knew he was in the presence of something extraordinary; he did not know that the extraordinary thing had been building toward this generation for four thousand years, that it had been carried by obscure scribes and battlefield surgeons and Venetian merchants and Northumbrian monks and Elizabethan intelligencers and all the rest, each adding what they could and passing what they had to the next. He knew only the man in front of him — the long figure in the mouse-coloured dressing gown, the hawk-face in the Baker Street gaslight, the voice saying that when you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth. He knew that man completely, in the way that a man can know another man after twenty years of close companionship in circumstances that test the limits of both. And that knowledge, rendered in his clear, warm, deceptively simple prose, has given the rest of the world access to something it would not otherwise have had.

We are eternally grateful for it.

Ⅳ. The Moriarty Thread

Throughout all the preceding entries, we have traced the persistence of a criminal organisation that has been known by many names and has never been entirely destroyed. In this generation it found its most explicit adversary and its most articulate description. Holmes, in the letter he wrote in 1895 for his own reference — a copy of which reached his son, and through his son the subsequent generations — described the organisation with the precision of a man who had spent three years of his professional life studying it: hierarchical, durable, insulated from prosecution by layers of apparent respectability, operating through legitimate commercial fronts, directing criminal activity at a remove from any individual who might be prosecuted for it. He had identified, he wrote, the structure. He had not destroyed it, because structures of this kind could not be destroyed by removing their personnel, however senior. He had disrupted it, significantly, at the level of its current direction. He expected it to recover.

He was correct. The record of subsequent generations confirms it. But Holmes's encounter with the organisation — the three years of close study, the confrontation at Reichenbach, the return and the destruction of Moran's network in The Adventure of the Empty House, the unwritten years of the Great Hiatus during which he gathered material across three continents — constitutes the most thorough engagement with the organisation's structure that any single member of the line had yet achieved. He did not win. The chronicles of no generation record a final victory. But he understood it as no one before him had, and the understanding he transmitted, in that 1895 letter, gave his successors tools they would need.

Dr. Watson knew about Moriarty. He wrote about him with the controlled horror of a man who had been given, by degrees, access to a truth he found genuinely frightening: that the pleasant, tolerably ordered world of late Victorian England was underlain by a criminal intelligence of the first order who had made himself, as Holmes said, the organiser of half that is evil and of nearly all that is undetected in it. Dr. Watson did not know that this criminal intelligence was the latest iteration of something very much older. He simply knew that his friend had spent years studying it, had risked his life to confront it, and had gone over a waterfall in Switzerland in the course of doing so. That was enough to be going on with.

The Final Problem is Dr. Watson's most personal account, and we must read it as such. The grief in it is real. The sense of an ending — of a light gone out of the world — is not a literary effect but an accurate record of what Dr. Watson felt, in his lodgings in Kensington, when he accepted that his friend was dead. He did not know that Holmes had calculated the Hiatus as a necessary strategic withdrawal rather than a final defeat. He did not know that Holmes was in Tibet, reading his Lhasa monastery records for whatever trace of the network's eastern operations might have passed through those mountains. He knew only that the man who had defined the last decade of his life was gone, and he wrote about it with the simplicity that real grief produces in good writers.

The Adventure of the Empty House — the return — is correspondingly joyful in a fashion Dr. Watson does not quite allow himself, as if he is embarrassed by the magnitude of his own relief. Holmes, characteristically, expected to resume where he had left off. Dr. Watson, characteristically, accommodated him. The next seven years produced thirty-three more accounts. We must note, which Dr. Watson does not, that this second period of active work coincided with the decade in which the organisation's recovery from the Reichenbach disruption made it most active and most dangerous. Holmes's return was not sentimental. It was operational. He came back because the work was not finished, and he continued until it was, or as near to finished as it was ever going to get.

Ⅴ. What Dr. Watson Knew and What He Did Not

Dr. Watson, across sixty accounts and a friendship of thirty years, came to know Holmes with a completeness that was the product of sustained, loyal, clear-eyed attention over time. He knew his methods; he knew his habits; he knew his limitations as well as his gifts, and he recorded the limitations with the same fidelity he gave the gifts, which is one of the reasons the accounts are trustworthy in a way that mere hagiography never is. He knew about the cocaine and the moods, the periods of black inertia between cases when the gift had nothing to exercise itself on and turned inward with uncomfortable results. He knew about the violin, the chemistry, the revolver shots into the sitting-room wall, the gasogene and the tobacco in the Persian slipper and the correspondence impaled on the mantelpiece by a jackknife. He knew Holmes's contempt for sentiment and his private capacity for it, which was greater than the contempt suggested.

What he did not know — could not have known, in the absence of this chronicle — was the full context of what he was witnessing. He did not know that the quality he observed, case by case, year by year, had been exercised by forty or more preceding generations in every civilisation that had shaped the world Holmes moved through. He did not know that the organisation his friend had dedicated his professional life to studying was the latest form of a structure that had been operating, under varying names and in varying instruments, since the temple scribes of Mesopotamia first learned that the gap between what was recorded and what was counted could be made to serve a private interest. He did not know that the 1895 letter — which Holmes wrote for himself, without telling Dr. Watson — would be read by a son Dr. Watson did not know Holmes had, and by that son's sons and daughters after him, and that the chain of understanding transmitted in that letter would continue to equip the line's members against the network's continuing operations for as long as both persisted.

He knew the man. He gave the man to the world. We, who knows the rest, are grateful for the gift, and will not presume to improve upon it.

Ⅵ. The Last Cases and the Sussex Retirement

Holmes retired to the Sussex Downs in 1903. He kept bees, as he had intended; he wrote his Practical Handbook of Bee Culture, with Some Observations upon the Segregation of the Queen, which was, by the account of those who knew the subject, a serious contribution to apiculture; and he pursued, in the downland air with the Channel visible from his garden on clear days, the reflective life that his temperament had never previously permitted. He was forty-nine years old. He had been working, in one form or another, since his early twenties. Dr. Watson visited. The Sussex cottage was the nearest either of them came, in their professional lives, to rest.

His Last Bow, published in 1917 but describing events of August 1914, shows Holmes emerging from that retirement at the request of his country — playing the American agent Altamont for two years, penetrating a German intelligence network, delivering its chief to the authorities on the eve of the war. Dr. Watson was present. The closing scene of that account — Holmes on the terrace, Dr. Watson beside him, the great German war about to begin, Holmes saying that there is an east wind coming such as never blew on England yet — is Dr. Watson at his literary finest, and it is the last piece of Dr. Watson's writing in which both men appear together in active collaboration.

The retirement proper resumed after the war, and the post-1918 accounts in The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes, the last collection, were written differently from the earlier ones. Some were narrated by Holmes himself; some by a third party; and Dr. Watson, in his editorial comments, is a man in his sixties writing about things that happened to both of them when they were younger, with the particular quality of retrospective regard that good memory and great affection produce. He published the last of the accounts in 1927. By then the events described were thirty, forty years past; the Baker Street rooms were long since occupied by others; and the world that had provided the cases — hansom cabs and gas-lit streets and the great colonial empire at its uneasy zenith — was gone as thoroughly as the Rome of the Twelve Caesars or the Athens of Pericles.

Holmes outlived all of it. He died, as the biographical record at the head of this book notes, at an age exceeding one hundred years, on the Sussex Downs he had chosen for his retreat. Dr. Watson had predeceased him. The last word on Holmes's lips was a woman's name. Some things belong to the man rather than the line.

Ⅶ. A Final Note on Our Debt to Dr. Watson

The reader who has come to this point through all the preceding entries of this chronicle — who has met the scribe of Ur in his temple, the Athenian physician in the agora, the Roman lawyer on the Palatine, the Byzantine notary in Constantinople, the Frankish lord in his hall, the Norse navigator on his longship, the Venetian merchant in the Rialto, the Benedictine scholar in his scriptorium, the Elizabethan intelligencer in his office, the Restoration lawyer with his seal-press, and all the rest — has been carried here, to Baker Street, to the gaslight and the Stradivarius and the cold cases spread across the bearskin rug, by the same unbroken thread. Each member of the line carried it forward without knowing, in most cases, quite what they were carrying. Dr. Watson, who carried nothing in his own name but gave everything to the preservation of his friend's, is perhaps the purest expression of what a chronicler can do.

As we record the unbroken line of genius, we must also record here our gratitude. To Dr. Watson, who saw the gift and named it, who preserved it in sixty accounts when the man who possessed it would have preserved nothing, who understood instinctively that this was the kind of thing the world needed to know about — without knowing, and without needing to know, the four-thousand-year story behind the knowledge.

He was, as Holmes said of him on more than one occasion and in more than one register, the best and wisest man he had ever known.

We can only agree.


The sixty accounts are elsewhere, better and complete.
The reader who has not yet read them has a great pleasure ahead.

SHERLOCK HOLMES (1854-1957) — The Holmes Chronicle | Andreas Breidenthal

SHERLOCK HOLMES

1854 — Yorkshire, England

He was born in the depth of a Yorkshire winter, the third son of William Scott Holmes and his wife Marie-Claude, and gave early evidence of a mind so singular in its operations that his family, accustomed as they were to analytical distinction, found themselves at a loss to account for it. He attended university — reading chemistry and pursuing his own irregular course of study in anatomy, law, and the properties of tobacco ash — before abandoning the formal life of a scholar for London and the career he had already determined upon: that of a consulting detective, the world's first and, as he was not reluctant to observe, its only. He established himself in rooms at 221B Baker Street in 1881, where his friendship with Dr. John H. Watson began at St Bartholomew's Hospital, and where Watson's careful chronicling of his cases preserved for posterity what Holmes's own disregard for celebrity might otherwise have consigned to oblivion. The cases were many and various — spanning three decades, touching every stratum of society from the highest to the lowest, and demonstrating again and again that quality which had expressed itself, in one form or another, in every generation of his line since the first scribe of Ur: the ability to read, from the smallest particular, the whole of a hidden truth.

He disappeared at the Reichenbach Falls in May of 1891, locked in final struggle with Professor James Moriarty, the Napoleon of crime; he was mourned by Watson and by London; and he returned in the spring of 1894 as if from the dead, having spent the intervening years in Tibet, Persia, and elsewhere, because the work was not yet finished. He retired to the Sussex Downs in 1903 to keep bees, write his monograph on the subject, and pursue, at last, a life of his own choosing. He came out of that retirement once more, in 1914, to break a German intelligence network at the request of his country; and then withdrew again, for good. He lived through two world wars: the first when he was in his sixties, a man of mature powers who understood what he was witnessing; the second when he was in his late eighties and early nineties, still sharp, still attending, still reading the pattern beneath the noise. He outlived his son, Sherlock Holmes II, by some eleven years. In his final decade, his grandson, Sherlock Holmes III, made the long journey back from America several times, and had the foresight — or perhaps the instinct, which in that family is much the same thing — to bring with him his young son, Sherlock Holmes IV, so that the boy might grow acquainted with his great-grandfather while there was yet time to do so. He died, on the evidence available, at an age exceeding one hundred years, and those who attended him in his final hours recorded that the last word on his lips was a woman's name. Watson had predeceased him. The chronicle records it simply. Some things belong to the man rather than the line.

Watson's published record has attracted, across the century and more since its appearance, a remarkable succession of actors, filmmakers, and writers who have sought to render it faithfully on screen and page. Watson noted, in private correspondence of the early 1920s, that Eille Norwood's silent film portrayals had caught something that surprised even him — the stillness above all, the quality of absolute attention that his written accounts had struggled to convey and that Norwood, without a word of dialogue at his disposal, had communicated through the simple act of watching. Watson had spent twenty years beside that stillness. He recognised it. The Granada Television series featuring Jeremy Brett is the most faithful rendering of Watson's published record that the screen has yet produced — Brett brought to the role a febrile intensity that those who knew Holmes only from the printed page might have found surprising, and those who understood him found exact. Peter Cushing rendered Holmes twice — in the 1959 Hammer film of The Hound of the Baskervilles and in the 1968 BBC series — with a precision and quiet authority that the record warrants and Watson would have recognised. A century after Watson set down his pen, Anthony Horowitz produced, with the blessing of the Holmes estate, two novels — The House of Silk and Moriarty — that demonstrate a rare and precise understanding of how Watson's mind worked: the instinct for what to record, what to withhold, and how to move between the two. That Horowitz was able to extrapolate from Watson's published record with such authority suggests he read it not merely as a reader, but as a student. The two Basil Rathbone films of 1939 — The Hound of the Baskervilles and The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — render Watson's Victorian record with distinction; their deeper significance to this record is addressed in the entry for Sherlock Holmes III. Mitch Cullin's novel A Slight Trick of the Mind, and Ian McKellen's luminous screen adaptation, depict Holmes in extreme old age — Sussex, 1947, the bees still kept, the mind finally and painfully failing, Watson long gone. Of all the dramatic and literary representations of the man, this comes closest to what the record suggests his final years contained.

It is difficult to imagine that Holmes, at the very height of his powers in the autumn of 1888, was untouched by the Whitechapel murders — the most notorious series of killings in British history, attributed to the unidentified assailant the press named Jack the Ripper, and never officially solved. Watson, whose records are the most reliable account available, published nothing on the matter. The chronicler is therefore not in a position to comment further. Lyndsay Faye's Dust and Shadow engages the question speculatively, and with considerable skill and genuine feeling for the period — but in the absence of Watson's testimony, speculation is all that any account of Holmes's involvement in these events can ever be.

The 1985 film Young Sherlock Holmes imagines Holmes and Watson as schoolboys together — a friendship forged in adolescence, full of adventure and early genius. It is joyful, inventive, and entirely its own thing. As we know, Watson first met Holmes at Barts in 1881, a grown man returning from war, with no prior acquaintance between them. Watson, no doubt, would still have been charmed by the ingenuity of the fiction.

Billy Wilder's 1970 film The Private Life of Sherlock Holmes is a work of considerable wit and melancholy that reaches into the silences of Watson's published record and asks what might lie behind them. Wilder understood that the most interesting things about Holmes were precisely the things Watson had chosen not to write about, and he pursued those gaps with the instincts of a master filmmaker. That his conclusions are his own rather than Watson's does not diminish the intelligence with which he reached them.

Adrian Conan Doyle and John Dickson Carr turned, in 1954, to the unpublished cases Watson himself had referenced but never recorded — those tantalising fragments scattered through the published accounts, the cases named but never told. To build such a compelling fiction from those fragments required not only a deep understanding of Holmes's methods and Watson's voice, but the imagination to reconstruct, from the barest of hints, what those cases might have contained. The Exploits of Sherlock Holmes demonstrates both, and demonstrates them well.

Nicholas Meyer's 1974 novel The Seven-Per-Cent Solution is a work of genuine cleverness that proposes Moriarty was a figment of a cocaine-disordered mind, and that Watson's own account of Reichenbach was a fabrication constructed to protect his friend's reputation. It is ingeniously argued and compellingly written. Holmes himself, one suspects, would have admired the audacity of the premise.

The two films featuring Robert Downey Jr. bring to Watson's Victorian London a tremendous energy and physicality that reimagines the familiar world of the cases with such confidence and period authenticity that Watson himself might have wondered, for a moment, why he had failed to record these particular events. They capture his world, and his man, with an imagination and flair that commands genuine respect.

Nancy Springer's Enola Holmes series, and its subsequent screen adaptations, introduce Holmes's younger sister — a young woman of remarkable gifts and considerable independence of spirit. The family record contains no such person. Springer's imagination, however, has conjured her so vividly, and with such evident understanding of the Holmes temperament, that one almost wishes the record were otherwise.

Laurie R. King's Mary Russell series is among the most sustained and accomplished works of Holmes pastiche in existence — a sequence of novels spanning decades, in which an ageing Holmes meets his intellectual match in a young woman of formidable gifts. It is sophisticated, deeply researched, and genuinely illuminating about the man it imagines. Though clearly fictitious, it portrays Holmes throughout with the honour and respect that he so richly deserves.

From the sealed letter of 1895, addressed to no one, filed among his chemical papers and transmitted to his son in due course:

The faculty does not rest. This is its single defect, and it is a significant one. In the intervals between problems of sufficient complexity, the mind, finding nothing adequate to engage it, turns on itself — methodically, without mercy, with the same precision it would otherwise direct toward the external world. Watson has witnessed this and recorded it honestly, though he has drawn back, always, from naming it plainly. I do not reproach him for the reticence. It is a quality in him I have come, across many years, to depend upon. What I will say here, for the benefit of whoever reads this after me, is simply: the faculty cannot be turned off. It can only be redirected. The bees are, in this respect, more satisfactory than Watson knows.

A note on the record:
The chronicler who has followed this line from its beginning — from the scribe of Ur who first bent his gift toward the hidden discrepancy in the grain tally — arrives now at the period closest to his own time, and finds the record both more abundant and more difficult than any that preceded it. More abundant because the modern world documents everything; more difficult because the modern world also obscures everything, and the members of this line, from the second Sherlock onward, appear to have understood this and acted accordingly. What follows is assembled from private memoranda, legal archives, a handful of unpublished intelligence summaries, and one annotated copy of a family document that passed through several hands before reaching this account. Where the record is thin, the chronicle says so. Where it is absent entirely, the chronicle says that too.

The organisation that the great detective knew as Moriarty's empire did not die with its Napoleon. It had not died with any of its previous commanders, across the four thousand years this chronicle spans. It reorganised. It found new instruments, new languages, new legitimate fronts behind which the same essential structure — hierarchical, durable, insulated from prosecution by layers of apparent respectability — continued its operations. The Whitechapel counting house that appears in the records of the second Sherlock's time bore a different name in each subsequent generation. The address did not change. Addresses, unlike names, are harder to fabricate from nothing.
