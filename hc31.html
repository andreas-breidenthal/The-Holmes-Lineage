BAAL-SHILLEK

~1150 BC — North Africa

The Account by Nahar-Iddin

These tablets were recovered in damaged condition; certain passages have been reconstructed from marginal notes and the secondary account preserved in the harbour-master's archive. The reader should understand that this scribe's purpose was the assessment of practical intelligence, not the embellishment of reputation, and that the events recorded here are set down because they bear directly upon the reliability of the survey method and the integrity of the records produced by it.

I.

The smell reached us before the sight of it.

I know the smell of a Tyrian lamp — the particular thick sweetness of cedar-oil burning in a clay cup, the smell of the counting houses and the guild halls and every room I have sat in taking dictation for thirty years. I know also the smell of a fire left untended, the sour char of wood that has burned past usefulness and begun to consume itself. And I know, though I had not known it before that morning, the smell of a man who has been dead upon a beach for the portion of a night.

It was Addir-Baal who found him, coming down from the camp at first light to begin the morning tally of the water jars. Addir-Baal was the youngest of the five men Baal-Shillek had brought ashore at the hidden anchorage — the one who always moved fastest in the mornings, who was always first to the tallies because his mind worked in quantities and he could not rest until the quantities were confirmed. He came back up the shore path with his sandals tracking wet sand and his face the colour of old linen, and he said nothing at all, which was how Baal-Shillek knew it was serious.

The anchorage had no name yet. It was a notch in the coastline, perhaps four days' sail west of the last marked water source, screened from the open sea by a long arm of pale rock and accessible through a gap that a single galley could navigate if the helmsman knew where to look. Baal-Shillek had found it in the second season — in that third year of what would prove the most methodical survey yet attempted of the North African coast, conducted not in the manner of the raiders or the casual traders who had preceded him, but with the systematic intent of a man who understood that information about a new country is the prerequisite of everything else. He had entered this notch in his tablets with the notation good holding ground, fresh spring behind the ridge, no population observed within a day's walk, and had shared the location with no one outside the survey party. The Tyrian merchant council received the position coded, in the navigational shorthand that only Baal-Shillek himself could read at speed. The Sidonian merchants, who had been following Tyrian expansion westward for two seasons with the patient acquisitiveness of men who prefer to profit from another's effort rather than hazard their own, had received nothing. It was, as Baal-Shillek had explained to me at the beginning of my attachment to the survey, the point of the entire enterprise: you record what is real, you protect what you have recorded, and you do not distribute information until you understand its value. The Sidonians were, in this calculus, one of the principal reasons for the code.

The dead man was Kuru-Baal, the second helmsman, a Tyrian by birth but of mixed blood, his mother's people from somewhere along the Levantine coast further south. He lay at the waterline where the small waves were still mouthing at his sandals, and he had been struck from behind with something heavy and edged — the mark on the back of his skull was sharp-cornered, not rounded, which Baal-Shillek noted immediately, crouching in the wet sand with the detachment of a man reading a tablet.

I stood behind him and felt my stomach shift in a way that had nothing to do with the motion of the sea. There was a great deal of blood, dried now to the colour of cedar-resin in the early light. The gulls had not yet found him, which meant he had not been dead so very long.

"Your tablets," Baal-Shillek said, without turning.

"I — yes." I opened the leather case I carried everywhere. The habit of thirty years.

"Record that the body lies with the head toward the water and the feet toward the ridge path. Record the wound. Record that his knife is still in his belt and his belt-purse is intact." He stood, brushing wet sand from his knees with the same gesture he used to brush clay dust from a completed tablet. "Someone did not want his silver. They wanted him."

Harbour-Captain Eshmunar arrived within the hour, having been roused from the galley where he and the remaining crew had slept. He was a competent man of middle years who had sailed for the merchant council for two decades and who possessed, as most competent men of middle years do, an absolute confidence in the sufficiency of his own experience. He looked at the body, he looked at the five men of the survey party who were gathered at the top of the shore path, and he reached the conclusion that his experience entitled him to reach.

"One of the locals," he said. "We were observed coming in. They waited."

"No local population has been observed within a day's walk," Baal-Shillek said. He was standing a little apart, looking not at the body but at the ridge above us, shading his eyes against the early light. "I noted it when we entered. Nahar-Iddin recorded it."

"A wandering group. Pastoralists. They would not be in your tablets."

"No. But their fire would be. We have been at this anchorage for three days. I have surveyed the ridge each morning. There has been no fire on the ridge or on the coastal plain to the west. The spring is the only water within half a day. Anyone camped within striking distance of this bay would need to use it." He finally turned and looked at Eshmunar with the expression I had come to recognise — not unkind, but possessing the specific quality of a man waiting for the obvious to become apparent to someone else. "There is no one on this coast who knew we were here. There is no one on this coast who knew of this anchorage at all. Therefore Kuru-Baal was not killed by a local. He was killed by a Tyrian."

The silence that followed was of the particular quality that silences have when everyone present simultaneously understands something and simultaneously wishes they did not.

Eshmunar looked at the five men on the path. They looked back at him.

"I will question them," Eshmunar said, with the restored authority of a man who has found a familiar procedure. "I will determine who had cause."

"You may question them," Baal-Shillek said. "I should like to observe."

II.

The five men were brought down to the beach, not to the body, but to the flat stretch of pale sand above the high-water line where a bleached timber lay half-buried, serving the survey camp as bench and worktable. Eshmunar set them in a loose group and stood before them with the practised stance of a harbour-captain who has mediated every variety of sailors' dispute and believes this constitutes expertise in human nature. He was, I think, not wrong about that belief within its proper limits. He was simply operating well outside them.

I positioned myself at Baal-Shillek's left shoulder and made ready to record. Baal-Shillek himself stood four paces back from the group, as though he were reading them from a slight distance — which, I came to understand, is precisely what he was doing.

Addir-Baal still had the look of a man whose morning had become something he had not planned for. Beside him stood Mattan the elder helmsman, a broad-shouldered man with a grey beard who had sailed the survey's first season and been indispensable on the second — a solid, unimaginative man whose visible emotion was the irritable discomfort of someone pulled from sleep for business that offended his sense of the practical. Next to Mattan was Hirom-Asher, the carpenter who maintained the galley's hull and fittings; a careful, quiet man who spoke rarely and observed everything, his eyes moving slowly across the group with the expression of professional assessment. Then Yehaw-Milk, the purser, who handled the trading goods and the silver, a thin nervous man whose fingers were moving even now, tallying some invisible quantity as a man might clench his fist against pain.

And at the end, standing slightly apart from the others as he usually did, Bar-Anat.

Bar-Anat was the secondary surveyor, Baal-Shillek's assistant, responsible for the confirmatory tablets that Baal-Shillek required as a check on his own primary notes. He was not a large man, nor a small one — he occupied the precise middle range of everything, which I think had served him well in ways that were only now becoming apparent. He had a round face and steady hands and he was watching Eshmunar with an expression of cooperative attention that was, in retrospect, too complete — the expression not of a man waiting to learn something, but of a man who has prepared himself to look as though he is.

At his hip, suspended from the leather strap that held his working tools, hung his bronze plotting rule: a flat measure some eight fingers long, its edges square and true, the kind of instrument that no surveyor of his quality would set aside even on a morning such as this.

Eshmunar questioned them each in turn. He had determined, within the half-hour, that Mattan had a grievance against Kuru-Baal over a dice debt from two seasons past — a fact that radiated from him with the satisfaction of a man who has found what he expected to find. He had determined that Addir-Baal was genuinely horrified. He had determined that Hirom-Asher had been on watch in the first half of the night and had observed nothing. He had determined nothing at all about Yehaw-Milk except that the man's fingers would not stop moving.

From Baal-Shillek, throughout, there was silence. He stood where he had positioned himself and watched. Occasionally, I observed, he was not watching the man being questioned, but the men who were not — reading, I supposed, the unguarded reactions of an audience that believed itself unobserved.

When Eshmunar had finished, he turned to Baal-Shillek with the expression of a man presenting completed work.

"Mattan had cause," he said.

"Mattan had a grudge over silver," Baal-Shillek agreed pleasantly. "A different matter." He came forward now, and there was the quality in his movement — unhurried, entirely deliberate — of a man who has been still for a reason and is now in motion for a reason. He walked along the line until he stood before Bar-Anat.

Bar-Anat met his eyes with the cooperative attention that had been his expression throughout, and held it — and I noted that he was the only one of the five who had not, in some small way, shifted his weight or adjusted his position during Baal-Shillek's approach. The others had reacted as the innocent react, with small involuntary attentions. Bar-Anat was very still, with the stillness of a man who has decided to be still.

Baal-Shillek looked at him for a moment with the expression I can only describe as the one a physician makes when the symptoms have confirmed his initial assessment, and then he turned away.

"The records," he said to me. "We will go to the spring."

III.

I must explain, for those who were not present and who read only this account, what the landing records are and why they exist.

Baal-Shillek was not, in the generation of his voyaging, the first Tyrian to sail west along the North African coast. But he was among the first to do so with the intent of a man conducting a proper survey rather than a raid or an opportunistic trade — and what distinguished him from those who had preceded him was precisely what he had built for this purpose: the record. Every man ashore at every anchorage is accounted for in the landing record: who went where, at what hour, for what purpose, and when he returned to camp. He had explained this to me at the beginning of my attachment to the survey with the patience he reserved for ideas he considered self-evident but found, to his mild exasperation, that others did not. A survey party ashore in an unknown country is a machine. A machine works only when every component is where it is supposed to be. The record tells me whether the machine is functioning. If a component is not where the record says it should be, I know the machine has a fault, and I find the fault before it causes a failure.

He had not, I think, envisioned the kind of failure we were now examining.

We sat by the spring in the thin morning shade of a stand of thorny scrub, and I unrolled the tablets onto the flat rock Baal-Shillek used as a working surface. The water came up out of the limestone with a sound like quiet breathing, and the insects in the scrub maintained their steady rasping hum, and somewhere below us on the beach the body of Kuru-Baal continued to be dead while Eshmunar stood over it looking at the horizon and failing to have useful thoughts.

"Begin with last night," Baal-Shillek said. He sat cross-legged on the rock with his hands open on his knees, his eyes half closed in the way they went when he was working — not vacant, the opposite of vacant, as though he were looking at something inside rather than outside.

I found the relevant section.

"The evening tally was taken at the third hour of darkness," I read. "All five men ashore were in camp at the third hour. Kuru-Baal had gone to the waterline at the second hour to check the mooring lines of the landing boat, which had been chafing in the afternoon. He was recorded returning at the second hour and one quarter. He reported the lines sound."

"And thereafter?"

"No further movement recorded. The watch was Hirom-Asher, first half of the night, Bar-Anat, second half."

Baal-Shillek was quiet for a moment. The spring breathed.

"Hirom-Asher reported nothing in his watch?"

"Nothing. His entry reads: no movement observed, sea quiet, wind from the north-west moderate."

"Bar-Anat?"

I looked. "Bar-Anat's entry reads: watch uneventful, relieved at first light. And then Addir-Baal's entry at first light recording that he went to the water jars and found the body."

"Mm." Baal-Shillek opened his eyes fully. "The water-jar tallies. All three days."

I found them and read them aloud: the morning counts, the evening counts, the recorded uses throughout each day. Baal-Shillek listened without interrupting, his lips moving very slightly, as though he were conducting some internal arithmetic that required more precision than ordinary thought allowed.

When I finished, he was quiet for a long time. Long enough that I began to hear the individual notes in the insect noise from the scrub, the way a man begins to notice the smell of a room he has been sitting in for a while.

Then he said: "Read me Bar-Anat's survey entries for the second day."

I found them. Bar-Anat was the secondary surveyor, responsible for the confirmatory record — he walked the same ground as Baal-Shillek's primary survey and recorded independently, so that the two records could be compared. His entries for the second day described the eastern ridge, the spring catchment, the depth of the soil in the small valley behind the anchorage.

"He records surveying the eastern ridge from the second hour after dawn to the fourth hour."

"Yes."

"And the spring catchment from the fourth hour to the midday."

"Continue."

"The small valley from the midday to the second hour of the afternoon. Then he records returning to camp and —" I stopped.

"Continue," Baal-Shillek said, in exactly the same tone.

"He records filling his water skin at the spring on return to camp."

"At what hour?"

"The entry says the second hour of the afternoon."

"And the morning water-jar tally on the second day."

I looked. "Taken at first light. All jars at the marked levels. Eleven men's portions per jar, four jars, thirty-seven portions remaining after the first day's draw."

"And the evening tally of the second day."

"Thirty-one portions remaining."

"Six portions drawn across the full day, between first light and the evening tally." Baal-Shillek looked at me with those clear, still eyes. "Bar-Anat records filling his water skin at the spring on return from survey. If he filled his skin at the spring, he would not have drawn from the jar. The jar count should reflect the other four men only. But I recall precisely — and I invite you to verify this from your own notes, Nahar-Iddin, since your confirmation is the point of the record — that on the second day, Addir-Baal drew one portion at morning, one at midday, and one at evening. Mattan drew one at midday. Hirom-Asher drew one in the afternoon. Yehaw-Milk drew one in the afternoon."

I counted. "Six portions."

"Six. Accounted for by five men, including Bar-Anat, who claims he drank from the spring and not the jar." He waited.

I counted again. "That is — if Bar-Anat drank from the spring, only five portions should have been drawn from the jars."

"Five. Not six."

I looked at the tablet. The tally was in my own hand. Six portions, taken throughout the second day. Thirty-seven minus six equals thirty-one, which matched the evening count precisely.

"Someone drew an extra portion," I said. I was beginning to understand, in the way that a man understands a landscape from a height that he could not perceive at ground level — the full shape of it becoming apparent all at once.

"Bar-Anat drew from the jar," Baal-Shillek said, "not from the spring. He was not at the spring at the second hour of the afternoon. His survey entry for that day is a fabrication. He was elsewhere." He paused. There was something in his expression at that moment — not warmth precisely, but the quality of a mind that has found what it came looking for and registers the fact with a satisfaction that is almost involuntary, the way a craftsman runs his thumb along a newly true joint. "The eastern ridge, I would estimate. The eastern ridge commands a view down the coastal plain to the north, and the coastal plain to the north is the direction from which a Sidonian factor might be expected to approach, if one had sent word — at some previous anchorage, using the navigational shorthand that the secondary surveyor, being responsible for the confirmatory record, has possessed since the first season — precisely where we would be found."

The insects rasped in the scrub. The spring breathed.

"He betrayed the survey," I said. I could hear my own voice as though from a slight distance.

"He sold it. There is a difference, in motive if not in consequence. And he has been methodical about it — which I find, I confess, rather more contemptible than mere passion would be. Passion at least has the dignity of being ungoverned. This was a ledger entry. A transaction." Baal-Shillek stood, brushing dust from his robe. "Someone paid Bar-Anat to bring a Sidonian factor to this anchorage — to let him see it, measure it, record it, before we return to Tyre with our report. Kuru-Baal saw something that night — a signal, a meeting, perhaps only a shadow where no shadow should be — and Bar-Anat had no choice left but the choice he made." He paused. "Kuru-Baal was a careful man. He noticed things. That was his value as a helmsman and his misfortune last night."

"And the factor?" I asked. "He has seen the anchorage. He is —"

"Out there." Baal-Shillek looked north, across the ridge, toward the pale immensity of the coastal plain. "He received water from the jar at the second hour of the afternoon of the second day. He would have waited for darkness to move — a man carrying intelligence through country he does not know does not walk in daylight. So he moved last night, after the second watch, when the darkness was deepest and Bar-Anat could confirm the timing." He calculated, visibly, in the way a navigator calculates a heading — not with effort, but with the ease of a man doing what he was made to do. "He is perhaps eight hours ahead of us on foot. He is carrying water for one day. He will need to find a source before the sun reaches its height today, which means he will move to the watercourse that feeds this spring — the one that runs north through the valley and surfaces at the base of the ridge two hours' walk from here." He looked at me. "If we move within the hour, we can take him before he reaches it."

I absorbed this. "And Bar-Anat."

"Bar-Anat first. But quickly." He was already moving toward the shore path, with a pace that was no longer unhurried. "The factor has no measurements, no depth-soundings, no spring yield. He has a view of the bay from a ridge and whatever Bar-Anat described to him. If we take him before he reaches Sidonian territory, he carries nothing that the merchant council cannot contain."

He walked away from the spring toward the shore path, and I gathered my tablets and followed. I held the leather case against my chest as I walked, which I had not done before. It seemed, abruptly, important to hold it.

IV.

Eshmunar had, in the time we were at the spring, arranged the five men back in a line on the beach and was engaged in a second round of questioning, having returned to Mattan with renewed conviction. The dice debt was expanding in his hands like a merchant's claim at arbitration. He looked up when Baal-Shillek appeared at the head of the shore path, and the expression he wore was that of a man who has arrived at his conclusion and requires only the formality of agreement.

Baal-Shillek walked past him entirely. He walked past Mattan. He walked along the line until he stood before Bar-Anat and said, in the tone of a man completing a routine accounting: "Your man is at the valley watercourse. We have two hours, perhaps less. I need the routing."

Bar-Anat said nothing. But something happened behind his eyes — a rapid, involuntary calculation — and that was enough.

"Not caught yet, then," Baal-Shillek said pleasantly. "I thought not. If he had been taken, you would have heard the sailors on the ridge. The question was whether you knew he hadn't been."

It was, I understood then, a test with a mechanism. If the factor had been caught, Bar-Anat's silence would have been safe. Because the factor had not been caught, Bar-Anat's silence had confirmed what Baal-Shillek needed confirmed: that Bar-Anat knew exactly where on the coastal plain the man was moving, and could not afford to be caught helping to locate him.

"Your plotting rule," Baal-Shillek continued, turning to Eshmunar. "Take it from him now, before he thinks to lose it. The wound on Kuru-Baal's skull is sharp-cornered, eight fingers wide. Bronze holds traces in the joints of the handle even when the face has been cleaned."

Bar-Anat moved then — not toward Eshmunar and not toward Baal-Shillek, but toward me.

I had not expected it. I do not think he had planned it as an act of desperation so much as an act of calculation: the records in my leather case were the only evidence that could not be disputed, the only evidence that predated his influence on any of the proceedings. He was on me before Eshmunar had registered the movement, one hand gripping the strap of the case, and for a moment that felt longer than it was we struggled for it — a scribe and a murderer, on a beach in a country without a name, contesting thirty years of records and three days of tallies and the arithmetic that was going to kill him.

I did not let go. I want that set down clearly, because I am a man of ledgers and weights, not a man of violence, and it would be easy to omit. I did not let go.

Eshmunar's sailors had him in another moment. They were not gentle about it, which under the circumstances seemed appropriate.

Bar-Anat was breathing hard. His steady hands were no longer steady, and had ceased to be steady in a way that was no longer merely the product of nerves — they had the tremor of a man who has committed himself completely to a course of action and found the course ended before he expected. He looked at the case in my arms and then at Baal-Shillek, and the cooperative attention that had been his expression all morning had gone entirely, replaced by something older and less composed: not fear, but the expression of a man who has watched a wall he believed solid reveal itself, stone by stone, to have been hollow all along.

Baal-Shillek said nothing for a moment. He had the theatrical habit of allowing silence to do work that most men would fill with words. The sea moved against the pale rock of the headland, patient and repetitive.

Then: "On the second day of our survey of this anchorage, you recorded filling your water skin at the spring at the second hour of the afternoon."

Bar-Anat said nothing.

"You drew six portions from the water jars that day, not five. One more than the other four men combined. One more than a man who had drunk from the spring would need." Baal-Shillek waited, in case there was an avenue Bar-Anat felt worth testing. There was not. The man on the beach was no longer the man who had offered his own tallies in counter-evidence; that man had departed the moment his hands closed on the leather case. What remained was someone simpler and more tired. "The sixth portion went to a man who came down from the eastern ridge and whom you provisioned from the jar because bringing him to the spring would have brought him into camp. A Sidonian factor, now somewhere on the coastal plain to the north, who has seen this anchorage from a ridge and has in his head whatever you have told him of its coordinates."

Bar-Anat looked at the sand. When he raised his eyes, the last of the calculation in them had gone.

"The Sidonian factor," he said. Flat. The voice of a man who has understood that accounting is complete and the balance is against him. "They have been two seasons behind you. They needed the western anchorages." His jaw worked. "Kuru-Baal saw the man on the ridge. He came to me at the second watch and said he would speak to you in the morning. I —" He stopped.

"No," Baal-Shillek said. "There was not."

He turned to Eshmunar. "You have his admission. Have your sailors search his kit for the plotting rule and test the handle joints. Then take four men and go north along the valley watercourse — quickly, because the man you are looking for knows this country no better than we do and will need water before midday. He is carrying whatever Bar-Anat could tell him about this anchorage, and none of it is in writing, because Bar-Anat is too careful to have put it in writing. Take him before he reaches the range of hills, and what the Sidonians purchased goes no further than a man's memory, which is not the same thing as a chart."

Eshmunar, to his considerable credit, asked no questions. He turned and began issuing orders to his sailors with the efficient authority of a man in his proper element. The factor was taken at the watercourse before the sun reached midday, which is recorded in Eshmunar's own account of the voyage and need not be repeated here.

Baal-Shillek walked to the water's edge and stood looking west along the coast, toward the unnamed distances that his tablets were slowly making legible. I stood beside him because it was where I stood.

The leather case was in my arms still. I had not set it down.

"Kuru-Baal's family," I said, because it seemed important to say it first.

"His full season's payment. I will note in my report that he died of a fever. The merchant council does not need to know that one of their scribes kept records that got a man killed."

I looked at him. "That is not what happened."

"No. But it is what the record will say, and the record is what survives." He looked at me with those clear eyes, and then at the case against my chest, and something crossed his expression that I could not quite name — not sentiment, but the thing that sits close to it in a man who does not permit himself sentiment. "You held onto it."

"It is the evidence," I said.

"Yes." He looked back at the coast, west, toward the pale indifferent distances. "It is also thirty years. We will complete the survey. We will enter this anchorage in the record under its proper name and with its proper depth-soundings and its spring yield and its holding-ground assessment. We will return to Tyre with a record that is accurate and complete."

I uncapped my stylus.

"What name," I asked, "shall I give this anchorage?"

He considered it for a moment, looking at the pale rock headland and the still water in the notch of the bay and the gulls that were beginning, finally, to find Kuru-Baal at the waterline.

"Khet-Baal," he said. "The hiding place of Baal. It is apt for a location that conceals itself, and for a man who was revealed."

I wrote it down.

That is the name it carries in the merchant council's records to this day, and the reason for the name is not recorded anywhere except here. The anchorage is productive. Three trading vessels used it in the season following our return. The water from the spring is good.

Bar-Anat was returned to Tyre in chains and tried before the guild council, whose records note only that he was convicted of the murder of Kuru-Baal, helmsman, by instrument, at an anchorage on the North African coast. The Sidonian factor is not mentioned. The survey is not mentioned. Eshmunar received the official commendation for the resolution of the matter, which was proper and expected.

Baal-Shillek, for his part, returned to his tablets.

Here the primary account ends. A marginal notation in a different hand — tentatively attributed to Nahar-Iddin himself, in a later period, the clay being of slightly different composition — reads as follows:

I have recorded this as it occurred. I do not know whether Baal-Shillek calculated, before we reached Bar-Anat on the beach, what Bar-Anat would do. I believe he did, because in thirty years I have never seen him arrive at a conclusion and be wrong. But he would not tell me. He said only that the record does not lie, if it is kept properly, and that a man who falsifies his record reveals himself by the false entry as surely as by a confession. A record is a promise about what happened. When a man breaks the promise, the break is visible. You must simply know how to look.

What I have not set down before, and set down now because the time for careful omission is past, is what it was to hold the case while Bar-Anat's hands were on the strap. He was stronger than I am. He was also, in that moment, not a secondary surveyor or a Sidonian agent or a puzzle in an accounting problem — he was a man who had killed once that morning and who understood that the leather case was the distance between his life and his ending. I understood it too. That is why I held it.

Baal-Shillek said afterward, only this: the method must account for human failure as well as natural obstacle. I have thought, since, that he meant Bar-Anat. I think now he may have meant me.

This tablet is deposited in the harbour-master's archive at the request of the navigator Baal-Shillek, who specified that it should be preserved in the practical record alongside his survey reports, as evidence of the method's completeness.

Nahar-Iddin, scribe, merchant council of Tyre.
