BAAL-SHILLEK

~1150 BC — North Africa

Tyre was beginning its great westward expansion in his generation — the tentative voyages along the North African coast that would, over centuries, become one of the most extensive colonial enterprises in the ancient world. Baal-Shillek was among the early navigators of that expansion: not the first to sail west, but among the first to sail west with the systematic intent of a man conducting a survey rather than merely a raid or a trading voyage, mapping the anchorages and the water sources and the populations of the coast with the methodical attention of someone who understood that information about a new country is the prerequisite of everything else.

He spent three seasons on the North African coast, ranging west along the shore and returning to Tyre with accounts of what he had found that were detailed, accurate, and notably free of the embellishment that most navigators’ tales contained. His reports were consulted by Tyrian merchants for two generations after his death, and the trading posts he identified became, in some cases, the foundations of settlements that lasted for centuries. He shared with his ancestor Akar-Teshub a particular disposition toward the world: a preference for what could be verified over what could be imagined, and a corresponding distrust of the convenient explanation.

The surviving account of this voyage was recorded by Nahar-Iddin, a guild scribe attached to the Tyrian merchant council, whose task was to note the practical value of each report rather than its narrative quality. He was not a sailor, but a man of ledgers and weights, whose own limitations — a preference for measurable certainty over inference — shaped what he chose to preserve. It is from his tablets, partially recovered and partially reconstructed, that the following account is drawn.

The Account Of Baal-Shillek Of Tyre

These tablets were recovered in damaged condition; certain passages have been reconstructed from marginal notes and the secondary account preserved in the harbour-master’s archive. The reader should understand that this scribe’s purpose was the assessment of practical intelligence, not the embellishment of reputation, and that the events recorded here are set down because they bear directly upon the reliability of the survey method and the integrity of the records produced by it.

The smell reached us before the sight of it.

I know the smell of a Tyrian lamp — the particular thick sweetness of cedar-oil burning in a clay cup, the smell of the counting houses and the guild halls and every room I have sat in taking dictation for thirty years. I know also the smell of a fire left untended, the sour char of wood that has burned past usefulness and begun to consume itself. And I know, though I had not known it before that morning, the smell of a man who has been dead upon a beach for the portion of a night.

It was Addir-Baal who found him, coming down from the camp at first light to begin the morning tally of the water jars. Addir-Baal was the youngest of the five men Baal-Shillek had brought ashore at the hidden anchorage — the one who always moved fastest in the mornings, who was always first to the tallies because his mind worked in quantities and he could not rest until the quantities were confirmed. He came back up the shore path with his sandals tracking wet sand and his face the colour of old linen, and he said nothing at all, which was how Baal-Shillek knew it was serious.

The anchorage had no name yet. It was a notch in the coastline, perhaps four days’ sail west of the last marked water source, screened from the open sea by a long arm of pale rock and accessible through a gap that a single galley could navigate if the helmsman knew where to look. Baal-Shillek had found it in the second season, had entered it in his tablets with the notation good holding ground, fresh spring behind the ridge, no population observed within a day’s walk, and had shared the location with no one outside the survey party itself. The Tyrian merchant council received the position coded, in the navigational shorthand that only Baal-Shillek himself could read at speed. It was, as he had explained to me with the patient precision he used for everything, the point of a survey: you record what is real, you protect what you have recorded, and you do not distribute information until you understand its value.

The dead man was Kuru-Baal, the second helmsman, a Tyrian by birth but of mixed blood, his mother’s people from somewhere along the Levantine coast further south. He lay at the waterline where the small waves were still mouthing at his sandals, and he had been struck from behind with something heavy and edged — the mark on the back of his skull was sharp-cornered, not rounded, which Baal-Shillek noted immediately, crouching in the wet sand with the detachment of a man reading a tablet.

I stood behind him and felt my stomach shift in a way that had nothing to do with the motion of the sea. There was a great deal of blood, dried now to the colour of cedar-resin in the early light. The gulls had not yet found him, which meant he had not been dead so very long.

“Your tablets,” Baal-Shillek said, without turning.

“I — yes.” I opened the leather case I carried everywhere. The habit of thirty years.

“Record that the body lies with the head toward the water and the feet toward the ridge path. Record the wound. Record that his knife is still in his belt and his belt-purse is intact.” He stood, brushing wet sand from his knees with the same gesture he used to brush clay dust from a completed tablet. “Someone did not want his silver. They wanted him.”

Harbour-Captain Eshmunar arrived within the hour, having been roused from the galley where he and the remaining crew had slept. He was a competent man of middle years who had sailed for the merchant council for two decades and who possessed, as most competent men of middle years do, an absolute confidence in the sufficiency of his own experience. He looked at the body, he looked at the five men of the survey party who were gathered at the top of the shore path, and he reached the conclusion that his experience entitled him to reach.

“One of the locals,” he said. “We were observed coming in. They waited.”

“No local population has been observed within a day’s walk,” Baal-Shillek said. He was standing a little apart, looking not at the body but at the ridge above us, shading his eyes against the early light. “I noted it when we entered. Nahar-Iddin recorded it.”

“A wandering group. Pastoralists. They would not be in your tablets.”

“No. But their fire would be. We have been at this anchorage for three days. I have surveyed the ridge each morning. There has been no fire on the ridge or on the coastal plain to the west. The spring is the only water within half a day. Anyone camped within striking distance of this bay would need to use it.” He finally turned and looked at Eshmunar with the expression I had come to recognise — not unkind, but possessing the specific quality of a man waiting for the obvious to become apparent to someone else. “There is no one on this coast who knew we were here. There is no one on this coast who knew of this anchorage at all. Therefore Kuru-Baal was not killed by a local. He was killed by a Tyrian.”

The silence that followed was of the particular quality that silences have when everyone present simultaneously understands something and simultaneously wishes they did not.

Eshmunar looked at the five men on the path. They looked back at him. Addir-Baal was still pale. Beside him stood Mattan the elder helmsman, a broad-shouldered man with a grey beard who had sailed the survey’s first season and been indispensable on the second. Next to Mattan was Hirom-Asher, the carpenter who maintained the galley’s hull and fittings, a careful quiet man who spoke rarely and observed everything. Then Yehaw-Milk, the purser, who handled the trading goods and the silver, a thin nervous man who counted things constantly — his fingers would move when he was anxious, tallying invisible quantities. And at the end, standing slightly apart from the others as he usually did, Bar-Anat, the second surveyor, Baal-Shillek’s assistant and the man responsible for the secondary record — the confirmatory tablets that Baal-Shillek required as a check on his own primary notes.

Five men. One of them had come down to this beach in the night and struck Kuru-Baal from behind.

Eshmunar straightened his shoulders with the air of a man reassembling his authority. “I will question them all. I will determine who had cause.”

“You will determine nothing from questioning,” Baal-Shillek said, not unkindly. “Men who choose their moment and their method do not leave their motive lying where it can be found by questions. The answer is in the records.” He turned to me, and there was something in his eyes that I had not seen before — not excitement precisely, because excitement is warm and his expression had the quality of cold water, perfectly still and perfectly clear. “Nahar-Iddin. You have the landing records from the last three days.”

“I have them.”

“And the fresh-water tallies.”

“The tallies also.”

“Then bring them. We will sit by the spring, where the shade is, and we will find our man.”

I must explain, for those who were not present and who read only this account, what the landing records are and why they exist.

Baal-Shillek’s survey method is — and I set this down not as flattery but as a professional assessment of practical value, which is my purpose — unlike any other method I have encountered in thirty years of recording navigators’ accounts. Most navigators describe what they have seen. Baal-Shillek records what occurred, and to whom, and when. Every man ashore at every anchorage is accounted for in the landing record: who went where, at what hour, for what purpose, and when he returned to camp. The purpose is efficiency — he explained this to me at the beginning of my attachment to the survey, with the patient precision that characterized all his explanations. A survey party ashore in an unknown country is a machine. A machine works only when every component is where it is supposed to be. The record tells me whether the machine is functioning. If a component is not where the record says it should be, I know the machine has a fault, and I find the fault before it causes a failure.

He had not, I think, envisioned the kind of failure we were now examining.

We sat by the spring in the thin morning shade of a stand of thorny scrub, and I unrolled the tablets onto the flat rock Baal-Shillek used as a working surface. The water came up out of the limestone with a sound like quiet breathing, and the insects in the scrub maintained their steady rasping hum, and somewhere below us on the beach the body of Kuru-Baal continued to be dead while Eshmunar stood over it looking at the horizon and failing to have useful thoughts.

“Begin with last night,” Baal-Shillek said. He sat cross-legged on the rock with his hands open on his knees, his eyes half closed in the way they went when he was working — not vacant, the opposite of vacant, as though he were looking at something inside rather than outside.

I found the relevant section.

“The evening tally was taken at the third hour of darkness,” I read. “All five men ashore were in camp at the third hour. Kuru-Baal had gone to the waterline at the second hour to check the mooring lines of the landing boat, which had been chafing in the afternoon. He was recorded returning at the second hour and one quarter. He reported the lines sound.”

“And thereafter?”

“No further movement recorded. The watch was Hirom-Asher, first half of the night, Bar-Anat, second half.”

Baal-Shillek was quiet for a moment. The spring breathed.

“Hirom-Asher reported nothing in his watch?”

“Nothing. His entry reads: no movement observed, sea quiet, wind from the north-west moderate.”

“Bar-Anat?”

I looked. “Bar-Anat’s entry reads: watch uneventful, relieved at first light. And then Addir-Baal’s entry at first light recording that he went to the water jars and found the body.”

“Mm.” Baal-Shillek opened his eyes fully. “The water-jar tallies. All three days.”

I found them and read them aloud: the morning counts, the evening counts, the recorded uses throughout each day. Baal-Shillek listened without interrupting, his lips moving very slightly, as though he were conducting some internal arithmetic that required more precision than ordinary thought allowed.

When I finished, he was quiet for a long time. Long enough that I began to hear the individual notes in the insect noise from the scrub, the way a man begins to notice the smell of a room he has been sitting in for a while.

Then he said: “Read me Bar-Anat’s survey entries for the second day.”

I found them. Bar-Anat was the secondary surveyor, responsible for the confirmatory record — he walked the same ground as Baal-Shillek’s primary survey and recorded independently, so that the two records could be compared. His entries for the second day described the eastern ridge, the spring catchment, the depth of the soil in the small valley behind the anchorage.

“He records surveying the eastern ridge from the second hour after dawn to the fourth hour.”

“Yes.”

“And the spring catchment from the fourth hour to the midday.”

“Continue.”

“The small valley from the midday to the second hour of the afternoon. Then he records returning to camp and — “ I stopped.

“Continue,” Baal-Shillek said, in exactly the same tone.

“He records filling his water skin at the spring on return to camp.”

“At what hour?”

“The entry says the second hour of the afternoon.”

“And the morning water-jar tally on the second day.”

I looked. “Taken at first light. All jars at the marked levels. Eleven men’s portions per jar, four jars, thirty-seven portions remaining after the first day’s draw.”

“And the evening tally of the second day.”

“Thirty-one portions remaining.”

“Six portions drawn across the full day, between first light and the evening tally.” Baal-Shillek looked at me with those clear, still eyes. “Bar-Anat records filling his water skin at the spring on return from survey. If he filled his skin at the spring, he would not have drawn from the jar. The jar count should reflect the other four men only. But I recall precisely — and I invite you to verify this from your own notes, Nahar-Iddin, since your confirmation is the point of the record — that on the second day, Addir-Baal drew one portion at morning, one at midday, and one at evening. Mattan drew one at midday. Hirom-Asher drew one in the afternoon. Yehaw-Milk drew one in the afternoon.”

I counted. “Six portions.”

“Six. Accounted for by five men, including Bar-Anat, who claims he drank from the spring and not the jar.” He waited.

I counted again. “That is — if Bar-Anat drank from the spring, only five portions should have been drawn from the jars.”

“Five. Not six.”

I looked at the tablet. The tally was in my own hand. Six portions, taken throughout the second day. Thirty-seven minus six equals thirty-one, which matched the evening count precisely.

“Someone drew an extra portion,” I said. I was beginning to understand, in the way that a man understands a landscape from a height that he could not perceive at ground level — the full shape of it becoming apparent all at once.

“Bar-Anat drew from the jar,” Baal-Shillek said, “not from the spring. He was not at the spring at the second hour of the afternoon. His survey entry for that day is a fabrication. He was elsewhere.” He paused. “The eastern ridge, I would estimate. The eastern ridge has a view down the coastal plain to the north, and the coastal plain to the north is the direction from which a certain type of visitor might be expected to come, if one had, at some previous anchorage, sent word that one was here and given the coordinates in navigational shorthand that — because one is the secondary surveyor and responsible for the confirmatory record — one has had full access to since the first season.”

The insects rasped in the scrub. The spring breathed.

“He betrayed the survey,” I said. I could hear my own voice as though from a slight distance.

“He sold it. There is a difference, in motive if not in consequence. Someone paid Bar-Anat to bring a competitor’s factor to this anchorage — to let them see it, measure it, record it, before we return to Tyre with our report. Kuru-Baal saw him on the ridge, or saw the signal, or asked a question at the wrong moment.” Baal-Shillek stood, brushing dust from his robe. “Kuru-Baal was a careful man. He noticed things. That was his value as a helmsman and his misfortune last night.”

He walked away from the spring toward the shore path, moving with the unhurried deliberateness he always moved with, and I gathered my tablets and followed.

Eshmunar had, in the time we were at the spring, arranged the five men of the survey party in a line on the beach above the body and questioned them each in turn, with the experienced efficiency of a harbour-captain who has managed disputes among sailors for twenty years and believes this qualifies him for every subsequent human problem. He had determined that Mattan had a grievance against Kuru-Baal over a dice debt from two seasons past, and this information was radiating from him with the satisfaction of a man who has found what he expected to find.

Baal-Shillek walked past Mattan entirely and stopped in front of Bar-Anat.

Bar-Anat was not a large man, nor a small one — he occupied the precise middle range of everything, which I think had served him well. He had a round face and steady hands and an expression of cooperative concern that he had been wearing since Addir-Baal returned from the beach. He looked at Baal-Shillek and his steady hands ceased to be steady.

Baal-Shillek said nothing for a moment. He had the theatrical habit — I had observed it before, though never in circumstances like these — of allowing silence to do work that most men would fill with words.

Then he said: “On the second day of our survey of this anchorage, you recorded filling your water skin at the spring at the second hour of the afternoon.”

“Yes,” Bar-Anat said.

“You drew six portions from the water jars that day, not five. One more than the other four men combined. One more than a man who had drunk from the spring would need.”

A longer silence. The sea moved against the pale rock of the headland, patient and repetitive.

“The sixth portion,” Baal-Shillek continued, “went to a man who was not in my landing record. A man who came down from the eastern ridge, which you had surveyed that morning — surveyed, I think, with particular attention to its lines of sight — and whom you provided with water from the jar because providing him with water from the spring would have brought him into camp and into visibility.” He looked at Bar-Anat with the expression of a physician reading symptoms. “You were not on the ridge to survey. You were there to receive someone. Kuru-Baal saw something that night — a signal, a meeting, perhaps only a shadow where no shadow should be — and you had no choice left but the choice you made.”

Bar-Anat said nothing. But his hands told the answer clearly enough, and Eshmunar, to his credit, was watching them.

“Who sent you?” Baal-Shillek asked. Not urgently. As though he were completing a tally.

Bar-Anat looked at the sand. “You will find out regardless,” he said, finally, with the flat resignation of a man who has understood that his particular situation has no improved versions. “The Sidonian factor. They have been two seasons behind us. They needed the western anchorages.”

“The Sidonian factor.” Baal-Shillek repeated it without inflection, as though adding it to an interior ledger. “And Kuru-Baal.”

Bar-Anat’s jaw worked. “He saw the man on the ridge. He came to me at the second watch and said he would speak to you in the morning. I — there was no other —” He stopped.

“No,” Baal-Shillek said. “There was not.”

He turned to Eshmunar, and his voice became the voice he used for practical summaries. “You have your man and his admission. The murder instrument will be among his survey tools — the edge-marks on Kuru-Baal’s skull are the right width for a bronze plotting rule, and Bar-Anat’s rule will have been cleaned, but bronze holds traces in the joints of the handle. Have your sailors search his kit.” He looked once more at Bar-Anat, and if there was anything in his expression beyond the completion of an exercise, I could not identify it. “The Sidonians will have a record of what they purchased from you. That is their way, as it is ours. The merchant council will recover the sum and attribute it to your estate. What they paid for will not reach them now.”

He walked away from the line of men and stood at the water’s edge, looking west along the coast, toward the unnamed distances that his tablets were slowly making legible. I stood beside him because it was where I stood.

“It will reach them eventually,” I said. I was thinking of the man on the ridge. The man who had been given water from a jar and had gone back into the coastal plain and was now walking north with everything he had seen.

“Eventually,” Baal-Shillek agreed. “But eventually is not now, and now is where we are.” He shaded his eyes. The coast ran on into the west, pale and indifferent. “We will complete the survey. We will enter this anchorage in the record under its proper name and with its proper depth-soundings and its spring yield and its holding-ground assessment. We will return to Tyre with a record that is accurate and complete and that does not contain the name of Bar-Anat in any entry that matters.” A pause. “Kuru-Baal’s family will receive his full season’s payment. I will note in my report that he died of a fever. The merchant council does not need to know that one of their scribes kept records that got a man killed.”

I looked at him. “That is not what happened.”

“No. But it is what the record will say, and the record is what survives.” He looked at me with those clear eyes. “You are the scribe, Nahar-Iddin. You know this better than I do.”

He was right. I did know it. Thirty years of understanding that the record and the event are not the same thing, and that the gap between them is where all human complexity lives.

I uncapped my stylus.

“What name,” I asked, “shall I give this anchorage?”

He considered it for a moment, looking at the pale rock headland and the still water in the notch of the bay and the gulls that were beginning, finally, to find Kuru-Baal at the waterline.

“Khet-Baal,” he said. “The hiding place of Baal. It is apt for a location that conceals itself, and for a man who was revealed.”

I wrote it down.

That is the name it carries in the merchant council’s records to this day, and the reason for the name is not recorded anywhere except here. The anchorage is productive. Three trading vessels used it in the season following our return. The water from the spring is good.

Bar-Anat was returned to Tyre in chains and tried before the guild council, whose records note only that he was convicted of the murder of Kuru-Baal, helmsman, by instrument, at an anchorage on the North African coast. The Sidonian factor is not mentioned. The survey is not mentioned. Eshmunar received the official commendation for the resolution of the matter, which was proper and expected.

Baal-Shillek, for his part, returned to his tablets.

Here the primary account ends. A marginal notation in a different hand — tentatively attributed to Nahar-Iddin himself, in a later period, the clay being of slightly different composition — reads as follows:

I have recorded this as it occurred. I do not know whether Baal-Shillek calculated, before we reached Bar-Anat, what Bar-Anat would confess, or whether he was conducting a demonstration of his method and trusted that the truth would confirm it. I believe the former, because in thirty years I have never seen him arrive at a conclusion and be wrong. But he would not tell me. He said only that the record does not lie, if it is kept properly, and that a man who falsifies his record reveals himself by the false entry as surely as by a confession. A record is a promise about what happened. When a man breaks the promise, the break is visible. You must simply know how to look.

This tablet is deposited in the harbour-master’s archive at the request of the navigator Baal-Shillek, who specified that it should be preserved in the practical record alongside his survey reports, as evidence of the method’s completeness. He said: the method must account for human failure as well as natural obstacle. It does.

Nahar-Iddin, scribe, merchant council of Tyre.
