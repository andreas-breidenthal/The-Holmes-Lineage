AKAROS OF SPARTA

~1030 BC — Sparta, Greece

The Dorian migrations had transformed the Peloponnese in the generations before his birth, and Sparta in 1030 BC was a settlement in the process of becoming what it would be — not yet the militarised state of the classical period, but already developing the intensity of communal organisation that would define it. Akaros lived there for a generation, his family’s name resuming its Greek form, and found in the Spartan culture something that both attracted and troubled him: a world that valued directness above all other qualities, that had no patience for the oblique or the ornamental, and that demanded of those who sought its respect a willingness to say, briefly and plainly, exactly what they meant.

He gave it that. Akaros was known in the Spartan community as a man whose assessments of people and situations were brief, accurate, and unadorned — a quality the Spartans valued more than most, having little tolerance for the extended argument. He raised his son in the same tradition of directness, and sent him east to Athens when the boy was old enough to require what Sparta could not provide: the wider intellectual formation that a mind of this inheritance needed if it was to develop its full range.

The account was preserved by Lysandros, a Spartan elder who served as both observer and participant in the matter. His record is laconic, shaped by the cultural discipline of his city, but it retains enough detail to follow the sequence of events. Where his account is sparse, it is because he considered the matter resolved without need for elaboration.

The Death of Diokles

Set down in the thirty-first year of my memory, that it not be lost entirely.

The body of Diokles was found at the gate of the threshing floor before the first watch had changed.

I will say what I observed, because Akaros told me once that a man who cannot say what he saw is useless to any inquiry, and I have no wish to be useless even in a document that no one may read. Diokles lay on his back. His cloak was arranged. His hands were at his sides. There was blood from a wound behind the left ear, and the ground beneath him was dry, though the earth three paces to the north was wet from where the irrigation channel had overflowed in the night.

A helot named Braxus was found sleeping near the storehouse wall not forty feet distant. He had blood on his right hand. The ephors had him seized before the sun had fully cleared the ridge.

This seemed, to everyone present, sufficient.

I had known Akaros for eleven years by this point. He had come to Sparta in the way that useful men sometimes come — not with announcement, not with the self-presentation of the sophist or the petition of the supplicant, but simply by being here one season and present the next, until his presence became unremarkable, and then indispensable. He was lean and angular, with a stillness about him that the younger men sometimes mistook for slowness until they had occasion to revise the judgment abruptly and at personal cost. He spoke seldom. When he spoke, men listened, because the alternative was to miss something that would not be repeated.

He had been in one of his difficult periods in the weeks before this. When his mind had nothing to work upon it turned against itself, and he would sit at the edge of the training ground for hours, watching the young men drill with an expression that was not interest and not boredom but something between them that I had no name for — a machine running without load, generating heat to no purpose. He had taken to spending the nights rearranging the stones of his courtyard into configurations that satisfied some internal geometry I could not follow, and then dismantling them before morning. I had learned not to speak to him during these periods unless I had something that would engage him. I had nothing. Then Diokles died, and the difficulty resolved itself.

He was at the threshing floor before I arrived, though I had come quickly. He was crouched near the body in the way he crouched when thinking — not in any attitude of reverence or examination that another man would recognise as such, but folded at the knees with his forearms resting on his thighs and his eyes moving in a pattern I had long since stopped trying to follow. The air smelled of trampled grain and of the oil from the lamps the ephors had brought, and beneath these, faintly, of the iron-and-salt smell of old blood. The eastern sky was just beginning to separate from the hills.

He did not look up when I arrived.

“The helot,” he said.

“Is already in the cell beneath the gymnasium,” I said. “Theron ordered it.”

“Yes,” he said. “Theron would.”

I waited. Around us the ephors moved with the brisk competence of men who consider a matter settled. Braxus had been found proximate to a dead man with blood on his hand. The inference was mechanical. The ephors were not unintelligent men; they were men whose function required them to process the evident and move toward resolution. I understood this, having been such a man myself before I understood that evident and true are not always the same word.

Akaros rose. He looked at the body for another moment. Then he looked at the wet earth north of the body, and at the dry earth beneath it, and at the gate post, and at the arrangement of the cloak. Then he looked at the position of Braxus’s sleeping place relative to the wall, the gate, and the body. He did all of this with the quality of attention that a man gives to a text he is memorising, and then he turned and walked toward the council house, and I followed, because this was what I did.

The council assembled at midmorning. Theron presented the case with the economy that the occasion seemed to demand. A helot. Blood. Proximity. Resolution proposed: execution at dusk, matter closed, the grain allocation adjusted accordingly.

Akaros stood at the edge of the room. He had the aspect of a man who had arrived early and was waiting for something to finish. When Theron concluded, there was a silence of perhaps four heartbeats.

Then Akaros spoke.

He said four things. I record them as he said them.

First: “The ground beneath Diokles is dry. The ground north of him is wet. A man does not fall where he has not walked, and a man does not walk on dry ground while the ground beside him is already wet unless he arrived before the channel overflowed — which the nightwatch records at the second hour. Diokles died before the second hour. Braxus was seen at the eastern well by two women drawing water at that hour. He was not at the threshing floor.”

He said this without looking at Theron. He said it to no one in particular, which was his habit. He did not raise his voice. A man in the back of the room leaned forward.

Second: “The wound is behind the left ear. A man struck from behind does not arrange his own cloak and lay his own hands at his sides. Someone arranged him. A helot who has killed does not arrange the body — a helot who has killed runs, or hides, or trembles. Arrangement is the act of a man who is familiar with the customs of the dead, and who wished Diokles to be found with dignity. This is not sentiment. This is strategy.”

The room was now quiet in a way that was different from its earlier quiet.

Third: “The blood on the hand of Braxus. His right hand. Braxus lost two fingers of his right hand at the quarry three summers ago. What remained bleeds when he sleeps on it badly. Three men in this room know this. One of them is the man who placed Braxus near the body and relied upon this knowledge.”

I watched the room as he said this. I will not record what I observed in individual faces. That is not the purpose of this account.

Fourth: “Diokles had been moving grain allocation irregularly for fourteen months. A man of good family who trains in the northern cohort had been supplementing his household from this arrangement. When Diokles decided the arrangement had become dangerous to him, the arrangement ended him first. The method required physical strength, intimate knowledge of the grounds, and the ability to move through the settlement after the second watch without question. The grain records are in the lower archive. They will take an hour to retrieve and perhaps a morning to read.”

He stopped. He had said, in total, perhaps two hundred words. He was done.

The grain records were retrieved.

They took less than an hour to read, because Akaros had told us exactly where to look.

The council deliberated for the remainder of the day. I was not present for all of it. What I know is that Braxus was released before dusk, given a blanket and a quantity of figs that was generous by any standard and extraordinary by the standards of what a helot could expect from a council that had nearly executed him that morning. He did not ask for explanation. He was Spartan in his way.

The man of good family — I will not write his name here; this record is laconic by design, and some information exists only to be known, not to be written — was reassigned within the fortnight to a campaign in the north, near the Thessalian border, where the winters are long and the action is intermittent and a man of good family may serve with full honour for many years at considerable distance from Sparta and the lower archive.

I asked Akaros, the evening after the council’s decision, whether this satisfied him.

He was rearranging stones in the courtyard again, though now with a different quality of attention — less furious, more contemplative, the engine running under appropriate load.

“Justice?” he said, not looking up.

“The man lives,” I said. “He will not stand trial. He will not answer.”

Akaros placed a stone.

“Diokles was not innocent,” he said. “The arrangement between them was not innocent. If I lay out every thread of this before the full council and it goes to formal trial, the families of three elders are implicated, two of whom have sons in active service, and the question of the grain supply becomes a public wound at a time when we can afford neither the wound nor the infection.” He placed another stone. “The guilty man has been removed from the position that made him dangerous. He will live in discomfort and reflect, which is more than Diokles is doing. Braxus is alive.”

“And the truth,” I said.

He looked up at me then. His eyes were direct in the Spartan manner, and also something else — something that had no Spartan name because Sparta had never required a word for it.

“You are recording this,” he said. It was not a question.

“I am,” I said.

“Then the truth exists,” he said, and returned to his stones. “It simply exists quietly.”

I record here, finally, what I did not record at the council: that Akaros knew which man before he entered the room. He had known from the threshing floor. The four statements he made were not the process of his reasoning. They were its conclusion, disassembled into steps small enough that a council of Spartan elders could follow them without feeling that they were following.

He gave us the appearance of deduction because we required the appearance. The deduction itself had already occurred, alone, in the early dark, while the rest of us were still deciding that the matter was settled.

I have lived in Sparta my whole life. I have known men who were strong, men who were disciplined, men who were brave in the particular Spartan manner that does not announce itself. I had not known, before Akaros, a man whose mind was itself a weapon — not a sword, which requires an arm, but something more like a catapult: a mechanism that, once set, cannot be stopped, and that hits what it aims at without asking whether the target deserves to be hit.

His son has gone to Athens. I hope Athens is ready for him.

It probably is not.
