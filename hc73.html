SEPTIMIUS HOLCUS

~130 AD — Carthage, North Africa

Bearing the name that would later belong to the African-born emperor Septimius Severus — a coincidence the family chose to regard as prescient — Septimius Holcus followed the family’s old Carthaginian connection back to North Africa, a connection that had been dormant since Publius crossed the sea three centuries before, but had never entirely vanished from the family’s sense of itself.

He served in the proconsular administration of Africa, based at Carthage — not the Punic city that Rome had destroyed in 146 BC, but the Roman colonial city that had risen on its ruins, prosperous, cosmopolitan, and entirely aware of the ghost it was built on. Septimius Holcus administered the grain supply of North Africa with a severity of method that left little room for approximation; he distrusted declared figures until he had tested them against the physical realities that produced them, and preferred the feel of a granary wall or the count of threshing marks to any written assurance.

The account survives in the working journals of Tiberius Cassian, a records clerk attached to the proconsular commission that later investigated the matter. Cassian’s habit was to write more than was required and to keep what he wrote. His record preserves not only the formal findings but the earlier observations of Septimius Holcus, whose reasoning Cassian did not always follow until the conclusion made it unavoidable.

The Matter of Lucius Dardanius

Recorded in the Working Journals of Tiberius Cassian, Clerk to the Proconsular Commission of Africa, in the Consulship of Gaius Junius Severus and Marcus Peducaeus Priscinus

The heat of Carthage in high summer is not the heat of Rome. Rome’s heat is a social event — it drives men to the baths, to the shade of colonnades, to complaint and conversation. Carthage’s heat is something older and less interested in human opinion. It presses down from a sky the colour of unglazed pottery, and it rises back up from streets that have been baking since before the Republic was a thought in anyone’s mind. The city remembers, in its stones if not in its inhabitants, what it was before Rome came with fire to remake it. On the worst afternoons, walking the streets near the old harbours, one had the not entirely comfortable sensation of trespassing on a ruin that had simply declined to lie flat.

It was on such an afternoon — the Ides of July, if my notes are correct, and I believe they are — that the matter of Lucius Dardanius was first brought to the attention of Septimius Holcus.

I had been attached to the proconsular commission for eleven months by that point, and I had learned to recognize the particular quality of stillness that settled over Holcus when a problem interested him. It was not the stillness of rest. Nothing about Septimius Holcus suggested rest. He was a lean man, narrow-shouldered in the way that suggested compressed rather than absent strength, with eyes that were grey or green depending on the quality of the light and a habit of going entirely motionless when his mind was working at speed, as though he had redirected all available energy away from his body and into whatever calculation was running behind his face. On the days when no interesting problem presented itself, he was terrible company — restless, sharp-tongued, given to dismantling perfectly serviceable administrative correspondence and rebuilding it into something that was technically more accurate and practically more offensive. He had twice reduced senior members of the commission to formal complaint. The Proconsul, who valued results over decorum, had each time expressed mild regret and done nothing.

He came from old stock, the Holci — one of those families whose history, if you followed it back carefully enough, arrived eventually at the African coast by a route that predated Rome’s ownership of it. Septimius was not a man who dwelt on ancestry, but I noticed that he had none of the colonial administrator’s subtle condescension toward Carthage, none of the habit of treating its present prosperity as Rome’s gift. He walked its streets as though he knew they had existed before him and would continue to do so.

The charge against Lucius Dardanius had been filed by Marcus Asellius Faber, the regional grain assessor, ten days before the matter reached us.

The allegation was substantial. Dardanius, as supervisor of the storage facility at Oea — one of the principal granaries in the Tripolitanian section of the African supply chain — stood accused of having diverted approximately one thousand modii of wheat over the course of the most recent harvest season. The grain, Faber’s charge stated, had been sold through private intermediaries to merchants operating along the coastal trade routes, with the proceeds divided between Dardanius and persons unnamed. The shortage had been discovered, Faber explained, when his quarterly assessment of the Oea stores revealed a figure substantially below what the harvest records predicted.

It was a clean and serious charge. The Proconsular administration was not indifferent to the grain supply — the African harvest fed a considerable portion of the empire, and a supervisor caught manipulating it could expect to end his career in a lead mine if he was fortunate and in the arena if he was not.

Dardanius was detained. He was fifty-three years old, I was told — a municipal man of modest means who had supervised the Oea facility for nine years without a mark against him. He denied everything with what witnesses described as a kind of bewildered persistence, as though he could not locate the correct argument to make because he could not locate the crime he was supposed to have committed.

Priscus Helvianus, our senior investigator, reviewed the charge and found it well-constructed. The records showed the predicted harvest figures. The assessment showed the shortfall. Dardanius was the man responsible for the facility between those two points in time. The logic, Helvianus told us in the commission’s morning session, was straightforward.

I recorded his assessment carefully. I recorded, equally carefully, the sound Septimius Holcus made in response — not quite a word, not quite a dismissal. Something between the two that managed to convey both.

“You have a comment,” said Helvianus. He had learned to ask rather than wait.

“The harvest figures,” said Holcus. “From whom do they originate?”

“From the regional assessor’s office. The records are Faber’s own.”

“Yes,” said Holcus, and then said nothing further for the remainder of the session.

He left for Oea the following morning, before the sixth hour. He did not invite me to accompany him. I accompanied him regardless, on the basis that my commission required me to maintain a complete record and that a complete record could not be maintained from Carthage. He did not object to this reasoning, which I interpreted as acceptance.

The road to Oea ran southwest along the coast. The sea to our right was the flat grey-green of old bronze, barely moving. The smell of it mixed with the smell of dust and whatever the scrub vegetation released when the sun pressed hard upon it — something resinous and slightly bitter, like an argument made in a language one half-understands.

Holcus rode without conversation for most of the journey, which I had learned to use productively. I reviewed my notes. He appeared to review nothing, but I had observed that he arrived at destinations knowing things about them that he had not appeared to seek out, and I had concluded that the absence of visible activity was not the absence of activity.

He spoke once, near the end of the second day’s travel. We had stopped to water the horses at a small way-station, and he was standing at the edge of the shade looking at the flat agricultural land that spread toward the horizon.

“Cassian,” he said, “what is the function of a declared figure?”

I considered this. “To represent reality,” I said. “To stand in place of a count or a measurement when the count or measurement is not—”

“No,” he said. “That is what it is supposed to do. What does it actually do?”

I thought about this more carefully. “It creates a fixed point,” I said. “A number that the record accepts as true and that subsequent calculations are built upon.”

“Yes,” he said. “And if someone wanted to create a shortage — not a real shortage, but a shortage in the record — what would they need?”

I saw where he was going, and felt faintly embarrassed that I had not arrived there myself. “They would need to control the original figure,” I said. “If the harvest records show more grain than was ever actually stored, then any subsequent assessment will show a shortfall. The shortage would exist in the documents before the grain supervisor ever touched a single modius.”

Holcus said nothing. He was, I had come to understand, not a man who congratulated people for thinking. He simply moved on.

The Oea facility stood at the edge of the town proper, enclosed by a wall of stone that had been repaired and added to in several visible eras, the joins between building periods as readable as growth rings in wood. The gate-hinge was iron, old and poorly maintained; it produced, as we entered, a sound like a stylus dragged across an inadequately smoothed wax tablet — a small ugliness that seemed to set the register for everything that followed.

The facility’s acting administrator — a nervous young man named Petronius who had been managing the facility since Dardanius’s detention — received us with the anxious hospitality of someone who wants very much to be helpful and is not entirely certain what help looks like.

Holcus ignored him comprehensively for the first quarter-hour and walked the facility.

I have seen men walk a granary before. They look at the stores, they count the sacks or measure the piles, they examine the seals on the amphorae. Holcus did all of this, but he did something else as well. He walked the walls. He ran his hand along the stonework at intervals. He examined the floors at the base of the storage bins with a lamp held close, noting the marks of compaction where grain had rested. He was in there for nearly two hours, and the expression on his face throughout was one of patient, slightly disappointed attention, like a physician examining a patient whose symptoms are not yet presenting clearly enough.

When he emerged, he sat on a block of limestone near the entrance and said nothing for a time. Petronius hovered. I wrote.

“The main storage bin,” Holcus said eventually, to no one in particular. “Fifteen feet on each interior wall. Stone floor. Marks of compaction consistent with approximately eight hundred modii maximum sustained load. Do you see?”

“No,” I said honestly.

“The wall at the north end. There is no settling at the base of the mortar above the first three courses. There never has been. The wall has never borne the pressure of grain stacked against it.” He paused. “This facility has a capacity, in practice, of approximately eight hundred modii. Perhaps nine hundred if managed with unusual care. Faber’s harvest records attribute to it a stored figure of just under two thousand.”

The afternoon heat sat on the courtyard like something physical. I wrote, and felt the stylus slick with sweat in my hand.

“The shortage,” said Holcus, “was written into the record before the season began. Dardanius never diverted anything. There was nothing to divert. The grain attributed to this facility in Faber’s figures was never here. It was declared here — attributed here on paper — while being stored, or sold, somewhere else entirely.”

Petronius made a sound like someone who had swallowed something surprising.

“Faber’s assessment,” I said slowly, “confirmed a shortage against figures that Faber himself had set. So the shortage, when it appeared—”

“Could only be attributed to the man in between. The supervisor. Who was, by that point, the only other figure involved.” Holcus stood, brushing limestone dust from his palms. “Dardanius was not incompetent enough to steal in a way that could be traced this easily. He was selected because he was honest and obscure — an honest man with honest records who would have no explanation for a shortage he had never created.”

The question of Faber’s confederates, and the route by which the displaced grain had moved — these were matters for Helvianus and the commission’s investigators, with their warrants and their guards and their access to the port records and the merchants’ registers. I do not believe Holcus was particularly interested in these questions. He had solved the problem that interested him, which was not who stole the grain but whether the grain had been stolen at all.

He wrote a formal summary of his findings — three-quarters of a page, precise and dry, containing the critical measurements and their implications — and submitted it upon our return to Carthage. It was, as documents go, devastating. The granary’s physical dimensions made Faber’s declared figures impossible. The shortage existed only in the record. The record had been created by Faber.

Faber was arrested eight days later. The commission found, in the investigation that followed, evidence that pointed toward a network of diversion that had been operating across three facilities in the Tripolitanian district for at least two years. Dardanius was released. I never met him. I am told he returned to Oea and his work, and that he managed the granary for another seven years without incident.

I asked Holcus, on the evening of our return from Oea, how he had known before we arrived that the problem would be in the declared figures rather than in Dardanius’s conduct.

We were sitting in the lower room of an inn near the harbour that he favoured for its proximity and its wine, which was rough and North African and tasted of the particular limestone of the region. He had been quiet since our return — not the animated, concentrated quiet of active reasoning but the flat quiet of a brain deprived of adequate stimulation, already restless for the next problem. He had been working his way through a set of administrative riddles he kept for exactly these periods — contradictory figures from old census records, logical puzzles constructed from actual cases, things that had no practical application but kept the mechanism moving.

He looked up when I asked the question.

“The charge was too clean,” he said. “A real embezzlement of that scale, executed over an entire season, produces evidence of many kinds — conversations, intermediaries, payments, discrepancies in conduct. Dardanius’s record contained none of these. He had no unusual expenditures, no known associations with traders, no change in behavior that nine years of colleagues could identify. When a crime is perfectly attested by documents and completely unattested by everything else, the documents are wrong.”

“And Faber?”

“Faber filed the original harvest attribution and conducted the final assessment himself. He was, therefore, simultaneously the origin of the expected figure and the author of the discovered shortfall. He was the only man whose testimony did not need to be fabricated, because his testimony was the fabrication.” He set down his wine cup. “I was not certain until I walked the walls. But I expected to find walls that confirmed what the logic required.”

“And if they had not?”

He considered this with the genuine interest of a man who does not find hypotheticals beneath him. “Then I would have been wrong,” he said, “and Dardanius would have been guilty, and I would have said so. The logic does not change what is true. It only proposes what is likely to be true, and invites the physical world to confirm or correct it.”

This struck me as so reasonable, and so entirely unlike the way most men I had known in administrative service approached their conclusions, that I wrote it down in my private journal rather than the official record.

I kept both, as I have always kept both. The official record will go to the archive and credit the commission. This account will go wherever private accounts go. I do not know whether anyone will read it. But Holcus was the kind of man who leaves a gap in the record if the record does not include him, and I have never been comfortable with avoidable gaps.

Lucius Dardanius was innocent. The grain had never been there. The shortage was a shadow cast by a number that should never have been written.

Septimius Holcus walked the walls, measured the stone, and read the truth from a mortar joint that had never been pressed by the weight it was supposed to have borne.

He was annoyed by it, in the end. Not by the crime — the crime was merely a crime — but by the clumsiness of the logic. He had expected something more elegant from a man who had managed a two-year diversion scheme across three facilities.

I believe he would have preferred a worthier problem.

I believe, knowing him as I did, that he was probably already looking for one.

These are the working journals of Tiberius Cassian, clerk to the proconsular commission of Africa. The commission’s formal findings in the matter of the Oea granary deficiency are filed in the records of the proconsular archive, Carthage, under the consulship of Severus and Priscinus. They do not mention Septimius Holcus. They credit the commission. This is correct procedure. This account is not procedure. It is record.
