AKAROS THE YOUNGER

~1420 BC — Athens, Greece

THE MATTER OF THE DROWNED GUARD OF THE LAURION INVENTORY

Set down by Menekles, Scribe of the Second Register, in the service of the Lawagetas of Athens, in the thirty-first year of the wanax

I.

The first thing Akaros the Younger did when he saw the body of the warehouse guard Pellos was smile.

I should explain that smile, because it alarmed me considerably and because understanding it is, I think, the key to understanding the man. It was not the smile of a man pleased by death. It was the smile of a man who has been handed a puzzle of rare quality and recognises it immediately as worthy of his full attention — the smile a craftsman wears when given a piece of flawed bronze that has defeated lesser workers. Private. Anticipatory. Faintly hungry.

The body lay in one of the lower storage rooms near the quay-master's office — a cool space, used in better circumstances for olive oil — and the smell, in the heat of that season, was considerable. Thessippos, who commanded the palace guard, remained near the doorway. I followed Akaros inside because I had been instructed to follow Akaros, and I will not claim I was comfortable doing so. The oil-lamp I carried gave unsteady light, and the shadows moved on the dead man's face in ways I did not enjoy observing.

Akaros crouched beside the body and was silent for a long time. What I saw in him was a quality of attention I had not previously encountered in a living person — complete, almost aggressive concentration, the kind that makes a man temporarily deaf to everything outside its object. He moved nothing. He touched nothing. He simply looked, with those depthless grey eyes that I never entirely became comfortable with, as though the body were a palace tablet written in a script he was in the process of translating.

"His hands," he said, eventually.

I looked at the hands. They were the hands of a man who had drowned — or so I assumed — which is to say I saw nothing informative in them. I said as much.

"His palms are unmarked," Akaros said. He spoke with the patience of a man explaining something elementary to a reasonably intelligent child, which was the mode he generally adopted with me, and which I have chosen to take as a compliment. "A man who drowns in harbour water — where are the marks? He would fight. Not necessarily against a person. Against the water. Against a piling, a rope, a hull-plank — whatever his hands found. The palms of a drowned man, particularly one who does not go willingly, carry the evidence of that final grasping. This man's palms are smooth. The skin is softened by water — he has been in the harbour, that part is true — but there is no trauma."

He turned the hands over. He pressed the skin of the forearms, watching it. He lifted the head with a gentleness that startled me and examined the back of the skull with the tips of his fingers.

"No blow," he said. He set the head down carefully, then did something I had not anticipated: he drew the dead man's lower lip down with his thumb, exposing the inner surface, and held the lamp close. "Look here, Menekles."

I looked. Along the inner lip and on the tongue, where the tissue was palest, there was a faint darkening — not bruising, not decay, but a dry, brownish discolouration of the kind I associated, from the palace dispensary records I had copied, with the residue of concentrated poppy preparation taken in quantity.

"I have seen that notation in the dispensary tallies," I said, before I had fully assembled the thought. "When a physician records a preparation that has — " I stopped.

"Yes," Akaros said quietly. "He was given a great deal of it. He went to sleep and did not wake, and someone then carried him to the water and placed him there to be found, and waited three days before raising the alarm." He stood. "The harbour smell is considerable. But the residue on the tissue does not fade as the external smell does. This is not a thing a man notices by accident. You know it because you have copied the dispensary records. I know it because I have seen it before." He paused. "He was placed in the water after death. He did not die by drowning, and he did not die by violence."

"Someone murdered him."

"Someone arranged for him to be found as a murdered man, which is a somewhat different thing." He looked at me with those grey eyes. "The poppy was a mercy. It also cost money — a quantity sufficient to ensure a man does not wake costs what a skilled craftsman earns in a month, and it requires either a physician's knowledge or a physician's supplier. Whoever arranged this had reach into those channels and was willing to spend on a cleaner end. That tells us something about them." He paused. "Tell me what you know of Dromios."

II.

I should explain, here, how this matter had come before us.

Dromios of the Laurion Consortium had arrived at the Lawagetas's hall that same morning in a state of controlled distress that I had noticed immediately as unusual. A man who has suffered a genuine loss arrives in pieces. Dromios arrived assembled. He was a broad man, heavy through the shoulders, with the careful eyes of a merchant who has spent thirty years watching other men's hands — a man whose commercial record I knew well from the palace tallies, whose weights always honoured their declared measure, whose word in a dispute carried the authority of a man who had never yet been shown wrong.

His account was as follows. He had been accumulating a private reserve of silver against a large purchase planned for the spring — Cretan copper, the finest grade — and this reserve, some forty ingots of standard palace weight, had been stored in a warehouse near the lower harbour, guarded by Pellos, who had served him for eleven years. Three days ago Pellos had not appeared at his post. Two days ago a fisherman had found the body. Yesterday Dromios had gone to the warehouse and found the silver gone — not the chests, not the jars, not the seals, but the silver itself, removed as though one ingot at a time over a long period.

Thessippos had already formed his conclusion before Akaros and I arrived. A harbour man named Eryx — known to move stolen goods, seen near the warehouse district before Pellos's disappearance — had departed Athens on a trading vessel two days prior. The solution was clean and the geometry was satisfying. Thessippos wanted only the Lawagetas's authorisation to send men after the vessel.

Akaros had stood near the hall's eastern column throughout this account, saying nothing. When Dromios and Thessippos had both finished, he asked only: Where is the body now?

III.

Back in the oil-store, I answered his question about Dromios with what I knew — the common knowledge of the commercial district, which was that Dromios had been in Athens twenty years, was prosperous and careful, had a house on the upper slope and two sons in the wool trade. I told him also what was slightly less common knowledge: that the Cretan copper purchase had been spoken of in the warehouses for months, and that the terms had become, within the past season, acutely unfavourable.

"How unfavourable?" Akaros said.

I knew this precisely because I had copied the relevant market intelligence reports as they came through the palace registry. The Cretan suppliers had, six months prior, concluded a separate arrangement with a Rhodian consortium, reducing the available stock of premium grade copper and driving the forward price sharply upward. Dromios had committed at the old price. The gap between what he had agreed to pay and what the copper was now worth in the Athenian market meant that completing the purchase would return him a loss of, at my estimate, twelve to fifteen ingots on the transaction — not ruinous in isolation, but ruinous for a man whose entire commercial standing rested on the reputation of never having miscalculated.

I told Akaros this.

He listened with his eyes half-closed, which was the expression he wore when he was most intensely engaged, though I did not yet know that. When I finished he said: "And what do you know of Eryx?"

Less. Eryx was a marginal figure — a carrier of ambiguous goods, the kind of man who existed in every Aegean harbour and was known to everyone and trusted by no one.

"Who told Dromios that Eryx had been seen near the warehouse?" Akaros asked.

I did not know. I had assumed it was common report.

"Find out," Akaros said. He was already moving toward the door. "And send word to the palace record-room. I want Dromios's inventory declarations for the past eight months — and any commercial transaction records touching the Laurion Consortium's Athenian accounts in the same period."

He stopped at the threshold and looked back at the body with an expression I could not read. "He served eleven years," he said quietly, to no one in particular. Then he walked out into the harbour light.

IV.

I am Menekles. I copy grain tallies and wool assessments and the inventories of the palace storerooms, and the one faculty I possess that occasionally proved useful to Akaros was precisely this: I can read the discrepancy between what a record declares and what the physical evidence of a warehouse actually contains. It is a narrow skill and not a dramatic one, but it is mine, and it was what he had been told when he asked the Lawagetas for a scribe who knew the difference between a number and a fact.

The records arrived before evening. I worked through them at the table in the house near the lower market that I had arranged for Akaros, while he sat on the step outside with a cup of the thin local wine he drank in quantities suggesting less pleasure than occupation — something to do with his hands while his mind moved faster than polite conversation permitted.

The inventory declarations told me the following. Eight months prior, Dromios had declared the warehouse contents as olive oil, wool-fleece, and one item recorded only as metal goods, weight notation, property of the Laurion Consortium. The declaration had been made to the quay-master's registrar in the standard form. I checked whether the registrar had visited. He had not. The declaration had been accepted on Dromios's bond.

The commercial transaction records were more interesting.

"Come and look at this," I said.

Akaros came in from the step and stood behind me. I pointed to a sequence of entries I had found in the secondary ledger — the one that recorded commercial transfers between Athenian trading members and external markets. In the period between four and six months prior, there were three entries recording the transfer of refined metal, trading surplus, Laurion origin through an intermediary factor to the Saronic trading district. The amounts were consistent with perhaps fifteen ingots each. Together, they accounted for forty-three ingots.

"I cannot read the factor's seal," I said. "It is partially damaged. But the transferring party is recorded as acting on behalf of the Laurion Consortium."

Akaros looked at the entries for a long moment. "Three transfers over two months," he said. "Careful. Not so frequent as to draw attention. Not so infrequent as to require moving a large quantity at once." He tapped the third entry. "From the Saronic district, the natural route is to Corinth. From Corinth it enters the broader Aegean trade." He straightened. "He sold it months ago, Menekles. The warehouse has been empty since before Pellos was given the poppy." He looked at me steadily. "We will find the factor's name by morning. When we go to the warehouse tomorrow, I want to show Thessippos what forty ingots of silver leave behind when they are removed — and what they leave behind when they were never there."

He went back to his step and his wine. I went back to the secondary ledger, and by the time the lamp burned low I had found the factor's name among the damaged entries: it was a man who worked exclusively for clients connected to the Laurion Consortium. I wrote it down. In the morning, I showed it to Akaros. He read it, set it aside, and said: "Good. That is the thread. Now we go to the warehouse."

V.

He was right about the warehouse. He was also, I would discover, right to have spoken of Dromios's thoroughness in a tone that contained something uneasy alongside the respect.

The warehouse was a low mudbrick building along the quay, its door-seal broken in the discovery and not reapplied. Inside, a bar of morning light fell through a single high opening across the floor. Akaros walked the perimeter first, running his fingers along the mudbrick, examining the join between wall-base and floor, crouching at each corner. Thessippos watched with the carefully neutral expression of a man who has decided to reserve judgment and finds it effortful.

"Wear," Akaros said, pointing to the floor, where I could see — now that he indicated it — a faint pattern in the dust. Not disordered disturbance, but a systematic regularity, like the traces of repeated, careful movement. "Forty ingots of standard palace weight. Perhaps eighteen, twenty minas each. Moving this quantity produces a characteristic pattern of repeated transit along the same path. This is not recent. The dust has resettled. This pattern is months old."

He examined the storage jars along the wall — olive oil, wool-fleece — and stopped at one marked with a weight notation.

"Open that one," he said to Thessippos.

"That is Dromios's sealed property."

"Yes," Akaros agreed pleasantly.

A pause. Then Thessippos broke the seal.

The jar contained sand. Clean, dry sand, of the kind found on the northern beaches. Packed with care, to weight.

Thessippos stared at it. I stared at it. Akaros had already moved to the doorway, where he examined the door's hinge-pin with the focused attention of a man reading a palace tablet.

"Come and look at this," he said.

The hinge-pin was new bronze, bright against the older patination of the door-plate, and the wood around it was freshly mortised — replaced recently and with care.

"This door has been rehung," Akaros said. "The old hinge was one that could be lifted from the outside without breaking the seal — a common shortcut in a building the owner accesses frequently. It was replaced, carefully and professionally, sometime in the last season. To ensure the official record would show an unbroken seal." He turned back to the room. "He thought of everything," he said, and I heard in his voice something that was not quite admiration and not quite respect, but occupied the space between them. "The sand, the door, the timing of the body's discovery, Eryx as a figure of convenient suspicion — each piece placed with intelligence." He paused. "Except the hands."

Thessippos wanted to arrest Dromios that afternoon. The impulse was understandable — a man of clear lines, confronted with evidence that had redrawn them all, reaches for the clearest line available.

It was at this point that the message arrived.

VI.

A boy brought it to the warehouse door — a folded piece of unbaked clay, soft enough to carry an impression but not set, a scribe's working tablet of the disposable kind. The message was in a commercial hand I did not recognise, and it was addressed to me by name.

The man from Mycenae would be wise to conclude his business and return home. The Laurion Consortium has friends in this city whose patience is not limitless. A scribe who copies the wrong things has been known to find his records questioned, his bond challenged, his position reviewed. These are not threats. They are observations, offered in a spirit of goodwill.

I looked up. Akaros was watching me with an expression I could not immediately classify. He had already read it over my shoulder.

"Give it to me," he said.

He turned it in his hands, examining the clay, the pressure of the stylus, the commercial hand. "Written quickly, but not in haste. He had this prepared." He looked at the doorway, at the boy, who had already gone. "He knew we were here. He has been watching this warehouse since the body was found." He handed the tablet to Thessippos. "Keep this."

For the first time since the matter had begun, Thessippos looked genuinely unsettled.

Akaros, by contrast, looked exactly as he had looked at the body of Pellos: as though something of rare quality had just announced itself.

VII.

That evening, a man came to the house near the lower market.

He came after dark, and he came alone, which was either bravery or a miscalculation of the kind that confident men make when they have spent twenty years being proved right. He was one of Dromios's factors — I recognised him from the harbour warehouses, a lean man named Sostias who handled the physical movement of goods and who had, I now understood, probably been the instrument by which Pellos had been given the poppy and carried to the water. He knocked at the door with the deliberate force of a man who wishes to be heard and not to seem afraid of being heard.

I opened it. He looked at me with the flat attention of a man who has been sent to deliver a message and intends to deliver it regardless of reception.

"My employer wishes to speak with the man from Mycenae," he said. "Tonight. At the warehouse."

"Your employer," I said, "may speak with him here."

Sostias's eyes moved past me to where Akaros sat at the table with his wine and his family's correspondence spread before him — a sealed chest of records, accumulated across Egypt and Ugarit and the Mycenaean heartland, the observation of a century deployed generation by generation in a dozen trading cities. He looked up at Sostias with those grey eyes.

"At the warehouse," Akaros said. "Yes, very well."

I began to say that this seemed profoundly inadvisable, and he spoke over me with the quiet firmness he used when a decision had been made and further discussion was a use of time he declined to make.

"Bring your stylus," he said. "And the secondary ledger."

He said nothing else. He did not mention Thessippos. I did not know, as we walked out into the dark harbour district behind Sostias, whether anyone knew where we were going.

VIII.

I will set down what happened in the warehouse that night as faithfully as I can, though the lamp was poor and my hands were not entirely steady and at least one moment of it I would prefer not to reconstruct too precisely.

Dromios was there when we arrived. He stood at the centre of the room — the same room, the same bar of light now replaced by the orange unsteadiness of two oil-lamps set on the storage jars — and he was not the man I had seen in the Lawagetas's hall. That man had been controlled, assembled, performing grief with professional precision. This man was still controlled, but the control was of a different kind — the control of a man who has decided that one problem can be solved by creating a larger one, and who has fully committed to that decision.

He looked at Akaros for a moment. Then he said: "You are a trader's agent from Mycenae. A man of no administrative standing in this city. What you believe you have found here will not survive contact with what I can demonstrate. My records are complete. My bond is unimpeached. My word in this community is worth more than the testimony of a foreign agent and a junior scribe." He paused. "Go home."

"That is a thorough assessment," Akaros said, with the equanimity of a man discussing grain prices. "It has only one difficulty."

I heard Sostias move behind me — not a dramatic movement, but a quiet, purposeful repositioning. When I turned, he was between us and the door. His hands were open at his sides, which is the position a man's hands occupy when he wishes them to be available at short notice.

The lamplight was poor. The warehouse smelled of old grain and harbour water. There was no sound from outside.

Dromios said: "The difficulty."

"The difficulty," Akaros said, "is this." He reached inside his robe and produced a clay tablet — not the threat tablet, which Thessippos held, but the secondary ledger page I had copied that afternoon, the one bearing the three transfer entries and the damaged factor's seal, the factor's name legible in the last entry, the quantities consistent with forty-three ingots of refined silver moved through the Saronic district across two months. He set it on the storage jar nearest to Dromios. "Menekles found this in the palace secondary ledger this evening. The Consortium's own commercial record, filed in the ordinary course, by your own factor, in the period during which the silver was supposedly sitting in this room." He let the silence hold for a moment. "The factor's name is recorded. The transfer quantities are recorded. The destination market is recorded." Another pause. "From the Saronic district, the route to Corinth is straightforward. From Corinth it enters the broader Aegean trade. I wrote, this afternoon, a letter to the Lawagetas's record-room describing where the silver will surface and when. That letter is sealed and deposited. If the silver arrives in Corinth with clean provenance, the matter is concluded. If it does not, the letter waits."

Dromios looked at the tablet. He looked at it for a long time.

"Your word," he said, finally, "and a scribe's reading of a damaged entry."

"And Thessippos," Akaros said.

I had not heard them arrive. I do not know, even now, precisely when they entered — whether they had been waiting in the dark outside since before Sostias knocked on Akaros's door, or whether they had followed us through the harbour district at a distance. Thessippos and two of his men were in the doorway. Sostias had moved aside without being asked.

The lamps moved in a breath of harbour air from the high opening. The room was very quiet.

Then Dromios said something I had not expected. He said: "He served me for eleven years."

"I know," Akaros said.

"I could not — " He stopped. The control had shifted, or perhaps it had simply become honest control rather than performed control. "The poppy was not nothing."

"No," Akaros said. "It was not nothing. It was a mercy inside a murder, and I will remember both of those things when I speak to the Lawagetas." He paused. "As will you, I think."

IX.

I sat with Akaros on the step of his house afterward, in the last of the night heat, the harbour smell drifting up through the lower market's quiet. He had the wine, as usual. He said almost nothing for a long time — not the taut silence of a thinking man but the quieter silence of one who has finished thinking and arrived somewhere he did not entirely enjoy.

He explained, in the following days, what was said to the Lawagetas, and what Dromios would do.

He would withdraw his claim, citing a re-examination of his records that had revealed an error in accounting. He would make a donation to the sanctuary of the relevant deity — the amount Akaros specified was substantial — in acknowledgment of his relief that no crime had occurred. He would arrange for Pellos's family to receive, from an unnamed source, a provision that would sustain them for the remainder of the mother's life. And he would know — clearly, without any ambiguity — that he was known. The silver's movement was on record. Akaros's letter sat in the record-room. If the ingots surfaced cleanly, the matter was concluded. If they did not, the thread could be pulled.

Thessippos asked what form public accountability would take.

Akaros said: "He has surrendered the profit. He has provided for the family of the man he killed. He will spend the remainder of his commercial life in this city knowing that one man holds the complete account of what he did, and that account can be placed before the Lawagetas in an hour. That is not nothing, Thessippos. That is not justice, either. The distinction is uncomfortable and real, and I make no attempt to dissolve it."

Thessippos was quiet for a long time after this.

Eryx's vessel came back on its own schedule. The harbour gossip had moved to something else. He returned to the city entirely unaware he had ever been a figure in the story at all.

X.

What I know, though it is not a matter of record, is that the silver — all forty ingots — surfaced in the Corinthian market with clean provenance some months later, exactly where and when Akaros had written that it would. He had told me to look for it. He was, again, correct.

The official record of the matter of the Laurion inventory notes only that a review of accounting procedures identified an error, that no crime had occurred, and that the case was closed. Thessippos received commendation for his prompt and efficient handling of the situation.

I have been asked, in the years since, to describe Akaros the Younger, and I find that I always return to the warehouse that night — Dromios standing in the lamplight with all his careful architecture coming apart around him, Sostias moving to block the door, and the moment before Thessippos arrived when the room was very quiet and I was not certain what Dromios had decided to do. Akaros stood throughout this with the complete stillness of a man who has placed his pieces and is waiting to see whether the board agrees with his reading of it. He did not look afraid. He did not look certain, either. He simply looked, with those grey eyes, entirely present.

He had the quality, which I have encountered in no other person, of making you feel that you were thinking slightly less clearly than was possible for you — that the gap between what you understood and what he understood was not a permanent condition but a temporary inadequacy that more attention might address. This was not unkind. He never made me feel stupid. He made me feel that stupidity was a problem I could solve, which is a different thing, and in some ways a more unsettling one.

He ate very little during a case and slept at irregular hours. He was a Mycenaean by origin — his father's representative in the Athenian trading community, arrived with a family archive of considerable age, accumulated across Egypt and Ugarit and the Mycenaean heartland. He carried that archive in a sealed chest wherever he went and spoke of it with something that was not quite reverence but was the closest he came to it — the accumulated sight of a century and more, a family that had trained itself, generation by generation, to look at what was actually there. When there was no case before him he was worse company — restless and slightly hollow, as though the part of him that animated the rest had gone elsewhere and left the body to manage as best it could.

He stayed in Athens seven more years after the matter of Dromios and the silver, and I was present for four further cases that I have also attempted to record, with the same inadequacies of understanding that characterise this account. He then followed a trade report north, toward the Thessalian coast, and I did not see him again, though I received once a clay tablet in a hand I did not recognise containing only a weight calculation and a phrase that referred, I believe, to a matter I had not been present for.

This account is placed in the palace records at his instruction and with the Lawagetas's knowledge, though not under any pressure from that administration. It is for whoever comes after, who will know how to read it.

But I will say this, before I close, because it is the thing Thessippos never asked and I never answered aloud: there were nights, in the years that followed, when I thought about Pellos. About the fact that a careful, intelligent man had judged it sufficient that his killer pay for a sanctuary donation and a dead man's mother's bread, and had framed that judgment in language so measured and so clear that even the commander of the palace guard had eventually, reluctantly, accepted it. Akaros was right that it was not nothing. He was right that it was not justice.

I have never been certain those are the only two options.

Written in the thirty-second year of the wanax, in the month of the new grain.

Menekles, Scribe of the Second Register.
