AKAROS THE YOUNGER

~1420 BC — Athens, Greece

He followed the trade routes north and west from Mycenae to Athens — a secondary palace-centre in the Mycenaean period, less rich and less powerful than Mycenae but active, its position on the Attic peninsula giving it access to the silver of Laurion and the trade of the Saronic Gulf. Athens in 1420 BC had not yet become the city it would be; it was a palace on a rock, a wanax with his warriors, a community managing the agricultural surplus of Attica and the trade dealings of the Aegean. Akaros the Younger arrived as his father’s representative in the Athenian trading community and stayed as his own man, as the family’s members had stayed in places across the breadth of the eastern Mediterranean world — reading the new context quickly, establishing a position within its particular economy of information and trust, and finding, within a few years, the local expression of the pattern that had been following the family, or that the family had been following, since Egypt.

He is called the Younger to distinguish him from his father, and in every way that the records allow us to assess, he was his father’s equal and his father’s student — the same cast of mind, the same facility with multiple languages, the same quality of attention that made him, in every room he entered, the person who understood most completely what was actually happening. He had absorbed the family archive and its century and a half of accumulated observation, and he brought to Athens the additional advantage of knowing what he was looking for before he began looking.

The record that follows survives in the hand of the scribe Menekles, attached to the Athenian palace administration. Menekles was diligent and exact in copying commercial and administrative matters, yet he was not trained to interpret the subtleties of technical inspection, and at several points in his account he notes his uncertainty as to why Akaros attended to certain details that seemed peripheral at the time. His record preserves both the sequence of events and the limits of his own understanding.

The Matter of the Drowned Guard of the Laurion Inventory

Set down by Menekles, Scribe of the Second Register, in the service of the Lawagetas of Athens, in the thirty-first year of the wanax

The air around the harbour district in the hot months is a thing a man does not quickly forget. Salt-rot and old fish-brine and the particular sweetness of cedar resin sweating out of the timber stacked along the quayside — this was the air of Athens in the season when the matter of Dromios and the Laurion silver came before the palace administration, and it is the air I associate, even now, many years later, with the first time I watched Akaros the Younger look at a dead man and begin, very quietly, to smile.

I should explain that smile before I explain anything else, because it alarmed me greatly when I first encountered it and because understanding it is, I think, the key to understanding the man. It was not the smile of a man who was pleased by death. It was the smile of a man who had been handed a puzzle of a particular and rare quality, and who recognised it immediately as worthy of his attention. Akaros the Younger smiled at the drowned body of the warehouse guard Pellos in the same way a craftsman smiles when he is given a piece of flawed bronze that has defeated lesser workers — with a kind of private, anticipatory pleasure.

I am Menekles. I copy grain tallies and wool assessments and the inventories of the palace storerooms. I am not a man given to narrative, and I ask the reader's patience for the inadequacies of this account, which I set down at Akaros's own instruction, and which he read through only once before returning it to me without comment. He did not correct it. He may have found the inaccuracies too numerous to address, or he may have considered them of no consequence. I cannot determine which, and that uncertainty is itself, I think, characteristic of the man.

The matter came to the palace administration on the second day after the new moon, when Dromios of the Laurion Consortium arrived at the Lawagetas's hall in a state of controlled distress that I noted at the time as unusual. A man who has suffered a genuine loss arrives in pieces. Dromios arrived assembled.

He was a broad man, heavy through the shoulders, with the careful eyes of a merchant who has spent thirty years watching other men's hands. He traded in silver — not the raw ore, which was the wanax's concern, but the refined and weighed ingots as they passed from the smelters into commercial circulation. He had, by his own account, been accumulating a private reserve of silver against a large purchase he intended to make in the spring — Cretan copper, the finest grade, which required substantial advance payment. This reserve, amounting to some forty ingots of standard palace weight, had been stored in one of the warehouse spaces near the lower harbour, guarded by a man named Pellos, who had been in his service for eleven years.

Three days ago, he said, Pellos had not appeared at his usual post. Two days ago, a fisherman had found the body of a man floating in the shallower water north of the main quay. The body had been identified by Pellos's brother as Pellos. And when Dromios had gone to inspect the warehouse, the silver was gone — not the chests, not the storage jars, not the seals, but the silver itself, as though it had been removed one ingot at a time over a considerable period and replaced with nothing.

The Lawagetas had called for Thessippos, who commanded the palace guard and who handled such matters in the ordinary course. Thessippos was a capable soldier who understood crime as a variant of military strategy: establish the enemy, assess his resources, pursue. He had concluded, rapidly and with professional confidence, that the killer and thief was a harbour man named Eryx, who was known to move stolen goods, who had been seen near the warehouse district in the days before Pellos's disappearance, and who had, with unfortunate convenience, left Athens on a trading vessel two days prior. He wanted authorisation to send men to intercept this vessel.

It was, as I recall it, a clean and satisfying account. Thessippos was pleased with it. Dromios received it with the careful gravity of a man absorbing a painful truth.

Akaros the Younger, who had been standing near the hall's eastern column since the interview began, said nothing until both men were finished. Then he said: "Where is the body now?"

They had laid Pellos in one of the lower storage rooms near the quay-master's office — a cool space, used in better circumstances for olive oil — and the smell was, in the heat of that season, considerable. Thessippos remained near the doorway. Dromios did not come at all, having pleaded the distress of his loss. I followed Akaros inside because I had been told to follow Akaros, and I will not claim that I was comfortable doing so.

Akaros crouched beside the body for a long time without speaking. The oil-lamp I carried gave unsteady light, and the shadows moved on the dead man's face in a way I did not enjoy observing. But I watched Akaros, because watching Akaros was more instructive than watching the body, and what I saw was the quality of attention I mentioned before — a complete, almost aggressive concentration, the kind that makes a man temporarily deaf to everything outside its object.

"His hands," Akaros said eventually.

I looked at the hands. They were the hands of a man who had drowned, or so I assumed, which is to say that I saw nothing informative in them. I said as much.

"His palms are unmarked," Akaros said. "A man who drowns in harbour water — where are the marks?"

I did not understand.

"He would fight," Akaros said, with the patience of someone explaining something elementary to a bright child, which was the mode he generally adopted with me and which I have chosen to take as a compliment. "Not necessarily against a person. Against the water. Against a piling, a rope, a hull-plank — whatever his hands found. The palms of a drowned man, particularly one who does not go willingly, carry the evidence of that final grasping. This man's palms are smooth. The skin is softened by water — he has been in the harbour, that part is true — but there is no trauma."

He turned the hands over. He pressed the skin of the forearms, watching it. He lifted the head with a gentleness that startled me and examined the back of the skull with the tips of his fingers.

"No blow," he said. "He was placed in the water after death, but he did not die by drowning, and he did not die by violence."

"Then how?" I asked.

Akaros set the head down carefully. He remained crouching, looking at the face with an expression I can only describe as professional sorrow — not grief, but the acknowledgment that something sad had occurred. "Poppy," he said. "The smell is faint now, and the harbour smell is considerable, but it is there. He was given a great deal of it. He went to sleep and did not wake, and someone then carried him to the water and placed him there to be found, and waited three days before placing the alert."

"Someone murdered him."

"Someone arranged for him to be found as a murdered man, which is a somewhat different thing. The poppy was a mercy." He stood. He looked at me for a moment with those depthless grey eyes that I never entirely became comfortable with. "Tell me what you know of Dromios."

I told him what I knew, which was the common knowledge of the commercial district — that Dromios had been in Athens for twenty years, that he was prosperous and careful and had a reputation for honouring his weights, that he had a house on the upper slope and two sons in the wool trade. I told him also what was slightly less common knowledge: that Dromios's Cretan copper purchase had been spoken of in the warehouses for months, and that the price he had agreed was aggressive, and that there were men who thought he had overcommitted.

Akaros listened to all of this with his eyes half-closed, which was the expression he wore when he was most intensely engaged, though I did not know that yet. When I finished, he said: "And what do you know of Eryx?"

I knew less. Eryx was a marginal figure — a carrier of ambiguous goods, the kind of man who existed in the harbour district of every Aegean port and was known to everyone and trusted by no one.

"Who told Dromios that Eryx had been seen near the warehouse?" Akaros asked.

I did not know. I had assumed it was common report.

"Find out," Akaros said. And then, in a different register, quieter and more private: "The silver was never in the warehouse."

He explained it to me that evening, sitting on the step of the house I had arranged for him near the lower market, in the last of the daylight, with a cup of the thin local wine he drank in quantities that suggested less pleasure than occupation — something to do with his hands when his mind was moving faster than polite conversation permitted.

"A man who is genuinely robbed," he said, "does not control the time of discovery. The loss is discovered when it is discovered — by the thief's departure, by the guard's absence, by the chance inspection. Dromios waited three days. Why three days?"

"To allow Eryx to depart," I offered, which was Thessippos's theory restated.

"To allow decomposition," Akaros said. "Three days in harbour water, in this heat. The body becomes... more generally a body. Harder to examine with precision. The poppy is harder to smell. The detail of the unmarked palms becomes, to a less attentive observer, simply the hands of a drowned man." He paused. "Dromios is a careful planner. He planned to the day."

"But why?"

"Because the Cretan purchase will ruin him if he completes it. The price he agreed was formed on intelligence that has since proven false — the Cretan market has moved. He is committed by his own word, and in this community his word is his value, and to withdraw would be to cease to exist commercially. So he does not withdraw. He reports a loss. He reports a loss equal, by remarkable coincidence, almost precisely to the advance payment he owes. He collects from the palace the administrative recognition of the loss — which is not repayment, we have no mechanism for repayment, but which releases him from the obligation to complete the purchase on the grounds of calamity beyond his control. The silver was never in the warehouse. It has been in his house, or in the house of a kinsman on the mainland, since before Pellos was given the poppy."

"And Pellos?"

Akaros was quiet for a moment. The lamp in the doorway behind us guttered in a breath of harbour wind, and the smell of salt and cedar came through the evening heat.

"Pellos knew what the warehouse did and did not contain," he said. "He had served Dromios for eleven years and was trusted accordingly. He could not be permitted to speak when the investigation began. But Dromios — and this I do believe — could not bring himself to commission a violent death for a man who had served him loyally. The poppy was a choice. An expensive choice, requiring a physician or someone with a physician's knowledge, and a decision to spend that money on a cleaner end. It does not mitigate what was done. But it is there."

I sat with this for a moment. "And Eryx?"

"Eryx was seen near the warehouse because Dromios arranged for him to be seen there, and arranged for the report of it to reach Thessippos's ears through the ordinary channels of harbour gossip. Eryx is probably entirely ignorant of his role in this. He is a useful shape — the kind of man a story needs, the kind that everyone already half-believes guilty of something."

The following morning Akaros went to the warehouse, and I went with him, and Thessippos came because he had decided that accompanying Akaros was more professionally prudent than not accompanying him, even though he had also decided that Akaros was wrong and that Eryx's vessel should be intercepted at once.

The warehouse was a low mudbrick building, one of four or five along that section of quay, smelling of old grain and the harbour. The door-seal had been broken in the discovery and not reapplied. Inside, the light came through a single high opening and lay in a bar across the floor.

Akaros walked the perimeter of the room first, which was not what I had expected. He ran his fingers along the mudbrick, examined the join between the wall-base and the floor, crouched at each corner. Thessippos watched this with the carefully neutral expression of a man who has decided to reserve judgment and finds it effortful.

"What are you looking for?" Thessippos asked, after some time.

"Wear," Akaros said. He pointed to the floor. "Forty ingots of standard palace weight. Perhaps eighteen, twenty minas per ingot. This is not a quantity a single man moves in an evening. Moving it produces — " he gestured at the floor, where I could see, now that he indicated it, a faint pattern in the dust: not disordered disturbance, but a systematic regularity, like the traces of repeated careful movement. "This is not recent. This pattern is months old. The dust has resettled."

He went to the wall where the storage jars stood and examined each in turn. The jars were sealed, their contents declared by the marks on their shoulders.

"What do these say?" he asked me.

I read them. Olive oil, wool-fleece, and one jar marked simply with a weight notation that I assumed referred to metal.

"Open that one," Akaros said to Thessippos, indicating the weight-marked jar.

Thessippos looked at him. "That is Dromios's sealed property."

"Yes," Akaros agreed pleasantly.

There was a pause. Then Thessippos broke the seal.

The jar contained sand. Clean, dry sand, of the kind found on the northern beaches. Packed with care, to weight.

Thessippos stared at it. I stared at it. Akaros did not stare at it, because he had already looked at everything he needed to look at and had moved to the doorway, where he stood examining the hinge-pin of the door with the focused attention of a man reading a palace tablet.

"The hinge," he said. I came to look. The hinge-pin was new bronze, bright against the older patination of the door-plate, and the wood around it was freshly mortised — replaced recently, and replaced with care. "This door has been rehung. The old hinge was, I think, one that could be lifted from the outside without breaking the seal. A common enough shortcut in a building that the owner accesses frequently. It was replaced — carefully, professionally — some time in the last season. To ensure that the official record would show an unbroken seal."

He turned back to the room. The bar of light had shifted with the morning's progress and now lay across the wall rather than the floor, illuminating the texture of the mudbrick with a low, raking angle.

"He is a thorough man," Akaros said, and I heard in his voice something that was not quite admiration and not quite respect, but occupied the space between them. "The sand, the door, the timing of the body's discovery, the introduction of Eryx as a figure — each piece placed with intelligence. He thought of everything." He paused. "Except the hands."

Thessippos wanted to arrest Dromios that afternoon. I think this impulse is understandable — he had been redirected from one conclusion to another, his professional efficiency had been demonstrated as insufficient, and arrest is a satisfying gesture, a clear line drawn. He was a man of clear lines.

Akaros sat with him in the Lawagetas's outer hall and spoke to him for a considerable time, and I copied what was said as faithfully as I could, though the exact form of Akaros's argument I cannot fully reconstruct. What I recall is this: that Akaros laid out the question of what a formal process would require. Witnesses to the silver's existence. Records of its deposit. Dromios's record-keeping was meticulous and would show nothing that contradicted him; the palace's own tallies would show only what Dromios had reported. The drowned man could be demonstrated, by someone with the relevant knowledge, to have been given poppy — but demonstration to the standard that the Lawagetas's administration required was a different thing from the knowledge of a Mycenaean trading-house agent. Eryx could be produced, examined, and demonstrated to be innocent, but Dromios could claim that Eryx had been previously known to him as suspicious, that his suspicion was honest and his report well-intentioned. A formal process would take months. It would expose the palace administration's commercial oversight to scrutiny that would be, Akaros noted gently, uncomfortable for several parties.

And — this was the part that I found most remarkable, and which I am least certain I have reconstructed accurately — Akaros said something about the Cretan purchase. He said that the Cretan market had indeed moved, and that the intelligence Dromios had acted on had been, in his assessment, honest intelligence that had simply proven wrong. He said that Dromios had built his position in Athens over twenty years with reasonable integrity, and that the Laurion Consortium, which employed many men and moved considerable commerce through the palace's quays, would be disrupted significantly by a public process against its most senior Athenian member. He said that the Lawagetas had, in his view, more to gain from a private resolution.

Thessippos asked what form a private resolution would take.

Akaros said that Dromios would withdraw his claim. He would cite, if asked, a re-examination of his records that revealed an error in accounting. He would make a donation to the sanctuary of the relevant deity — Akaros suggested the amount, which was substantial — in acknowledgement of his relief that no crime had in fact occurred. He would arrange for Pellos's family to receive, from an unnamed source, a provision that would sustain them for the remainder of the mother's life. And he would, Akaros said, know — clearly and without any ambiguity — that he was known.

Thessippos was quiet for a long time after this. I could see him calculating — not, I think, the ethics, but the administrative reality that Akaros had laid before him. Eventually he said: "And Eryx's vessel?"

"Let it come back on its own schedule," Akaros said. "Eryx has done nothing. The harbour gossip that named him will have moved on to something else by the time he returns. He will not even know he was a figure in this."

I accompanied Akaros to Dromios's house the following morning. I do not know everything that was said, because Akaros spoke with Dromios privately for a period, and when he emerged his face was as composed as it always was, giving away nothing that was not intended for giving.

What I know is that Dromios, within the week, filed the corrected accounting. The donation reached the sanctuary. I do not know what became of the provision for Pellos's family, but I believe it was made; Akaros was, in my experience, precise about the commitments he extracted.

What I also know, though it is not a matter of record, is that the silver — all forty ingots — was found some months later, in a legitimate transaction, in the Corinthian market, having passed through channels that I could trace, if I chose to trace them, back to a warehouse in the Saronic trading district. Akaros had told me to look for it. He had told me where it would likely surface. He was, again, correct.

I have been asked, in the years since, to describe Akaros the Younger, and I find that I always return to the same evening — the step of his house, the thin wine, the last of the harbour light. He was not a comfortable companion. He had the quality, which I have since encountered in no other person, of making you feel, in his presence, that you were thinking slightly less clearly than was possible for you, that the gap between what you understood and what he understood was not a permanent feature of existence but a temporary inadequacy that more attention might address. This was not unkind. He never made me feel stupid. He made me feel that stupidity was a problem I could solve, which is a different thing and, in some ways, a more unsettling one.

He ate very little during a case and slept at irregular hours. He spoke several languages with a fluency that he deployed so naturally that I sometimes forgot he had not grown up in Athens — Mycenaean Greek, of course, but also the Cretan dialect that he had learned from his father's archive, and enough of the Levantine trading tongue to conclude a negotiation without intermediaries. When there was no case before him, he was worse company — restless and slightly hollow, as though the part of him that animated the rest had gone elsewhere and left the body to manage as best it could.

He collected nothing. He owned, as far as I could determine, two changes of clothing, a set of weights and measures, and a small archive of his family's correspondence that he carried in a sealed chest wherever he went. He spoke of this archive with something that was not quite reverence but was the closest he came to it — the records of a century and more of the same quality of attention, deployed in Egypt and Ugarit and the Mycenaean heartland, the accumulated sight of a family that had trained itself, generation by generation, to look at what was actually there.

He stayed in Athens seven more years after the matter of Dromios and the silver, and in that time I was present for four further cases that I have also attempted to record, with the same inadequacies of understanding that characterise this account. He then followed a trade report north, toward the Thessalian coast, and I did not see him again, though I received, once, a clay tablet in a hand I did not recognise that contained only a weight calculation and a single phrase that I believe referred to a case I had not been present for.

I include this account in the palace records at his instruction and with the Lawagetas's knowledge, though not, I should note, under any pressure from the Lawagetas's administration. The official record of the matter of the Laurion inventory notes only that a review of accounting procedures identified an error, that no crime had occurred, and that the case was closed. Thessippos received commendation for his prompt and efficient handling of the situation.

This is as it should be. The account above is for whoever comes after, who will know how to read it.

Written in the thirty-second year of the wanax, in the month of the new grain.

Menekles, Scribe of the Second Register.
