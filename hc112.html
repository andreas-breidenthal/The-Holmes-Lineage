HENRY HOLMES

~1379 — Yorkshire, England

Henry Holmes dropped the Norman “de” from the family name — a small act that marked a large shift. The Holmes family was English now, rooted in English soil, operating within English law, and bearing an English name without qualification or prefix. The fourteenth century had made them so, as it had made so much else: the plague, the wars, the slow erosion of the French connection that the Conquest had imposed, had all worked together to produce a North Riding gentry family indistinguishable, to the eye, from a dozen others in the same county. The Chronicler notes that Henry did not mark this change as a break with the past, but as the natural continuation of it.

The distinction lay in the mind. The Lollard controversy was burning through the Church in Henry’s time, and the question of where a man stood on Wycliffe and the reforming party was not merely theological but potentially fatal — Henry IV had made heresy a capital offence in 1401, and the flames at Smithfield were not metaphorical. Henry Holmes kept the family out of it entirely, which required not passivity but active, daily intelligence — the reading of which neighbours were observed, which conversations were reported, which enthusiasms were safe to express and in whose company. He understood the dangers of his moment with a precision that his descendants would have recognised immediately, even across five centuries. The Chronicler observes that his caution was not fear, but control — a discipline applied continuously, not intermittently.

The record of what follows survives through the notes of Thomas Rede, a cleric of modest rank who acted as both witness and scribe in the matter. Rede was a careful man, trained in the habits of ecclesiastical record, but unaccustomed to the sort of attention he received when placed in Henry’s presence. His account reflects both his diligence and his unease, particularly where he did not fully understand the implications of his own observations.

The Affair of the Ellershawe Sike

Set down by Thomas Rede, Cleric, in the Year of Our Lord 1402

Of the several matters in which I served as witness to Henry Holmes during the winter of his thirty-eighth year, the affair of the Ellershawe Sike is the one I am most reluctant to set down and most compelled to record. I write in retrospect, as I always must, and I warn the reader of what I have learned through long association with that singular man: I am a reliable recorder of what I saw, and an unreliable interpreter of what it meant. The gap between those two things is, I have come to understand, precisely where Henry lived.

The winter of 1401 into 1402 was a cold one, and not merely in the matter of weather. The Act De Heretico Comburendo had passed through Parliament the previous year and had settled over the country like frost over standing water — silent, invisible, and lethal once tested. The burning at Smithfield was known to every man in the North Riding, and most men had drawn from it the reasonable conclusion that theological enthusiasm, of whatever variety, was a luxury they could no longer afford. Fear does not require particularity. It requires only the knowledge that fires, once lit, do not inquire carefully after the precise nature of a man's opinions before they proceed.

It was in this season — the ground iron-hard, the dykes sheeted with grey ice, the smoke from the village hearth-fires lying low and still in the windless air — that Gilbert Maunsell came to Thornfield.

I was at my writing when I heard the horse in the yard below, and I record now what I observed from the upper window before descending, because Henry later required me to account for it precisely.

The animal was a good roan cob, well-kept but road-tired. It was dark with sweat across the shoulders and withers, suggesting hard riding over some distance. More notable to me at the time—though I confess I attached no particular significance to it—was the absence of any second horse, or even his usual groom, the lad Wat. A man of Maunsell's station did not ordinarily ride alone on winter roads. When I later remarked upon the boy's absence, Maunsell’s face darkened. "The lad has spent too much time of late loitering near the merchant-stalls in the borough," he said. "I’ll have no magpies privy to my errands." I attributed this to the irritability of a man under strain and left the matter there.

I was wrong to leave it there. Henry, I later understood, had extracted from precisely this detail a chain of inference I had not troubled to begin.

Maunsell himself was perhaps fifty years of age, with the weather-marked face common to men who pass their lives in the open and the watchful, measuring eyes common to men who have passed those same lives in difficult times. He was well-dressed without ostentation and held himself with the particular careful dignity of a man to whom dignity has come through persistence rather than birth. When he was shown to Henry's outer chamber, he stopped at the threshold as though he had not yet determined whether to enter or to retreat, and stood there long enough that the cold off his coat became perceptible across the room.

He refused the offer of fire.

I noted this as the eccentricity of a man too agitated to feel cold. I was wrong about this also.

Henry was reading. He did not raise his eyes.

"Come in, Master Maunsell," he said. "Close the door behind you, if you would. The cold you have brought with you is preferable to what you have been standing in the yard deciding."

Maunsell came in and closed the door. His hand, as he turned from the latch, moved briefly to his breast — a quick, involuntary press of the palm, arrested before it completed itself — and then fell to his side.

I believed, at the time, that it was a gesture of cold or of nerves. I did not consider what it signified.

Henry set down his document and looked at Maunsell for the first time.

"You rode alone," he said.

"I did."

"From Crayke."

Maunsell's expression altered — not greatly, but perceptibly. "How did you—"

"The mud on your boots is the clay-marl particular to the low ground north and west of Crayke, which is not found in the drier chalk country between here and York. The pattern of it suggests a route taken before the morning frost had fully thawed the surface — you left early and you left quietly." Henry's tone was without drama; he might have been reading from a document. "Your horse is well-tended, which is to say you are not a man who ordinarily goes without a groom. That you did so this morning, and in weather discouraging to solitary travel, tells me the matter you have come about is one you have not wished to share with your household. The gesture you made at your breast when you turned from the door tells me that the source of your distress is something you are carrying upon your person."

He paused.

"I should add," he said, "that the Act confirmed in this last Parliament narrows considerably the range of distresses capable of producing this particular combination of secrecy and urgency in a man of your standing. The letter, Master Maunsell, if you please."

The freeholder stood very still for a moment. Then he reached into his breast and produced a folded sheet of parchment.

It was a single sheet, good quality, written in a clerk's hand — regular, practised, and entirely without personal character. Such hands are taught, not grown, and this one had been taught well. The content was as follows, rendered here without improvement:

That the writer was in possession of certain knowledge — namely, that Gilbert Maunsell of Crayke kept within a locked chest in his solar a portion of the Gospel of Saint Matthew rendered into the English tongue, the translation being that of the late John Wycliffe, condemned heretic — and that this knowledge would, in the natural course of things, be communicated to the ecclesiastical authorities of the diocese, unless Master Maunsell would agree in writing, before witnesses, to abandon all claim to the strip of common pasture known as Ellershawe Sike.

I read it twice. The mechanism of it struck me as both clever and terrible. To deny was to invite investigation. To confirm was to confess. To comply was to invite the next demand.

Henry read it once, returned it to Maunsell, and sat back.

"Do you possess the text?" he said.

Maunsell looked at me.

"Master Rede is here as a witness to a civil matter," Henry said. "He is not here in his capacity as a man of the Church, and what passes in this room does not travel beyond it. You may answer."

A silence, of the kind that contains a decision being made.

"I do," Maunsell said. "My wife's father brought it into the house before the statute. It has been there fifteen years."

"You have read it."

"I have read it."

Henry nodded slowly. "Very good. Now. This strip of pasture — Ellershawe Sike. Tell me who else has a claim upon it."

Maunsell explained at some length. The Sike was a narrow strip of seasonal grazing running along a beck between his land and the adjacent holding of one Robert Fychan, another freeholder. The boundary had been disputed for two generations, a matter of a conflicting grant and an ambiguous survey. Maunsell's claim was the stronger in law, he believed, but Fychan had agitated the matter persistently, lately aided by the counsel of a merchant named Aldwick. This Aldwick held the margin immediately to the east and, as Maunsell noted with some bitterness, had long coveted the Sike as a passage for his wool-wains to reach the lower ford.

When he had finished, Henry stood.

"I must think for a moment," he said, and went to the window.

I observed Maunsell during this interval. He was watching Henry with the expression of a man who has placed his hand on a wheel he cannot stop, uncertain whether this constitutes rescue or additional catastrophe.

After some minutes, Henry turned.

"The author of this letter," he said, "is not Robert Fychan."

I confess this surprised me. The logic had seemed obvious: the Sike was disputed, Fychan was the disputing party, and the letter named the Sike as its price. I had assembled the chain without effort and was waiting only for Henry to confirm it.

"The hand is a clerk's hand," Henry said, before I could speak. "Fychan, whatever his virtues, is not a man of clerical education. He dictates when he must and signs where he is told. This letter was not dictated — a dictated letter carries the rhythms of speech, and this one carries the rhythms of a man composing directly onto parchment. Someone wrote this letter who habitually writes. That person is not Fychan." He paused. "Fychan may yet be part of the matter. But he is not the author of it."

"Then who—" Maunsell began.

"That is what I intend to determine. I will require the names of every clerk who has had occasion to handle documents relating to the Sike dispute. Every man who has drafted, copied, witnessed, or recorded anything touching on that boundary in the last five years."

Maunsell produced three names. Henry wrote them down and studied them without apparent emotion.

"There is," he then said, in the tone of a man raising a question he has already answered, "a fourth. The dispute has passed through the consistory record once, I believe, regarding a tithe boundary adjacent to the Sike. Who held the pen in that record?"

Maunsell did not know. Henry said he would determine it.

Henry spent that afternoon in a manner he did not fully account for to me until later. He visited the records of two disputes — one at the manor, one in the borough court — and compared their hands against the letter, which Maunsell had left with him. He also, by means I did not then understand, caused an inquiry to be directed toward a man named Aldwick — not a freeholder, not a clerk, but a small merchant of the district who had, some years prior, acquired an interest in land immediately adjacent to the Sike on its eastern margin.

He returned to Thornfield in the early dark, in good spirits, which with Henry meant a settled rather than an elevated mood.

"Sokewell," he said, removing his cloak.

I looked up. "The consistory clerk?"

"The same." He sat. "His hand is not an exact match — he was careful enough to vary his letterforms — but he was not careful enough about his spacing habits, or about the manner in which he forms the descender on his g. A man may alter the shape of his letters; he rarely alters their relationships to one another. The letter was written by Sokewell."

"Then Sokewell is our man."

Henry looked at me with the expression that I had learned, across many years, to interpret as patient disagreement.

"Sokewell," he said, "is the pen. He is not the grievance. A clerk in the consistory of this diocese does not conceive a scheme to extort a freeholder's pasture rights for his own benefit. Where would the benefit lie? He has no land adjacent to the Sike. He has no quarrel with Maunsell. He has," Henry continued, pulling the three names Maunsell had given him from his sleeve, "a considerable debt to a merchant named Aldwick, incurred two years past and not since discharged, and Aldwick has a very particular interest in that eastern margin."

I turned this over. "Aldwick instructed him."

"Aldwick commissioned the letter, yes. Fychan is the nominal claimant — his name would appear on any written agreement ceding the Sike, which provides the transaction with a legal surface. Aldwick is the beneficiary of the eastern margin that would open to him once the Sike boundary shifted. Sokewell provided the instrument." Henry folded the names away. "Three men, one letter, one strip of winter pasture, and one terrified freeholder. It is not a complicated arrangement. It merely required a sufficiently frightened man at its centre."

"And the knowledge of the Wycliffe text?"

Henry was silent for a moment.

"That," he said carefully, "is a separate question. Someone knew what was in Maunsell's chest. That knowledge could have come from the household only. I have a thought about it, but I will not name the person until I am certain, and I may choose not to name them at all."

He went the following morning to find Sokewell.

I was not present. What I know of the conversation I know from Henry's account, given that evening with a brevity I have come to recognise as accuracy rather than evasion.

He found the clerk at his records in the borough court and spoke with him privately, for some quarter of an hour. He did not threaten. He did not produce the letter. He demonstrated, by a systematic recounting of the spacing habits and descender formations in the letter as compared with Sokewell's court records, that the authorship was not a matter of allegation but of arithmetic. Sokewell, Henry said, was a frightened man rather than a wicked one, and frightened men, once shown that concealment is unavailable, are generally inclined toward economy.

The clerk confirmed Aldwick's commission. He confirmed Fychan's nominal involvement—the freeholder had been brought in after the letter's composition, his land claim used as legal cover in exchange for a small consideration. Most crucially, he confirmed that the knowledge of the Wycliffe text had come to Aldwick through the boy Wat, whose gaming debts in the borough had made him a ready tool for a merchant of Aldwick’s reach. Henry, having noted both Maunsell’s distrust of the lad and Aldwick’s geographical interest in the Sike during our initial interview, had merely used the clerk to bridge the two facts.

An understanding was reached — not in writing, because nothing in the matter could be set down — that the letter would be withdrawn, that Aldwick would make no further movement on the eastern margin, and that the Sike would remain in its disputed but undisturbed condition.

Fychan was not confronted. Aldwick was not named aloud. Sokewell returned to his records.

The letter was, I believe, burned. I was not shown the act, but Henry said so, and I have never known him to lie about a matter of fact.

We rode back to Thornfield in the early afternoon, along a track that ran beside the frozen beck. The sky was the colour of pewter and the wind had found us again after two days of stillness.

I asked him about the servant.

"What of them?" he said.

"You know who told Aldwick about the text."

"I believe so, yes."

"And you will not pursue it."

"The servant is young," Henry said, "and is in debt for reasons I will not detail, and was very likely told that the information would be used only to pressure Maunsell in a civil matter, not to endanger his life. The letter we found was not a letter of accusation. It was a letter of commerce. The servant may not have understood that the statute made the distinction immaterial." He paused. "Or may have understood precisely, and was desperate enough to proceed regardless. In either case, the servant is not the architect, and Maunsell would derive no benefit from knowing."

"The statute remains," I said.

"The statute remains," he agreed. "It is a blunt instrument — a political tool dressed in theological clothes — and its application to a man of Maunsell's sort, possessing a fifteen-year-old manuscript inherited by marriage, is not a matter that benefits from official attention." He looked ahead along the track. "I am not a judge, Thomas. I am not the Church, and I am not the Crown. I determine what occurred. What is done with the knowledge is a separate question."

I have thought about this often in the years since. It is not a satisfying answer, and I do not believe Henry intended it to be. It was simply the truest thing he could say.

We did not speak again until Thornfield was in sight.

For the sake of any reader who has followed the matter and wishes the chain set out plainly:

The letter was composed by Walter Sokewell, clerk, whose hand betrayed him through the consistent relationships of his letterforms even when their shapes had been altered. He acted under commission from a merchant named Aldwick, who desired access to a margin of land that would become available if the Sike boundary were shifted under legal cover of Fychan's existing claim. Fychan, nominally the named beneficiary, was the instrument of legal respectability and knew enough to be complicit and too little to be the mind of the scheme. Knowledge of the Wycliffe text came to Aldwick through a member of Maunsell's household, exploited rather than recruited, and the text was chosen as a lever precisely because of the new statute — not out of religious conviction on any party's part, but because the law had made a domestic book into a portable weapon.

Robert Fychan was not confronted because his role was subsidiary and because confronting him served no purpose that could be advanced. Walter Sokewell returned to his records, apparently without incident. Aldwick retained his existing properties and made no further movement on the eastern margin, at least within the period of my observation.

The Wycliffe manuscript, to my knowledge, remained in Maunsell's chest.

Whether it is there still, I cannot say. I record only what I know.

Thomas Rede, Cleric. Thornfield. February, anno Domini 1402.
