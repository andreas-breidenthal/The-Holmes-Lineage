AKAR-TESHUB

~1270 BC — Hittite Anatolia

The name was a declaration of belonging — “Akar” preserved from the family’s Semitic deep past, “Teshub” the Hittite storm-god whose cult dominated the religious life of the Anatolian plateau. The combination said: I am of this world and of another, and I move between them. Akar-Teshub served as a border official in the western Hittite administration, in the towns that controlled the passes between the coastal lowlands and the Anatolian interior — positions of considerable strategic importance, because through those passes moved not only goods but information, and the Hittite empire’s security depended on knowing what was moving in both directions.

He was, in the language of a later age, a counter-intelligence officer — responsible for identifying the merchants who were gathering information for Mycenaean or Egyptian purposes under the cover of legitimate trade, for assessing the loyalty of the local populations in the contested frontier zone, and for detecting the false declarations and the concealed cargoes that the border crossing generated in abundance. He was precise and he was thorough, and the towns he administered were among the quietest on the western frontier. Those who served under him attributed this to his diligence. Those who had worked against him, had any of them survived to reflect on it, might have attributed it to something harder to name.

The record of his work was preserved by Sura, a junior scribe attached to the imperial gatehouse. Sura was trained in the keeping of seals and manifests but possessed a restless attention to detail, often returning to entries to adjust wording or clarify counts. He wrote not only what was declared, but what he believed he had witnessed, and in doing so left traces of his own uncertainty. His account, copied and recopied in administrative archives, provides the sequence that follows.

The Account Of Sura

I have kept records for eleven years. I know the weight of a clay tablet before I press a single mark into it — the way the grey earth cools differently in summer and winter, the particular resistance it offers to the stylus at different hours of the day. I record what is declared, and I record what I observe, and I have learned that these are not always the same list.

What follows is true. I have considered, over many years, whether it should be written at all. I have decided that it should, though I have adjusted certain details of certain names, and I will explain at the end which details and why. The official record — the one that stands in the imperial archive at Hattusa, the one that credits the western frontier administration with the successful identification and neutralisation of an Aegean intelligence channel — does not mention Akar-Teshub. It mentions the administration. It mentions the procedure. It mentions the system.

Akar-Teshub was the system. But he would never have said so himself, and in any case he is gone now, and what he thought does not appear to have troubled him greatly.

I write this because someone should.

The smell of the pass at Arinna in the third month of summer is specific and unpleasant in a way that becomes, over years, almost comforting: the dung of oxen, the rancid fat used to grease axle-pins, the particular mineral sharpness of the limestone dust that the west wind carries up from the coastal lowlands and deposits on everything — your lips, your tablets, the inside of the great cedar-log gate that groans on its bronze hinges each morning like an animal in pain. I knew the sound of that hinge better than I knew my own heartbeat. When it began to groan differently — when the pitch changed because the socket had worn unevenly, or because someone had moved the gate in the night — I noticed.

This is relevant. Remember it.

Akar-Teshub arrived at Arinna in the seventh year of the Great King’s reign, in the middle of the busiest trading season, with no ceremony and two attendants. The previous administrator had been a broad, confident man named Halwi who laughed often and kept excellent records of what he had been told. Akar-Teshub was narrow and contained. He did not laugh. He kept records of what he had observed. I understood immediately that my duties were about to become more demanding.

His eyes were the first thing I noted. Not their colour — grey, like the clay — but their quality of attention. Most officials look at you and are already looking past you, toward the next thing that requires looking. Akar-Teshub looked at you and the impression was of something being measured. Not judged — measured. As though you were a cargo, and he was calculating the discrepancy between your declared weight and your actual weight.

He spent his first three days reading the gate records. All of them. Eleven years of my work, and the seven years of Piyassilis before me. He did not ask questions. He sat in the gatehouse with a lamp — cedar oil, always cedar oil in Arinna, the pine varieties were reserved for the temple — and he read, and occasionally he set a tablet aside in a separate stack, and he read on.

On the fourth day he came to find me.

“The merchant Kirunda of Arzawa,” he said, without preamble. “How many times has he passed this gate?”

I told him what I knew: Kirunda was a regular trader, tin and bronze-work moving east from the coastal markets, wine and oil moving west on the return. He had been passing this gate for at least six years. His seals were in good order. His declarations had never varied significantly from his actual cargoes. He was, in the language of the checkpoint, clean.

“How many times,” Akar-Teshub said again, “has he passed this gate?”

He waited. I went to the records. I counted. I told him: thirty-one eastward crossings and twenty-nine westward returns. He nodded once, as though I had confirmed something he already knew.

“And what month does he not come?”

I stared at him. He waited with the patience of a man who has given the question to the air and is prepared for it to hang there until the answer arrives on its own.

I went back to the records. It took some time. When I finished, I felt a coldness that had nothing to do with the limestone dust or the altitude.

He had never, in six years, made a crossing in the second month of spring.

I brought him the tablet with the seasonal tallies. He did not look at it immediately. He was standing at the gate opening, looking out at the road, his arms crossed, his eyes distant and somewhat absent — the way they went when whatever was happening inside him was too large for his face to contain.

“Sit,” he said, gesturing at the bench beside the gate-post. It was the bench where I spent most of my working hours. I sat.

He took the tablet then, glanced at it, set it down.

“You are a careful man, Sura,” he said. It was not a compliment. It was an observation. “You return to entries. I saw the adjustments in the second-year records. The wording changes. A ‘load of tin’ becomes ‘fourteen ingots of tin, each of standard gatehouse weight.’ Why?”

Because the first description had seemed imprecise, I told him. Because imprecision in records is a door left open.

He looked at me for a moment with the measuring expression.

“Tell me what you know of the Mycenaean probe at the Seha River valley. The one in the spring of year four.”

I knew of it in the way that all frontier officials knew of such things: a report had come through from the military command, the passes had been placed on elevated watch, the watch had been reduced after several weeks. The probe had come to nothing. The Aegeans had withdrawn.

“And the probe at Wilusa the following spring?”

The same. And again the spring after that.

“Spring,” he said. “Specifically the second month. Each time.”

I was beginning to see the shape of what he was looking at. But the shape seemed too large and too terrible, and I think I may have preferred not to see it clearly, because I said something inadequate about coincidence.

He made a short sound that was not quite a laugh.

“Kirunda of Arzawa does not cross in the second month of spring,” he said. “In each of the three years in which a Mycenaean probe occurred — not a raid, Sura, not a raid, a probe, which requires preparation, which requires knowledge of troop dispositions and supply routes and the reliability of local chieftains — Kirunda was not here. He was elsewhere. He was on the coast. He was, I would suggest, in conversation with people who sailed in from the west.”

I said: but his cargoes are legitimate. His seals are good. We have inspected his loads.

“Yes,” said Akar-Teshub. “They are exactly legitimate. Exactly. In six years his declared weights have never varied from his actual weights by more than a tenth of a unit. His seals are perfect. His manner at the gate is neither too nervous nor too easy. He has given us nothing to hold him on. He is very good.”

He paused. The gate-hinge groaned in the wind, its worn socket producing the lower note that I had been hearing for three weeks and had not yet reported because I had intended to report it on the morning of the day after next when the maintenance roster came due.

“Which is itself a thing worth noting,” he continued, “since genuine merchants are almost never perfect. They undercount, they overcount, they forget a bundle, they argue about a seal. Kirunda never argues. He moves through this gate as though he has rehearsed it. Which he has. Over six years, he has rehearsed it into a performance of innocence so complete that it became invisible.”

He turned to look at me directly.

“The gate-hinge,” he said. “It changed pitch approximately three weeks ago. You noticed.”

I said I had been intending to report it.

“I know. I am not criticising the hinge. I am noting that you heard the change. You have been at this gate for eleven years and you hear when something moves differently.” He picked up the seasonal tally tablet again. “So have I. Kirunda moves differently. He has always moved differently. We simply did not have enough seasons recorded to hear it.”

He set the tablet down with a finality that I recognised, though I could not have explained how, as the end of the observation phase and the beginning of something else.

“When,” he asked, “does Kirunda next come east?”

I consulted the pattern. Within the month, I told him. His eastward crossings fell roughly every seven to eight weeks.

“Good,” said Akar-Teshub. He did not say what was good about it. He walked back into the gatehouse and resumed reading.

I should record this, because it seems to me important and because no official document would think to note it.

Akar-Teshub, when there was no problem to occupy him, was a different person. Or perhaps the same person from whom the organising principle had been temporarily removed — the way a loom, when the warp threads are cut, collapses into a pile of wood and cord that no longer resembles anything useful.

He slept badly. He walked the walls at night. I know this because I am a light sleeper myself, and I heard his sandals on the stone. During the three weeks between his analysis of the Kirunda pattern and Kirunda’s next expected arrival, he twice reviewed the entire archive, not because he had missed anything — I think he could not miss anything; it was not in his nature — but because a mind such as his, left without sufficient material to work on, begins to consume itself.

His stimulant was riddles. Not the priestly kind, not the poetic kind — the administrative kind. He would take a discrepancy — a single mina’s weight unaccounted for in a copper cargo from seven years ago — and pursue it through the records until he found the explanation, which was usually mundane (a miscount, a wet ingot, a scribe’s hurried wedge-mark). The explanation never satisfied him for long. He needed a new problem immediately.

There was a period of perhaps four days during the second week of waiting when the problems ran out. The archive had been fully examined. No new travellers of interest had come through. The routine declarations were routine. I watched him sit in the gatehouse with the cedar-oil lamp and do nothing. He did not eat. He did not speak. He stared at the wall above the door in a way that made the two gatehouse guards visibly uncomfortable.

On the fifth day a caravan came through with a disputed seal — not a forgery, simply a seal that had been recut after the original ring cracked, and the proportions were slightly changed — and the arguments about its validity lasted most of the morning. Akar-Teshub emerged from the gatehouse, resolved the matter in approximately six minutes by identifying the original seal-cutter’s workshop from the particular tool-line left in the clay, and went back inside looking considerably more alive.

His one other concession to appetite was pomegranate. He ate them in extraordinary quantity when they were available and ignored all other food with equal indifference. I record this because it is true and because the specificity of it seems to me to matter — a man who can be precisely located by his habits, and who knows this, and does not care.

Ramuwa-Telab was the military commander of the Arinna district, a solid and capable officer who had held the post for four years and administered it with professional diligence. He was not, I think, a foolish man. He was a correctly functioning part of the imperial machine, which means he operated by the procedures the imperial machine had provided, and those procedures had not been designed to account for Akar-Teshub.

When Akar-Teshub came to him with the Kirunda analysis — the seasonal pattern, the correlation with the three probes, the argument from the man’s excessive perfection — Ramuwa-Telab listened with the expression of a man who has been presented with a set of objects and asked to believe they are a weapon.

“This is a series of dates,” he said, when Akar-Teshub finished. “A series of dates and an inference.”

“Yes,” said Akar-Teshub.

“I cannot detain a merchant in good standing on an inference. I cannot search his records — his personal records, not his cargo manifest — without authorisation from Hattusa, which takes thirty days in good weather and currently there is not good weather.”

“I know,” said Akar-Teshub.

“I cannot send a man to follow him on the coastal road without specific grounds, because if it is discovered and he is in fact innocent, we have created a diplomatic problem with Arzawa, whose loyalty we are not currently in a position to strain.”

“I know all of this,” said Akar-Teshub, with the patience of a man who has already anticipated every wall and is simply waiting for their enumeration to conclude. “I am not asking you to do any of those things.”

Ramuwa-Telab looked at him.

“What are you asking?”

“I am asking you to tell me everything you know about the content of the three Mycenaean probes. Not the official reports. What the scouts actually brought back. What was in the dispatches that did not make it into the final summaries because it was assessed as too speculative.”

Ramuwa-Telab was silent for a moment.

“And then?”

“And then I would like two of your men — in civilian dress, not military — at the gate when Kirunda comes through. Not to detain him. To observe. And when he passes, I would like one of them to take the western road at a comfortable distance and note where Kirunda stops, and who comes to his camp in the evenings, and whether any of those visitors are the same people who appeared in the speculative sections of the probe dispatches.”

“That,” said Ramuwa-Telab slowly, “is within procedure.”

“Yes,” said Akar-Teshub. “I thought it might be.”

I record this exchange because I was present, and because it illuminates the nature of Akar-Teshub’s particular genius. He knew, always, which walls he could not climb and which doors he could walk through without appearing to push. He did not fight the procedure. He found the gap the procedure had not thought to close.

Ramuwa-Telab provided the men. He also, at Akar-Teshub’s quiet suggestion, forwarded a carefully worded report to the western military command, adjusting the scheduled patrol timing on one specific mountain route — not cancelling it, simply moving it by four days. This adjustment was real. It appeared in the official schedule.

If Kirunda was what Akar-Teshub believed him to be, that information would reach the west, and the west would rely on it, and that reliance would become the proof.

Kirunda arrived on the forty-third day after Akar-Teshub’s analysis. I recognised him before he cleared the final bend in the road — the particular angle of his lead ox, a grey animal with a damaged left horn that Kirunda had been travelling with for at least four years. He came in the mid-morning, which was his habit. He had fourteen animals, a reasonable load for the season, and two servants who had been with him on previous crossings.

I processed him myself, as I always had. His seals were in order. His declaration — tin, copper ingots, three sealed jars of what he described as oil for ritual use — matched his cargo within acceptable tolerance. His manner was exactly what it had always been: polite without warmth, efficient without haste. He asked after no one. He offered nothing unrequested. He paid his gate tax in correctly sized silver pieces and moved his animals through into the eastern holding area for the standard overnight rest period.

He was perfect, as he had always been.

Akar-Teshub watched from the upper wall throughout. He did not come down. He did not approach Kirunda. He watched with the air of a man observing a piece of mathematics work itself out, and when Kirunda’s caravan was through the gate, he descended and walked into the records room.

I followed him.

“Did you see anything?” he asked me.

I had been watching carefully, because by then I understood that he expected me to watch carefully and report honestly, which is not the same as reporting flatteringly. I told him: the cargo was consistent, the seals were good, the servants showed no unusual nervousness. One thing only had caught my attention — the three jars of ritual oil were loaded differently from how I would have expected. They were in the centre of the load rather than padded on the exterior, which was the normal handling for fragile sealed vessels. They were nested, as though the priority was keeping them stable rather than protecting them from impact.

Akar-Teshub looked at me for a moment.

“As though their contents should not be shaken,” he said. Not a question.

He sent word to Ramuwa-Telab’s men that the primary interest was not Kirunda’s evening contacts. It was the sealed jars. And he went into a period of absolute stillness — not the ennui of waiting, but the focused stillness of a man who has placed a trap and is now simply waiting for physics to do its work.

The ritual oil was oil. I must record this exactly, because it matters for what followed.

The outer layer of each jar was oil, correctly sealed, of the quality and type consistent with what had been declared. The men who opened them — Ramuwa-Telab’s people, on the fourth day of Kirunda’s eastward journey, at a waystation where the merchant had stopped — reported oil.

Akar-Teshub, who had ridden there himself through the night on what I assume was the energy that difficult problems produced in him — replacing sleep entirely with forward motion when the solution was close enough to taste — asked them to describe the weight of the jar after the oil was poured out.

They described it.

“Too heavy,” he said.

The base of each jar was clay. Unfired clay — the same colour and texture as the fired exterior, but not fired, and therefore still workable. Embedded in the base of each jar, wrapped in oiled linen against the moisture, were small flat pieces of worked bone.

On the bone pieces, in a script that was not Hittite cuneiform, not Luwian hieroglyph, not Akkadian — a script that Akar-Teshub identified as a western Aegean syllabary, related to systems he had studied from captured documents during his earlier posting on the coast — were marks.

He read them in the waystation courtyard in the early morning while Kirunda slept.

He was quiet for a long time afterwards.

Then he said, to Ramuwa-Telab’s officer: “Reseal the jars. Exactly as they were.”

The officer stared.

“Exactly as they were,” Akar-Teshub said again, in the tone he used when repetition was not for emphasis but for precision. “And return them to the load. And let the merchant proceed.”

I learned what was on the bone pieces later, in fragments, and I will record what I understand of it without claiming certainty.

They were not military intelligence. They were not troop dispositions or supply routes or assessments of local loyalty, though they may have begun as that kind of traffic years earlier. What Kirunda was carrying east, on that crossing, was a communication addressed to a recipient in the inner territories — a communication that, if Akar-Teshub’s reading was correct, described the movement of the very patrol whose schedule had been adjusted four days earlier.

The adjusted schedule. The one that had been changed quietly, as a trap.

Kirunda had the false schedule. He was carrying it east. Which meant he had received it from someone at the western command — someone who had access to the military dispatch system and was feeding it outward and then, through Kirunda, back to Mycenaean contact handlers who would use it to time their next probe.

If Akar-Teshub arrested Kirunda now, the recipient in the interior would be warned. Kirunda would be interrogated, would reveal what he could or would not, and whoever was receiving the messages at the other end would vanish into the administrative landscape of the empire, and they would begin again with a different channel and a different merchant, and the next probe would come from a direction no one was watching.

So he let him go.

I had found Akar-Teshub on the upper wall when he returned, in the middle of the night, and I had asked him — because by that point I felt I had earned the right to ask — what would happen now.

“Now,” he said, “Kirunda delivers his adjusted intelligence. The recipient in the interior passes it west. The Aegeans plan their probe around a patrol route that no longer exists. The probe fails. The channel becomes unreliable in the eyes of the people who have been paying for it, and they close it down.” He paused. “Or they do not close it down, and the next cargo Kirunda carries east contains corrected information that exposes my adjustment — which tells me the recipient in the interior has military access — and I know which part of the interior to look at.”

“And Kirunda?” I asked.

He was quiet for a moment.

“Kirunda will die,” he said, “or he will not. He has been carrying this traffic for six years. He has made choices. The choices have consequences that extend beyond this gate.” He said it without pleasure. I was not sure, in the darkness on the wall above the cedar-log gate with its worn hinge, whether what I heard in his voice was coldness or something that would require more honesty to name. “I cannot arrest a man for a pattern of dates. I cannot hold a man for the weight of an oil jar. And if I could, I would lose the interior channel and gain nothing but one ruined merchant.”

He walked down off the wall without waiting for me to respond.

The Mycenaean probe in the spring of year eight failed. The official military report attributed this to improved patrol distribution in the western sectors. The report is not wrong, exactly. The patrol distribution was improved. The improvement was suggested by Akar-Teshub in a memorandum to the western command — filed under the category of routine administrative optimisation — six weeks before the probe occurred.

The interior contact was identified eighteen months later. I know this because Akar-Teshub told me, and I believe him, though he told me only that the channel was closed and not how it was closed. I did not ask.

Kirunda of Arzawa was killed by bandits on the coastal road two years after the crossing I have described. The official record of his death is ordinary. I have no reason to believe it was not ordinary. I have chosen not to examine it too closely, because some questions, once asked, require honest answers, and I am not certain the honest answer would be one I could record and remain the kind of scribe I have chosen to be.

The official credit for the failure of the year-eight probe went to the western military administration. Ramuwa-Telab received a commendation. He earned it, I believe — he gave Akar-Teshub the men and the latitude and the access to the speculative dispatches, and a commander who had refused all three would have made what followed impossible. The system, as I said, received the credit. This is correct. A system that cannot function without a singular mind is a fragile system, and the empire does not survive on fragility.

Akar-Teshub was transferred the following year to a different posting, further north, on a route where a different pattern had begun to emerge.

He came to say goodbye — not to me, exactly; he came to make a final notation in the gate records, and I happened to be present, as I generally was. He looked older. Not in the way of a man who has been ill, but in the way of a man who has spent a year processing something large and not yet finished with it. He stood at the gate for a moment before he left, looking at the hinge.

“That socket,” he said, “should have been replaced three months ago.”

“Yes,” I said.

He looked at me. The measuring quality in his eyes was, for a moment, something else — something warmer, or perhaps simply less contained, the way the same limestone looks different in different light.

“Your records,” he said, “are the best I have encountered on any posting.”

He walked out through the gate and did not look back.

I recopied this account three times over the years that followed, adjusting the wording toward precision and away from sentiment, which is what he would have done. I have changed the name of the waystation. I have changed the number of animals in Kirunda’s caravan. I have not changed anything that matters.

The cedar-oil lamp is guttering. The gate-hinge has been replaced, and the new one is silent, which I find I do not entirely prefer.

I am Sura, junior scribe of the imperial gatehouse, and this is what I witnessed. What I have written here, I have written truly.

Colophon: Let the next scribe who copies this tablet note that the numbers in the seasonal tally are exact. I counted them myself, twice.
