AKAR-TESHUB

~1270 BC — Hittite Anatolia

THE ACCOUNT OF SURA

They found Pala at the base of the western escarpment on the morning of the third day of the second month. The climb down to where he lay was not possible after dark, not safely, and the consensus among the garrison was that he had walked too close to the edge in poor light. I accepted this reading less easily than my superiors did. Pala had grown up on the escarpment road. He had walked it since childhood. Men who grow up on high roads do not fall from them in calm weather.

I am Sura, junior scribe of the imperial gatehouse at Arinna. I have kept records for eleven years. I know the weight of a clay tablet before I press a single mark into it — the way the grey earth cools differently in summer and winter, the particular resistance it offers to the stylus at different hours of the day. I record what is declared, and I record what I observe, and I have learned that these are not always the same list.

What follows is true. The official record — the one that stands in the imperial archive at Hattusa, the one that credits the western frontier administration with the successful identification and neutralisation of an Aegean intelligence channel — does not mention Akar-Teshub. It mentions the administration. It mentions the procedure. It mentions the system.

Akar-Teshub was the system. But he would never have said so himself, and in any case he is gone now.

I write this because someone should.

I.

The smell of the pass at Arinna in the third month of summer is specific and unpleasant in a way that becomes, over years, almost comforting: the dung of oxen, the rancid fat used to grease axle-pins, the particular mineral sharpness of the limestone dust that the west wind carries up from the coastal lowlands and deposits on everything — your lips, your tablets, the inside of the great cedar-log gate that groans on its bronze hinges each morning like an animal in pain. I knew the sound of that hinge better than I knew my own heartbeat. When it began to groan differently — when the pitch changed because the socket had worn unevenly, or because someone had moved the gate in the night — I noticed.

This is relevant. Remember it.

It was Ramuwa-Telab, the military commander of the Arinna district, who sent for Akar-Teshub — not because he believed anything was wrong, but because the western military command had been looking for a suitable posting for a specialist administrator, and the garrison at Arinna had a vacancy. He mentioned Pala's death in his request as a matter of procedure, the way one reports the state of a hinge or the level of the grain stores. A fact. He did not regard it as a commission.

Akar-Teshub arrived in the middle of the busiest trading season with no ceremony and two attendants. His name said something of what he was before he spoke a word: "Akar" from his family's Semitic past — a lineage that had moved through the great trading networks of an earlier generation — and "Teshub" for the Hittite storm-god whose cult dominated the religious life of the plateau he had come to administer. A man of this world and of another, as I came to understand. One who moved between them with the ease of long practice.

The previous administrator had been a broad, confident man named Halwi who laughed often and kept excellent records of what he had been told. Akar-Teshub was narrow and contained. He did not laugh. He kept records of what he had observed. His eyes were grey, like the clay, and their quality of attention was not the quality of a man who looks at you while already looking past you toward the next thing. He looked at you and the impression was of something being measured. Not judged — measured. As though you were a cargo, and he was calculating the discrepancy between your declared weight and your actual weight.

I understood immediately that my duties were about to become more demanding.

He spent his first days reading the gate records. All of them. Eleven years of my work, and the seven years of Piyassilis before me. On the second day I came in at the second hour and found him standing, not sitting, bent over the bench with the lamp held close to a single tablet — not the seasonal tallies, not the cargo manifests, but a smaller record, a supplementary notation in Piyassilis's hand from six years prior. It described a fragment of fired clay recovered from a traveller who had died of fever at the waystation — a fragment bearing marks that Piyassilis had recorded as "unknown western script, possibly decorative, set aside in the curiosity archive as of no administrative relevance."

Akar-Teshub did not notice me enter. After a moment he straightened, replaced the tablet with a precision that was itself a kind of emphasis, and turned and saw me. He said nothing. He went back to reading.

On the fourth day he came to find me.

"The merchant Kirunda of Arzawa," he said, without preamble. "How many times has he passed this gate?"

I told him what I knew: Kirunda was a regular trader, tin and bronze-work moving east from the coastal markets, wine and oil moving west on the return. He had been passing this gate for at least six years. His seals were in good order. His declarations had never varied significantly from his actual cargoes. He was, in the language of the checkpoint, clean.

"How many times," Akar-Teshub said again, "has he passed this gate?"

He waited. I went to the records. I counted. I told him: thirty-one eastward crossings and twenty-nine westward returns.

He nodded once, as though I had confirmed something he already knew.

"And what month does he not come?"

I stared at him. He waited with the patience of a man who has given the question to the air and is prepared for it to hang there until the answer arrives on its own.

I went back to the records. It took some time. When I finished, I felt a coldness that had nothing to do with the limestone dust or the altitude.

He had never, in six years, made a crossing in the second month of spring.

I brought Akar-Teshub the tablet with the seasonal tallies. He was standing at the gate opening, looking out at the road, his arms crossed, his eyes distant in the way they went when whatever was happening inside him was too large for his face to contain.

"Sit," he said, gesturing at the bench beside the gate-post. I sat.

He took the tablet, glanced at it, set it down. He did not look satisfied. He looked the way a man looks when a thing he has suspected for some time has finally condescended to confirm itself.

"You are a careful man, Sura," he said. Not a compliment — an observation. "You return to entries. A 'load of tin' becomes 'fourteen ingots of tin, each of standard gatehouse weight.' Why?"

Because imprecision in records is a door left open, I told him.

"Yes," he said. "Tell me what you know of the Mycenaean probe at the Seha River valley. The spring of year four."

I knew of it as all frontier officials knew of such things: a report from military command, the passes on elevated watch, the watch reduced after weeks. The probe had come to nothing. The Aegeans had withdrawn.

"And the probe at Wilusa the following spring?"

The same. And the spring after that.

"Spring," he said. "The second month. Each time." He picked up the seasonal tally again and held it at a slight angle, as though looking at it from a different position might yield a different reading. It was a gesture — I would come to recognise these gestures — not of uncertainty but of emphasis, performed for no audience except the question itself. "Kirunda is absent in the second month of spring. The Mycenaean probes occur in the second month of spring. This has happened three times."

I said something inadequate about coincidence.

He made a short sound that was not quite a laugh, and turned to look at me with a directness that I had not yet learned to meet without flinching.

"A probe is not a raid," he said. "A raid requires only armed men and a direction. A probe requires knowledge — troop dispositions, supply routes, the reliability of local chieftains. It requires a source. In each of the three years in question, Kirunda was not here. He was on the coast. He was in conversation with people who sailed in from the west, and he was carrying what they needed east, through this gate, in cargo we inspected and found clean." He set the tablet down. "He is very good."

I said: his seals are perfect. His declared weights have never varied by more than a tenth of a unit. We have found nothing to hold him on.

"Precisely," said Akar-Teshub, and now there was something that might have been pleasure in his voice — not warmth, but the particular satisfaction of a man who has named the exact nature of the trap he is standing in. "In six years, Kirunda has never miscounted, never argued at the gate, never presented a seal worn to ambiguity. Genuine merchants undercount. They overcount. They forget a bundle. They argue. Kirunda moves through this gate as though he has rehearsed it into a performance of innocence so complete it became invisible." He paused. The gate-hinge groaned in the wind, its worn socket producing the lower note I had been hearing for three weeks. "Which is, of course, the most useful kind."

He turned.

"The gate-hinge. It changed pitch approximately three weeks ago. You noticed."

I said I had been intending to report it.

"I know. You heard the change. You have been at this gate for eleven years and you hear when something moves differently." A pause, precisely weighted. "So have I."

He picked up his lamp and walked back toward the gatehouse. At the doorway he stopped.

"Pala worked the morning watch on Kirunda's last crossing."

It was not a question.

I said: yes. He had.

"And died eleven days later." He did not look back. "Bring me the curiosity archive. The western script fragment Piyassilis catalogued in year two. I want it on my desk before the second lamp."

II.

I should record the nature of those waiting weeks, because it illuminates something that no official document would think to preserve.

Akar-Teshub, when there was no problem to occupy him, was a different person — the same man from whom the organising principle had been temporarily removed, the way a loom, when the warp threads are cut, collapses into a pile of wood and cord that no longer resembles anything useful. He slept badly. He walked the walls at night. He ate almost nothing except pomegranate, which he consumed in extraordinary quantity when available and whose absence he registered with the focused displeasure of a man denied a working instrument.

He examined the Piyassilis fragment every evening for three days. It was a small thing — a piece of fired clay no larger than my palm, with marks pressed into it that I had always assumed decorative. I watched him study it with the cedar-oil lamp held close, turning it in his hands, setting it at different angles. On the third evening he put it down and said, to no one in particular: "Not decorative. Syllabic. Western coastal system — related to the scripts captured at the Assuwa campaign, two generations back." He looked at me across the table. "A traveller died of fever carrying a message he never delivered. Six years ago. Through this gate."

I felt the coldness again.

"Before Kirunda began his crossings," I said.

"Before Kirunda." He picked up the fragment again, weighing it in his palm as though its mass might tell him something additional. "Someone was using this route before Kirunda. Kirunda is not the origin of the channel. He is a replacement. Which means the channel has roots here — in the pass, in the gate, in someone who has been present throughout." He set the fragment down with the finality of a man who has located the precise point at which to apply pressure. "Pala was new. Three seasons. He would not have been present throughout."

The implication landed before I could turn away from it.

"He found something," I said.

"He corrected a record," said Akar-Teshub. "Or he asked a question. Or he simply noticed something move differently, and the wrong person observed him noticing." He stood. "We will need Ramuwa-Telab."

There was a period — four days, during the second week of waiting — when the archive had been fully examined and no new travellers of interest had arrived. I watched Akar-Teshub sit in the gatehouse and do nothing. He did not eat. He did not speak. He stared at the wall above the door in a way that made the two guards visibly uncomfortable.

On the fifth day a caravan came through with a disputed seal — not a forgery, simply recut after the original ring cracked — and the arguments lasted most of the morning. Akar-Teshub emerged, resolved the matter in six minutes by identifying the seal-cutter's workshop from the tool-line left in the clay, and went back inside looking considerably more alive. The guards relaxed. I understood by then that this was simply the nature of the instrument.

III.

Ramuwa-Telab listened to the full presentation with the expression of a man who has been given a set of objects and asked to believe they are a weapon.

"This is a series of dates and an inference," he said.

"Yes," said Akar-Teshub.

"I cannot detain a merchant in good standing on an inference. I cannot search his personal records without authorisation from Hattusa, which takes thirty days in good weather. I cannot send surveillance on the coastal road without specific grounds, because if Kirunda is innocent, we have created a diplomatic problem with Arzawa, whose loyalty we cannot currently afford to strain."

"I know all of this," said Akar-Teshub, with the patience of a man who has already anticipated every wall and is simply waiting for their enumeration to conclude. "I am not asking you to do any of those things. I am asking you to show me the speculative content of the three probe dispatches — what the scouts brought back that did not make it into the final summaries because it was assessed as too uncertain. And I am asking whether any of those dispatches describe a source at the Arinna gate."

Ramuwa-Telab was very still.

"Pala fell," he said.

"Pala worked Kirunda's last crossing and died eleven days later." Akar-Teshub's voice remained entirely level. "Men who grow up on escarpment roads do not fall from them in calm weather. I would like to see the speculative dispatches before Kirunda arrives. I would like to know the shape of what he is serving."

Ramuwa-Telab looked at him for a long moment. When he spoke again something had shifted — not fear exactly, but its near relation: the expression of a man accepting that what he preferred not to be true may be true.

"And then?"

"Then I would like two of your men at the gate when Kirunda passes — civilian dress, not military. When his caravan moves east, one follows at distance and notes his camps, his visitors, whether any faces match the descriptions in your speculative dispatches." Akar-Teshub paused. "And I would like you to forward a report to the western military command adjusting the scheduled patrol timing on the northern mountain route — moving it four days, not cancelling it. The adjustment should be real. It should appear in the official schedule."

Ramuwa-Telab's expression shifted toward something more careful. "You are baiting a trap."

"I am making a correction to the patrol schedule," said Akar-Teshub pleasantly, "which is within the ordinary authority of this garrison." He held Ramuwa-Telab's gaze. "If the correction travels west by an unusual route, we will know the shape of the channel. If it returns to us in the cargo Kirunda carries east — if the probe is then timed around a patrol that has been moved — we will have the proof the procedure requires."

"That," said Ramuwa-Telab slowly, "is within procedure."

"Yes. I thought it might be."

I record this exchange because I was present, and because it illuminates the nature of Akar-Teshub's particular genius. He knew which walls he could not climb and which doors he could walk through without appearing to push. He did not fight the procedure. He found the gap the procedure had not thought to close.

The speculative dispatches, when Ramuwa-Telab produced them, contained a single reference to a source at the Arinna gate — three words in a scout's shorthand, assessed as unverified and therefore excluded from the official summary. The source was described only as the one who adjusts the records.

I am a careful man. I adjust records toward precision. I sat with that phrase for two days before I understood that it described not me, but someone who had been doing the opposite — and that Pala, who was meticulous and new and would have noticed an adjustment made for the wrong reason, had perhaps noticed exactly that.

IV.

Kirunda arrived on the forty-third day after Akar-Teshub's analysis. I recognised him before he cleared the final bend — the particular angle of his lead ox, a grey animal with a damaged left horn he had been travelling with for four years. He came in the mid-morning. Fourteen animals. Two familiar servants.

And one I did not recognise: broad across the shoulders, with the watchful lateral gaze of a man assessing exits rather than looking at what was in front of him. He handed over a lead rope before I had indicated which animals required inspection — a small thing, possibly nothing. I wrote it down.

I processed the crossing myself, as I always had. Kirunda's seals were in order. His declaration — tin, copper ingots, three sealed jars of ritual oil — matched his cargo within acceptable tolerance. His manner was exactly what it had always been: polite without warmth, efficient without haste. He asked after no one. He paid his gate tax in correctly sized silver pieces and moved his animals through to the eastern holding area.

He was perfect, as he had always been.

Akar-Teshub watched from the upper wall throughout. When the caravan cleared the gate he descended and went directly to the records room.

"Did you see anything?" he asked.

I told him: the ritual oil jars were loaded in the centre of the pack, not padded on the exterior as fragile sealed vessels should be. Nested for stability rather than protection — as though their contents should not be shaken. And the unfamiliar servant: the anticipatory movement, the lateral gaze, the quality of attention that was not a guard's attention but something more specific.

Akar-Teshub looked at me for a moment.

"He is not Kirunda's man," he said. "He belongs to whoever receives the cargo at the eastern end." He sat down, which was unusual — he generally worked standing. "The jars are the method. The servant is the verification — someone sent to ensure the cargo reaches its destination intact, and to confirm the contents have not been disturbed in transit." He was quiet for a moment. "Which means the recipient does not entirely trust Kirunda."

He sent word to Ramuwa-Telab's men: primary interest was the sealed jars. Secondary interest was the broad-shouldered servant. He added, without particular emphasis, that the servant should not be allowed to separate from the party unobserved.

Then he went into a stillness — not the ennui of waiting, but the focused stillness of a man who has set a trap and is now performing, for any observer, the appearance of a man who has done nothing at all.

V.

I will describe the fourth night of Kirunda's eastward journey, because I was there and the official record omits it entirely.

Akar-Teshub had ridden through the dark on the energy that difficult problems produced in him, arriving at the waystation before dawn. I came on a second horse — I am not a natural horseman, and the road was unkind, and I will not claim otherwise. Ramuwa-Telab's men were already posted when we arrived: two at the gate, two at the rear enclosure where the caravans tethered their animals.

The broad-shouldered servant was not in the sleeping quarters.

One of Ramuwa-Telab's men reported this. Akar-Teshub said nothing. He looked once at the rear enclosure, once at the storage area on the western side where the sealed jars had been stacked under an awning — lighter than the heavy cargo, set apart. Then he was moving, and I was following, because by that point following was what I did.

The servant was at the jars, crouched in the shadow of the awning with a shielded lamp, working at the seal of the outermost vessel with practiced hands. He heard us at a distance I would have thought too great for hearing and came upright in a single movement with a bronze knife already drawn.

I will not claim I was unafraid. The knife was real and the man was larger than me and the space between the stacked cargo and the waystation wall was narrow. What I did — and I record this from accuracy, not pride — was step into the space on Akar-Teshub's left when the man feinted right, because it seemed to me that the left side was where the pressure needed to be. Ramuwa-Telab's soldiers managed the rest. It was not elegant. I had a cut along my forearm that was superficial and bled more than it warranted.

Akar-Teshub, once the man was secured, looked at my arm with diagnostic precision. "Press it," he said. Then he turned to the jars.

He poured the oil from the outermost vessel and asked Ramuwa-Telab's men to describe the weight of what remained.

They described it.

"Too heavy," he said.

The base of each jar was clay — unfired clay, the same colour and texture as the fired exterior but still workable. Embedded in the base of each jar, wrapped in oiled linen, were small flat pieces of worked bone. And on the bone pieces, in marks that I recognised the moment I saw them — because I had spent three evenings watching Akar-Teshub study the Piyassilis fragment by lamplight and had learned the shape of what I was looking at — were the same angular impressions. The same western coastal syllabary. Not decorative. Not unknown.

I understood, looking at those marks, that Piyassilis's dead traveller and Kirunda's sealed jars were the same channel, interrupted for a season by a fever and resumed with a better courier. I understood that the fragment in the curiosity archive was not a curiosity at all. It was the proof that someone at this gate had been receiving this traffic for longer than six years, and had been adjusting records to hide it.

Akar-Teshub read the bone pieces in the waystation courtyard while Kirunda slept.

He was quiet for a long time.

Then he said to Ramuwa-Telab's officer: "Reseal the jars. Exactly as they were. Return them to the load. And let the merchant proceed." A pause. "Not the servant. The servant does not proceed. Hold him on attempted theft from cargo he was employed to guard. Kirunda will not protest this."

He was right. Kirunda, presented with this accounting in the grey light of dawn, looked at Akar-Teshub for a single moment — a measuring look, the first I had seen him give, the look of a man revising his understanding — and said nothing, and moved his animals east with his sealed jars and his declaration of innocence worn as smoothly as a robe fitted over many years.

At the gate of the waystation Akar-Teshub fell into step beside him.

I had not expected this. I do not think Kirunda had expected it either.

"The oil jars," said Akar-Teshub, pleasantly, in the tone of a man remarking on the weather, "are nested for stability. A man who carried ritual oil across these roads for six years would know that fragile sealed vessels require padding on the exterior, not nesting at the centre. You have always known this — your earlier crossings show it. The change in loading method is recent. Whoever packed the jars this time was not thinking about oil."

Kirunda walked. He did not break stride. He did not look at Akar-Teshub.

"The second month of spring," said Akar-Teshub. "You have never crossed this gate in the second month of spring. Three times in six years, Mycenaean probes have tested our western positions in that month. I have the records. Sura kept them." He paused. "You are very good. Your seals are perfect, your weights are exact, your manner is precisely calibrated. You have rehearsed this crossing into a performance of innocence so complete that it became invisible."

Kirunda stopped. It was a controlled stop — the stop of a man who has decided to stop, not a man who has been halted. He turned and looked at Akar-Teshub with the full quality of his attention for the first time.

"You have nothing," he said. His voice was level. "A pattern in a scribe's seasonal tally. Jars loaded by a servant whose methods I cannot control. The word of soldiers who opened sealed merchandise without authorisation." He held Akar-Teshub's gaze. "I am a merchant in good standing. My seals are correct. My declarations are within tolerance. You know what I am and you cannot touch me, and we both understand this, and so I will continue east and you will return to your gate and we will not speak of this again."

He said it with the quiet certainty of a man who has identified the limits of his opponent's reach and is standing precisely outside them.

Akar-Teshub looked at him for a moment.

"Yes," he said. "You are entirely right."

He stepped aside. He made a small gesture — the bureaucratic gesture of a gate official clearing a traveller through. Kirunda looked at him for one further second, recalibrating, and then turned and walked his animals east.

Akar-Teshub watched him go. He was smiling, which I had not seen him do before. It was not a warm smile. It was the expression of a man who has just confirmed the final measurement he required.

"He knows the cargo has been read," he said quietly, to me. "He does not know what I know and what I do not know. He will send a warning east. Tonight, or tomorrow — he will find a way to signal his recipient that the channel is compromised." He turned. "Which means his recipient will move. And when his recipient moves, we will be watching where he moves from."

VI.

I will record what happened in the three weeks following Kirunda's departure, because it is the part the official record most thoroughly obscures.

The bone pieces contained what Akar-Teshub had anticipated: the adjusted patrol schedule, transcribed in the Aegean syllabary, addressed to a recipient described only by a mark — a specific seal impression that appeared on no merchant's registration at Hattusa, but which Akar-Teshub had seen before, in the captured documentation from his earlier coastal posting, as the personal cipher of a Mycenaean contact handler.

The adjusted schedule had travelled from the western military command to Kirunda to the jars. Which means someone inside the military dispatch system had fed it outward. Ramuwa-Telab, when Akar-Teshub presented this to him, sat very still for a considerable time.

"My dispatch office," he said.

"Or the western command's," said Akar-Teshub. "The patrol adjustment was filed through both. The question is which copy was accessed, and by whom, and when." He set the bone piece on the table between them. "But I think we will not need to answer that question through the records. I think Kirunda will answer it for us."

He was right.

Kirunda did not send a signal that night or the next day. He was, I suspect, too careful for that — he had survived six years by never acting in haste. But on the fourth day after his departure, a messenger arrived at the garrison from the western command: routine correspondence, routing a minor administrative query back through the Arinna gate for Ramuwa-Telab's attention.

The messenger had been on the road for two days. He had left the western command the day after Kirunda's caravan passed through its vicinity.

Akar-Teshub asked to see the messenger's travel documentation. He examined it in the way he examined everything — with the lamp held close, turning the tablet, saying nothing. Then he handed it back.

"The western command," he said to Ramuwa-Telab, "has a clerk named Ushhiti."

Ramuwa-Telab looked at him.

"He handles the patrol schedules for the northern routes. He joined the command three years before the first probe. He has a brother in the coastal trading towns." Akar-Teshub set the lamp down. "The messenger carried a secondary tablet — personal correspondence, declared as such, which we inspected and found to contain nothing of administrative concern. We found nothing because we did not know what we were looking for. I would like the messenger detained for one additional day on a pretext, and I would like the secondary tablet re-examined. Specifically the clay at the base of the seal impression. Specifically its weight."

The secondary tablet was heavier than fired clay of its dimensions warranted. The base was unfired. There was nothing embedded in it — no bone pieces, no text. There did not need to be. The tablet was a signal, not a message: a mark pressed into unfired clay that matched the cipher on the bone pieces. It said: move.

Ushhiti was removed from the western command's dispatch office three days later, on grounds of administrative irregularity, and transferred under escort to Hattusa for examination. I do not know what examination at Hattusa entailed for a man in his position. I have not asked.

Ramuwa-Telab, when this was done, sat in his office with the administrative transfer documents in his hands and looked at the wall for some time.

"He was there when I arrived," he said. "He was there when my predecessor arrived." He set the documents down. "He was at the Arinna gate before Kirunda. Before Piyassilis's dead traveller."

"Yes," said Akar-Teshub. "The channel is older than we thought. Kirunda was not the origin. He was a courier for a network that had already spent years learning the shape of this pass." He stood. "The network will find another courier. Another channel. But it will take time, and time is what we have purchased."

He looked at the documents on the table.

"Pala corrected something," he said. "Or he noticed something that did not match. Ushhiti was not at the Arinna gate — but someone told Kirunda's network that a new gatekeeper had a careful eye and an inconvenient habit of returning to entries." A pause. "We will not prove this. I want you to know it nonetheless."

Ramuwa-Telab looked at him. He looked, for the first time in my observation of him, like a man who is carrying something personal.

"I will note it," he said, "in the record that does not go to Hattusa."

VII.

Kirunda of Arzawa was killed by bandits on the coastal road two years after the crossing I have described. The official record of his death is ordinary. I have chosen not to examine it too closely, because some questions, once asked, require honest answers, and I am not certain the honest answer would be one I could record and remain the kind of scribe I have chosen to be.

The official credit for the failure of the year-eight Mycenaean probe — which came, as anticipated, and failed to find the patrol it had been timed around — went to the western military administration. Ramuwa-Telab received a commendation. He earned it: he gave Akar-Teshub the men and the latitude and the access to the speculative dispatches, and a commander who had refused all three would have made what followed impossible. The system received the credit. This is correct. A system that cannot function without a singular mind is a fragile system, and the empire does not survive on fragility.

Akar-Teshub was transferred the following year to a different posting, further north, on a route where a different pattern had begun to emerge. The towns he left behind at Arinna were, in the assessment of the western command, among the quietest on the frontier. They would not have been able to say exactly why.

He came to say goodbye — not to me, exactly; he came to make a final notation in the gate records, and I happened to be present, as I generally was. He looked older. Not in the way of illness, but in the way of a man who has spent a year processing something large and has not yet finished with it. He stood at the gate for a moment before he left, looking at the hinge.

"That socket," he said, "should have been replaced three months ago."

"Yes," I said.

He looked at me. The measuring quality in his eyes was, for a moment, something else — something warmer, or perhaps simply less contained, the way the same limestone looks different in different light.

"Your records," he said, "are the best I have encountered on any posting."

He walked out through the gate and did not look back.

VIII.

I recopied this account three times over the years that followed, adjusting the wording toward precision and away from sentiment, which is what he would have done. I have changed the name of the waystation. I have changed the number of animals in Kirunda's caravan. I have not changed anything that matters.

The scar on my forearm healed cleanly, which is more than I deserved for the rashness of the thing I did. But there are moments when the records demand a witness who will step into the space on the left, and that is not rashness — it is a different kind of accuracy.

The cedar-oil lamp is guttering. The gate-hinge has been replaced, and the new one is silent, which I find I do not entirely prefer. Pala would have reported the change in pitch the morning he heard it. I know this because I knew him, and because his record of that last working day is the most precise thing in the archive — every weight exact, every seal described, every cargo notation complete. He was that kind of man. What was done to him was done because he was that kind of man.

I am Sura, junior scribe of the imperial gatehouse at Arinna, and this is what I witnessed. What I have written here, I have written truly.

Let the next scribe who copies this tablet note that the numbers in the seasonal tally are exact. I counted them myself, twice.
