HOLMAR OF THE MEROVINGIANS

~490 AD — Frankish territory

Clovis I converted to Catholic Christianity in 496 AD — an act of political calculation as much as spiritual conviction, aligning the Frankish kingdom with the bishops of Rome and the remaining Roman population of Gaul in a single stroke of extraordinary strategic intelligence. Holmar of the Merovingians was a child in the Frankish world when the conversion reshaped it, and he grew up in the court it had transformed — recognising as an adult what he had absorbed as a boy: the gift applied at the scale of kingdoms, the reading of a situation — religious, political, demographic — and the identification of the single decisive act that would reshape it permanently.

He served in the Merovingian administration through the consolidation of the Constantinian realm, managing the correspondence between the court and the ecclesiastical hierarchy that was now the kingdom’s most important political relationship. He was a Catholic, as all sensible men became Catholic in that decade, and he wore the faith with the same comfortable practicality with which his ancestors had worn their successive identities — sincerely enough to function, without allowing it to obstruct his judgement.

The account that follows is preserved in the hand of Lucius, a clerk attached to the royal chancery in Reims, whose duty it was to copy, collate, and retain records of correspondence between the court and the bishops. His notes reflect what he was permitted to see, and the order in which he encountered it, but his record preserves the sequence of events with care, and his uncertainty at certain points is itself part of what survives.

The The matter of the Bishop of Nicaea and certain correspondence

Set down as faithfully as I am able, omitting those particulars which, were they to become known, would serve no one.

The fog that settles on Reims in the deep months does not blow in from anywhere. It rises. It comes up from the ground itself, from the river, from the low places between the old Roman walls, as though the earth is exhaling something it has held since the legions left. I have lived in this city seven years and I have not grown accustomed to it. The mornings smell of wet stone and old iron and something else I cannot name — the scent of a place that has been more things than it currently is, and remembers all of them.

It was on such a morning that I first saw the bishop’s mule tied at the gate.

I say the bishop’s mule because I recognised the animal before I recognised its owner — a grey-brown creature of particular ugliness, with a brand on its left flank that I had catalogued in correspondence the previous autumn, when the Bishop of Nicaea had sent three letters in rapid succession regarding the disposition of certain church properties in the valley of the Aisne. I had filed those letters. I remembered the seal. The mule’s brand matched what I had copied. It is the clerk’s habit to notice these things, though I confess I did not, at that time, understand what the noticing was worth.

Holmar was awake.

This is not a thing that can be stated simply, because Holmar’s relationship with sleep was not the relationship most men have with it. He did not retire in the evenings and rise in the mornings. He collapsed, occasionally, into a state resembling death, for periods ranging from two hours to two days, and then erupted into a condition of absolute, frightening attention that made the men around him feel slow and slightly guilty for having ordinary minds. I had been his clerk for fourteen months. I had learned to read the signs: the stillness that was not peace but calculation; the fingers that moved against his thigh as though writing something only he could see; the particular quality of his silence, which was different from the silence of a man thinking and different again from the silence of a man who has finished thinking and is simply waiting for everyone else to catch up.

He was in the latter state when I arrived that morning. He was standing at the window of the correspondence room, looking down at the courtyard, and he said, without turning:

“The Bishop of Nicaea has not slept. He has ridden through the night. He does not wish to be seen arriving.”

“I saw his mule,” I said.

“Yes.” He turned then, and I saw the expression that I had come to understand was not contempt but something more precise — a mild, professional disappointment, as at a calculation that contains no interesting errors. “You saw his mule. What else did you see?”

I had seen a courtyard, a gate, a fog. I said so.

“You saw a man’s travelling cloak folded across the saddle-pack rather than worn, in weather that demands it be worn. You saw boots that have been wiped but not cleaned — the mud is gone from the toe but remains in the seam, which means he stopped outside the city to present himself decently, but did so quickly, without light, without a servant. You saw a letter-case carried under the arm rather than in the pack, which means he does not trust the contents to be out of his reach.” He moved away from the window. “He is frightened of something he has received in writing. He has come to me because he cannot go to the court directly. Whatever is in that letter, he believes it would destroy him if the wrong person saw it first.”

He sat, and picked up a wax tablet he had apparently been working on before I arrived, and appeared to lose interest in the matter entirely.

“Shall I bring him up?” I asked.

“He has been waiting in the antechamber for twenty minutes,” Holmar said, without looking up. “He will have composed himself sufficiently by now. Yes. Bring him up.”

The Bishop of Nicaea was a man of perhaps fifty-five years, broad in the frame, with the hands of someone who had worked before he had prayed and had not entirely forgotten it. He had been a bishop for eleven years. He had navigated the conversion — the great seismic conversion, the one that had remade everything — with, as far as the official record showed, exemplary grace: he had written the right letters to the right men, attended the right councils, and emerged on the correct side of a transformation that had consumed less careful ecclesiastics entirely.

He sat across from Holmar with the posture of a man trying very hard not to appear afraid, which is to say, he was visibly afraid.

“I have received a letter,” he said.

“So I understood,” Holmar said. He was looking at the bishop with that quality of attention I had come to find unnerving — not the attention of a man who is listening, but of a man who is reading. “May I see it?”

The bishop produced it from beneath his cloak. He had not put it in the letter-case, I noticed. He had kept it against his body. Holmar took it, unfolded it, read it once, and set it on the table facing upward. He did not read it again. He looked instead at the bishop.

“Tell me the accommodation they refer to,” he said.

The bishop’s jaw tightened. “I don’t know what accommodation—”

“Bishop.” Holmar’s voice did not change in volume or warmth. It simply became more precise. “I manage the correspondence between this court and the ecclesiastical hierarchy of the Constantinian realm. I have done so for nine years. There is no bishop in this kingdom whose dealings with this court I do not have filed, summarised, and remembered. I ask not because I do not know there is an accommodation, but because I wish to hear your account of it, which will differ from the official record in ways that are relevant.”

A silence. Outside, the fog pressed against the window.

“In the winter of 484,” the bishop said finally, “before the — before everything changed — I received a request from the court of Syagrius. The last Roman ruler in Gaul. He was asking for the Church’s assistance in — in a matter of tax collection. There were Gothic foederati in three valleys who had not paid the Roman levy in four years. He wished the Church to — to apply persuasion. Spiritual persuasion.”

“And you did.”

“I wrote three letters. To three of my priests. Advising them to make clear to their congregations that — that the obligations of God and of Caesar were not in conflict.”

“You used the Church’s authority to collect Roman taxes from Gothic foederati on behalf of a Roman governor,” Holmar said, “two years before Clovis defeated that same ruler and absorbed his territory into the unified realm.”

The bishop said nothing.

“And the letter you have received suggests that this accommodation — service to Rome against the Franks — constitutes a disloyalty to the current regime, and threatens to bring it to the court’s attention unless certain conditions are met.”

“Yes.”

“What conditions?”

The bishop looked at his hands. “The letter names no conditions. It says only that the matter is known, and that the writer will determine, in time, what use to make of the knowledge.”

Holmar was quiet for a moment. Then he picked up the letter again. He did not read it. He held it, turning it in his fingers, feeling its weight and texture with a thoughtfulness that seemed almost medical.

“You may go,” he said.

The bishop blinked. “I — I beg your pardon?”

“I will send for you when I have something to tell you. Go. Eat something. Sleep, if you are capable of it.” He set the letter down. “Leave this with me.”

I have said that Holmar’s relationship with sleep was unusual. His relationship with food was stranger. He did not eat during a case. He did not, as far as I could observe, notice hunger. He sat in the correspondence room for the remainder of that day and most of the night with the bishop’s letter and seven years of filed correspondence spread across the table, and he moved through it with a systematic, unhurried attention that I found both impressive and slightly frightening.

He sent me for specific files. Not categories of files — specific letters, specific dates, specific senders. He knew what he was looking for before he looked for it, which is the only explanation I have for the precision of his requests.

“The letters from the Council of Orléans, the winter session,” he said at one point. “The attendance record and the drafts of the three disciplinary motions.”

I found them. He read them. He set them aside.

“The correspondence from Bishop Remigius regarding the preparation of the baptismal ceremony. All of it. Including the drafts.”

I found those too. There were eleven documents. He read each one and arranged them in a sequence that did not correspond to their dates.

“Now,” he said, holding the bishop’s threatening letter beside one of Remigius’s drafts, “do you see?”

I looked. I saw two letters. I said so.

“You see two letters,” he said, with that particular patience that was not quite patience. “I see one hand attempting to disguise itself as two different hands. Look at the e. The way the second stroke lifts before the scribe intends it to — a habit, not a decision. Look at the spacing between words when a line approaches the right margin. A man who was taught by someone who pressed from the left. Both letters. The same teacher. Possibly — though this I cannot confirm from examination alone — the same man.”

He set both letters down.

“Someone in the ecclesiastical hierarchy of this kingdom,” he said, “wrote a threatening letter to a bishop. They did not write it to extract money or favours. They named no conditions because they want nothing material. They wrote it to test him. To see whether, under pressure, he would run to the court — which would reveal him as a man who distrusts the Church — or whether he would run to the Church — which would reveal him as a man who distrusts the court — or whether he would go to ground and say nothing, which would reveal him as a man with something to hide.” He paused. “He came to me. Which is the correct answer, though the people who sent the letter did not anticipate it as a possibility.”

“Who sent it?” I asked.

He did not answer immediately. He was looking at the attendance record from the Council of Orléans.

“The new order,” he said, “requires loyalty tests. The Church has absorbed a great many men who served the old arrangements — Roman governors, Arian accommodators, political Christians who wore the faith like a travelling cloak and removed it when the weather changed. Bishop Remigius and his circle are building something permanent. They need to know who can be trusted. A threatening letter is a crude instrument, but it is efficient.” He closed the file. “The Bishop of Nicaea is being evaluated, not destroyed.”

“And the accommodation? The Roman tax matter?”

“Is irrelevant,” Holmar said. “Every bishop in this kingdom made accommodations. Constantine knew this. The emperor did not convert because his bishops were pure — he converted because the bishops were useful, and the bishops were useful because they were already embedded in the social fabric of Gaul, which required them to have dealt with whoever was governing that fabric at the time. An accommodation made in 484 to a Roman governor is, in the new political reality, evidence of pragmatism. It is not a crime. It is a qualification.”

He stood, moving to the window. The fog was still there. It had not shifted in twelve hours.

“The letter is not a threat,” he said. “It is an audition.”

The following morning, he sent for the Bishop of Nicaea, and he also sent — this was the part that surprised me, and I record it because it surprised me, which means it was deliberate — a note to the chancery of Bishop Remigius, requesting that the archdeacon responsible for the winter correspondence might be available to attend a brief administrative consultation.

The archdeacon arrived first. He was a young man, thin-faced and careful, with the eyes of someone accustomed to reading other people’s letters and writing nothing incautious in his own. He sat in the antechamber and waited.

The Bishop of Nicaea arrived and was shown to the correspondence room. Holmar kept him standing.

“I know who sent the letter,” Holmar said, without preface. “I know why. And I know what you should do.”

“Who—”

“That is not the useful question. The useful question is what the sending of the letter means, and what your response to it should be.” He moved to the table, where the filed correspondence was still spread. “The accommodation of 484 is not a liability. It is a record of a man who served the institution of the Church consistently across a change of regime — who used the Church’s authority as the Church’s authority, in the service of order and of obligation, regardless of which temporal power was making the request. Bishop Remigius does not need bishops who were loyal to the new capital before the conversion. He cannot find any — there were none. He needs bishops who were loyal to the Church before the conversion, and whose loyalty to the Franks now is an extension of that prior loyalty, not a replacement of it. Your letter of 484 demonstrates exactly that.”

The bishop stared at him. “They are not trying to destroy me.”

“They are trying to determine whether you are destroyable. There is a difference. Men who panic when threatened are dangerous in a hierarchy — they make unpredictable alliances, they seek protection in wrong places, they become liabilities. Men who hold steady, or who find the right counsel, are reliable.” He picked up the threatening letter. “You will not respond to this letter. You will do nothing. Within thirty days, you will receive an invitation — an entirely ordinary administrative invitation — to attend a meeting in Remigius’s chancery. You will attend. You will be offered a role in the new synodal structure that is being built. You will accept it.”

He held the letter out to the bishop, who took it.

“Burn that,” Holmar said. “It has served its purpose.”

The bishop left.

Holmar walked to the antechamber, where the archdeacon was waiting, and sat across from him with a pleasant and entirely uncommunicative expression.

“I have nothing for the chancery today,” he told the young man. “I apologise for the inconvenience. The matter resolved itself.”

The archdeacon looked at him for a moment with those careful, reading eyes, and then nodded, and left.

When he was gone, Holmar returned to the correspondence room and began filing the documents back in their correct places.

“You wanted him to know you knew,” I said.

“I wanted him to know that the channel between this court and the Church runs through me,” Holmar said, “and that anything passing through that channel — including loyalty tests conducted in the form of anonymous letters — is known to me. I wanted him to carry that information back to Remigius’s chancery without my having stated it directly, which would have required a response, which would have been complicated.” He closed a file. “Now Remigius knows that I know how the Church works. And I know that Remigius knows I know. And neither of us will need to discuss it, which is the ideal condition for a working relationship.”

He sat down. He was suddenly, visibly exhausted — the collapse that followed the energy, as predictable as the tide.

“Is there anything else?” I asked.

“There is always something else,” he said, closing his eyes. “But it can wait until I have slept.”

I have set this account down as faithfully as I am able, which is to say I have recorded what I witnessed and have omitted what I was not present for and what I was asked not to include.

The Bishop of Nicaea received his invitation, as Holmar said he would, and was given a position in the new synodal structure, where he served with distinction for fourteen years. The official record of the synodal appointments credits Bishop Remigius with the identification and elevation of his subordinates, which is accurate, as far as it goes.

The threatening letter is not in any archive I have access to. I assume it was burned.

Holmar continued to manage the correspondence between the court and the ecclesiastical hierarchy for a further eleven years, until Clovis’s death, after which he retired to a property in the valley of the Marne and, as far as I know, applied his particular gifts to the management of his own estate, where there was presumably less fog and fewer bishops.

Whether he was the first man to do what he did in the way that he did it, I cannot say. I suspect not. The capacity he possessed — the reading of a situation at every level simultaneously, the identification of the thing beneath the stated thing — is not a skill that can be acquired through study, or I would have acquired it, having watched him for fourteen months and copied more of his correspondence than I care to calculate.

I record this account for the same reason I record everything: because someone should, and because the official record will not.

Lucius, clerk of the royal chancery at Reims, in the fortieth year of the reign of Clovis, King of the Franks.
