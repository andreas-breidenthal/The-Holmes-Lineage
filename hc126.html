SHERLOCK HOLMES II

1892 — London, England

He was born in 1892 in Montenegro, the twin son of Sherlock Holmes and Irene Adler — born during the Great Hiatus, in the years when London mourned a detective who was not, in fact, dead. His mother was a woman of formidable intelligence who understood, with great precision, the value of invisibility; she raised him with the careful habits of someone who knew that knowledge, properly ordered, was the only reliable protection a person could acquire. He was brought to England in due course, and raised in respectable obscurity in Holborn, his parentage a matter of inference rather than record.

The boy grew up knowing, in the way that such things are known — not told but observed, deduced, confirmed by the particular quality of his own mind when set against the minds of those around him — exactly who his father was. When his father returned from the Great Hiatus in 1894, the acquaintance was made; and when the Great Detective retired to Sussex in 1903, it continued — quietly, discreetly, and on terms that suited both men’s instinct for operating without advertisement.

Watson encountered Holmes II once in that same autumn of 1903, when the boy was ten years old and already possessed of that quality of watchful attention that the old doctor, who had spent twenty years in its company, could not mistake. He noted, in a private memorandum that survived among his papers, that the boy’s eyes were his father’s exactly — and that when introduced to a stranger, he had spent the first thirty seconds in a silence that was not shyness but assessment. Watson did not record what conclusion the boy had reached. He recorded that the stranger, a solicitor of his acquaintance, had been visibly unsettled by it. What Watson also noted, though he did not dwell on it, was that the boy had apparently just come from the Sussex Downs, and that he spoke of his father with the matter-of-fact ease of someone who had recently been in his company.

The record that follows is drawn from the surviving notebooks of John H. Watson II, son of Dr John Watson, who had been raised in the shadow of his father’s long association with the elder Holmes and who, by temperament and training, inherited both a habit of careful observation and a willingness to set down what he saw without embellishment. It is through his hand that the account of the following inquiry has been preserved, and his voice, where it intrudes, reflects the perspective of one who stood close enough to the work to witness its method, though not always close enough to grasp its full structure until after the fact.

The Death of Mr. Septimus Crane

From the Notebooks of John H. Watson II, written in the winter of 1921, concerning events of October 1913

I have debated with myself, across the eight years that separate these events from the moment of their writing, whether the account that follows ought ever to be set down at all. My father, had he lived to read it, would have recognised in its central figure something that would have moved him deeply — not the method, which he had seen before in its purest form, but the particular quality of the loneliness behind the method, which was both inherited and entirely the man’s own. He would also, I think, have advised caution.

The officer whose name appears in these pages was tried under a provision that permitted the suppression of the trial’s evidentiary record, and was hanged in a courtyard at the Tower of London on a Tuesday morning in the spring of 1914 with no more public ceremony than the discharge of a debt. The official record attributes his identification to the Directorate itself. It does not mention Sherlock Holmes II.

It never does.

I have decided to write it down because I was there, because I saw what was done and how it was done, and because the method deserves a witness, as my father always said it did. A truth that goes unrecorded is not preserved; it is merely delayed in its extinction.

I set it down now, and trust to time.

The fog that October had a particular quality I have not encountered before or since. London fogs are frequently described — my father made something of a speciality of them — but this one carried in its composition something industrial and specific: a sulphurous underlayer from the Holborn gas mains that had been disturbed by resurfacing works in High Holborn the previous week, and above that the more familiar autumnal rot of the plane trees, and above that, when the wind came off the river, the cold iron smell of a city that was, though none of us yet understood it, preparing unconsciously for war.

Holmes had sent a note to my rooms in Montague Street at half past six in the morning. It read:

Holborn. The dead man near Cursitor Street. Come before they move him. Bring your father’s notebook — the brown one, Morocco binding — you will understand why when you arrive. — H.

The body had been found at approximately five in the morning by a post-sorter named Griffiths. By the time I arrived, the constable had been reinforced by an Inspector from the CID, a solid, careful man named Prentiss.

Holmes was already there.

He was crouched beside the body with the focused stillness of a man reading a document he finds genuinely absorbing.

“He’s a government cipher clerk,” I said.

Holmes looked up at last.

“War Office, specifically,” he said. “His name is Septimus Crane. He was killed between midnight and two in the morning by a single blow to the base of the skull… He had, also at the time of his death, been carrying a document. He is no longer carrying it.”

He rose.

“Now,” he said, “the question is not who killed him. That will resolve itself within the hour. The question is which document he was carrying.”

The matter proceeded from there with a speed and precision that I will not attempt to fully reconstruct in this space. Holmes identified, through a combination of cipher analysis, administrative inference, and the reading of institutional behaviour, a breach within the Directorate of Military Operations. A cipher clerk had discovered a hidden layer within a decoded message. He had attempted to carry it to a superior. That superior had killed him and taken the document.

Within nine hours, Holmes had reduced the field to a single man: Major Hugh Calloway.

The evidence, once assembled, was overwhelming — financial motive, familial connection to the foreign intermediary, access to the cipher material, and the administrative misstep of filing multiple transfer requests in the early hours following the murder.

“The technical case is complete,” Holmes said. “You have everything you need.”

Major Hugh Calloway was arrested, tried in closed session, and executed in April of 1914.

The official record credits his identification to the Directorate.

Holmes’s name does not appear.

Septimus Crane’s sister received a pension. She told me she did not fully understand what her brother had done, but that she was glad somebody had.

I did not tell her about the fog, or the lamp at Cursitor Street, or the manner in which a man had been read, completely, within a single day.

Some things are better as inferences.

— J.H.W. II, December 1921
