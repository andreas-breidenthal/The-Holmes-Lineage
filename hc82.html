HOLMAR THE FRANK

~460 AD — Frankish territory

By Holmar the Frank’s generation, the family had completed its absorption into the Frankish world — the Gothic origins faded, the Rhine crossing two generations past, the name now sounding native to the country north of the Loire where the Franks were consolidating their hold on what had been Roman Gaul. He was a Frank in the way that his ancestors had been Romans and before that Greeks and before that Phoenicians: thoroughly, for as long as it lasted, and with one eye always on the horizon.

He administered land in the service of a Frankish noble house, a role that combined the functions of estate manager, legal officer, and intelligence agent — for the assessment of neighbouring estates, the loyalty of tenants, and the intentions of rival lords was not separable, in that world, from the daily business of keeping an account. He had his father’s gift for reading documents and his grandfather’s gift for reading men, and he applied both to the service of a family that was not his own with the professional detachment of someone who understood that competence was its own form of security.

The account that follows was recorded by Marcus, a notary in the household of Childeric, who kept careful notes of court proceedings and private consultations alike. His record is partial, shaped by what he was permitted to observe and by what he understood of the matters before him, yet it preserves with fidelity the sequence of events and the manner in which Holmar conducted himself.

The death of Wulfric the steward

Recorded by Marcus, notary to the household of Childeric, lord of the eastern lands, in the year of our Lord four hundred and ninety-two. What I have set down here is what I was permitted to observe. What I have omitted, I omit for reasons that will become apparent to any careful reader.

The body was found at the boundary stone between the estate of Childeric and the estate of his neighbour Gundomar — found, it was said, in the grey hour before dawn, by a swineherd who had no business being there and who could not satisfactorily explain what business he did have. The dead man was Wulfric, who had served Gundomar as steward for eleven years, a careful man, thin-fingered and methodical, known throughout the district for the precision of his accounts and the smallness of his personal habits. He had been struck once across the back of the skull. His scrip was intact. His boots — good boots, better than his station warranted — were still on his feet. Nothing had been taken from him, which is the first thing a man notices, and the first thing a careful man notes as significant.

It was Gundomar himself who came to Childeric with the report, arriving before the morning had properly established itself, his horse sweated and his expression arranged into the correct proportions of grief and outrage. He named a tenant of Childeric’s outer fields — a man called Hesso, who had been in dispute with Wulfric over the grazing line for the better part of two seasons — as the likely culprit. The logic was plain, he said. The boundary stone was the seat of the quarrel. The quarrel was Hesso’s. Hesso therefore was the man.

Childeric sent for Holmar.

I had seen Holmar at work before, and I had learned by then not to mistake his stillness for inattention. He sat in the hall and received Gundomar’s account without expression, his long hands resting flat on the table before him, his eyes moving between Gundomar’s face and the middle distance with the regularity of a man reading alternate lines of a document. When Gundomar had finished, Holmar thanked him with a courtesy so precisely calibrated as to contain no warmth whatsoever, and asked three questions.

He asked: at what hour had Gundomar last spoken with Wulfric the previous day?

He asked: had Wulfric carried any documents with him when he departed the estate?

He asked: was it usual for Wulfric to walk that road after dark?

Gundomar answered the first question with confidence — mid-afternoon, after the counting of the autumn tithe — and the second with dismissiveness — a steward always carried papers, this was unremarkable — and the third with a slight rearrangement of his features that I noted and that Holmar, I am certain, noted before me.

Not usual, Gundomar said. But Wulfric was a man of his own habits.

Holmar thanked him again, with the same precise courtesy, and asked to see the body.

The boundary stone stood at the edge of a woodlot, where the tree line broke and the cleared land of both estates met in a narrow strip of disputed pasture. The grass was wet. The morning had produced a low mist that sat in the hollows and made the distances uncertain. I had followed Holmar out of professional duty and found myself regretting the decision as the cold settled into my writing hand.

Holmar crouched beside the dead man for a long time without speaking. He examined the wound — that single blow, delivered from behind and above, by a right-handed man of ordinary height striking someone who had not been running, had not turned, had perhaps been standing still and waiting. He examined the boots. He examined the scrip, lifting from it three wax tablets and a folded skin of parchment, which he read in sequence with the same neutral attention he would have given to a bill of sale, then replaced without comment. He examined the ground around the body in expanding arcs, moving outward from the stone in a pattern that, from a distance, looked aimless and was not.

When he stood, he turned to me with the expression of a man who has finished reading a letter and understood every word of it.

“The swineherd,” he said. “Where does he live?”

The swineherd was a frightened man, which was not in itself remarkable. A man who discovers a corpse at a boundary stone before dawn has reasons for fright that require no elaboration. But Holmar had developed a theory about frightened men — he shared it with me as we walked, in the manner of a craftsman explaining his tools to an apprentice who has expressed no interest in the craft.

“There are two kinds of frightened men,” he said, his long stride carrying him easily over the wet ground while I laboured to keep pace. “Those who are frightened by what they have seen, and those who are frightened by what they have done. The second type does not look at you when they speak of the thing. The first type cannot stop looking at you, because they want to confirm that you believe them.”

“And which type is the swineherd?”

“I haven’t spoken to him yet,” Holmar said, with a patience that was not unkind. “I am explaining the distinction so that you will know which to observe for.”

The swineherd looked at us the entire time he spoke. He was a small man who smelled of woodsmoke and the animals he kept, and he could not have arranged his expression into deception if his life had depended upon it, which — in those days, in that territory — was a real possibility and not a figure of speech. He had found the body. He had not been there to find it — he had been, he admitted in the reddening manner of a man confessing to something minor in the shadow of something vast, meeting a girl from Gundomar’s milk-house at the woodlot edge, a thing that had been happening since midsummer and that his wife did not know about. He had come to the boundary stone and found Wulfric already cold.

He had not, he swore it, seen anyone. But he had, when pressed, heard something — a horse, moving away at a walk through the woodlot, deeper into Gundomar’s land. This he had not mentioned because he had not wished to be noticed having been where he was.

Holmar thanked him and advised him, with a gravity that the swineherd found disproportionate to the situation, to tell his wife about the girl before someone else found occasion to.

We returned to the hall of Childeric, and Holmar asked for the boundary records.

This is where I must make a confession of professional embarrassment. I had been present at the production of those records two seasons prior. I had copied three of them myself. I knew their contents, or believed I did, in the way that a man who has written a thing believes he has read it. Holmar proved to me, in the space of one hour, the difference between transcription and comprehension.

The disputed boundary was not simply a matter of where a stone stood. It was a matter of what a stone certified. Eleven years ago, before Wulfric had come into Gundomar’s service, Gundomar had borrowed a significant sum from a consortium of merchants in the manner common to lords who require immediate silver and prefer to defer the consequences — pledging future tithe revenues from three of his northern fields as security, with a schedule of repayment that had been carefully documented and as carefully neglected. The debt had passed through hands. It had been restructured twice. And two seasons ago, it had landed, through the mechanism of one merchant’s death and another’s acquisition of his papers, in the possession of Wulfric himself — who had, in the methodical way of a man who understood accounts, been quietly assembling the documentation necessary to present a legal claim.

“He was going to the boundary stone,” Holmar said, not to me but as though speaking a thing he was confirming for himself, “because the boundary stone is where such matters are formally presented under the old custom. A creditor may meet a debtor at the boundary of his estate and present the claim before witnesses. Wulfric had assembled his witnesses — there were two, noted in his personal tablets, who were to meet him there at dawn.”

“Then they are suspects,” I said, because something had to be said.

“They are frightened men who went home when they found the stone unattended and the hour past and thought better of their involvement. I will speak to them if you require it, but I do not require it.” He set the parchment down. “Gundomar knew what Wulfric was carrying. He knew the hour. He knew the road, because it is his road — Wulfric could not have reached that stone without walking through Gundomar’s eastern woodlot, which means Gundomar knew, or could have known, precisely when and where his steward would be standing still, in the dark, waiting.”

He paused. The lamp between us smelled of rendered fat, and the hall had grown very quiet in the way halls grow quiet when the fire settles and the night deepens.

“The blow,” Holmar continued, “was patient. A man who fears being found out strikes in panic — multiple blows, uneven, driven by feeling. This was one blow. Precise. The blow of a man who has decided, thought it through, and gone to the place in advance to wait. The scrip was not taken because taking it would have raised precisely the question it was taken to prevent — where are the documents? By leaving it, and leaving it apparently undisturbed, he hoped the tablets would be seen as routine steward’s papers, and that in the confusion of a tenant dispute, no one would read them carefully.”

I looked at the tablets. At the three lines of notation in Wulfric’s small, regular hand, naming the amount, the parties, and the date of intended presentation.

“He didn’t expect anyone to look,” I said.

“He expected Hesso to be arrested before anyone thought to look,” Holmar said. “Which would have been the conclusion of a man following the obvious path. The quarrel was real. The grievance was real. Gundomar simply borrowed it.”

What followed was not a simple matter, and I will not pretend otherwise.

Holmar brought his conclusions to Childeric in private, and I was not present for that conversation, being a notary and not a confidential advisor, and the distinction mattered. What I observed was the outcome: that Gundomar was summoned, that the debt documents were produced, that Gundomar’s expression upon seeing them was the expression of a man who has reached for a weapon and found the scabbard empty. What I observed was that Childeric — who was, in his way, a careful lord — accepted the presentation of evidence with the gravity it warranted, and that certain arrangements were made.

Holmar told me, afterward, what those arrangements were. He did not need to. He chose to, because he understood that I would record this, and that the record was the point.

“Gundomar will pay the debt,” he said. “The full sum, with the customary interest, to a fund that Childeric will administer for the benefit of Wulfric’s widow and children, of whom there are four. Gundomar will cede the disputed pasture to Childeric’s estate, formally and by document, which resolves the grazing question entirely and removes the boundary stone as a site of future contention. And Gundomar will, in recognition of his long service to the stability of the district, be permitted to retain his estate and his position, and this matter will be concluded.”

I looked at him.

“He killed a man,” I said.

“He did.”

“And he will not—”

“Hesso will not be charged,” Holmar said, which was not what I had asked but was what I needed to hear first. “Wulfric’s widow will not spend the winter without silver. The four children will be educated. The debt is paid. The pasture is settled. Gundomar will administer his estate for the remainder of his life in the knowledge that Childeric holds the documentation, that I retain a copy, and that any recurrence of his particular method of resolving financial difficulties will result in a conversation of a very different character.”

“That is not justice,” I said.

“No,” Holmar agreed, without apparent distress. “It is what justice produces when it operates through institutions capable of administering it. The alternative is a feud, a counter-claim, Gundomar’s allies on one side and Childeric’s on the other, and the pasture soaked in a great deal more blood than it can reasonably absorb.” He looked at me with the expression of a man who has made this calculation before and found it does not become easier. “I am not content with it. I am resolved to it. There is a difference, which I would ask you to record.”

I have recorded it.

I have recorded also — because Holmar asked me to, and because the habit of accurate notation is the only virtue a notary can claim without embarrassment — that Hesso the tenant came to Holmar three days later with a gift of bread and a young goat, and that Holmar accepted the bread and returned the goat with the explanation that he had nowhere to keep it.

I have recorded that the milk-house girl married the swineherd’s eldest son the following spring, which simplified several complications at once.

I have recorded that Wulfric’s eldest daughter, who had her father’s thin fingers and her father’s eye for a column of figures, entered Childeric’s household as a junior account-keeper at Holmar’s recommendation, and that she was, by all accounts, exceptionally good at the work.

These things are not in the official record of Childeric’s court, which notes the peaceful resolution of a boundary dispute and the unfortunate unsolved death of a steward on a contested road.

The official record is correct, as far as it goes.

This account goes further. I set it down in the knowledge that no one who matters will read it in my lifetime, and in the hope that someone who needs it will read it after.

— Marcus, notary, in the service of Childeric
Written in the forty-third year of my life, in the hall of the eastern estate, by lamplight, in the cold.
