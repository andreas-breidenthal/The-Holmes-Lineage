GUNNNARR HOLMR

~990 AD — Yorkshire, England

Gunnarr Holmr grew up in the reign of Æthelred — not the later years of crisis and Danegeld, but the earlier decades when the administration inherited from Edgar the Peaceful still functioned with something of its predecessor’s order, and when the North Riding enjoyed a period of relative stability that suited a man of Gunnarr’s type: methodical, observant, trusted equally by both communities, without the sharp edges that periods of crisis tend to produce and periods of peace make unnecessary.

He worked as a wool assessor and market overseer in the North Riding — an official role in the local economy, responsible for certifying weights, resolving disputes between traders, and detecting the falsifications of measure and quality that were the endemic fraud of every medieval market. He was very good at it. He had, colleagues noted, a peculiar facility for identifying adulterated goods — wool mixed with inferior fibres, ale diluted beyond what custom permitted, grain that had been stored wet and dried artificially before sale — by examination alone, without needing to be told what he was looking for. He simply looked, and found it. He was not a popular figure among those who had something to conceal; he was well regarded by everyone else.

The account of his work is preserved in part through the notes of Wulfstan’s successor, a younger clerk named Wulfstan, whose hand shows both the precision of training and the strain of witnessing the scale of what was unfolding. Wulfstan’s record is not as complete as earlier accounts, but it captures with clarity the moments when Gunnarr’s attention moved from isolated irregularities to patterns that demanded explanation.

The Casebook of Wulfstan

Set down in the thirty-second year of Æthelred’s reign, that those who come after may know what the record of the court cannot say openly.

There is a particular quality to the fog that settles on the Ouse valley in the last weeks of autumn. It presses against the face, carrying the smell of wet stone, tallow, and river iron. It was in such a fog that I received the message that Ásmundr Feit had been found dead among his bales.

Ásmundr Feit was a wool merchant of middling standing, neither poor nor wealthy, who had built his trade over twenty years. His warehouse stood behind the market cross. His death was a disruption rather than a grief.

The message came while I was completing certification records. Gunnarr Holmr was already at the warehouse by the time I arrived. He was always already there.

Gunnarr was perhaps forty years of age, lean, with grey eyes that seemed to miss nothing. He carried himself with the economy of motion of a man who had learned that most movement is unnecessary. He had periods of deliberate idleness, followed by sudden, complete focus when a problem presented itself. The change was startling: something in him shifted from stillness into function.

He worked with dried rosemary between his fingers, crushing it as he thought. He said it clarified thought. I do not know if this was true, but with Gunnarr the distinction between truth and method was not always meaningful.

I found him crouched beside the body, not looking at it but at the floor and surrounding bales. The lamp beside him filled the warehouse with the sour sweetness of tallow.

Ásmundr lay between the second and third rows of bales. His face was undamaged. His skull was not.

The reeve Oswulf was already present. He believed, correctly within his framework, that the task was to identify who had most motive for murder and proceed accordingly.

Ketill Ormsson was named immediately. A debt dispute had occurred days before. Oswulf intended to arrest him before midday.

Gunnarr did not agree or disagree. He said only: “I am still looking at the floor.”

He examined the second row of bales — not those nearest the body — and pressed his thumb into the fleece.

“These have been altered,” he said. “The outer layer is original fleece. The interior is inferior stock, mixed and dried artificially.”

He moved through all six certified bales. They were identical in condition.

“Six bales,” he said. “The same six I certified.”

The implication was immediate: substitution after certification.

Gunnarr traced the method step by step. The substitution required prior knowledge of which bales had been selected, access to the warehouse, and preparation of replacement goods. This was not opportunistic crime. It was planned in advance.

He examined the warehouse walls and found scrape marks in the mortar — fresh, low to the ground. The replacement bales had been staged against the wall, hidden behind the originals, then moved after his inspection.

“Someone was present during the certification,” he said. “And someone communicated the selection afterward.”

Attention turned to Leofstan, the warehouse boy. He had been present during the inspection and had access to the keys. His father had been guarantor for Ketill Ormsson’s debt.

Under questioning, he confessed to passing information in exchange for debt relief. He claimed no knowledge of murder.

Gunnarr concluded that Ketill had used the information to retrieve original certified wool, replacing it with inferior stock for sale elsewhere. Ásmundr had then discovered the substitution upon re-entry to the warehouse.

Further examination of the doorway revealed scrape marks at shoulder height consistent with a heavy load being carried sideways through the entrance. Gunnarr identified the likely path of removal and the number of missing bales.

Four bales were missing. Their capacity matched the discrepancy in the records.

Gunnarr identified the night watchman, Ku-Enlil, as the only individual capable of removing such goods repeatedly without record, due to his unrestricted access during night hours.

He reconstructed the sequence: removal over time, concealment within the official accounting records, and eventual discovery by the assessor.

“That is why he is dead,” he said of Ásmundr. “He saw the discrepancy.”

The watchman was arrested.

Later, I asked Gunnarr whether the boy had been culpable. He answered that ignorance of consequence does not remove participation in cause, but that judgment lay elsewhere than his role.

He did not pursue the boy further. He had answered only what he had been asked.

The official record credited the reeve’s process. That was correct in form, if incomplete in substance.

What remained was Gunnarr’s method: the reading of absence, the detection of substitution, and the reconstruction of intent from irregularity.

He did not speculate. He observed, compared, and resolved.

Nothing, in my experience of Gunnarr Holmr, was ever truly hidden.
