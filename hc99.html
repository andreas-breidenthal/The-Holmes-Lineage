HALFDAN HOLMR

~1020 AD — Yorkshire, England

The name Halfdan — half-Dane — was, by the time he bore it, almost an anachronism; the Danelaw had dissolved into Cnut’s England, and then into the restored English monarchy after him, and the distinction between Danish and English in the North Riding was less one of culture than of memory. Halfdan Holmr was English in everything that mattered legally and Danish in the oral traditions of his household — the stories told at the fire, the names given to children, the particular cast of a family mind that had come, centuries ago, from Vestfold.

He served as a reeve — a local official responsible for the Crown’s interests in a district of the North Riding — in the unsettled years after Cnut’s death, when the succession lurched between Danish and English claimants before settling, temporarily, on Edward the Confessor. The position required constant negotiation between the administrative continuity that the local community needed and the political volatility that successive royal changes produced, a negotiation that Halfdan conducted with the family’s characteristic facility for finding the position that was simultaneously principled and survivable. He was a careful man and an honest one, which in the world of 1040 required a certain physical courage as well as intellectual habit.

The record of his work is preserved in the hand of Wulfstan, the same clerk who had earlier documented Gunnarr’s assessments, now older and more practiced, his script tighter and more deliberate. Wulfstan’s notes reflect both the discipline of a trained scribe and the strain of recording events that moved beyond isolated fraud into something that threatened the stability of local governance itself. His account survives in the margins of administrative tallies, where he recorded not only outcomes but the sequence of observations that led to them.

An Account Set Down by Wulfstan

For the preservation of truth where the official record must, of necessity, remain silent.

The beck had been running dark for three days before anyone thought to look upstream.

That is how I remember it beginning — not with the body of Æthelric the reeve, which we found soon enough, but with the water. The beck that fed the mill at Thornby ran black in the mornings, not the clean black of peat-water from the moor but something thicker, something that left a residue of iron-smell on the stones. The mill-wife, Gytha, had complained of it to her husband, who had done nothing, because complaining to a mill-wife was a thing one endured, not acted upon. I note this only because Halfdan, when I mentioned it to him much later, stopped quite still and said: “Of course. The body was in the water before it was on the bank. Someone moved him. That is the first thing they gave us.”

He had a way of saying us that made you feel briefly clever, before the full weight of what he meant settled on you and you understood you had contributed nothing whatsoever.

Æthelric had ridden into the Thornby district on a Tuesday, carrying the assessment writs that gave him authority to survey landholding records on behalf of the Crown. This was the ordinary work of a reeve’s circuit — the long, grinding business of verifying that the hidage returns matched the land, that the plough-teams recorded existed in fact as well as parchment, that the sokemen who claimed their strips had not quietly absorbed a neighbor’s holding during the confusion of the last succession. It was work that made a man unpopular. Æthelric had been good at it precisely because he was not susceptible to the usual pressures — neither the offer of a night’s hospitality that became a week’s, nor the quiet suggestion that a reeve who found nothing irregular might find his own interests well looked-after.

He was found on the Thursday morning, face-down in the shallows where the beck curved below the old ash-tree. His assessment records were gone.

Sigeberht, the king’s officer for the Riding, arrived from York by Friday noon. He was a capable man in the way that large administrative bodies sometimes produce capable men — he knew procedure, he knew how to question a household, he knew that a murdered royal official required a murderer found quickly, because an example not made promptly ceased to be an example. He had, by the time Halfdan arrived on Friday evening, already identified his most likely suspect: a sokeman named Thorkel, who had quarreled with Æthelric the previous year over the boundary of a strip near the wood-pasture, and who had been seen speaking with the dead man on the Wednesday.

Halfdan arrived late, on a horse that had been ridden harder than anyone expected a reeve’s horse to be ridden. He had come forty miles in a day and a half, which told me something about the urgency with which he had read my message, though he would not have said so. He was not a large man — medium height, medium build, with the particular leanness that comes from a body that burns through food worrying at problems — but he had the eyes. I had first observed those eyes in his grandfather’s account, which I had copied as a young clerk, and I recognized them now: the pale, steady, unsettling grey of a man who is not looking at the world so much as reading it.

He stood at the beck’s edge in the last of the Friday light and said nothing for a long time.

“The body,” he said finally. “Where exactly?”

Sigeberht showed him the marks on the bank. There were bootprints in the clay, confused by the morning’s foot-traffic, but Halfdan crouched and examined them without touching anything, his cloak gathered around him against the October cold.

“The current runs east,” he said. “If a man fell here and drowned, or was struck and fell, the current would carry him to the bend at the mill-race. He was not carried to the bend at the mill-race. He was here.” He looked upstream. “Where does the beck pass through enclosed land before this point?”

“Ragnald Orrson’s water-meadow,” Sigeberht said. “Half a mile.”

“Yes,” Halfdan said, as though he had already known.

He did not explain further. He rarely did, until he was ready.

The night was the worst kind of October night — not cold enough for frost, but with a damp that found the gaps in every garment and settled in the bones. We sat in the reeve-hall at Thornby, which smelled of tallow-smoke and the particular staleness of a building that is used for administration rather than living. Sigeberht’s men had brought Thorkel in for questioning, and the sokeman sat in the outer room looking more bewildered than frightened, which I had learned, from years of watching Halfdan’s people, was often a more reliable sign of innocence than protestations.

Halfdan did not speak to Thorkel. He asked instead to see the original landholding records for the Thornby district — the copies kept at the hall, which Æthelric would have been checking against whatever he carried. Sigeberht produced them, faintly reluctant, because producing records for a visiting reeve from another district implied a degree of scrutiny that institutional dignity resisted.

Halfdan spread them on the table and began to read.

I say read, but it was something more than reading. He turned pages at irregular intervals, sometimes going back, sometimes holding two sheets at once as though comparing them. His lips moved occasionally but made no sound. He asked for a lamp to be brought closer, and then another lamp. He had not eaten since arriving, and I knew better than to mention it.

After perhaps two hours, he made a sound — not a word, precisely, more the small vocal signal of a man who has confirmed what he suspected and is now deciding what to do about it.

“Wulfstan,” he said, without looking up. “You have been keeping administrative records for this district for how long?”

“Fourteen years,” I said. “Since before Cnut’s death.”

“Then you will know,” he said, “that the customary notation for a disputed holding — one where title is contested between two parties — uses a particular mark. A small cross, in the margin.”

“It does.”

“And you will know how many disputed holdings there are, currently, in the Thornby district.”

“Three,” I said. “The Birkby strip, the wood-pasture boundary near Thorkel’s land, and the meadow-land at Orrson’s northern edge.”

“There are fourteen,” Halfdan said.

He turned the record toward me. I looked. I looked again.

The marks were there — fourteen marginal crosses, designating fourteen parcels of land as disputed, which meant that their hidage assessments were suspended pending resolution, which meant that the tax liability on those parcels had not been collected for, in some cases, three years. The marks were small and precise and made with the same ink as the surrounding text. They were, to any eye but the most careful, entirely convincing.

Except that I had been keeping records in that hall for fourteen years, and I knew every disputed holding in the district, and three of those crosses designated parcels I had personally recorded as fully settled.

“They were added after the fact,” I said. I heard my own voice as though from a distance.

“After the fact,” Halfdan agreed. “The ink is subtly different on the crosses — darker, because it was applied to a page that had already dried and aged. The pen-pressure is fractionally heavier, because the forger was working slowly and carefully, which is the opposite of what a scribe does when writing quickly in the ordinary course. And the crosses are in the correct position on the page — the correct customary position — which means the forger was familiar with administrative notation. This was not an outsider’s work.”

He paused. The lamp nearest him guttered in some draught I had not felt.

“Æthelric would have found this,” Halfdan said. “He was comparing his own copies — the writs he carried — against the hall records. He would have seen the discrepancy. He was a careful man who could not be bought, so he was killed, and his writs were taken. Without his copies, there was no external record against which the forgeries could be detected.” He looked up at last. “Except that the forger made one error.”

He turned back two pages in the record and pointed.

I bent over the lamp to look. The entry for one of the disputed parcels — the meadow-land at Orrson’s northern edge — had the marginal cross. But the text of the entry itself referred to the parcel’s tax having been “rendered in full for the third year of the King’s peace.”

A disputed holding, by definition, could not have rendered tax in full. The two facts, occupying the same line, contradicted each other irreconcilably. The text had been written first, honestly, recording payment received. The cross had been added later, to make that payment disappear from the district’s liability ledger — to suggest the tax was still suspended, still uncollected, still outstanding.

Collected, but not remitted. Not to York. Not to the Crown.

The meadow-land at Orrson’s northern edge.

Sigeberht said: “You are suggesting Ragnald Orrson.”

“I am not suggesting,” Halfdan said. “I am observing that the systematic falsification of fourteen holdings, carried out over at least three years, required sustained access to the hall records; that Ragnald Orrson, as the district’s largest landholder and the man who has historically hosted the assessment sessions in his hall — not this hall, which is a more recent arrangement — would have had that access; that his own holding appears four times in the falsified entries; that Æthelric’s body was found downstream from Orrson’s water-meadow; and that Orrson’s holdings represent the single largest block of the diverted tax liability, which, across three years at the standard hidage rate, amounts to a sum sufficient to buy a great deal of silence.”

The room was very quiet. The tallow-smell had grown oppressive.

“Thorkel’s quarrel with Æthelric last year,” I said, slowly. “Over the wood-pasture boundary.”

“Was manufactured,” Halfdan said. “Look at the entry for the wood-pasture. The disputed-holding cross is among the oldest of the forgeries — the ink is marginally less dark than the others. Orrson inserted it early, perhaps two or three years ago. A dispute that does not exist in fact but exists in the record gives a man named Thorkel a reason to be angry at an assessor. A man with a reason to be angry is a useful thing to have nearby when an assessor turns up dead.”

Sigeberht was quiet for a moment. He was a man who had built his professional life on linear procedure, and I could see the procedure reassembling itself around the new information with the grinding effort of something very heavy being pushed uphill. “I cannot enter Orrson’s hall without the appropriate authority from York,” he said. “He is a king’s thegn.”

“Then send to York,” Halfdan said. “I will wait.”

He said it with perfect equanimity, as though he had not ridden forty miles in a day and a half, as though he had not spent two hours reading in lamplight, as though the solution were not already entirely assembled in his mind and requiring only the mechanism of law to close around it. I have spent enough years in the presence of this family to recognize what that stillness costs them. It is not patience. It is will, imposed over everything that wants to move.

He sat down, finally, in the chair by the wall.

“Is there anything to eat?” he asked.

The authority came from York on the Monday. By that time Halfdan had spent the weekend in a state that I can only describe as productive dormancy — he slept a great deal, woke at strange hours to sit by the fire with whatever problem occupied him (on the Saturday night I found him with a set of carved tallysticks, rearranging them in sequences I did not ask about), ate when food was placed in front of him, and spoke very little.

On the Sunday evening he asked me, without apparent connection to anything that had preceded it: “Was there a boat?”

I had not thought about a boat. “At the mill,” he said, “there is a mooring stake. I observed a rope-mark on it — the particular compression of a mooring line, not the short tether of a tied animal. A boat was kept there recently that is no longer there.”

“Orrson has a boat,” I said. “For the mill-pond, in the reed-cutting season.”

“Yes,” Halfdan said. “A body is heavy. A man who has just killed a careful royal official, and who needs to move that body before dawn, and who lives half a mile upstream, and who has a boat — would use the boat. And when the boat had served its purpose, would be wise to move it.”

He went to sleep shortly after. I sat for a while in the hall’s damp darkness, listening to the beck outside and trying not to think about Æthelric’s face in the water.

They found the boat, pulled into the reeds on the far side of Orrson’s water-meadow and covered with cut rushes. They found, in Orrson’s locked chest, a set of records in his own hand that were — I can only describe them as the working documents of the forgery: the original listing of parcels, the calculated amounts, the record of what had been diverted and to where.

Ragnald Orrson, confronted with the records in his own hall in the presence of Sigeberht’s men, did not run. He was a thegn, and running would have been beneath him, and I think also that he was in some way relieved, the way men sometimes are when the weight they have been carrying is finally named aloud. He was a broad, grey-haired man who had clearly once been physically powerful, and he sat in his own chair with his hands flat on the table and said, quite calmly: “He would not take payment.”

“No,” Halfdan said. “He would not.”

“It would have ruined me.”

“Yes.”

There was nothing else said between them. There did not need to be.

What I did not record in the formal proceedings, and record here only because the formal proceedings are now closed and Orrson has been given to York for judgment, is this:

Halfdan, in the hour before Sigeberht’s men moved on the hall, sat with me in the Thornby reeve-hall and said a thing I have been turning over since.

He said: “The falsification was not simple greed. Look at where the money went — not to Orrson’s personal enrichment, or not primarily. The diverted sums paid for a new mill-stone for Gytha’s husband. They paid the heriot on two sokemen’s hides when those sokemen could not raise it themselves. They paid for the repair of the dam that protects the low-lying strips from the spring flood.” He paused. “He was stealing from the Crown to maintain the district.”

I said: “That does not excuse the killing of Æthelric.”

“No,” Halfdan said. “Nothing excuses that. Æthelric was a good man, and Orrson killed him because Orrson, having convinced himself his stealing was virtuous, could not face the consequence of being seen as a thief. That is the logic of men who steal for good reasons — eventually, the good reason and the self-interest become indistinguishable, and they begin to make larger and larger exceptions.” He looked at the lamp. “I want you to record that. Not in the proceedings. Here.”

“Why?”

“Because Sigeberht will report to York that a forger and murderer has been found and punished, and York will report to the King that the district records have been regularized, and the King will record that the system functions. None of those records will contain the fact that the system that functioned perfectly enough to catch Orrson also failed completely to notice him for three years. Or that the holdings he was nominally defrauding are, several of them, marginally better off than they would have been under strict remittance.” He was quiet for a moment. “I am not saying he was right. I am saying that the record should contain that it was complicated. Otherwise we learn nothing.”

I am a clerk. Recording what I am told to record is my function. Recording this, in the margins of the tally-sheets where no one will think to look for a generation or two, is possibly an act of some small courage, or possibly mere vanity. I am not certain which.

But Halfdan asked me to. And in fourteen years of watching this family do their work, I have not yet found a case where what they asked me to notice turned out to be without significance.

The beck ran clear again by the Tuesday. I mention this not because it tells us anything, but because in the record of difficult things, small mercies deserve their place.

— Wulfstan, clerk, Thornby district, in the twentieth year of my service
