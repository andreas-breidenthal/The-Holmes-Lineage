TIBERIUS HOLCUS

~90 BC — Rome, Roman Republic

Tiberius Holcus, born just twenty years later than the man who would end the Republic, watched Caesar’s career with the wary, analytical attention that the times required. He served in the legal administration—a iuridicus of the second rank, responsible for the administration of justice in a district of the city—and maintained, through the years of the First Triumvirate, the crossing of the Rubicon, and the subsequent civil wars, a position of professional competence and political neutrality that was harder to sustain than it sounds.

He had learned from his father that the law is most necessary when most under threat, and from his own experience that those who administer it in such times must distinguish between the law as it is and the law as it ought to be, and serve the first without losing sight of the second. He compiled, in the last years of his life, a private commentary on the distortions that emergency powers had introduced into Roman jurisprudence—never published, circulated among colleagues, treated as the observation of a careful and disheartened man who had seen clearly what was happening and had found no way to prevent it.

The record of the matter that follows is preserved in the notebooks of Decimus Aelius Varro, a court clerk assigned to Tiberius Holcus during his tenure as iuridicus. Varro’s habit was to copy not only the formal proceedings but the marginalia—marks, pauses, and questions—which he believed revealed more than the judgments themselves. His account is therefore unusually exact in its detail, though not always in its interpretation.

The Notebooks of Decimus Aelius Varro

Copied in His Own Hand During His Service Under the Iuridicus Tiberius Holcus

The Tiber stank worse in autumn than in any other season. I record this not as complaint but as fact, because it is the smell I most associate with the morning Publius Naevius Cursor came to our office above the Velabrum, and because Tiberius Holcus, who claimed to have excised all irrelevant sensation from his awareness, raised his head from his tablets when Cursor entered and said, before the man had spoken a single word: “You have been standing at the river’s edge. The south bank, near the Trigarium. You have been there since before the second hour.”

Cursor, a junior iuridicus of the Regio Transtiberim, stopped in the doorway. He was perhaps thirty-five, with the broad honest face of a man whose family had farmed in Latium for generations before an uncle’s patronage had pushed him into the courts. He looked, I thought, like a man who had not slept.

“How—”

“The mud on your sandals,” Holcus said, returning to his tablets. “The colour is particular. The Tiber deposits a grey-brown silt along the south bank of the Trigarium that is distinct from the darker mud of the Prata. The Trigarium mud dries to a paler tone. You have been standing, not walking—your heel impressions are deeper than your toe impressions, the posture of a man who waits. The mud is half-dry, which at this morning’s temperature requires approximately three hours. You did not go home before coming here. Your toga is the same one you wore yesterday—I saw you in the Forum Boarium at the ninth hour—and there is ink on your left forefinger that has been there long enough to begin flaking.”

He set down his stylus and looked at Cursor properly for the first time.

“Whatever you have been turning over in your mind at the river’s edge, you have decided you cannot resolve it yourself. Sit down, Naevius Cursor. Have Varro pour you something warm. Then tell me what is in that case-satchel you are gripping as though it might escape.”

I poured the wine. Cursor sat. He opened the satchel with the care of a man handling evidence he does not entirely trust himself to interpret, and he laid six wax tablets on the work table between himself and Holcus.

“The docket of Gaius Servilius Trio,” he said. “A freedman of the house of Servilius. Charged six weeks ago with falsification of a property contract. The case was heard before the Praetor Urbanus. The verdict was acquittal.”

Holcus did not touch the tablets. He looked at them.

“And you believe the verdict was obtained through the falsification of court records.”

“I believe,” Cursor said carefully, “that the records themselves have been falsified. Whether this affected the verdict—”

“Show me.”

Cursor arranged the tablets in a line. “This is the initial registration of the charge. This is the list of witnesses called. This is the record of the emergency suspension of the standard evidentiary procedure—invoking the authority of the Dictator’s reorganisation of the courts, so that the usual requirements for corroborating witnesses were set aside. This is the transcript of the single witness who then testified. This is the praetor’s written finding. And this”—he tapped the final tablet—“is the standard docket record, the summary entry in the official roll. The entry records the case as concluded by normal procedure. No emergency suspension is noted.”

He sat back. “The emergency suspension happened. I was in court. I saw the praetor invoke it. But the docket says it did not happen. Someone has altered the record.”

Holcus was quiet for a long moment. The sounds of the Velabrum rose from below—a cart, an argument about the price of garum, a child. The lamp on the work table smelled of cheap tallow. He reached out at last and took the first tablet.

“How long have you been in the courts, Cursor?”

“Seven years.”

“And before the Dictator’s reforms, how many witnesses were required to support a property falsification charge?”

“Two. The complainant and one corroborating—”

“And after the reforms?”

Cursor paused. “One. Under the emergency provisions. But only where the praetor invoked—”

“Yes,” Holcus said. “Where the praetor invoked. Tell me, in the seven years of your service, how many times did you see a praetor invoke the emergency suspension before the reforms?”

“I never did. It was an extraordinary measure.”

“And after?”

Cursor’s broad face was troubled. “More often. Perhaps... I cannot say precisely. The procedure became less—remarked upon.”

“Less remarked upon,” Holcus repeated. He set the tablet down and took the sixth, the summary docket. He held it close to the lamp. The smell of the tallow mixed with the distant stink of the river. “Varro. Your eyes are younger than mine. Come and tell me what you observe about this entry.”

I came and looked. The docket entry was written in a competent administrative hand—the kind of neutral, trained script that the court copyists produced, individual enough to belong to a person but compressed and habitual, the writing of a man who had filled ten thousand such entries and thought about none of them. The ink was consistent throughout.

“The ink is the same,” I said. “Colour, consistency. I see no sign of erasure—no roughening of the wax surface, no shadow of an earlier impression.”

“No,” Holcus agreed. “There is not. Cursor, are you certain you saw the praetor invoke the emergency suspension?”

“Completely. I can name three others who witnessed it.”

“Then why,” Holcus said, “is there no evidence of alteration in the record?”

He stood. He had been in his languid mode all morning—reclined, half-reading, the particular stillness he maintained when nothing was sufficiently interesting to animate him—but something had shifted in the last minutes. He began to move around the work table, not quickly but with that focused, economical energy that I had learned to watch for, the movement of a man whose mind has already arrived somewhere his body is still approaching.

“Sit, both of you, and attend to me. I am going to explain what happened in the docket of Gaius Servilius Trio, and I am going to do it in the order that it happened, not in the order that it appears to have happened, which is the confusion you have been suffering from, Cursor, for—I would estimate—the past four days, since you first noticed the discrepancy.”

Cursor said nothing. He had, I noted, stopped gripping the case-satchel.

“Begin,” Holcus said, “with what the reform actually changed. Not what it appeared to change. You know the Dictator’s reorganisation of the courts of last year—”

“Everyone knows it,” Cursor said.

“Everyone knows its surface. The reform created, within the existing court structure, a category of case that could be resolved under the emergency procedure: single witness, no corroboration required, praetor’s discretion supreme. What no one stated explicitly—because stating it explicitly would have been politically inconvenient—is that the reform also changed the record-keeping requirement.”

He paused. Outside, the argument about garum had resolved itself; someone had apparently paid.

“Under the old procedure, an emergency suspension was an extraordinary measure, applied rarely, and therefore required specific notation in the docket—precisely because it was an exception that needed to be visible. Under the new procedure, the emergency suspension is no longer an exception. It is one of two recognised and equally legitimate procedural modes. And when a thing is no longer an exception, it is no longer required to be noted as one.”

He stopped in front of the sixth tablet—the summary docket—and tapped it with one finger.

“The docket entry is correct. It records the case as concluded by—I will read the precise phrase—‘concluded by the praetor’s authority under current statute.’ That is accurate. That is precisely what happened. Under the new record-keeping guidance, which came into effect by administrative circular three months ago, emergency suspensions are no longer to be separately flagged. They are subsumed under the general praetorian authority. The docket does not say the emergency suspension did not occur. It simply does not mention it. It is not required to.”

Cursor stared at him.

“Then the records have not been falsified.”

“No.”

“Then—” He stopped. Tried again. “Then the trial was conducted lawfully.”

“Every procedural step was lawful. The charge was registered lawfully. The emergency suspension was invoked lawfully—the praetor has explicit statutory authority to do so. The single witness testified. The praetor found for acquittal. The record was entered according to current administrative guidance. At every point, the procedure was followed exactly as it is currently required to be followed.”

The room was quiet for a moment.

“But,” Cursor said, and the word came out slowly, as though he was not certain he was permitted to say what came after it. “But before the reforms—before last year—a single witness and no corroboration would have been grounds for immediate dismissal. The charge would never have reached the testimony stage. Trio would have been convicted.”

“Almost certainly,” Holcus agreed. “The evidence of the original complainant was, by all accounts, substantial. Under the old procedure, it would have been corroborated, the case would have proceeded normally, and Trio would have been found guilty of falsification. Under the new procedure, the praetor is not required to hear corroborating evidence. He heard one witness, exercised his discretion, and acquitted. All entirely lawful.”

“But it is—” Cursor searched for the word. “It is wrong.”

“Yes,” said Tiberius Holcus. He said it without particular emphasis, as a man states a mathematical result. He walked back to his position at the table and sat down. The energy that had animated him while he was speaking appeared to be receding, like water from a shore. “It is wrong. But it is the law. Those two things now coexist in our courts in a manner that would have seemed incoherent, ten years ago, to any trained jurist.”

Cursor sat with it.

I watched him. He had the look of a man who has spent four days trying to prove that a document has been tampered with and has just been told, with complete authority, that the document is entirely accurate—and who finds this conclusion worse than fraud would have been.

“Then there is nothing to report,” he said at last. “No crime. No falsification. No corruption.”

“There is nothing to report to the authorities,” Holcus said. “Which is a different statement.”

“What is the difference?”

Holcus was quiet for a moment. He picked up the stylus he had set down when Cursor entered and turned it between his fingers—a habit I had noticed before, the physical equivalent of considered silence.

“Cursor. You are a careful man. You have been in the courts seven years. You saw the emergency suspension invoked. You checked the docket and found it unrecorded. You did not simply accept this—you spent four days at the river’s edge trying to understand it. That is the quality that a court requires in its officers. It is also, at present, the quality that can make a court officer’s life very difficult.”

“You are saying I should not pursue this.”

“I am saying that there is nothing to pursue in the legal sense, because no law has been broken. I am also saying that if you were to circulate an account of what you had observed—the gap between what the court procedure was and what it now is—this would not be received as the observation of a careful man. It would be received as political dissent, and you would be treated accordingly.”

He set the stylus down.

“Write up what you witnessed. Date it. Seal it. Do not file it with the court rolls. Keep it.”

“To what purpose?”

“There may come a time when the record of what was normal, before, becomes important. When people need to know not only what the law says, but what it used to say, and when it changed, and how. That time is not now. But human affairs have a way of cycling, and records have a way of surviving.”

He nodded at the six tablets.

“Take those with you. They are your property—you brought them. And Cursor.”

Cursor, who had begun to gather the tablets, paused.

“The man who benefits from this acquittal—Trio, the freedman. Do you know whose household he actually serves? Not formally. Actually.”

Cursor looked at him for a moment. “There are names associated with the Servilii. Political names.”

“Yes,” said Holcus. “There are always political names. That is all I wished to establish. Good morning, Naevius Cursor. If you find yourself at the river again, I recommend the north bank. The mud is different but the view is preferable.”

Cursor left. The door closed. The sounds of the Velabrum rose and fell.

Holcus sat at his work table and did not pick up his tablets. He looked at the lamp for a long time—the cheap tallow lamp that smelled of rendered fat and filled the room with a light that was yellow rather than white. Outside, someone had begun hawking dried figs.

“Varro,” he said.

“Yes.”

“You copy everything. The proceedings, the marginalia.”

“It is my habit. Yes.”

“Copy this. All of it. Include your own observations—what you saw, what you smelled, what you heard from the street. Future men will read law the way we read Twelve Tables—as a document from a world they can only partially imagine. They will want to know what the lamp smelled like.”

He picked up his stylus.

“The law,” he said, with the tone of a man finishing a private thought that had been running for a long time, “is most itself when it is not being used to do something else. We have had a decade of it being used to do something else, and the damage is in the records now. In documents that are completely accurate and therefore tell a kind of lie that forgery never could, because forgery can be detected, and corrected, and condemned. This cannot. This has been authorised.”

He said nothing further. He returned to his tablets. The morning continued as mornings do—the fig seller moved on, a dog barked somewhere in the direction of the Forum Boarium, the Tiber went on stinking—and I copied what I had witnessed, including the smell of the lamp, as he had asked.

I do not know what became of Gaius Servilius Trio, or of Publius Naevius Cursor, though I believe Cursor served out his tenure without incident—he had understood, in the end, what was and was not survivable to say aloud. I note that the administrative circular Tiberius Holcus cited, the one removing the separate notation requirement for emergency suspensions, appears nowhere in the official court archives. It has not been found. I have, in these notebooks, Holcus’s word that it existed, and my own memory of a man who was not given to imprecision. I record this discrepancy as what it is: a thing whose absence is its own kind of evidence, in the manner that Holcus himself would have appreciated.

—Decimus Aelius Varro, the year of the consulship of Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus and Marcus Licinius Crassus
