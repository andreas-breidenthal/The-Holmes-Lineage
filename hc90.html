SIGR HOLMR

~710 AD — Vestfold, Norway

The name was fully Norse now — Sigr Holmr, victory on the flat water — and the man matched it. He grew up in the hall culture of Vestfold, learned the sea from boyhood, and combined his father’s analytical intelligence with the physical courage and appetite for risk that the Norse world demanded of anyone who wished to be taken seriously within it. He was not primarily a warrior, though he could fight; he was primarily a reader of men and situations, and in the complex politics of the Vestfold jarls — the shifting alliances, the inheritance disputes, the careful calibration of tribute and loyalty that kept the peace between rival houses — that faculty was worth more than any number of swords.

He served as a skald of sorts — not primarily a poet, but a keeper of the oral record, a man who remembered the genealogies and agreements and precedents that governed the relationships between families. In a world without written law, such men were the law; their memories were the archive. Sigr Holmr’s memory was, by the testimony of those who relied on it, impeccable — not merely retentive but analytical, capable of finding in the accumulated record of the past the precise precedent that illuminated the dispute of the present. He was consulted from considerable distances by people who needed to know what had been agreed, and by whom, and when.

Among those who listened most carefully to his accounts was Gunnvor, a woman attached to the household of the thing-lawman, responsible for committing certain proceedings to parchment when disputes required formal record. Her writing was careful but selective, and her account reflects both the limits of what she could observe and the necessity of choosing what to preserve. It is through her record that the following case is known.

The Testimony of Gunnvor Arnvidsdóttir

Set Down at Skiringssal

These words I commit to parchment at the request of Jarl Ulfheðinn Brandisson, that the proceedings of the Vestfold assembly in the third year of his lordship be faithfully preserved. I am Gunnvor, daughter of Arnviðr, scribe to the thing-lawman Ketill Ormsson. What I witnessed, I record. What I did not witness, I have taken from those who did. The errors are mine. The truth belongs to the men I write of.

The killing happened before the fires were lit for the second evening of the assembly, which is to say it happened in that grey and indeterminate hour when the light was neither day nor dark and the smell coming off the fjord was the cold iron smell that precedes rain. I know the smell because I was standing at the edge of the lawman’s tent, watching the clouds come down over the water, when the shouting began.

The dead man was Þorvarðr Eilífsson, called Þorvarðr the Red for the colour of his beard, a landowner of middling consequence from the southern reaches of Vestfold. He was found between the horse-lines and the cooking fires — that trampled, muddy corridor where the assembly’s livestock shifted and stamped — with a single wound to the back of the neck, delivered with something narrow and heavy, a spike or a pointed hammer. He had not drawn his own weapon. He had not, it appeared, heard the man who killed him.

He was found by a thrall fetching water, and the thrall’s shout brought the assembly guards, and the guards brought Ketill Ormsson, and Ketill Ormsson sent for Sigr Holmr.

I had seen this happen before — the sending for Sigr Holmr — and I had observed that it was not a comfortable thing for Ketill, who was a conscientious man and a capable administrator of law, but who was not, as he himself once said to me when he thought I was not attending closely, capable of the particular kind of attention that the unusual demanded. He managed the usual with considerable skill. The unusual required Sigr Holmr.

I followed at the proper distance and wrote down what I could.

Sigr Holmr was where he often was in the evenings of the assembly: seated apart from the main fires, in the company of no one, working at some private problem that had given his face the quality of a hawk watching a field. He was not a tall man, but he had the kind of stillness that makes a man seem larger than he is, the stillness of something coiled rather than something resting. His hair was the particular dark amber that goes almost black in poor light, and he wore it tied back in the manner of a man who finds it an inconvenience. His eyes, when the guard came to fetch him, came up from wherever they had been with the speed of a bird taking flight — a sudden, unsettling alertness where a moment before there had been apparent vacancy.

He said nothing. He rose, belted on nothing — he carried no sword to such gatherings — and walked to the horse-lines.

Ketill met him there. The guards had kept the area clear, which was well, because the ground told a considerable story to anyone disposed to read it.

“Þorvarðr the Red,” Ketill said. “Found here. No witnesses to the act.”

“The thrall who found him,” Sigr Holmr said. “Where is she now?”

“Returned to her master’s camp. She was badly frightened.”

“She should be found and questioned before she has spoken at length with her master.”

He crouched, examined the wound without touching it, and then lifted his head slightly as though reading something in the air.

“Who found him first,” he said, “the thrall or the man standing at the south corner of the horse-lines with the grey cloak who has been watching us since we arrived?”

We turned. There was indeed such a man.

He was Gunnarr Hrólfsson. And there was history between his family and the dead man.

What followed I record precisely: the accusation implied, the defence offered, the observation made not of words but of their alignment with consequence. Gunnarr stated that the death helped him nothing. This was true.

Sigr Holmr walked away to think.

He examined the ground. He examined the horses. He examined the spaces where men had stood long enough to leave a pattern in the mud. And then he stood still until the pattern resolved.

When he returned, he asked not about Gunnarr, but about a family no one present had considered.

“Tell me,” he said, “everything you know about the family of Vigdís Ingólfsdóttir.”

From the records, and from memory, we reconstructed an old grievance — a broken betrothal, a dishonour judged insufficient by law and preserved beyond law by memory. The words of the dead, carried forward.

“The grudge remains,” Sigr Holmr said. “And it has matured.”

He returned us to the ground and showed us what he had seen: the waiting place of the killer, the familiarity with the horse, the tool of the coast, the smell of pine pitch that matched a single boat drawn up on the shore.

The Ingólfsson boy was found.

He did not deny the killing.

He said only that the account had been settled.

Ketill delayed judgment. The law required time to consider what memory had already decided.

That evening, when the fires were lit and the assembly returned to its noise and order, I asked Sigr Holmr what he had told the lawman.

He answered after some thought.

“I said that the boy killed a man who carried a debt he did not know he owed, for an injury inflicted by a man long dead, in satisfaction of a judgment rendered by a thing that no longer sits.”

He looked at me directly.

“The law will speak,” he said. “But the grief that produces such acts — the law has no procedure for that.”

He turned back to the fire he did not sit beside.

“Write that down,” he said. “Someone ought to know it.”

I have written it down.

I was there.
