NANNA-IDDIN

~2020 BC — Nippur, Mesopotamia

THE SACRED BOUNDARY-RECORD OF NINLIL'S SOUTHERN FIELD

Recorded by Lugal-esh, apprentice of the tablet-house of Enlil's Temple, Nippur, in the year the governor of Isin sent his third delegation

For the first time in eleven days, Nanna-iddin’s bench was empty at the hour of the first lamp-lighting. I had begun to wonder if the lament-tablet had finally defeated his spirit, but as the sun touched the temple eaves, he returned. There was a smudge of fresh clay on his thumb and the restless, hawk-like energy in his step that usually signaled the end of a period of stillness. He had just resumed his seat when the house-servant of Sharrum-ilu arrived, and I knew then that the silence of the lament-tablet was not merely broken, but discarded.

I had been watching the silence for those eleven days from my place at the copying bench. The tablet — a literary composition from the old Ur III archive, something to do with a lament — sat untouched before him in the lamp-light. His eyes moved across it, and occasionally he pressed two fingers against his mouth as though holding something in, and otherwise he was still with the particular stillness of a man in whom immobility is a form of concentration so complete it resembles sleep. Only his eyes gave him away.

The house-servant handed his message to the senior librarian, who brought it to Nanna-iddin. He read it. He set it down. He looked at the lament-tablet for a moment longer — the look of a man promising to return — and then he rose from his bench with a sudden, settled energy, as though something locked had been released, and said: "Come."

This was how I was brought into the matter of the Boundary-Record of Ninlil's Southern Field.

I will say something of Nanna-iddin before I proceed, because future readers of this tablet will require it. He was named for the moon-god of Ur — Nanna has given — and he had grown up on the road north to Nippur, his father having read the signs of the old city's approaching destruction with the clarity that characterised the family's engagement with catastrophe: seeing it, preparing for it, and departing before it arrived. He arrived in Nippur carrying a child's memory of Ur at its height and a refugee's education in the provisional nature of all certainty. He found employment in the temple scribal school, where his facility with cuneiform — inherited and honed from childhood — made him useful in ways that mere copyists were not. A copyist reproduced what was on the tablet. Nanna-iddin understood it, which made him the man the school's administrators brought their difficult texts to: the damaged tablet, the ambiguous phrase, the disputed reading. He resolved these problems with the confidence of someone for whom understanding is not the goal of reading but its natural consequence.

He was not young when I knew him, but he was not old. He occupied that interval between those conditions that some men hold for a decade and others seem to skip entirely. He was spare in his person and economical in his movements — a quality I had mistaken for indolence in my early months, before I understood it as conservation. He did not move when he did not need to move. He did not speak when silence served him better. When he was not working, he became first still, then restless, then sharp in a way that made the senior scribes find reasons to be elsewhere. His relief in the empty hours was to set the apprentices increasingly elaborate riddles drawn from the legal archive — inheritance disputes and field boundaries and contradictory royal decrees — and to dismiss our answers with a precision that was not unkind but was not kind either. He had memorised, as far as I could determine, every boundary survey in the school's collection, and he deployed this knowledge as a carpenter reaches for a tool, except that the carpenter must look first.

But I am recording what I witnessed. Let me return to it.

It was the season of the dry wind. The dust came in through the reed matting of the tablet-house and settled on the shelves in a fine grey film, so that the clay of the older tablets and the dust of the air became, over time, indistinguishable from one another. Oil lamps burned in the inner rooms where the light did not reach, and the fat they consumed was old and had not been rendered cleanly, so there was always beneath the smell of the dust and the reed and the dry air a lower smell, animal and slightly rotten, that one stopped noticing only by living with it long enough.

Nanna-iddin has told me more than once that the conditions of a place are themselves a kind of text, and that a man who does not read them will fail to read the tablets within them correctly. I believe, now, that he had been reading the tablet-house for some time before any of us medication that a crime had been committed within it.

Sharrum-ilu was the chief administrator of the tablet-house's legal archive—a large man with the bearing of someone who had learned, relatively late in life, that bearing was something one could acquire. He received us in the outer room of his office, where four oil lamps burned against the dust-dimmed light. He was nervously tapping a heavy, yellowed ivory stylus against the edge of his table—a distinctive instrument, thicker than a standard reed and worn to a curious, slanted asymmetry at the tip. "The House of Ur-Ningirsu," he said, and the name alone seemed to chill the air. "They are not a family accustomed to losing. Their patriarch has already seen one witness in this dispute flee to the marshes, and another was found silenced in a dry well. They have made it known that if the Boundary-Record does not vanish, the tablet-house itself may find its patronage... curtailed."

He then told us that a junior copyist named Imgur-Enlil had been found that morning in possession of a small quantity of silver he could not satisfactorily account for, and that Imgur-Enlil had been present in the archive room on two of the three days since the tablet's placement. He looked at Nanna-iddin with the expression of a man presenting a completed calculation.

Nanna-iddin said nothing. He was looking at the lamps.

"You want me to find the tablet," he said, finally — which was not quite the same thing as agreeing with what Sharrum-ilu had said.

"I want the matter resolved before the hearing," Sharrum-ilu said. "With certainty. The dispute cannot proceed without the boundary record, and the family's advocates will use any ambiguity in the handling of this archive to argue that our documentation is unreliable. The family" — he paused, and something moved behind his composed face — "are not a family accustomed to losing. There have been threats. Informal ones."

"What kind of threats?" Nanna-iddin asked.

"The kind that name no specific harm. The kind that do not need to."

Nanna-iddin let the silence sit for a moment. Then: "The junior copyist. Where is he now?"

"Confined. In the store-room off the eastern corridor."

"And the grain receipt found in the tablet's location — where is that?"

Sharrum-ilu gestured to a small tablet on his table. It was a standard field-receipt, the kind produced in quantity, the clay a lighter colour than the archive tablets. Nanna-iddin looked at it without touching it. Then he asked to see the archive room, and the place where the Boundary-Record had been stored.

I will try to record accurately what I witnessed in the archive room, though I confess that I did not understand the significance of most of it until afterward.

The room was long and narrow, shelved on both sides with the tablet-cases the school used for long-term storage — clay boxes, sealed, with summary labels pressed into their lids. It smelled of old clay and of the particular slightly sweet smell that comes from clay fired a very long time ago and since sealed. At the far end, there was a single lamp bracket, unlit. The tablet's case was on the third shelf of the left wall, at approximately the height of a man's chest, in the second case from the left.

Nanna-iddin looked at the case. He did not open it. He looked at the shelf above it, and the shelf below it. Then he turned and examined the door of the archive room — the hinge, specifically. It was a wooden door with a bronze hinge-pin, and the pin had been recently oiled; there was a small quantity of oil still visible at the joint, slightly tacky in the dry air.

"Who maintains this room?" he asked.

"Ur-Namma. The senior librarian."

"When was that hinge last oiled?"

Sharrum-ilu looked at the hinge with the expression of a man asked to recall whether clouds had been present on a particular afternoon. "I couldn't say."

"It has been oiled in the last two days," Nanna-iddin said. "Probably last night or the night before. The oil is fresh but has begun to set. It is a careful oiling — not a maintenance application, but deliberate. Applied to make the door open quietly."

Sharrum-ilu began to speak, and Nanna-iddin said, without particular emphasis, "Not yet."

He then examined the dust on the floor. There was a great deal of it—the dry season had been thorough—and it lay in a fine even film across the stone except in two places: a path of disturbance in front of the third shelf, left wall; and a smaller, more peculiar disturbance slightly further along. I looked more carefully, and I saw what held Nanna-iddin's eye. In front of the fourth case, the dust bore a double impression—a heavy rectangular mark where a case had been set down, and a second, overlapping mark where it had been dragged slightly before being lifted. It was as though a heavy object had made a brief, temporary home there. Above this disturbance, the label on the fourth case was facing inward, its blank clay back turned to the room in a silent admission of haste.

Nanna-iddin stood before it for a long moment. He did not crouch down. He looked at it from above and from the side. He looked at the case itself and something in his expression changed almost imperceptibly, as though a very small calculation had resolved itself. He did not touch the case.

He asked to see Imgur-Enlil.

The junior copyist was young — perhaps my own age, perhaps a year younger — with the look of a man who had not slept and had spent the wakeful hours in a state of alarm that had grown genuine through repetition. He was sitting on the floor of the store-room with his knees drawn up, and he stood when we entered with the hasty respect of someone very frightened who wishes to demonstrate that he understands the gravity of his situation.

Nanna-iddin sat down on a storage jar turned on its side and looked at the boy for a moment. "Tell me about the silver," he said.

Imgur-Enlil told us, in the stumbling way of someone describing something true that he knows sounds false: that he had found the silver — three small pieces, wrapped in cloth — in the carrying-bag he used to transport his work materials to and from the archive room. He had not placed it there. He had not taken the tablet. He had been in the archive room on the days in question because his current assignment required him to copy a set of land-grant records from the case adjacent to where the Boundary-Record had been stored.

"On the first day," Nanna-iddin said, "or on the second?"

Imgur-Enlil blinked. "Both days."

"Describe your carrying-bag."

He described it: a standard reed bag, the kind all junior scribes used, with a cord tied through two holes at the top. He kept his blank tablets, his stylus case, and his lunch cloth in it.

"You store it where, during the working day?"

"In the outer room. There is a shelf — all the apprentices use it for their bags during work hours."

I confess that at this point I could not contain myself. "He was in the room on both days," I said to Nanna-iddin. "He had access to the correct location. He cannot account for the silver. Surely—"

Nanna-iddin turned his head and looked at me with the expression he used when I had said something he had already resolved. "A man careful enough to plan a substitution tablet and oil a hinge will not leave the payment in a communal bag." He turned back to the boy. "The case adjacent to the land-grants where you were working — the one four places along the third shelf from the left. When you were working there on either day, did you notice anything unusual about it? Anything out of the expected order?"

The boy's distress shifted briefly toward confusion — a sign, in my experience, that he was hearing something unexpected enough to displace the fear. He thought. "On the second day," he said slowly, "the label on that case was facing the wrong way. Usually they all face outward. That one was facing in. I thought someone had replaced it carelessly."

"Did you correct it?"

"No. It wasn't my case. I didn't want to interfere."

Nanna-iddin stood from the storage jar and said, in the tone of someone reaching the end of a calculation: "You did not take the tablet. Go and wash your face."

He did not tell us anything until we were back in Sharrum-ilu's office with the grain receipt on the table between us. This withholding was not cruelty but the necessary architecture of explanation. If he had told us each thing as he observed it, we would have had observations. By gathering them and releasing them at the point of their complete significance, he gave us understanding, which is a different and more lasting thing.

He picked up the grain receipt and held it to the lamp. "This was made within the last five days. The clay is still slightly responsive to pressure at its thickest point — press it here and you will feel it yield very slightly. A tablet three days old at most. It was not made before the crime. It was made to be placed in the Boundary-Record's location after the Boundary-Record was removed. Someone wanted the shelf to appear occupied."

Sharrum-ilu was looking at him steadily. He was a man who had learned composure, and he was employing it now. "That is consistent with what I told you. The junior copyist—"

"Was in the room at the correct time, has silver in his bag that he cannot explain, and does not have the tablet," Nanna-iddin said. "These are three separate facts. The instinct is to connect them into one story. The story is: he took the tablet and sold it, and was careless about concealing the payment. But if that story is true, he must also have been careful enough to make a convincing replacement tablet and install it in the correct location. He must have oiled the door hinge to enter quietly. And he must have then left the silver in a bag stored, during working hours, on a shelf accessible to everyone in the outer room."

He set the grain receipt back down. "A man careful enough to plan a substitution will not leave the payment in a communal bag. These facts do not belong to the same person."

The lamp flame moved in a draught from somewhere, and the shadows on the wall shifted and settled.

"The replacement tablet required someone who could produce a plausible grain receipt. The forms are standard, but it requires access to clay, a stylus, and some facility with the script. Every scribe in this school possesses those things. But the replacement was placed in the correct position on the shelf, which requires knowledge of the archive's organisation that is not general. And the case four places along — the one with the reversed label, which Lugal-esh and I both observed during our inspection — was used to store the Boundary-Record itself, temporarily, on the day it was removed. Someone placed it there, and then took it away in the night that followed, by someone who oiled the hinge to enter quietly."

"The dust disturbance in front of that case," I said, because I had followed it now, "was made by someone taking something heavy out and then replacing it."

"Taking it out alone would leave one pattern; replacing it alone would leave another," Nanna-iddin said. "The pattern in front of that case shows both, in sequence. The pattern in front of the Boundary-Record's original location shows only removal. Something large was moved from one location to the other on the same day, and then removed entirely in the night, by a person who required silence to do it." He looked at Sharrum-ilu. "Someone who knows this archive well enough to know which case was empty. Someone who knows that the junior copyist works adjacent to that case. Someone with access to the outer room where the apprentices' bags are kept."

Sharrum-ilu rose from his chair.

It was a small movement — the rising of a man who has made a decision, with the controlled deliberateness of someone who has rehearsed it. He moved to the table and reached past the grain receipt. In his hand, when he straightened, was a short ivory stylus — a senior administrator's instrument, heavy and well-made, not the reed stylus of the scribal school. His hand was steady.

"If this tablet," he said, with a careful, even precision, "were to be destroyed before a formal accounting could be made of what you have observed, the record of your observations would be your word and the boy's word against mine. I have served this school for sixteen years. You have served it for four. The boy is a junior copyist who was found with unexplained silver." He looked at the grain receipt. "A man of your perception, Nanna-iddin, would be wise to consider whether his observations might benefit from revision."

Nanna-iddin had not moved. He stood watching Sharrum-ilu with the stillness he brought to a difficult tablet, but his eyes were not the eyes of a man studying text. They were the eyes of a man who has set a scene and is watching it perform.

"I have considered it," he said.

He reached into the fold of his robe and placed on the table a second tablet — smaller than the grain receipt, baked harder, with the dense pressed script of the legal archive's administrative records.

"This is a copy I made at dawn," he said, "when I visited the archive-keeper's quarters before the school had fully woken. It is an extract from Ur-Namma's maintenance log recording the last oiling of the archive room hinge. The date is fourteen days past. The hinge I examined this morning was oiled within the last two days. The discrepancy is documented. This copy is already with the temple's chief scholar, who received it from me before I returned to the tablet-house to meet the servant you sent." He paused. "You cannot destroy observations that are already in another man's possession. The ivory stylus you are holding is the very instrument you used to write the grain receipt. I noted the slanted wear of its tip the moment we entered this room; the same asymmetry is present in the impressions on the receipt you placed on this table. Set it down."

I had not known this. I looked at him, and there was nothing in his expression that acknowledged my surprise.

"You cannot destroy observations," he continued, in the same even tone, "that are already in another man's possession. The stylus you are holding is the instrument you used to write the grain receipt. I recognised it when I came into this room — it is an unusual size, ivory rather than reed, and the impression it leaves in fresh clay has a slight asymmetry from wear. The same asymmetry is present in the grain receipt you placed on this table as evidence of what you wished us to believe." He met Sharrum-ilu's eyes. "Set it down."

A very long moment. The fat in the lamp made a small sound, a faint crackle, as it consumed itself.

Sharrum-ilu set the stylus down.

He looked at the door. He looked at the copy of the maintenance log. He looked at Nanna-iddin, and whatever calculation he was performing arrived at its result, and the composed face he had been maintaining came apart in the way that composed faces do when the composure is no longer serving any purpose.

"Where is the tablet now?" Nanna-iddin asked.

A silence. Then: "With the advocate's household. I had no means to retrieve it once it was placed."

"The advocate's household is less than a morning's journey. The hearing is in four days. If the tablet were returned to its correct location in this archive before that hearing, in the night, by a person unknown, the matter of its disappearance would resolve itself without the requirement for any official process." He paused. "The school would benefit from that resolution. The dispute would proceed on documented grounds. A junior copyist would be returned to his work. The temple's chief scholar, who currently holds a copy of the maintenance record, would have no cause to act upon it." He let the silence exist for a moment. "And the advocate's household would no longer have the Boundary-Record in their possession."

"Which would leave them in a considerably more difficult position," Sharrum-ilu said carefully, "than if they had never received it."

"The Boundary-Record, read correctly — and I have read it correctly, as it was brought to me for restoration three months ago — favours the temple's position. The family's advocates received it because they were told it favoured theirs. They were misinformed. Its return to the archive is therefore also in their interest, if they have the understanding to recognise it." Nanna-iddin folded his hands. "The family, I am told, are not accustomed to losing. It would be unfortunate for all parties if they were to press their claim with a document that undermines it."

What Sharrum-ilu said next I will not record. It was not part of the matter. He left the office shortly afterward, and Nanna-iddin and I walked back through the tablet-house to the room where the lament-tablet waited.

Imgur-Enlil was restored to his copying work the following morning. The Boundary-Record was found in its correct location on the morning of the third day. The hearing proceeded. The chief scholar received no further communication from Nanna-iddin on the matter of the maintenance record, and appears not to have considered the copy significant in itself, as its meaning was contingent on knowledge only Nanna-iddin possessed. I do not know the outcome of the dispute, because Nanna-iddin did not consider it relevant and I had learned by then to align my sense of relevance with his on such questions.

Sharrum-ilu was removed from his position some months later, for reasons the official record attributes to irregularities in the grain accounts. This is probably also true. Nanna-iddin had, in the period before the tablet matter, remarked once — in the tone he used for observations he considered settled — that a man dishonest in one kind of accounting was rarely honest in another.

I asked him, once, why he had not brought the matter to the temple authorities at the time he first knew the answer.

"Because the record would have had to follow him," he said. "And the record of a senior administrator convicted of theft from the sacred archive, in a city that is currently demonstrating its stability to three rival states, would do more damage to the school than one administrator's quiet departure." He set a tablet down. "The tablet is back. The boy is back. The hearing proceeds. This is what I was hired to achieve."

"But he threatened you," I said. "He stood there with a stylus and threatened to destroy the evidence and accuse us both."

"Yes," Nanna-iddin said. There was something in his voice I could not name — not satisfaction, precisely, but the recognition of something expected that has arrived exactly as anticipated. "He did."

"And you had already removed the danger before the conversation began."

"I had already removed the danger before I entered his office." He looked at me. "That is the correct order. You arrange the conditions first. Then you speak. A man who speaks first and arranges afterward is not conducting an investigation. He is conducting a conversation, which is a different and considerably less useful thing."

He picked up the lament-tablet and held it to the lamp-light.

"There is a line in this," he said, after a moment, and his voice had taken the tone it used when something in a text had finally resolved itself, "that I have been misreading for eleven days. It is not a description of the city's destruction. It is a description of the moment before the destruction — when the gods have already decided and the city does not yet know. The dread is in the not-knowing." He looked at me. "That is the condition I find most useful to cultivate in those who have done me harm. They know the net has closed. They do not know when it will tighten, or by whose hand, or what it holds. Write that down."

I wrote it down.

Here the account ends. This account was copied from the teaching tablets of the house of Enlil by a later hand. The original tablets of Lugal-esh were much damaged at their edges; where the restoration is uncertain I have marked the clay. The matter of the boundary dispute is recorded separately in the governor's records as having been resolved in the temple's favour. Nanna-iddin's name does not appear in those records.
