''' NANNA-IDDIN (~2020 BC) — The Holmes Chronicle | Andreas Breidenthal

NANNA-IDDIN

~2020 BC — Nippur, Mesopotamia

He was named for the moon-god of Ur — Nanna has given — in the years when his father was already reading the signs of the city’s approaching destruction with the clarity that characterised the family’s engagement with catastrophe: seeing it, preparing for it, and departing before it arrived. Nanna-iddin grew up on the road north to Nippur, the city of Enlil, carrying with him a child’s memory of Ur at its height and a refugee’s education in the provisional nature of all certainty.

Nippur was a sacred city rather than a political one — less vulnerable than Ur had been, its temple complexes serving as a kind of neutral ground in the wars between the successor states that were dividing the old Sumerian world among themselves. Nanna-iddin found employment in the temple scribal school, where his facility with cuneiform — inherited and honed from childhood — made him useful in the copying and administration of the legal and religious texts that the school maintained. He was not merely a copyist; he was a reader, and the distinction mattered. A copyist reproduced what was on the tablet. Nanna-iddin understood it, which made him the man the school’s administrators brought their difficult texts to — the damaged tablet, the ambiguous phrase, the disputed reading. He resolved these problems with the confidence of someone for whom understanding was not the goal of reading but its natural consequence.

Alongside him worked a junior apprentice named Lugal-esh, whose task was to record the process of restoration itself for the temple archives. Lugal-esh did not always understand what he copied, but he understood that what he witnessed was unusual, and he wrote it down with care. His record, later preserved among the school’s teaching tablets, is the source of the account that follows. He noted what was done, what was said, and the order in which it occurred, but he also noted his own uncertainties where they arose, for he was conscious that the meaning of events sometimes exceeded the understanding of the observer.

The Sacred Boundary-Record Of Ninlil’s Southern Field

Recorded by Lugal-esh, apprentice of the tablet-house of Enlil’s Temple, Nippur, in the year the governor of Isin sent his third delegation

It was the season of the dry wind. The dust came in through the reed matting of the tablet-house and settled on the shelves in a fine grey film, so that the clay of the older tablets and the dust of the air became, over time, indistinguishable from one another. Oil lamps burned in the inner rooms where the light did not reach, and the fat they consumed was old and had not been rendered cleanly, so there was always beneath the smell of the dust and the reed and the dry air a lower smell, animal and slightly rotten, that one stopped noticing only by living with it long enough.

I record this not because it is pleasant to recall but because Nanna-iddin has told me more than once that the conditions of a place are themselves a kind of text, and that a man who does not read them will fail to read the tablets within them correctly. He said this first on the morning that Sharrum-ilu the administrator sent for him, and I have reason to believe he had already been reading the tablet-house for some time before any of us understood that a crime had been committed within it.

Nanna-iddin in the season of his greatest working was not a young man, but he was not old. He was somewhere in the interval between those conditions that some men occupy for a decade and others seem to skip entirely, passing from one to the other in the space of a year’s grief or exertion. He was spare in his person and economical in his movements in a way that I had come to understand was not indolence — though it resembled indolence closely enough to have deceived me in the early months of my apprenticeship — but rather a form of conservation. He did not move when he did not need to move. He did not speak when he did not need to speak. When a difficult tablet was placed before him, he would sometimes sit with it untouched for so long that a visitor would assume he had forgotten it, and then he would reach out and turn it to the light and say, with the evenness of a man reading a grain receipt, exactly what it said and what it meant and why the previous three scholars who had attempted it had read it wrong. He had, in this period, been sitting for eleven days with a literary composition from the old Ur III archive — something to do with a lament — and he had not yet touched his reed stylus. He sat with it in the lamp-light and his eyes moved and occasionally he pressed two fingers against his mouth as though holding something in, and otherwise he was still.

When he was not working, he was considerably more difficult to be near. He had a habit, in the empty hours, of setting the apprentices increasingly elaborate riddles drawn from the legal archive — riddles about inheritance disputes and field boundaries and the precedents established by contradictory royal decrees — and then dismissing our answers with a precision that was not unkind but was not kind either. He had memorised, as far as I could determine, every boundary survey in the school’s collection, every contract, every adoption record, and every list of divine epithets, and he deployed this knowledge without apparent effort in the way that a carpenter reaches for the appropriate tool, except that the carpenter must look at his tools first. His one relief was the riddles. Without them, when the dry season made the work sparse, he became first still, then restless, then sharp in a way that made the senior scribes find reasons to be elsewhere.

It was on one of these empty mornings, the eleventh of his stillness before the lament-tablet, that Sharrum-ilu’s house-servant came to the tablet-house with a message, and Nanna-iddin’s eyes changed in a way I had not seen before. Not excitement, precisely. Something more controlled than excitement. The look of a man who has been waiting for something without knowing he was waiting for it, and who recognises it the moment it arrives.

Sharrum-ilu was the chief administrator of the tablet-house’s legal archive — a large man with the bearing of someone who had learned, relatively late in life, that bearing was something one could learn. He received us in the outer room of his office, where four oil lamps were burning against the dust-dimmed light, and he had the expression of a man who has been wearing a composed face for several hours and is beginning to find it heavy.

He told us the following: that the Boundary-Record of Ninlil’s Southern Field — a tablet of considerable age and administrative importance, documenting the legal boundaries of temple land that was currently the subject of a dispute with the estate of a powerful local family — had been placed in the archive three days ago for inspection prior to the dispute hearing. That it had been placed there by himself, witnessed by the senior librarian Ur-Namma. That this morning, when it was required for the hearing’s preliminary documentation, it was not where it had been placed. That in its location was instead a different tablet — a grain receipt of no significance — and that the original was nowhere in the archive.

He then told us that a junior copyist named Imgur-Enlil had been found, that morning, in the possession of a small quantity of silver that he could not satisfactorily account for, and that Imgur-Enlil had been present in the archive room on two of the three days since the tablet’s placement. He looked at Nanna-iddin with the expression of a man presenting a completed calculation.

Nanna-iddin said nothing. He was looking at the lamps.

“You want me to find the tablet,” he said, finally, which was not quite the same thing as agreeing with what Sharrum-ilu had said.

“I want the matter resolved before the hearing,” Sharrum-ilu said. “With certainty. The dispute cannot proceed without the boundary record, and the family’s advocates will use any ambiguity in the handling of this archive to argue that our documentation is unreliable.”

“The junior copyist,” Nanna-iddin said. “Where is he now?”

“Confined. In the store-room off the eastern corridor.”

“And the grain receipt that was found in the tablet’s location — where is that?”

Sharrum-ilu gestured to a small tablet on his table. It was a standard field-receipt, the kind produced in quantity, the clay a lighter colour than the archive tablets. Nanna-iddin looked at it without touching it. Then he asked to see the archive room, and the place where the Boundary-Record had been stored.

I will try to record accurately what I witnessed in the archive room, though I confess that I did not understand the significance of most of it until Nanna-iddin explained it to me afterward, and there may be elements of the explanation that I have rendered imperfectly in the transcription. Nanna-iddin has told me that this is acceptable — that an imperfect record of a witnessed truth is more valuable than a perfect record of a reconstructed one — but I am aware that future readers of this tablet will not be able to ask him to clarify, and so I will note my uncertainties where they arise.

The archive room was long and narrow, shelved on both sides with the tablet-cases that the school used for long-term storage — clay boxes, sealed, with summary labels pressed into their lids. It smelled of old clay and of the particular slightly sweet smell that comes from clay that has been fired a very long time ago and has since been sealed. At the far end, there was a single lamp bracket, unlit. The tablet that was missing had been stored on the third shelf of the left wall, at approximately the height of a man’s chest, in a case that Sharrum-ilu indicated was the second from the left in that row.

Nanna-iddin looked at the case. He did not open it. He looked at the shelf above it, and the shelf below it, and then he looked at the floor in front of the shelving. Then he turned and looked at the door of the archive room — the hinge, specifically. It was a wooden door with a bronze hinge-pin, and the pin had been recently oiled; there was a small quantity of oil still visible at the joint, slightly tacky in the dry air.

“Who maintains this room?” he asked.

“Ur-Namma,” Sharrum-ilu said. “The senior librarian.”

“When was that hinge last oiled?”

Sharrum-ilu looked at the hinge with the expression of a man being asked to recall whether clouds had been present on a particular afternoon. “I couldn’t say.”

“It has been oiled in the last two days,” Nanna-iddin said. “Probably last night or the night before. The oil is fresh but has begun to set. It is a careful oiling — not the usual maintenance application but deliberate. Applied to make the door open quietly.”

Sharrum-ilu began to say something, and Nanna-iddin said, without particular emphasis, “Not yet.”

He then examined the dust on the floor. There was a great deal of it — the dry season had been thorough — and it lay in a fine even film across the stone except in two places: a path of disturbance in front of the third shelf, left wall, which was consistent with someone standing there and moving things; and a smaller disturbance slightly further along the same shelf, four cases to the right, which was less obvious but which Nanna-iddin stood before for a long moment. He did not crouch down. He looked at it from above, from the side, and then he looked at the case that occupied that position on the shelf, and something in his expression changed almost imperceptibly, as though a very small calculation had resolved itself.

He asked to see Imgur-Enlil.

The junior copyist was young — perhaps my own age, perhaps a year younger — with the look of someone who had not slept in the night and had spent the sleepless hours rehearsing distress until it had become genuine. He was sitting on the floor of the store-room with his knees drawn up, and he stood when we entered with the hasty respect of someone who is very frightened and wants to demonstrate that they understand the seriousness of their situation.

Nanna-iddin sat down on a storage jar turned on its side and looked at the boy for a moment. Then he said, in the same even tone he used for everything, “Tell me about the silver.”

Imgur-Enlil told us, in the stumbling way of someone describing something true that they are aware sounds false: that he had found the silver — three small pieces, wrapped in cloth — in the carrying-bag he used to transport his work materials to and from the archive room. He did not know how it had got there. He had not taken it. He had not taken the tablet. He had been in the archive room on the days in question because his current work assignment required him to copy a set of land-grant records from the case adjacent to the one where the Boundary-Record had been stored.

“On the first day,” Nanna-iddin said, “or on the second?”

Imgur-Enlil blinked. “Both days.”

“Describe your carrying-bag.”

He described it: a standard reed bag, the kind all the junior scribes used, with a cord tied through two holes at the top. He kept his blank tablets, his stylus case, and his lunch cloth in it.

“You store it where, during the working day?”

“In the outer room. There is a shelf — all the apprentices use it for their bags during the work hours.”

Nanna-iddin was silent for a moment. Then he said: “The case adjacent to the land-grants where you were working — the one four places along the third shelf from the left. When you were working there on either day, did you notice anything unusual about it? Anything displaced, or out of the expected order?”

Imgur-Enlil’s expression shifted slightly from distress toward confusion, which in my experience of frightened people is a sign that they are hearing something unexpected enough to temporarily displace the fear. He thought. “On the second day,” he said slowly, “the label on that case was facing the wrong way. Usually they all face outward. That one was facing in. I thought someone had replaced it carelessly.”

“Did you correct it?”

“No. It wasn’t my case. I didn’t want to interfere.”

Nanna-iddin stood up from the storage jar and said, in the tone of someone reaching the end of a calculation: “You did not take the tablet. Go and wash your face.”

He did not tell us anything until we were back in Sharrum-ilu’s office with the grain receipt on the table between us. This was, I came to understand, characteristic — the withholding was not cruelty but the necessary architecture of explanation. If he had told us each thing as he observed it, we would have had observations. By gathering them and releasing them together at the point of their complete significance, he gave us understanding, which is a different and more lasting thing.

He picked up the grain receipt — the tablet found in the place of the missing Boundary-Record — and held it to the lamp. He said:

“This was made within the last five days. The clay is still slightly responsive to pressure at its thickest point — press it here and you will feel it yield very slightly. A tablet three days old at most. It was not made before the crime; it was made to be placed in the Boundary-Record’s location after the Boundary-Record was removed. Someone wanted the shelf to appear occupied.”

Sharrum-ilu was looking at him steadily. He was a man who had learned composure, and he was using it now. “That is consistent with what I told you. The junior copyist—”

“Was in the room at the correct time, and has silver in his bag that he cannot explain, and does not have the tablet,” Nanna-iddin said. “These are three separate facts. The instinct is to connect them into one story. The story is: he took the tablet and sold it, and was careless about concealing the payment. But if that story is true, he must also have been careful enough to make a convincing replacement tablet and install it in the correct location, and he must have oiled the door hinge before he left, and he must have then left the silver in a bag that is stored, during working hours, on a shelf accessible to everyone in the outer room.”

He set the grain receipt back down. “A man careful enough to plan a substitution tablet and oil a hinge will not leave the payment in his communal bag. These facts do not belong to the same person.”

The lamp flame moved in a draught from somewhere, and the shadows on the wall shifted and settled.

“The replacement tablet,” he continued, “required someone who could produce a plausible grain receipt. That is not difficult — the forms are standard — but it requires someone with access to clay, a stylus, and some facility with the script. The junior copyists all have this. So does every senior scribe in the school. But the replacement was placed in the correct position on the shelf, which requires knowledge of the archive’s organisation that is not general. And the case four positions along — the one with the reversed label — was used to store the Boundary-Record itself, temporarily, on the day it was removed. Someone put it there, and then took it away again at the end of the day, and forgot to reverse the label when they replaced it.”

“How do you know it was there?” I asked — I could not help myself — “and not somewhere else?”

He looked at me with the expression he used when I had asked something that was not entirely foolish. “The dust disturbance in front of that case was made by someone taking something heavy out and then replacing it. Taking it out alone would leave one pattern; replacing it alone would leave another. The pattern in front of that case shows both, in sequence. The pattern in front of the Boundary-Record’s original location shows only removal. Something large was moved from one location to the other on the same day, and then removed entirely in the night that followed, by someone who oiled the hinge to enter quietly.”

He looked at Sharrum-ilu. He had been looking at him, I realised, throughout most of this — not exclusively, but returning to him with a consistency that I had not attended to until now.

“There is one other thing,” Nanna-iddin said. “The Boundary-Record documents a legal dispute. Its disappearance before the hearing is advantageous to the family whose estate disputes the boundary. Someone in this school has communicated with that family’s advocates, or with the family itself, and has received payment for the record’s removal. The silver in Imgur-Enlil’s bag was placed there afterward, to provide a suspect. Three small pieces — enough to look like a junior copyist’s reward for a modest theft, not enough to be the actual price of a document of this significance.”

He folded his hands.

“You commissioned the Boundary-Record’s restoration three months ago,” he said to Sharrum-ilu. “Before it came to the archive, it came to me, for reading. You were the one who brought it and the one who collected it. You are one of perhaps four people in this school who know the archive well enough to use that second case as a temporary location. You have access to clay and a stylus and the outer room where the apprentices’ bags are kept. And you wanted this resolved before the hearing with, you told me, certainty — meaning that you wanted to know, before you were asked, how much I had observed.”

The room was very quiet. The fat in the lamp made a small sound, a faint crackle, as it consumed itself.

Sharrum-ilu looked at him for a long time. Whatever he had prepared to say in this eventuality, if he had prepared anything, he did not say it. He looked at the grain receipt on the table. He looked at the door.

“Where is the tablet now?” Nanna-iddin asked.

Another silence. Then: “With the advocate’s household. I had no means to retrieve it once it was placed.”

“The advocate’s household,” Nanna-iddin said, “is less than a morning’s journey. The hearing is in four days. If the tablet were returned to its correct location in this archive before that hearing, in the night, by a person unknown, the matter of its disappearance would resolve itself without the requirement for any official process.” He paused. “The school would benefit from that resolution. The dispute would proceed on documented grounds. A junior copyist would be returned to his work, which is, I understand, behind schedule.”

Sharrum-ilu looked at him for a long time.

“And the advocate’s household,” he said carefully, “would no longer have the document in their possession.”

“Which would leave them in a considerably more difficult position than if they had never received it,” Nanna-iddin said, with an equanimity that I found, in retrospect, remarkable. “The boundary record, read correctly — and I have read it correctly, as it was brought to me — favours the temple’s position. The family’s advocates received it because they were told it favoured theirs. They were misinformed. Its return to the archive is therefore also in their interest, if they have the capacity to understand their interest.”

What Sharrum-ilu said next I will not record. It was not part of the matter. He left the office shortly afterward, and Nanna-iddin and I walked back through the tablet-house to the room where the lament-tablet waited.

Imgur-Enlil was restored to his copying work the following morning. The Boundary-Record was found in its correct location on the morning of the third day. The hearing proceeded. I do not know the outcome of the dispute, because Nanna-iddin did not consider it relevant and I had learned by then to align my sense of relevance with his on such questions.

Sharrum-ilu was removed from his position some months later, for reasons the official record attributes to irregularities in the grain accounts. This is probably also true. Nanna-iddin had, in the period before the tablet matter, remarked once — in the tone he used for observations he considered settled — that a man dishonest in one kind of accounting was rarely honest in another.

I asked him, once, why he had not brought the matter to the temple authorities — to the ummia, the chief scholar, or to the city governor’s office. He was silent for a moment, turning a tablet in his hands.

“Because the record would have had to follow him,” he said. “And the record of a senior administrator convicted of theft from the sacred archive, in a city that is currently demonstrating its stability to three rival states, would do more damage to the school than one administrator’s quiet departure.” He set the tablet down. “The tablet is back. The boy is back. The hearing proceeds. This is what I was hired to achieve.”

“But he—” I began.

“Has been dealt with by consequences he will not recover from, more completely than any public sentence would have managed,” he said. “He cannot return to administration. He cannot provide references. He cannot explain his departure without explaining the reason for it. The law would have imprisoned him for a season and then released him into a city with a grudge and a story. This way, he has neither.”

He picked up the lament-tablet and looked at it in the lamp-light.

“There is a line in this,” he said, after a moment, and his voice had changed to the tone it used when something in a text had finally resolved itself, “that I have been misreading for eleven days. It is not a description of the city’s destruction. It is a description of the moment before the destruction, when the gods have already decided and the city does not yet know. The dread is in the not-knowing.” He looked at me. “That is the condition I find most useful to cultivate in those who have done me harm. Write that down.”

I wrote it down.

Here the account ends.
This account was copied from the teaching tablets of the house of Enlil by a later hand. The original tablets of Lugal-esh were much damaged at their edges; where the restoration is uncertain I have marked the clay. The matter of the boundary dispute is recorded separately in the governor’s records as having been resolved in the temple’s favour.
Nanna-iddin’s name does not appear in those records.
