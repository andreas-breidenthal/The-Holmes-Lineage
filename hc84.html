HOLMAR OF AUSTRASIA

~520 AD — Austrasia, Frankish Territory

Austrasia — the eastern Frankish realm, centred on the Meuse and Rhine valleys — was the more Germanic half of the Frankish world, rougher and less Romanised than the south, and Holmar grew up in it with the adaptability that had characterised his family across a dozen cultures and five centuries. The Merovingian dynasty was at the height of its power and already, to a careful observer, beginning to show the internal fractures that would eventually produce the long crisis of the rois fainéants — the do-nothing kings — and the rise of the Carolingian mayors of the palace.

Holmar was a careful observer. He served as a legal officer in the Austrasian court, adjudicating disputes between the Germanic and Romanised populations according to the separate legal codes each community used — a role that required him to hold two incompatible systems of law simultaneously in his head and find, between them, outcomes that both parties could accept. He was, in the terminology of a much later age, a remarkably effective mediator. The secret, as he might have explained it, was simple: listen to what each man says, identify what he has not said, and find the fact he is most afraid of you discovering. The rest follows naturally.

The record of these proceedings is preserved in the notes of Aurelius, a court clerk attached to the Austrasian judiciary, who was responsible for copying testimony and preserving rulings. His account reflects both what he observed and the limits of his understanding; he records faithfully the sequence of events, though not always the full significance of what he witnessed at the time.

The Third Account of Holmar of Austrasia

Set down by Aurelius, Clerk to the Judiciary, in the year of our Lord five hundred and fifty-two, in the reign of Theuderic’s successors, at Metz.

There are days in the court at Metz when the hall smells of wet wool and grievance in equal measure. The autumn had come early that year, driving rain sideways off the Rhine plain and through every gap in the stonework that the old Roman builders had not thought to seal — which was, after three generations of Frankish occupancy, a considerable number of gaps. The rushes on the floor had not been changed since the feast of Saint Remigius and they gave off, in the damp, a smell of something ancient and unhappy. The oil lamps along the eastern wall smoked badly in that weather; the tallow was river-rendered, cut with something cheaper, and it left a greasy, animal smell on everything it touched, including the documents one was attempting to read.

I record these details because Holmar, when he arrived that morning, remarked on all of them within the first thirty seconds of crossing the threshold, before he had seen the parties, before he had been told the nature of the complaint, and before, as far as I could determine, he had any particular reason to remark on anything at all.

“The plaintiff,” he said, removing his travelling cloak and handing it to no one in particular — I caught it — “is a man of some property but present anxiety. He arrived before dawn. He has been standing long enough for his left boot to have left a distinct compression in those rushes by the pillar, but he is too agitated to sit. The accused has been here perhaps an hour, no more, and is angry rather than frightened.” He paused. “What is the charge?”

I told him: livestock theft. Specifically, the alleged theft of four oxen, two milch cows, and a quantity of breeding swine from the estate of Lucius Flavianus Carus, Gallo-Roman landowner and citizen, by one Wigbald, a Frankish settler whose holding lay adjacent to the Carus estate along the Moselle.

Holmar looked at me with the expression I had come to associate with what I can only describe as professional satisfaction — a slight tightening at the corners of the eyes, a barely perceptible straightening of the spine. It was the look of a man who has been handed a puzzle and has already, before touching it, guessed the shape of the solution.

“Under which code,” he asked, “has the charge been filed?”

“Salic law,” I said. “Germanic procedure.”

“Naturally,” said Holmar.

He crossed to his seat and heard the matter in full: Carus’s accusation, Wigbald’s furious denial, and the testimony of a herdsman who claimed to have seen cattle driven along the road in darkness.

Then Holmar asked a different question.

“Aurelius,” he said. “The Carus estate. What is its Roman legal status?”

I answered: registered property, governed by Roman law. Which meant that any sale required written documentation.

“And such documents,” Holmar said, “would be held by the registrar at Metz.”

He requested the records.

The hall waited two hours.

When the documents arrived, Holmar found what he expected: a bill of transfer, dated before the alleged theft, recording the sale of precisely those animals to a dealer in Trier.

The livestock had not been stolen.

They had been sold.

Carus confessed under the weight of silence rather than accusation. Debt had driven him to attempt a fraudulent claim under Salic law, where witness and oath might suffice in place of documentation.

Wigbald, who had faced ruin, said only: “You would have destroyed me.”

Holmar dismissed the charge.

He did not pursue the fraud further. He instructed me to record only that the evidence did not support the claim.

Later, when the hall was empty, I asked him why.

“There are webs,” he said, “and there are strands. Today we addressed a strand.”

He paused, then added, as if in afterthought:

“A man has two keys. One opens every lock. One opens none. Which is more valuable?”

I answered incorrectly.

“The one that opens none,” he said, “because it tells you where the locks are.”

He requested the registrar’s records for three further quarters.

“There is a pattern in these frauds,” he said. “I have not yet found the shape of it.”

He went out into the rain.

I wrote what he told me to write.

I also wrote this.
