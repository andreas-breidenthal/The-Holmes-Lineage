AMENEMHAT-NAKHT

~1780 BC — Thebes, Egypt

The Karnak Land Register

Translated from the Hieratic script,
as set down by the hand of Sennefer

I.

I remember the smell before I remember anything else.

Cedar oil and old papyrus and the dry rot that attacks the records when they have been stored damp — I knew that smell. Every scribe in the department knew it. But on the morning of the fourteenth day of the second month of Peret, when I arrived at the archive chamber before the other scribes and found Amenemhat-nakht already standing in the doorway, I smelled something that did not belong: the sharp, concentrated bite of fresh natron. Not the faint residual sharpness left by the floor-cleaners — their work had long since dissipated — but the stronger, unmistakable concentration of natron used to treat fresh papyrus.

Someone had been at work here in the night.

I said so. Amenemhat-nakht did not answer. He stood in the doorway for what felt to me like a very long time, looking at the shelves. He did not touch anything. He simply looked, with the particular quality of stillness he had when something had just confirmed a conclusion he had been approaching for some time. I had learned, in the seven months of working alongside him, to be made uneasy by that stillness. Motion I could interpret. Stillness, in him, meant that something had already been decided.

Then he turned and said, with a calm that I found, under the circumstances, almost offensive: "Go and eat your bread. We have a great deal of walking to do today."

I should say something about who I am and why I am the one writing this account, since it is not, in any useful sense, my account. I am Sennefer, junior priest-scribe of the land records department, son of a farmer of the nome of Waset, trained in letters at the temple school and in land administration under the direction of Hapu, Overseer of Temple Lands. I am twenty-three years old.

The man whose account this actually is was called Amenemhat-nakht — Amun is foremost, he is strong. It was a name that said something about the moment he was born into: Thebes pressing its claim, its god ascending toward the supreme position in the pantheon, and a family with the habit of attaching its given names to whichever divinity was ascendant. He was not of the hereditary priesthood, which is closed and passes through blood. He was what the temple's growing estate network required and rarely acknowledged: a literate administrator of exceptional precision, assigned to the department responsible for land records — the documentation of the holdings from which Karnak drew its revenues — and trusted, within limits that suited his superiors, to confirm what was already believed.

He did not confirm what was already believed. This is the account of what he found instead.

I have been asked by the inspectorate to write it because Amenemhat-nakht declined. He said he found the writing of accounts about himself to be a waste of papyrus. He said I should write it, since I had been present and had, in his words, "an honest face and a legible hand." He also said — this I believe he intended me to hear — that a man who writes down only what he himself observed cannot exaggerate, which would serve everyone's interests.

This is, I believe, the closest he comes to a compliment.

II.

I should begin where he says all honest accounts must begin: with the smell of a lie.

But the lie did not begin on the morning of the natron. The lie was older than my assignment to this department, older perhaps than my employment at the temple. To understand the morning in the archive, I must first walk you to the boundary of the Third Estate of the Eastern Holdings, where Amenemhat-nakht first showed me what he was capable of.

He had been in the department for three years before I arrived. I had been told, in the way one is told things about a senior colleague, that he was peculiar — that he kept strange hours, ate very little during an investigation, and was frequently found in the archive at night, reading documents whose relevance to his assigned work was not immediately apparent. I was told he was brilliant and difficult to manage. I was told these things as cautions. I did not know, at the time, that they were also a kind of awed testimony.

When Hapu assigned me to assist in the boundary survey, I thought the work would be tedious. Hapu had made it clear, in the understated way he made things clear, that the survey was expected to confirm existing knowledge. The grain shortfall was real but explicable — flood variation, crop disease, the gradual shifts in irrigation — and the survey's purpose was documentation. He said this in the placid tone of a man who has already read the conclusion and is now waiting for the pages to catch up.

Amenemhat-nakht said nothing to contradict this. He said very little to me about his intentions. He handed me the measuring cord on the first morning and told me to watch the ground.

III.

On the third day of the survey, near the southern boundary of the Third Estate, he stopped walking and crouched alongside one of the boundary stelae. It was a limestone marker, incised with the name of the estate and the titles of the overseers who had witnessed its placement in the reign of Amenemhat I, worn now by decades of river wind and carrying on its face the grey-green crust of lichen that accumulates on stone left undisturbed in wet years.

I stood with the measuring cord and waited. He was examining the base of the marker with an attention I did not understand.

After a moment he said: "Tell me what you notice about the lichen."

I looked. I said the lichen was thick on the north face and the upper portions of the stone, as one would expect from a marker in this location, receiving the morning moisture from the river direction.

He said: "Look at the east face."

I looked. The lichen on the east face was thin, patchy, and oriented — if lichen can be said to have an orientation — in a way that seemed inconsistent with the stone's current position, as though the patterns had formed when the face was exposed to different conditions.

I said: "The stone has been moved."

He said: "Approximately seven years ago, I would estimate, based on the recovery rate of the growth. Moved inward." He stood and looked along the line the boundary should have described. "There is a strip of productive land between where this marker now stands and where it originally stood. Forty cubits wide. Walk with me."

We walked. The strip was there — cultivated, green in the growing season, fed by an irrigation channel that Amenemhat-nakht traced upstream to its point of diversion with the particular focused quiet of a man reading a text he has already partly translated. Someone was farming this land. Its grain was being harvested. It was not being delivered to the temple.

I said: "Flooding could move a marker."

He said: "Yes. Flooding moves markers outward, toward the flood plain. This one was moved inward, away from the flood plain, into the temple's holding. The soil around the base has been compacted and re-settled. The disturbed layer is below the current surface. This was done carefully." He paused. "Not carefully enough."

He noted the dimensions of the strip in the document record he kept strapped to his side — a leather case I had not initially understood the purpose of, and which I came to regard as something between an armoury and an archive. Then he said we should speak to the farmers.

IV.

I had expected him to approach the farmers as an official — to state his authority, invoke the seal of the inspectorate, place them in the position of subjects before an administrator. He did none of this. He arrived at the first farmstead in the dust of mid-afternoon and accepted the bread and beer that were offered to a traveller without hesitation, and sat with the farmer and his eldest son in the shade of the grain store, and asked about the harvest, and about the irrigation channel, and about the nome's water rights in dry years, in the easy, specific way of a man who had grown up knowing what questions farmers could answer. I later learned he had not grown up knowing anything of the kind — that his facility was the product of attention rather than origin, which struck me then, and strikes me still, as a more impressive thing.

The farmer told him about the boundary document without being asked directly. He produced it from a clay jar wrapped in linen — the instinct of a man who has learned to keep evidence of his rights close — and laid it on the mat between them with the careful gravity of the illiterate placing a document they cannot read but know to be important.

The seal was Hapu's. The signature was Hapu's. The document stated that the strip of land had been reallocated to a secondary agricultural account of the temple, that this was a routine administrative adjustment, and that the farmer should direct any questions to the department of land records.

There was no corresponding document in the archive. I knew this because I had spent several evenings in the archive at Amenemhat-nakht's direction, copying records, and had seen no trace of it.

Amenemhat-nakht thanked the farmer, returned the document, and walked away. He walked for some time without speaking. I had learned not to fill the silences.

At last he said: "The grain does not vanish. It is redirected. Someone receives it. Someone signs for it." He looked at me. "We are going back to the archive."

V.

What followed was not dramatic. I want to be honest about this, because I think the inspectorate expects a story of sudden revelation, and that is not what it was. It was six nights of lamp-work in the archive — cedar-oil light, the smell of old papyrus, the distant percussion of the night-time temple rituals — in which Amenemhat-nakht read and I copied and occasionally he asked me to locate a specific document and I located it and he looked at it for a long time and said nothing useful.

I asked him once, at the third or fourth night, what we were looking for.

He said: "The mechanism. The grain moved through accounts. We are looking for the hands that moved it."

I did not understand, then, that he meant this literally.

The transfer accounts were elegant — I will say this now, though I did not understand it at the time. They told a coherent story of grain moving between internal temple accounts: legitimate movements, individually plausible, following the patterns one would expect from a department that processed a great deal of grain between a great deal of sub-accounts. An auditor reading quickly would see a record in order. The misdirection was not absence but addition — additional entries, additional transfers, a paper chain that led the grain to a secondary contractor account and then allowed it to vanish into the fiction of agricultural distributions.

The entries had been written in three different hands. Two I recognised as belonging to scribes who had rotated through the department in previous years. The third I did not recognise, and I said so.

Amenemhat-nakht handed me a document — a routine survey account written in his own name, for reference — and placed beside it one of the fraudulent transfer entries. He said: "Look at the horizontal strokes in the transfer entry. Look at how they begin."

I looked. The strokes thickened at their left ends, as though the hand that formed them pressed harder on the entry stroke than on the exit.

I looked at Amenemhat-nakht's own document. His strokes did not do this.

He placed a third document between them — a routine authorisation signed by Hapu in the course of normal departmental business, one of dozens in the files. I looked at the horizontal strokes.

They thickened at their left ends.

The realisation came to me slowly. Amenemhat-nakht watched me arrive at it with the patient, unsettling attention of a teacher who already knows the answer and is waiting to see how long the student needs.

I said: "The third hand is Hapu's. He was writing as someone else. As—" I stopped.

He waited.

I said: "As me."

He said: "As you. Or more precisely, as a version of you that had authorised a series of fraudulent internal transfers. The imitation is good but imperfect. His arm carries a habit he cannot entirely suppress." He gathered the documents. "The question is what he intends to do with the identity he has been constructing. That is what the natron will tell us."

I did not understand this then. I understood it on the morning of the fourteenth day of the second month of Peret, when I walked into the archive and smelled what did not belong.

VI.

He sent me, that morning, to stand outside the east entrance of the archive with a message for Hapu — a routine notice of a minor discrepancy in the survey measurements, the sort of thing that would require the overseer's attention and draw him away from his private office. It was a fiction. I wrote it in my own hand, sealed it with my own small seal, and delivered it to Hapu's assistant with the instruction that it was not urgent.

I confess I did not know, at that moment, exactly what Amenemhat-nakht was doing inside the archive. He had told me enough, and no more. He had told me that a document had been placed in the eastern holdings section during the night — a document treated with natron to suggest aging, positioned where the inspectorate would find it at month's end. He had told me this document would bear my writing, or something close to it. He had told me that the man who placed it was not yet aware it had been found.

The rest I waited for in the dust of the east court, watching the cedar gate-hinge that had needed oil for three months, listening to it complain in the morning wind, until Amenemhat-nakht appeared in the doorway and gestured for me to follow.

He took me to the document. It was filed in the third tier of the eastern holdings section, tucked behind a survey summary from two reigns prior. It was written in a hand that was mine and was not mine — better than the transfer entries he had shown me at the lamp, more practised, as though the forger had been refining the imitation over a period of months. It purported to trace the missing grain through a series of transfers that I, Sennefer, had authorised in violation of my position and in concealment from the overseer.

It was dated fourteen months ago.

The natron treatment was recent. The sharpness of it had not fully dissipated, which meant the treatment had been applied within the past day or two, and the document had been placed in the archive during the preceding night. Someone had worked quickly. The inspectorate was arriving within the week.

I stood looking at my own name on a document I had never written, at my own hand as someone else had constructed it, and felt a sensation I do not have adequate words for — something between vertigo and rage, but colder than either, and with a particular quality of helplessness, as though I had been reading a story in which, I now discovered, I was a character.

Amenemhat-nakht said: "You were not the target of the scheme. You were its conclusion. The boundary markers were moved before you arrived at this department. The contractor account was created before you arrived. The pen-stroke entries in the transfer records were made in imitation of your hand, because you were new and your hand was not yet well-known. You were the shape of a crime that had already occurred — pressed into wet clay and then lifted. When the inspectorate arrived, they would have found the impression and called it you."

I said: "You knew this was coming."

He said: "I suspected the mechanism for some time. I did not know the timeline until this morning. The natron tells me he has become aware that the inspectorate's arrival is sooner than he calculated. He worked in the night. He made small errors he would not otherwise have made." He looked at the document with what I can only describe as professional displeasure. "The imitation is better than his previous attempts. He has been practising."

I asked him what we were going to do.

He said: "Tonight, you and I will be in the archive after the evening meal. I will tell Hapu that I wish to share the conclusions of my survey with him and that I require your presence, since you conducted portions of the boundary work. He will agree. He believes my survey has confirmed only the primary discrepancy — the moved markers — and that I have not yet traced the mechanism." He paused. "He believes he has a few days remaining."

I said: "And when he realises you know everything?"

Amenemhat-nakht considered this with the equanimity of a man calculating a flood-year from rainfall records. He said: "That is when the evening becomes interesting."

VII.

I want to set down what happened that night accurately, because the inspectorate's questions suggested they doubted certain details, and I have an honest face and a legible hand and I was present.

Hapu arrived at the archive in good humour. This I remember clearly — the settled ease of a man who is managing a situation and believes the management is proceeding well. He had brought date wine, a gesture of collegial warmth that, in retrospect, says something about the confidence with which he viewed the evening.

Amenemhat-nakht had arranged the chamber as I had not seen it arranged before. The three comparison documents were already on the table — the fraudulent transfer entry, the genuine account in my hand, and the routine authorisation in Hapu's. They were placed with the particular deliberateness of a man who has thought about where each thing should be. The natron-treated forgery was not visible. I knew it was in the leather document case at Amenemhat-nakht's side.

Hapu settled into his seat. He poured the wine. He asked about the survey.

Amenemhat-nakht began with the boundary markers.

He described the moved stelae — their positions, the evidence of deliberate relocation rather than flood movement, the lichen patterns on the east face inconsistent with the stone's current orientation. He described the strip of productive land, its dimensions and yield across seven years, its approximate contribution to the total shortfall. He placed before Hapu the farmer's account of the reallocation document — Hapu's seal, Hapu's signature, no archive copy. He did all of this in the same tone in which he had walked the boundaries with me: patient, specific, without accusation, as though he were reading a list of survey measurements aloud.

Hapu said the markers had been moved by flooding. He said the reallocation document was filed in a subsidiary register he could produce in the morning. He said this with composure, and with the slightly aggrieved authority of a man of eleven years' experience who is being questioned by a subordinate. He reached for his wine.

Amenemhat-nakht said: "I have examined all the subsidiary registers."

Hapu set down his cup.

Amenemhat-nakht moved to the transfer accounts. He described the three hands, the paper chain, the contractor account that existed only as a line in a record and had no physical presence in any transaction register. He described the particular leftward thickening of the horizontal strokes in the third hand — Hapu's habit, the pressure of a man who had spent his career writing with his arm at a particular angle, which he could not entirely suppress when he wrote as someone else. He placed the three documents on the table between them. He invited Hapu to examine the strokes.

Hapu did not look at the documents. He looked at Amenemhat-nakht.

Then he looked at me.

I had been standing near the east wall, as Amenemhat-nakht had positioned me, and I will not pretend I was not frightened. Hapu was a large man — this is something the documents do not convey, but I remember it clearly — a man who had spent years walking estate boundaries and carrying survey equipment, and when he looked at me across the table in the lamplight, there was something in his face that was not the face of an administrator managing a misunderstanding. It was the face of a man calculating how the situation had changed and what it now required.

He said, very quietly: "The young scribe has been helpful to you."

Amenemhat-nakht said: "He has."

Hapu said: "It would be unfortunate if his involvement in this survey were to draw additional scrutiny to the records he personally handled during his first weeks in the department. New scribes are sometimes careless. Errors are made. An overseer's responsibility is to identify such errors." He paused. "This would be an unpleasant outcome for a young man of otherwise good character."

It was a threat. I understood it as a threat. What I did not understand — what I could not see from where I was standing — was that it was also the last move Amenemhat-nakht had been waiting for.

He reached into the leather case. He placed the natron-treated forgery on the table.

He said: "This document was filed in the third tier of the eastern holdings section during the night of the thirteenth day of this month. It purports to trace the missing grain through a series of transfers authorised by Sennefer. It was treated with natron to suggest aging. The treatment was applied within the past two days. The inspectorate arrives within the week." He looked at Hapu. "The document is the threat. You are making it now because you believe it exists only in the archive, that Sennefer has not seen it, and that I do not know it is there. You are making a threat with a weapon I have already taken from your hand."

Hapu was very still.

I heard, from somewhere deep in the temple, the single note of a bronze bell — the call to the night offering. The cedar gate-hinge complained in the wind. A lamp on the far shelf had begun to gutter.

Hapu stood.

He did not stand to leave. He stood the way a man stands when he has decided that the only remaining move is a physical one — and I had a moment, a single moment, in which I understood with complete clarity that I was in a room with someone who had redirected a decade's worth of temple grain and had spent the past year building a case that would have destroyed me in order to protect himself, and who was no longer thinking about documents.

He reached for the natron-treated forgery.

Amenemhat-nakht had his hand over it before Hapu reached the edge of the table.

What happened then was not elegant. I will set it down as I witnessed it. Hapu moved to take the document by force, and Amenemhat-nakht moved the document behind him and stood, and for a moment they were two men in a lamp-lit room with a table between them and eleven years of institutional authority on one side and nothing on the other except absolute certainty. Hapu was larger. Amenemhat-nakht did not move back.

I had, by this point, crossed to the archive doorway. I will confess that this was partly instinct and partly the instruction Amenemhat-nakht had given me before Hapu arrived, which I had not entirely understood at the time: If the evening becomes interesting, go to the door and remain there. You are not a large man. Your function is as a witness, not a combatant.

I stood in the doorway. I did not move.

Hapu looked at me standing in the doorway. He looked at Amenemhat-nakht standing between him and the document. He looked at the three comparison documents still laid out on the table, which he had never touched — which bore none of his marks, none of his handling — and which would go to the inspectorate exactly as they were.

He sat down.

The silence lasted for a long time. The guttering lamp on the far shelf produced a thin thread of black smoke that moved toward the ceiling and dissolved.

Amenemhat-nakht sat down across from him. When he spoke, his voice had returned to its survey-measurement register, and this, I think, was more frightening than any anger could have been.

He said: "I have not yet delivered any of this to the inspectorate. I wished to speak with you first." He waited. "I want Sennefer's name formally cleared before any other action is taken — a document, sealed by the inspectorate, that cannot be revisited. I want it written before I submit my report, so that it exists independently of what you may or may not choose to do next."

Hapu said: "And in return?"

Amenemhat-nakht said: "In return, I note in my report that you cooperated fully when presented with the evidence, and that your cooperation was substantive and complete. The inspectorate is not unaware that full cooperation has value." He paused. "You may also wish to consider that a man who has operated this particular scheme for eleven years will have knowledge of the temple's broader financial arrangements that the inspectorate will find of considerable interest. This knowledge, disclosed voluntarily, is worth a great deal more than the same knowledge disclosed under compulsion."

It was not, I understood later, mercy. It was the offer of a rational choice to a man who had just run out of irrational ones. Amenemhat-nakht had removed every other option from the room before he sat down.

Hapu looked at him for a long time.

He said: "You have been watching me for the whole of this investigation."

Amenemhat-nakht said: "Yes."

Hapu said: "How long did you know?"

Amenemhat-nakht said: "The boundary markers indicated the direction. The archive indicated the method. You indicated the timeline, in the way you managed Sennefer's anxiety over the past weeks — telling him the matter was under control, keeping him calm enough to remain at his post. That told me you needed him present and undisrupted until the inspectorate arrived and found what you had prepared for them." He paused. "The natron this morning confirmed what I had already concluded. I have been collecting the elements. This morning I collected the last one."

Hapu was silent for a moment. Then he said, with a flatness that I think was beyond calculation by that point and had become something more honest: "What are you?"

Amenemhat-nakht said: "A scribe. We should discuss the terms."

VIII.

I will not set down the full terms here, as they are recorded in the inspectorate's files and I am told those records are, for once, relatively complete. I will say only what I know directly.

Sennefer's name — my name — was cleared formally in writing, with the seal of the inspectorate, before Amenemhat-nakht submitted his survey report. The document exists. He gave me a copy on the day it was issued, without ceremony, as one might hand over a survey measurement that had been requested.

Hapu cooperated. The extent of his cooperation I understand only partially — there were other matters, broader matters, that the inspectorate investigated in the months following, and which are not mine to set down here. He was removed from his position. What followed, the inspectorate's records note with an unusual delicacy, is not entirely specified. He was a man of eleven years' legitimate service before the longer game, whatever its origin, had consumed him. I do not know what he believed he was building toward.

I asked Amenemhat-nakht once, when it was over, whether he had known Hapu would try to take the document — whether the moment of physical threat had been anticipated or had surprised him.

He said: "A man who has spent years operating a scheme of this kind has become accustomed to the belief that documents are what control outcomes. He would move toward the document before he moved toward anything else. I had accounted for this."

I said: "You are smaller than he is."

He said: "Yes. I also had the document."

I said: "That is not the same as physical safety."

He considered this as though it were a fair-play problem, a question of evidentiary weight. He said: "No. But you were standing in the doorway. A witness changes the calculation. He would know that anything that happened in that room would be set down by the person standing in the doorway with an honest face and a legible hand."

I had been used as a safeguard. I found, and find still, that I do not mind this as much as perhaps I should.

IX.

I was asked by Amenemhat-nakht to write this account and I have done so. I have set down what I saw and what I understood at the time, and I have tried to indicate clearly where my understanding came later, since honesty requires the distinction.

What I know about him, after seven months and this particular matter, I can set down briefly:

He slept very little during an investigation. He ate almost nothing. He smelled always of the river mud from the boundary walks and of the cedar oil of the archive lamps, and I came to associate this smell with the particular quality of his attention — that focused, patient, relentless attention that made things that were hidden become visible, not by force, but by the accumulation of observation until there was nowhere left for them to hide.

He refused a formal commendation from the chief priest of Amun. He said the temple records were sufficiently complicated already. The chief priest, I understand, was not pleased by this response, but noted it.

He moved, fourteen months later, to a position in the royal treasury's inspectorate. I do not know who recommended him. The appointment document says only that the recommendation came from the chief priest, on information from sources not specified. I suspect the sources were the inspectorate themselves, who had seen what I had seen and reached the same conclusion I had reached: that this was a man who should not be wasted on one department's grain accounts.

I have thought about him often in the years since. I have thought about the moment in the archive when he placed his hand over the document and did not move back, and I have thought about what it requires of a person to stand like that — not because they are unafraid, I think, but because the calculation has already been made and found to come out the same way regardless of the size of the man across the table.

I do not know if he was ever afraid of anything. I know that he was afraid of imprecision, of incomplete evidence, of the gap in a record that a dishonest hand might later fill. I know that on the morning he first showed me the lichen on the boundary marker — crouching in the dust of the estate boundary, pointing to the inconsistency in the growth pattern with the particular delight of a man who has just been given exactly the evidence he was looking for — he looked, for a moment, as though the world had confirmed something it owed him.

I have never forgotten that look. It was the look of a man for whom the visible world was always, in the end, a document — and who trusted, beyond any evidence I could offer to shake this trust, that every document tells the truth, provided you know how to read it.

X.

What follows is written in the margins of the preceding account, in the hand of Hapu, Overseer of Temple Lands. It appears to have been added after the inspectorate had completed their initial review and before the account was sealed.

He sat across from me in the archive and told me what he knew. He told me all of it, in the correct order, without flourish or anger, as a man reads aloud a list of commodities. I had believed myself, until that moment, to be managing a situation. I understood, in that moment, that I had been managed.

When he finished, he said: "You may wish to know that I have not yet delivered the natron-treated document to the inspectorate." He paused. "I wished to speak with you first."

I asked what he wanted.

He said he wanted nothing for himself.

I have been making choices about land records for eleven years. I know something about how the apparent and the actual come apart. I know when a document has been prepared. I know when a case has been constructed.

He had constructed a case against me that had no gaps, no assumptions, no leaps that were not bridged by evidence. He had also, simultaneously, constructed the only path forward that preserved anything worth preserving — and had laid it before me as though it were the obvious choice, the mathematically inevitable choice, the choice any rational man would make once he had read the evidence correctly.

He was not wrong.

I have added this note because the inspectorate's account will credit the systematic review of the land records, and Amenemhat-nakht's name will appear in it once, briefly, as a junior scribe who assisted in the survey. This is the way of these things. The credit moves upward; the work stays where it occurred.

I did the work he needed me to do in that archive room. I produced the grain of truth that his argument required. I do not say this with pride. I say it because the record should reflect, somewhere, what actually occurred, and who actually saw it.

He sat across from me and offered me a rational choice when all the irrational ones were gone. He was perhaps thirty years old. He showed no anger, no fear, and no triumph. He showed only the settled certainty of a man who has read every document in the room and found them to be in order.

I do not know what he is. I know that he walked the boundaries with a knotted cord and a pot of red ochre and saw things in the ground that eleven years of experience had trained me not to look for. I know that he sat in that archive for three years and read everything in it, and that when he finally spoke, there was nothing left to say that he had not already accounted for.

Hapu, Overseer of Temple Lands. Third year of the reign of Khaneferre, may he live forever.
The inspectorate's official summary attributes the discovery of the Karnak land register discrepancies to the thoroughness of the regular audit process and commends the department for its diligence. Amenemhat-nakht is mentioned once, as a junior scribe who assisted in the survey.
He served in the inspectorate for twenty-two years. He had no family of record. He is described, in a brief notation appended to his retirement document by a treasury official whose name is partially illegible, as follows:

"Difficult to manage. Solved everything given to him. Ate very little during a case."
