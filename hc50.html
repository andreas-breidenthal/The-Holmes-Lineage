HOLKOS OF ATHENS

~580 BC — Athens, Greece

He returned from the west to Athens at the moment when Athens was becoming the city it would be — Solon’s reforms two decades past, the legal and constitutional framework of the democracy being assembled, the agora developing into the genuine centre of civic life that his ancestor Holkos-Akaros had helped to establish three centuries before. Athens in 580 BC was a city in which the quality of a man’s argument mattered more than the rank of his family, and Holkos arrived with both good arguments and a good family, which gave him advantages he was not slow to use.

He taught — not formally, but in the manner of the time, through conversation in the stoas and the gymnasium, engaging the young men of the city in the examination of ideas with a method that owed more to his family’s tradition of practical reasoning than to any formal philosophical school. He created around him a small community of men who had learned to distinguish between what they knew and what they merely believed — a distinction that Athens, in the decades that followed, would find occasion to test.

Among those who listened was Demetrios, son of a minor clerk in the agora, who possessed neither wealth nor influence but an uncommonly careful memory. He did not understand why Holkos lingered over small details, but he trusted that the elder’s attention to such things would, in time, justify itself. It was Demetrios who set down what follows, preserving not only the words of Holkos but the manner in which they were spoken and the steps by which conclusions were reached. I, Demetrios, write as faithfully as I am able, though I cannot claim to always understand the full reach of what I observed.

The Account Of Demetrios

Set down in the third year of the archonship of Damasias, that those who come after may know how truth was separated from mere expectation, and how a man may be wronged by his own certainty more grievously than by any thief.

The morning had come in off the Saronic Gulf with a weight to it — not the clean heat of high summer but something thicker, a salt-damp that settled into the colonnades of the agora and made the lamp-smoke cling at shoulder height rather than rising properly toward the sky. The oil-sellers near the Stoa of the Herms burned inferior stuff that season; I remember it distinctly because the smell followed you everywhere, a rancid sweetness like figs left too long in the press, and it coloured the whole affair in memory with a quality of something slightly spoiled.

I had found Holkos in his usual condition of that period — which is to say, in no condition at all. He was seated on the low stone bench behind the fuller’s establishment where the wall holds the morning shadow longest, and he had not, so far as I could determine, moved since I had left him the previous evening. Before him lay a broken piece of pottery, an ostrakon, and he was examining it with the particular quality of attention he gave to things that other men had finished examining and discarded. His sandals were beside him but not on his feet. He had, I knew, been awake all night — not from any evidence he would have offered willingly, but because the lamp in the window above had been burning when I came through the lane at the false dawn.

This was the period of his worst languor. Solon’s great reforms had settled into the city like a man settling into a chair — the urgency of their arrival past, the real work of living under them only beginning — and Holkos found in this interval between crisis and consequence very little to occupy a mind that required, as he once told me with a frankness I did not then fully appreciate, the same quality of fuel that a forge requires: not warmth, but heat sufficient to work metal.

“You are walking at the pace of a man who has something to tell me,” he said, without looking up from the ostrakon.

“There is a merchant at the north end of the agora,” I began.

“There are always merchants at the north end of the agora, Demetrios. That is where merchants are. Proceed with more precision.”

I told him what I had heard. A man named Philon — a fabric-trader of moderate reputation, known at the scales but not especially well-regarded in the associations — had arrived that morning making a considerable noise about a theft. He claimed that a sum had been taken from him by a man he had trusted with access to his storehouse, a freedman named Kritos who had worked for him across three seasons. The sum, as Philon described it, was substantial — enough to matter, though I could not independently verify the figure. He had gone first to the Scythian archers who kept order in the agora, and they had referred him, as they referred most matters of uncertain category, to the archon’s clerk. The archon’s clerk, a methodical man named Sosias who knew my father, had listened carefully and then suggested, in the way that officials suggest things they cannot officially suggest, that there were other avenues of inquiry that might prove more productive than the formal process of accusation, which was slow and required witnesses.

Holkos set down the ostrakon. Not immediately — he completed whatever examination he was conducting first, turning it once more in his fingers — and then he set it down with the deliberateness of a man who has finished with a thing and does not intend to return to it.

“Kritos,” he said.

“You know him?”

“I know of him. He purchases thread from the woman near the south gate — good thread, better than his wages ought to support, which I had noted without, until this moment, finding useful. Bring Philon here. And Demetrios —” he paused, reaching for his sandals “— say nothing to him about what I do or do not know. He will perform better without the expectation of an audience.”

Philon was a wide man, wide in shoulder and in voice, with the particular confidence of one who has spent decades being correct about the price of things and has allowed this competence to expand beyond its proper domain into a general assumption of correctness about everything. He arrived at our corner of the agora with the energy of genuine indignation — or what presented itself as genuine indignation, which is a thing I had begun, in my time with Holkos, to learn to hold at a careful distance from indignation itself.

He told his story at once, without waiting to be asked. Kritos had worked for him three seasons. He had trusted Kritos with access to the storehouse on the hill road, above the Kerameikos, where he kept silver and goods both. Two days prior, he had gone to the storehouse to prepare a consignment for a buyer from Corinth. The silver — a sum he named with the specificity of a man who had been repeating the number to himself all morning — was gone.

Holkos heard all of this without speaking. He sat with his hands resting on his knees in the position I had come to recognize as his particular mode of attention — not stillness exactly, but a quality of directed stillness, as though he had arranged his body so that none of its energy would be wasted on anything other than listening.

When Philon had finished, there was a pause.

“When did you last verify the silver was present?” Holkos said.

Philon blinked. “I — it was there. It was always there.”

“That is not what I asked. When did you last see it? Count it? Have physical confirmation of its presence?”

The wide man opened his mouth and then, with visible effort, closed it again. He was thinking. I watched him think, and I watched the quality of his certainty shift almost imperceptibly — not disappear, but adjust, like a merchant correcting a weight on a scale.

“Before the festival of Hephaestus,” he said. “I counted it before the festival.”

“And the festival was — “

“A month past.”

“So the silver was present one month ago. Very good. That is a fact. What occurred between one month ago and two days prior that caused you to believe the silver would still be present?”

“Nothing occurred. Why would anything occur? Kritos was reliable.”

“Reliable,” Holkos repeated the word with the tone of a man examining a fabric whose quality he has not yet determined. “What does reliable mean to you, in practical terms? What did Kritos reliably do?”

Philon listed things — opening and closing the storehouse, receiving deliveries, keeping the inventory record on the wax tablet. He spoke with the fluency of a man describing what he had assumed rather than what he had observed. I had begun to hear the difference, by that point.

“The inventory record,” Holkos said. “May I see it?”

“I — it is at the storehouse.”

“You came to accuse a man of a serious crime,” Holkos said, gently, “without the record he kept of the goods in his care.”

It was not said unkindly. That was what I found, over the years, most difficult to convey to others who had not witnessed it — the absence of cruelty in it. He was not mocking Philon. He was merely presenting him, very carefully, with the shape of his own reasoning.

“I can retrieve it,” Philon said, in a smaller voice.

“Let us speak further before you trouble yourself. Who else had access to the storehouse?”

“Only Kritos.”

“The key. How many copies were made?”

A pause. “Two. I have one. Kritos had one.”

“Has Kritos returned the key?”

The pause this time was longer.

“He has not returned it,” Philon said slowly, “because I did not ask for it. I dismissed him twelve days ago — there was a dispute about the inventory count, I believed he had been careless with the wine jars — and I told him to leave. I did not think —”

“You did not think to ask for the key,” Holkos said. “Twelve days ago. And the silver was present one month ago. Between the festival of Hephaestus, one month ago, and the dismissal of Kritos twelve days ago, who entered the storehouse? Was it opened? For what purpose?”

Philon’s wide face had taken on a quality I can only describe as concentrated distress — the expression of a man doing arithmetic that is not resolving in the direction he had anticipated.

“I was in Piraeus,” he said, “for nearly three weeks. The silver was not needed. The storehouse would have been opened only for — “ He stopped. He started again. “There was a consignment received. From the Corinthian agent. Kritos would have received it.”

“And recorded it, presumably, on the wax tablet you have not brought with you.”

“Yes.”

“So we have established the following,” Holkos said, and here he turned to me very slightly — a small gesture, so small that Philon did not register it, but I understood it as my instruction to attend carefully. “We know the silver was present one month ago. We know a man was subsequently dismissed and retained a key. We know the storehouse was opened, for legitimate purposes, during an interval you cannot fully account for. We know there was a dispute about inventory before the dismissal. We do not know when the silver was taken, if it was taken. We do not know by whom. We do not know whether the inventory dispute and the absence of silver are connected, separate, or whether the absence of silver is connected to something else entirely.”

He let this settle.

“What we do not have,” he continued, “is evidence. We have expectation. You expected the silver to be there because you expected Kritos to be reliable. Kritos was dismissed under disputed circumstances. The silver is not there. You have concluded that Kritos took the silver. But tell me, Philon — when you dismissed Kritos twelve days ago, what reason did you give him?”

The answer came slowly. “I told him he had been falsifying the inventory count.”

“And had he been?”

Silence.

“You believed he had been,” Holkos said. “Which is not the same thing.”

I will not extend the account of what followed beyond what is useful for the reader’s understanding. Holkos asked, over the course of the next hour — sitting in that lane while the lamp-smoke drifted and the oil-sellers called their prices and Athens conducted its ordinary morning — perhaps forty questions, no more. He asked them without impatience and without apparent direction, the way a man drawing a net through water moves without haste because he knows the current will do the work. He asked about the Corinthian buyer, whose consignment had been received. He asked about the nature of the inventory dispute — what specifically had been miscounted, and by how much, and in which direction, and whether the error had favoured Philon or favoured the goods. He asked about Philon’s own conduct before his departure for Piraeus.

He did not tell Philon what he was looking for. He did not need to. By the time the questions were finished, Philon had told it to himself.

The inventory dispute had concerned a quantity of Eastern dye — krokos, the expensive kind — that had appeared in the record but not in the storehouse. Philon had assumed Kritos had stolen it or sold it without authorisation. What emerged, under questioning, was that Philon himself had taken the dye to Piraeus to show the buyer there, intending to bring it back, and had sold it there instead when the price offered exceeded his expectation, and had — in the press of the journey and the excitement of the transaction — neglected to inform Kritos or update the record. Kritos had been dismissed for a discrepancy that Philon had created.

The silver: Philon, under the slow pressure of Holkos’s questions about his Piraeus journey, recalled that he had withdrawn a portion of the sum before he departed — for expenses, for gifts, for the small transactions of three weeks away from home — and had not returned what remained unused to the storehouse but had placed it, as a temporary measure that had apparently ceased to be temporary, in a sealed jar in his own house, where his wife managed such interim arrangements and where, it developed, it remained.

Kritos had not been in the storehouse. There was no evidence he had entered it after his dismissal. The key, when sent for, was found at his lodgings, hanging on a peg near the door, unremarkable, unused.

Philon left our corner of the agora a considerably quieter man than he had arrived. He did not thank Holkos. I do not think he was capable, at that moment, of the equanimity required for gratitude. He walked back toward the north end with the careful gait of someone who has discovered that the ground beneath them is less certain than they had believed, and who is now testing each step.

When he was gone, Holkos picked up the ostrakon again. I watched him turn it in his fingers.

“The merchant will be angry for some time,” I said. “At himself, I think. But he may also be angry at you.”

“He may,” Holkos agreed. “Men rarely enjoy discovering that the wrong they have suffered was administered by their own hand. It is a particular species of humiliation — the victim and the thief being the same person.”

“What did you see that made you certain it was — nothing?”

He set the ostrakon down again and looked at me directly, which he did not always do when he was explaining things; sometimes he explained to the middle distance, as though the lesson were for the air.

“I saw nothing that made me certain of anything,” he said. “That is precisely the point. Philon arrived here certain. He had constructed a complete account of events — Kritos trusted, Kritos dismissed, silver absent, Kritos guilty — and each part of the account was emotionally coherent, which he had mistaken for being evidentially coherent. The feeling of the story was correct. The architecture of it — the trusted servant, the betrayal, the loss — is as old as household economy. He had fitted the facts into the story rather than building the story from the facts.”

“But how did you know to ask about the dye?”

“I did not know about the dye. I knew about disputes. A dispute about inventory that leads to dismissal and is not resolved by producing the missing item is interesting. If Kritos had stolen the dye, where was it? Philon had not recovered it. Kritos, a freedman of moderate means, had not visibly prospered. The dye had simply — vanished. Items do not vanish. They go somewhere. The most probable somewhere, in the case of valuable goods in the possession of a merchant about to travel, is: with the merchant.”

He was quiet for a moment.

“The harder lesson,” he said, “is the one about Kritos. A man was dismissed. His reputation in the agora is injured — I know of at least two traders who declined to use him this season on account of what Philon said when he dismissed him. He has suffered a real injury, and Philon will offer him an apology that is inadequate, if he offers one at all, because the form of the apology required — public, complete, generous — is exactly the form that would expose Philon’s own carelessness to the same public that heard the accusation.”

“Can nothing be done?”

Holkos looked at me with what I had come to recognise as his expression of moderate patience.

“You are asking whether justice is automatic,” he said. “It is not. Justice requires someone to insist upon it, and the person best positioned to insist upon it for Kritos is Kritos, who is a freedman with no powerful connections and a fresh reputation for disputed inventory. The agora is not the cosmos, Demetrios. It does not arrange itself.”

He picked up the ostrakon a final time, examined it, and placed it in the fold of his himation with the gesture of a man pocketing something that might, in an unspecified future, be useful.

“What is that?” I asked.

“A record shard. Quite old — older than this agora, I think. The name scratched on it is partially worn, but the first letters are clear enough.” He paused. “Someone, once, thought this name worth recording. We no longer know in what connection. That is the honest condition of most records, if you go back far enough.”

He said nothing further. The lamp-smoke drifted. The oil-sellers called their prices. Below us, at the north end of the agora, a wide man walked carefully home to find the silver he had misplaced, and I sat with my wax tablet and tried to write down what I had seen, though I was not yet sure I understood it.

Thus ends my account of the matter of Philon the merchant. I have written it as completely as I am able, including those portions that do not reflect well on a citizen of this city, because Holkos taught me that an account which preserves only the flattering portions is not a record but a monument, and monuments are for the dead. He was not yet dead when I wrote this. He was, in fact, at that moment eating an olive, which he had apparently produced from somewhere about his person, and examining a second ostrakon he had found beside the first.

I set this down faithfully. I cannot always say I understood it.

— Demetrios, son of Nikanor
