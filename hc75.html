CONSTANTINUS HOLCUS

~210 AD — Byzantium

Constantinus Holcus was born in Byzantium in the worst decades of the third-century crisis — the fifty years of plague, invasion, and imperial instability that saw Rome come closer to dissolution than at any point since Hannibal stood at the gates. He served in the legal administration of the eastern empire through those decades with the steadiness of a man who understood that institutions are most necessary precisely when they are most threatened, and that the administrator who keeps the records accurate and the procedures intact through a period of chaos is performing a service that history rarely adequately acknowledges.

He lived long enough to see Diocletian’s great administrative reforms begin to stabilise the empire and to watch, in his final years, the early stirrings of the Christian question that would define the next century. He did not live to see Constantine’s conversion or the founding of the new capital — those events belonged to his son’s generation — but he understood, from the patterns he had spent a lifetime reading, that both were coming.

The surviving account of his work during the legal confusion following the accession of Maximinus Thrax is preserved in a court copyist’s folio attributed to one Theodoros, a junior clerk whose careful transcription preserves detail faithfully but whose marginal frustrations make clear that he often struggled to follow the full extent of Holcus’s reasoning. It is from this document that the following narrative is drawn.

The Affair of the Duplicated Grants

Drawn from the Folio of Theodoros, Junior Copyist of the Eastern Imperial Registry, in the Consulship of Diocletian and Maximian

The smell of the Registry in summer is not a smell that leaves a man. It is the smell of old papyrus souring in damp air, of lamp-oil pressed from the inferior olives they send east when the better grades go to Rome, and beneath those, always, the smell of river-water seeping through foundations that were laid in a more optimistic century. I had grown accustomed to it over four years of service. I had grown accustomed to the sounds, too — the scratch of reed styluses, the thump of seal-dies, the particular creak of the upper archive door that announced the arrival of anyone who climbed to the older records.

I had not grown accustomed to Constantinus Holcus.

He arrived at the Registry each morning before any of the other senior administrators, before the lamps were trimmed, and he was often still there when I departed in the evening. I do not say he worked during those hours. More often he sat, motionless, in the way that a lizard on a warm stone is motionless — not resting, but attending, every sense directed inward to some computation invisible to the observer. On those mornings when he sat so, I would enter, see him installed in his chair with his long fingers pressed together beneath his chin and his grey eyes fixed on the middle distance above my head, and I would withdraw to my copying-bench in silence. To interrupt that stillness was, I had learned, to receive a look of such flat displeasure that one felt the interruption had been a kind of violence.

But there were other mornings — and these were more unsettling, in their way — when I arrived to find him already in motion, when the lizard had become something faster. On those mornings he moved about the archive with a quick, angular energy that seemed almost too large for his body, pulling folios from shelves and replacing them with the decisive snap of a man who knows exactly which volume he wants and cannot understand why the others are in his way. On those mornings he spoke rapidly and continuously, sometimes to me, sometimes, I suspected, to no one in particular, and the thoughts came out already in the middle, as though the beginning and the end were obvious and only the connecting tissue required externalisation.

I record this not because it is flattering to my superior — I am a copyist, not a flatterer — but because the account of the Duplicated Grants case would make no sense without understanding the man through whose mind it passed.

The affair began with a smell that was not the usual smell.

I noticed it first in early August, when the heat in Byzantium makes the city feel like a cooking-vessel with the lid on — the sea-light white and painful, the streets compressed between their walls, the water in the public troughs growing green by midday. The smell was burning wax, and it was wrong because it was coming from the direction of the sealed archive, and the sealed archive had not, to my knowledge, been opened in three weeks.

Holcus noticed it at the same moment. I know this because he was in one of his languid phases — had been for several days, sitting with a copy of Ulpian’s Institutes in his lap that I was reasonably certain he had not actually opened — and the smell reached us both and he was across the room before I had finished identifying it, his whole posture transformed, the book abandoned on the floor with no more ceremony than a man drops a tool he no longer requires.

“The archive,” he said. Not a question.

The door was unlocked. This was the first wrongness. The second was the wax — a seal-die had been used on the floor, not the table, and recently; the impression was still soft at its edges when Holcus crouched over it with the small lamp he had taken from my hand without asking. The third wrongness was the absence of Gaius Metrophanes, the archival clerk whose duty it was to be present whenever the sealed room was opened, and who was not there.

Holcus looked at the soft wax for a long time. Then he looked at the shelf from which a folio had been recently removed — the dust on that section of wood was disturbed in the shape of the missing volume. Then he looked at the door-frame, which bore a faint scrape at approximately waist height that was fresh enough to show raw wood beneath the old paint.

“Someone was in a hurry,” I offered, feeling that I ought to contribute something.

“Someone was in a hurry,” he agreed, which surprised me, as he rarely agreed with anything I said without qualification. “But not with the seal. With the leaving.”

He said nothing more for some time, and I did not ask, having learned that questions posed before he was ready to answer them produced only silence with an impatient quality to it.

Gaius Metrophanes was found two days later in the lower cistern on the western side of the city, in water that had been sitting long enough in the August heat that the retrieval was an unpleasant business for everyone involved except Holcus, who observed the proceedings with the calm attention he brought to all problems that interested him.

Centurion Lucius Rufianus, the garrison officer assigned to the matter by the military prefect, was a broad-shouldered man with a competent face and the manner of someone who has learned to be efficient with unpleasantness. He looked at the body and then at Holcus and then at me — I had become, through some administrative accident that I did not remember agreeing to, the person who accompanied Holcus to crime scenes, which was not in my original contract of service — and he said, “Drowned. Or fell.”

“Or was placed,” Holcus said.

Rufianus’s expression did not change much, but something shifted behind it. “The cistern is on a public street. Someone would have seen.”

“The cistern access is in an alley. The alley is, at most times, unobserved. He was placed there at night, which means the person who placed him knew the alley, knew the cistern, and was confident that a man of Metrophanes’ size could be managed without assistance.” Holcus was walking around the body with his hands clasped behind his back, and his shadow moved oddly in the lamp-light. “He did not drown. The water here is barely chest-deep. He was placed after death, and the death was not drowning. Look at the hands.”

I looked. I will not record in detail what I saw, because some details belong in medical reports and not in administrative folios. I will record only that Holcus was right, and that Rufianus, after a moment, acknowledged it.

“The hands tell you he did not drown,” I said, later, when we were walking back toward the Registry through streets that were still warm from the day’s heat even at the second hour of night. “How did you know to look at the hands first?”

“Because,” Holcus said, in the tone he used when something was self-evident, “the hands are where a man leaves the story of his last minutes. If he drowned, the hands would tell one story. If he was struck, another. If he was held under — though this is not holding-under — another still. The hands are the index to the body’s history. A good reader always checks the index first.”

He said nothing more. I wrote it down when I got home, as I had begun to do with everything he said, because I had the uneasy sense that I was recording something that ought to be recorded, even if I was not always certain what it meant.

There is a moment in the investigation of fraud, Holcus told me the following morning — he was in his energetic phase again, having apparently not slept — when the document stops being a document and becomes a machine. This was, he said, the moment that interested him.

I did not understand this and said so.

“A document is inert,” he said, pulling folios from the archive shelving with his quick, decisive motions. “It records. A machine has moving parts, and the moving parts must move in a particular sequence, or the machine does not work. The land-grant process under Diocletian’s new administrative framework is a machine. Grant is proposed. Survey is conducted. Survey is witnessed. Seal of local authority is applied. Grant is registered in the provincial folio. Provincial folio is forwarded to the central registry. Central registry seal is applied. The machine has eight steps. Every step produces a document. The documents, laid in sequence, form a chain that a reasonable official can follow.”

“Yes,” I said. I had processed several hundred such chains and found them tedious but not mysterious.

“Now,” Holcus said, setting a folio on the table between us and opening it to a registration page, “look at this.”

I looked. It was the registration of a land parcel in the Thracian territory east of Adrianople, recorded in the provincial folio in the name of one Marcus Severinus Afer, dated to the previous autumn.

“The provincial seal is applied in sequence before the central registry seal,” Holcus said. “Always. The machine requires it. The provincial office processes, and then forwards to us. We receive, and then seal. Our seal is, in every case, newer than theirs.”

“Yes.”

“In this registration, our seal impression is older.”

I looked again. The seal-die left characteristic marks in the wax — small imperfections in the bronze that an attentive copyist learns to recognise, because they are the difference between an authentic document and an imitation. The impression of the central registry’s seal on this registration bore an imperfection — a slight burr at the lower edge of the eagle’s wing — that I recognised as characteristic of the die that had been replaced the previous winter. The provincial seal bore the clear lines of fresh bronze.

The central registry seal on this document was older than the provincial seal that should have preceded it.

“This is impossible,” I said.

“It is not impossible,” Holcus said, with the particular satisfaction of a man who has been waiting for that response. “It is an instruction. It tells us that the central registration was prepared first and then sent backward through the machine, to be equipped with a provincial seal after the fact. This is not an error. Errors go one way. This went the other way. Someone here — in this registry — created the central registration before the provincial process had occurred, and then arranged for the provincial documentation to be added later.”

The room felt colder than it was.

“The same parcel of land,” Holcus continued, placing a second folio beside the first, “appears in the provincial record of another district, registered six months later in the name of Quintus Domitius Placidus. Two men believe they own the same field east of Adrianople. Both have what appear to be complete documentation chains. Both have paid the registration fees. Only one of them has genuinely purchased land. The other has purchased a fabrication.”

“How many times?” I asked.

His expression, which was not a warm expression, became, if anything, more concentrated. “That,” he said, “is what we are going to determine today.”

The answer was eleven. Eleven parcels of land in the Thracian and Bithynian territories, each sold twice under cover of the administrative reorganisation — the chaos of the new forms, the new procedures, the new registration requirements that Diocletian’s reforms had imposed across the eastern provinces — each with a documentation chain that would satisfy any ordinary inspection, and each with the tell-tale impossibility that Holcus had identified: the central seal appearing before it could have been applied, and a provincial record created afterward to fill in the gap.

The process required, Holcus explained — to me, and then to Rufianus, and then to the provincial prefect, who was a large man named Cassius Avitus who had the look of someone who had not expected his afternoon to contain this — a clerk with access to the central seal. A clerk who could produce registration documents without the genuine provincial record existing yet, using the seal that created legitimacy. A clerk who had understood that the reorganisation created a window: buyers in Bithynia could not easily verify provincial records in Thrace, and buyers in Thrace could not easily verify what had been registered centrally, and in the middle of that distance and confusion, eleven pieces of land had been sold eleven extra times, and the fees had gone somewhere that was not the imperial treasury.

“There are,” Holcus said, “four clerks with access to the central seal. Two of them are old men whose hands have trembled too long to press a die with the precision these impressions require. One of them has been posted to Alexandria for the past eight months and has demonstrably not been in Byzantium. The fourth is Appius Calvinus Rufus, who has been in this registry for six years, who has a property in the Lycian territory that he could not have purchased on his clerk’s salary, and who was on duty in the sealed archive on twelve separate occasions during the period in which these registrations were created — I have checked his duty roster against the dates — and who was also the last person to speak with Gaius Metrophanes before Metrophanes went to examine the old registration records and discovered something that made him alarmed enough to return to the sealed archive after hours.”

The room was very quiet. The prefect looked at Rufianus. Rufianus looked at Holcus. I looked at my notes, because looking at my notes was something to do that was not looking at anyone else.

“You cannot know,” Avitus said, “that Metrophanes found something alarming.”

“He had been assigned to clear old registration records from the pre-reform period,” Holcus said. “A routine task that put him in direct contact with the original grants for several of the parcels that had subsequently been sold twice. He would not have understood immediately what he had found — the discrepancy is subtle and requires a trained eye. But Metrophanes was a careful man who kept careful records of his own work. I found his personal notation-tablet in his desk this morning.” He produced a small wax tablet, the kind every clerk carries. “He had written the parcel numbers of three of the eleven grants, with a question mark beside each. He was going to ask someone about them. He went to the sealed archive, I believe, to compare what he had found in the old records against the new registrations. Someone was already there.”

“You found his notation-tablet,” I said, “this morning. When I was not here.”

“You were not here yet,” Holcus said, without any particular emphasis.

“You went through a dead man’s desk before the official examination.”

“I found the notation-tablet. Yes.”

The prefect cleared his throat. “Where is Calvinus Rufus now?”

“At his desk,” Holcus said. “I checked before coming here. He has been at his desk all morning, which is, in itself, the behaviour of a man who is working very hard at appearing normal. A man with no reason for anxiety would have taken the morning off. It is August. Everyone takes mornings off in August when they can.”

They found the property records in Calvinus’s house that afternoon — the separate accounts, the letters from buyers in three provinces, the careful double-ledger that was, Holcus said, examining it with something that might have been professional admiration, quite good work. The man had a talent for administrative documentation that had been misdirected.

Calvinus himself, when Rufianus’s men arrived, sat very still for a long moment and then said, with a strange precision, “He was going to find the third one. The property outside Nicomedia. That one, they would have traced back very quickly.”

He said nothing else for a long time.

“Metrophanes had a daughter,” I said, when Holcus and I were walking back to the Registry in the late afternoon, the sea-light beginning to go orange and the streets filling with the particular smell of evening in August — bread from the bakeries, fish from the harbourside, the old smoke of a city that has been burning lamps for centuries. “A daughter and a wife. He was, by all accounts, a careful and honest man.”

“He was,” Holcus said.

“He noticed something wrong in the records and went to look more closely, and that is what killed him.”

“Yes.”

“Is that,” I asked, and I was aware, even as I asked it, that it was not a question suited to an administrative folio — “is that something that troubles you?”

He walked three steps before answering.

“The careful and honest man who notices the discrepancy in the records,” he said finally, “is performing the exact function that the careful and honest records-system is designed to produce. He did what he was supposed to do. The tragedy is that the system which produced him did not also protect him. That is a systems failure.” He paused. “Whether it troubles me is not a question with useful implications. Calvinus Rufus will be tried. The property in Lycia will be seized. The buyers will be partially compensated from the forfeited estate. The records will be corrected.” Another pause, shorter. “Metrophanes’ family will receive the death-in-service provision. I have already submitted the paperwork.”

I had not known he had done this. It was not his responsibility to do it.

“You submitted it this morning,” I said. “Before you came to the prefect.”

“The prefect would have taken several days to process it through the standard channels,” he said. “The family has immediate expenses.”

He did not say anything else, and I did not ask anything else, and we walked back to the Registry in the ordinary sounds of the city — a gate-hinge somewhere on a side street, creaking in a particular minor key that I have never been able to forget — and he went straight to his chair and sat in the lizard-stillness again, and I went to my copying-bench and began to write this account, which I have tried to make as accurate as possible, and which I suspect no one will read for a very long time.

One postscript, which I include because it seems relevant to any future reader’s understanding of the case’s final disposition.

The property in Lycia — the one purchased with the fraudulent proceeds — was, after the forfeiture proceedings, found to contain a secondary deed, registered before the fraud began, in the names of Calvinus’s two sons, who were minors. Holcus reviewed this secondary deed for approximately four minutes, set it down, and then told Avitus that it appeared to have been registered before the fraudulent activity commenced and was therefore not subject to forfeiture under the relevant statute.

Avitus looked at him for a long moment. “You’re certain?”

“I am certain,” Holcus said, “of what the statute says.”

The property was not forfeited. The sons — who were twelve and nine years old, and who had, as far as any investigation revealed, no knowledge of their father’s activities — were permitted to retain it under the guardianship of an uncle.

I asked Holcus afterward whether the secondary deed was genuine.

He looked at me with his grey eyes and said nothing.

I wrote this down too, in my private notes, not in the official folio. Some records, I have concluded, are better kept separately.

Here ends the account of Theodoros, copyist, in the forty-third year of his life, which was the second year of the joint consulship. The official records of the Registry credit the resolution of the Duplicated Grants fraud to the office of the Provincial Prefect, whose investigators, working in coordination with the garrison, identified and apprehended the guilty party. This is accurate in the narrow sense. I leave the fuller record to whatever future reader the gods — or, I suppose, whatever power supersedes them — may eventually provide.
