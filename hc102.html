ROBERT HOLMR

~1110 AD — Yorkshire, England

Robert Holmr grew up in the reign of Henry Ⅰ — the Beauclerc, the scholar-king, the man who brought a measure of administrative order to his father’s conquest — and came of age in the chaos of the Anarchy, the civil war between Stephen and Matilda that tore England apart from 1135 to 1154 and reduced large parts of the country to the condition that the chroniclers described, with unusual precision, as a time when God and his angels slept. Yorkshire was not the worst of it, but it was not untouched, and Robert Holmr spent the middle decades of his life managing an estate through conditions of radical uncertainty.

He managed it well. He had a Norman name and an English temperament and his family’s imperturbable habit of reading the situation as it actually was rather than as he might have wished it to be, and in the Anarchy that combination was worth more than allegiance to either claimant. He supported order where it existed, kept his head down where it did not, and emerged from the civil war with the estate intact and his reputation for sound judgement enhanced by the simple fact of having survived a period when a great many men of property had not. He was, in the understated fashion of his age and position, a man whose evidence settled cases — whose word, in the local courts that resumed their function under Henry Ⅱ, was accepted without question.

The account that follows is preserved in the hand of Father Cuthbert, a cleric attached to the emerging circuit of royal justices in the north. Cuthbert’s record is uneven in places, written in haste and revised in margins where the ink runs thin, yet it carries a particular clarity when describing procedure and testimony. He was not a man trained to interpret, but to observe and preserve what he observed. Where he departs into judgment, his language becomes uncertain; where he remains with facts, his record is precise. It is from this tension that the present account is drawn.

The Testimony and Actions of Robert Holmr

During the worst of the Anarchy, when the roads between York and the Pennine edges were held by no man with certainty.

The messenger reached me at Vespers, when the light had already gone grey and the frost was forming on the stones of the yard. He was Aldric of Thornfeld's man, or had been — for Aldric, he told us between gasps, was dead upon his own hall floor with his skull broken, and neither God nor any of his servants had heard a sound in the night to account for it.

I had ridden up to Holmr's manor that same afternoon on a matter of tithe records, and so it was that I found myself standing in the yard beside Robert Holmr when the news arrived, watching his face in the last of the winter light.

He was a lean man, Holmr. Not tall, but with a quality of stillness about him that could be mistaken for patience and was not — it was attention, a particular kind of attention that never quite switched off, the way a mill-wheel does not stop merely because you have covered the race. He had been sitting by his fire when I arrived, doing nothing at all that I could see, and his steward had told me in a low voice that he had been sitting so for three days, and that this was not unusual following a period of great exertion, and that I should not mention it.

He looked at the messenger, then at me.

"Thornfeld," he said. "You know the hall?"

I told him I had ridden past it.

"We will go now," he said. "Before anyone else arrives and obscures what there is to see."

I ought to have objected. I was a clerk, not an investigator, and the night was freezing and the roads uncertain. But there was something in his manner — a sudden quality of concentrated purpose, as if the stillness of three days had simply been a coiled spring waiting for release — that made objection feel beside the point. I called for my horse.

The ride to Thornfeld took us through bottom land that smelled of standing water and old rot, the kind of cold that is not dramatic but simply permanent, the kind that gets into the joints and stays. The track crossed a wooden bridge over a stream that had recently flooded and left debris in the willows — old thatch, a piece of wool-sacking, something long and squared at one end with an iron tip, the kind of thing a carpenter leaves behind or a farmhand discards. Holmr's torch threw shadows that moved in a way the darkness did not require them to move. By the time the gate of Thornfeld manor rose out of the murk, the iron hinge of which had a particular shriek — iron on iron, the sound of a man refusing to admit something is wrong — I was cold enough that I had stopped thinking about theology and was thinking only about whether the hall fire was still burning.

It was. That was the first thing.

Aldric of Thornfeld lay where he had fallen, between the high table and the central hearth, face down on the rushes. The rushes in that part of the hall were old — they smelled of the autumn and of something below the autumn, of the summer before it — and they had been disturbed in a radius of perhaps four feet around the body, scuffed and pushed aside in a pattern that Holmr spent some time examining on his hands and knees with the torch held low, before he rose and handed the torch to me without comment.

The wound was to the back of the head. The weapon had not been found. There was blood, but not as much as I expected, and Holmr remarked on this quietly, more to himself than to me. Then he walked the perimeter of the hall slowly, pausing at each wall. When he reached the corner beside the door to the pantry passage, he crouched and spent a long moment examining something on the floor that I could not see clearly from where I stood. He did not point it out to me. He rose and continued his circuit.

Against the north wall, between two hanging pegs, stood a low aumbry of old oak, its iron hasp closed over an iron staple. It was a common enough piece of furniture for a hall of that standing — the sort in which a lord kept his better parchments, his sealed deeds, the correspondence he did not wish his household to handle freely. What struck me, now that I think of it, was how clean it was. The hanging pegs above it bore cloaks dusty at the shoulders; the floorboards around it were unremarkable; but the aumbry itself had been recently wiped, or moved, or both. There was a straight edge in the dust where its base met the floor on three sides, and on the fourth side — the side facing the room — the dust was entirely absent, as if something had been set down there and shifted more than once.

Holmr did not touch it. He looked at it for the space of perhaps six breaths, then turned away.

Present in the hall were three persons besides ourselves and the messenger: Godwin the steward, a compact and careful man of perhaps fifty who held himself with the particular dignity of a servant who has long understood that he is the practical intelligence of a household; Aldric's eldest son, Rannulf, who was perhaps twenty-five and whose eyes were red and whose hands could not find a resting place; and a reeve from the southern field, a man called Oswin, who had been summoned from his cottage when the body was discovered and who stood near the door in the manner of a man who very much wished to be elsewhere.

Rannulf spoke first. He said his father had been killed by Matildan partisans who resented his support for King Stephen, and that everyone in the county knew it, and that justice required a military response.

Holmr looked at him for a moment. Then he walked to the door of the hall and examined the bar.

"The bar was drawn from the inside," he said.

Rannulf said that the partisans had forced the window shutter.

Holmr walked to the window shutter. He examined it. He came back to the body.

"The shutter latch is on the inside," he said. "It is drawn. It was not forced. There are no marks of forcing on the outside of the frame — I looked before we entered, when the torch was lower." He paused. "There was also no disturbance in the mud beneath the window. It rained two days ago. The ground is soft. A man climbing through would have left evidence that would be visible to any observer who chose to observe."

Rannulf said something about the hardness of the threshold stone.

"The threshold stone," Holmr said, "does not extend to the flower bed your father kept beside the east wall, which is where any man climbing that window would have placed his feet. The soil there has been turned recently for winter preparation and would hold a print admirably. There are no prints."

He said all of this without heat, in the tone of a man reading a list of figures. Then he turned to the reeve, Oswin, who had not yet spoken.

"You were summoned from your cottage," Holmr said. "How far is that?"

Oswin said it was perhaps half a mile by the south track.

"And who summoned you?"

Oswin glanced at Godwin the steward. "The steward's boy came for me, sir. Just before Prime."

"The steward's boy," Holmr repeated. He did not pursue it. He asked Oswin what his duties were, and Oswin explained them — the southern field, the winter sowing, the boundary questions that always arose this time of year with the adjacent holding at Marston Flatt, where the old lord had been disputing the eastern ditch since Michaelmas, and where the new arrangement had not yet been settled in writing to anyone's satisfaction, and where there were those who said the late lord had recently made certain decisions in the matter that his son did not yet know about.

Oswin said this last part with the particular care of a man who has said slightly more than he intended and is hoping no one will notice.

Holmr noticed. He looked at Oswin for a moment with an expression I had not seen him use before — not sharp, precisely, but concentrated, as if a lens had been adjusted.

"We will speak of Marston Flatt presently," he said, in a tone that made clear this was not a deferral but a delay with a destination. Then he asked Godwin to bring him ale, and when Godwin turned to the buttery passage, Holmr's eyes went immediately to the ring of keys at Godwin's belt. Among the domestic keys — the buttery, the larder, the grain store, each worn smooth and dark with long use — there hung one key that was neither worn nor dark. It was a small flat key of the kind fitted to a box-lock or a document aumbry, and it was bright. Bright as if it had been cut recently, or cleaned recently, or both.

I did not remark upon it. I was a clerk, and it seemed to me a steward might carry such a key in the ordinary course of things. I thought no more about it until much later.

It was later, when the steward had brought ale and bread that no one ate, that Holmr spoke at length.

"You are wondering," he said to me, without looking up from the parchment he had by then drawn from the aumbry, "why I asked Godwin for the key before asking Rannulf."

He set the parchment on the table.

"Because Rannulf did not know the box existed. Or rather — he did not know what it contained. Godwin did. Godwin has carried the key to that aumbry for the past three weeks, since Aldric completed the Marston Flatt arrangement and gave it into Godwin's keeping for the sealing. Rannulf believed the negotiation was still in progress. He did not know his father had already concluded it, in terms that granted him nothing."

He smoothed the parchment. It was a short document — a quarter-sheet, closely written, with a seal already affixed at the foot.

"Aldric settled the eastern ditch question in favour of the Marston Flatt holder. In exchange for what consideration I do not yet know, though the phrase in perpetuity and without further claim by heirs suggests he anticipated his heir's objection. Rannulf had been promised that land since boyhood. He had borrowed against its eventual possession. When Aldric told him — and I believe Aldric told him, that night, at the high table, which is why the rushes are disturbed outward from the body rather than toward it, indicating a conversation that ended badly rather than an attack from behind — Rannulf struck him with the first thing that came to hand."

He looked toward the corner beside the pantry passage, where I had seen him crouch and examine something earlier in the evening.

"The tally-rod from the winter accounts. It would have been resting in the corner stand, where the steward keeps his counting materials. It is not there now. I found iron-scrape marks on the floor stones in that corner consistent with the rod having been swept out in haste, and a partial impression in the old wax at the hearth-edge where it landed before being moved. Oswin," he continued, without raising his voice, "you were not summoned by the steward's boy. You came of your own accord, because you saw Rannulf cross the lower field before first light, going toward the stream. I think you know what you think you saw him carry."

Oswin, by the door, was very still.

"The debris in the willows," I said, before I had decided to speak. I was thinking of the ride in — the old thatch, the wool-sacking, the thing I had taken for a fence post.

"Yes," Holmr said. He did not elaborate.

Rannulf had gone the colour of tallow. He said nothing. He looked at Godwin, and Godwin looked at the floor, and the bright key at Godwin's belt caught the torchlight once and was still.

What followed I will set down plainly, without the reasoning behind it, because the reasoning was Holmr's and I do not trust myself to reproduce it faithfully.

Holmr did not send for the sheriff. The roads were held by no one, and an assize in the middle of civil war would have produced either impunity or a feud, and possibly both. He spoke with Rannulf alone for some time in the pantry passage. When they came out, Rannulf's face was still grey but his hands had found their resting place. The Marston Flatt arrangement was confirmed as settled. A sum was named, to be paid in instalments, which I understood to be both a penance and a practical resolution of the debt Rannulf had incurred. Godwin was retained as steward and given to understand that his knowledge of certain events was to remain his knowledge alone.

It was not justice in any form I had been taught to recognise. Holmr seemed to find this unremarkable. When I said as much on the road back, he was quiet for a while before answering.

"Justice is what survives," he said. "The Marston Flatt family keeps their ditch. Rannulf pays. The church at Thornfeld will be repaired — I have his word on it, which under the circumstances carries more weight than a seal. The land does not go to feud." He paused. "What would an assize have given you that this does not?"

I had an answer, but I did not give it. I was a clerk, and the night was cold, and I had begun to think it was not the question he was really asking.

Here Cuthbert's marginal note reads: I set this down as it occurred. I do not record my own conclusions, as I was not asked for them and am uncertain I have reached any. The matter appears in no official record that I can find. The Thornfeld hall-roll for the following year records the Marston Flatt renegotiation as completed under the late lord's instruction. The church at Thornfeld was repaired in eleven forty-four. Rannulf of Thornfeld died in eleven seventy, a man of reputable local standing.

I have thought about this matter for thirty years. I am still thinking.
