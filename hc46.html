HOLKOS THE ASSYRIAN

~700 BC — Western Anatolia

The Record Of Nerab

Being an account set down in the eighteenth year of service, during the passage westward through the hill country beyond the Euphrates ford at Carchemish

I.

The morning we lost Shamash-iddin, the road had been quiet for three days.

This is the detail that mattered. I did not understand it to be a detail at the time. I understood it to be a relief, the way a man understands the absence of rain to be pleasant without asking where the rain has gone. My master would have asked. He asks about every absence.

His name is Holkos, and he is known in certain circles as Holkos the Assyrian, which is an anachronism he wears with the same ironic tolerance he applies to most things that other men find merely aggravating. He spent twenty years at the centre of the imperial intelligence apparatus — reading the dispatches that determined which governors lived and which were recalled, tracing the slow collapse of the eastern frontier in the changing tone of the Babylonian correspondence — and of all those men, he alone had begun moving the family's assets west two decades before anyone else understood that there was somewhere to move them. He converted property into portable wealth, selected his library for portability rather than completeness, and was in western Anatolia when Nineveh fell in 612 BC. His family intact. His wealth preserved. I have been in his service eighteen years. My function is to record, and I set this account down now — while the smell of that particular stretch of dust still lives in my nostrils when I close my eyes — because it is the kind of account that some portion of me does not wish to survive. But my master would say that a record with portions omitted is not a record but a decoration, and he would be right, as he is almost always right about things that do not require a human being to feel them.

II.

We were seventeen days out of Harran, moving through the hill country where the empire's western hand had grown thin and uncertain, the garrison posts spaced too far apart, the local dynasts paying their tribute to whichever power seemed most present in any given season.

The escort numbered twenty: eight of Holkos's own hired men and twelve provided by the local administrator at Carchemish, whose name was Bel-iqisha. I had formed an impression of Bel-iqisha before I ever encountered a letter bearing his seal, the way one forms an impression of a man's household from the conduct of his servants. His twelve men were correct in every procedural particular and wrong in some quality I could not at first name. They were too attentive to the baggage — specifically, too attentive to the cedar boxes that held the copied texts — in a manner that was not curiosity but inventory. Holkos had noticed this also. He said nothing about it to me. He said nothing about it to anyone, which was itself a form of speech I had learned to interpret.

Three days before Shamash-iddin disappeared, a sealed letter arrived for Holkos at a waystation stop, carried by a courier who wore the dust of the Carchemish road. My master read it, folded it, and placed it inside his robe. He did not discuss its contents. When I inquired, as I was permitted to do, whether it required a reply, he said: "Not yet. Perhaps not at all." His face was the face he wore when a conclusion he had already suspected had been confirmed in writing. That evening he spoke privately to Ilu-eriba, the heavyset Harranite who was the most capable of his hired men. I was not present. I noted the conversation's occurrence and its duration — nearly a quarter-hour — and I noted that afterward Ilu-eriba moved differently through the camp: not with alarm, but with the gathered attention of a man who has been told to remain ready.

I did not connect these things. I was a scribe, not a strategist. I recorded them because I record everything, and because my master has always maintained that a detail's importance reveals itself in retrospect or not at all.

The caravan was forty animals — twelve bearing the cedar boxes of copied texts, the rest carrying provisions, commercial goods Holkos used to explain his movement to officials who required explanations, and eleven members of the household staff who had survived the sorting that preceded departure from Harran.

Shamash-iddin was the youngest of the scribes. Sixteen years old. Fourteen months in the household, assigned initially to inventory lists and routine correspondence, advancing by degrees to the copying of astronomical observations, which he performed with a precision unusual in someone his age. He was a thin boy with ink-darkened fingers and the habit of moving his lips slightly when he worked through a difficult passage. He had no enemies anyone had identified. He had no debts. He had no attachments in the hill country that would explain a voluntary disappearance.

He was simply gone.

III.

I must set down here a detail I recorded at the time without understanding its significance.

On the night before the night Shamash-iddin disappeared — the third-from-last night, the last of the quiet ones — I was awake past the second hour, finishing a letter I had been composing by lamplight. The camp was still. The fire had died to orange coals. I was sitting close enough to feel their heat against my shins when I heard movement near the animals' line.

It was Kuri, the water-carrier. He rose from his sleeping place with the careful, pained gait of a man trying not to wake the men around him — he had a complaint of the bladder, something he mentioned with neither embarrassment nor urgency, the way men mention a condition they have lived with long enough to regard as weather. I watched him shuffle to the camp's perimeter and crouch there a long while, looking north, in the manner of a man whose discomfort has made him wakeful beyond the need that drove him there.

He was still for some time. Then he turned and, seeing my lamp, came to me. He had the look of a man who has witnessed something he does not know the name for.

"Scribe," he said quietly. "I saw three men on the ridge."

I looked up from my letter. "Travellers?"

"Moving down, not across. And they carried something between them." He paused, searching for words as men do when the words available seem too large for what they mean. "Like a rolled carpet. But the carpet breathed."

I wrote it down. I wrote it down as I wrote everything — not because I understood it, but because my master's first instruction to me, eighteen years prior, had been: Record what you observe. Meaning is a later enterprise.

Kuri returned to his mat. I finished my letter. The northern ridge had the particular quality of emptiness that a scribe learns to observe when he has spent enough years working beside a man who finds meaning in the arrangement of absences. I looked at my notation. I looked at the ridge. I did not know what I had seen. I blew out my lamp.

IV.

The escort captain, one Kibri-Dagan — a competent man of the professional type who followed procedure as a priest follows liturgy — informed Holkos of the boy's absence at the third hour of morning. I was present. I watched my master receive the news with an expression that did not change, which I had learned to read as the expression he wore when a conclusion he had already formed had been confirmed.

But it was what followed the announcement that established, for me, the full register of what Holkos was. He did not ask where the boy might have gone. He did not ask whether a search had been mounted. He looked instead at Kibri-Dagan, at the captain's twelve Carchemish men standing behind him in the grey pre-dawn, and he said:

"Your men were positioned between the scribes and the baggage again last night."

Kibri-Dagan blinked. "My lord, it is standard formation —"

"It is not standard formation. Standard formation positions the escort at the caravan's perimeter. Your men have been positioned inward, between the cargo and the people, since the third day out of Harran." He said it without heat, in the way a physician names a symptom — not accusing, merely cataloguing. "When was he last seen?"

"At the night halt. He took his meal with the other scribes."

"Which night halt."

Kibri-Dagan paused. "The one before last, my lord."

"So he has been missing since the previous night, and you are informing me now, as the caravan prepares to move."

The silence that followed was its own answer.

I watched my master stand very still in the grey pre-dawn light, looking at nothing visible to the rest of us. He was a tall man, lean in the way that years of preferring thought to food will make a man lean, with a long jaw and eyes that had in them something of the quality of a military surveyor's instrument — the sense that distance was being measured precisely and without sentiment. He wore Assyrian robes only in private. On the road he dressed as a Harranite merchant, which suited him rather better than it should have, and which he seemed to find vaguely amusing, in the way he found most things amusing that other men found merely useful.

"We do not move the caravan," he said.

Kibri-Dagan straightened. "My lord, the checkpoint at the next station —"

"Is two days hence. We have time. We do not move the caravan." He turned to me. "Nerab. Your tablet."

I had it already. I had learned not to be without it when he used that tone.

V.

What followed was the alternation I had come by then to expect from him, though it never ceased to unsettle me. For the first hour he did nothing. He sat on a flat stone at the caravan's perimeter and appeared to be watching the hills to the north with the vacant attention of a man watching weather form. He declined food. He declined water. He allowed a fly to land on his hand and did not move to dispel it. The hired men watched him and said nothing, but I could see in the manner they avoided looking directly at him that they thought he was performing a religious observance, which was close enough to accurate that I did not correct them.

Kibri-Dagan, who was a man of practical temperament, came to me privately.

"The caravan must move. The checkpoint requires our arrival within the window of the safe-passage document. If we are late, the document is void. If the document is void —"

"I understand," I said.

"— then we must explain ourselves to the checkpoint commander, who will want to know why we are late, and then he will want to know what we are carrying, and then —"

"I understand," I said again.

"Your master sits on a rock."

"Yes."

"Looking at the hills."

"Yes."

Kibri-Dagan looked at me with the expression of a man who is considering whether the people he works for are, in the final analysis, sound. "The boy has probably run," he said. "Boys run. Especially boys carrying important correspondence through hostile country who begin to understand what that means. He is on the road back to Harran by now, or he has found a farm and is offering his labour for bread. He will not be found on this road."

"Tell him that," I said, nodding toward Holkos.

Kibri-Dagan looked at my master on his stone. He decided not to.

VI.

Then, without transition or explanation, Holkos stood. The fly departed. He moved with a purposeful energy that seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere at once, the languor abandoned like a garment he no longer required. He moved back along our own route, four hundred paces to the ground where the scribes had slept two nights before.

I followed, tablet ready. Kibri-Dagan followed at a distance, maintaining the expression of official scepticism that was, I had come to understand, his form of hope.

The ground told a story. I could see that it told a story the way I could see that a text written in Elamite told a story — the marks were clearly meaningful, arranged in an order that was clearly deliberate, in a language I did not speak.

Holkos spoke it for me.

"Three men," he said, crouching at the edge of the sleeping area. "Not from the caravan. You see the sole pattern here — this cut across the heel is a local cobbler's mark, the style worn in the garrison town we passed two days before Carchemish. Our hired men came from Harran. Kibri-Dagan's men came from Carchemish itself, which uses a different sole entirely." He moved forward ten paces. "They waited. You see where they stood — the ground is compressed but not scuffed; these were patient men, not nervous ones. Patient men are professional men. This was not opportunism."

"Bandits," said Kibri-Dagan, from behind us. "The road has bandits. We should report to the checkpoint —"

"Bandits take everything they can carry and leave quickly," Holkos said, without looking up. "They took one boy and nothing else. One sleeping boy from a caravan with twenty armed escorts, which they navigated without alerting anyone." He moved again, following something invisible to me. "These men knew exactly which sleeping mat to approach. They knew the boy's position in the camp. They had a specific reason for wanting this particular scribe and no other."

I felt something cold move through me that had nothing to do with the morning air.

"He copies the astronomical tablets," I said slowly.

Holkos looked at me. It was the look he gave when I had arrived, unexpectedly and slightly late, at a conclusion he had been waiting for.

"He copies the astronomical tablets," he agreed. "Which contain, embedded in their observations, the cipher-key to the correspondence records of the household's commercial arrangements. Which records, if decoded by someone who understood what they were reading, would identify every asset we have moved west, every contact we have established in the Lydian sphere, and every arrangement that makes our continued existence in this part of the world dependent on not being examined too closely by any interested party."

He let that settle. Then: "The local administrator at Carchemish. Bel-iqisha. He provided the escort. He knew our composition and our route and the nature of our cargo — or suspected enough of it to want to know more. The men with the garrison-town soles are his instruments. The question is not who. The question is where they have taken the boy, and how quickly."

"But how," said Kibri-Dagan, with the careful tone of a man being educated against his will, "can you know they have taken him north? The ground tells you three men waited and departed. It does not tell you the direction."

Holkos stood and looked at him with something I had learned to identify as patience at its most disciplined.

"The ground tells me the direction," he said. "But more useful: Kuri the water-carrier tells me the direction. He was awake in the second hour of two nights past. He saw three men moving toward the northern ridge with a burden that was, in his words, like a rolled carpet that breathed." He paused. "I spoke to Kuri this morning while you watched me sitting on a rock, Kibri-Dagan. But the observation was Nerab's first. He recorded it on the night it occurred."

He looked at me as he said this. Kibri-Dagan looked at me also, with some revision occurring behind his eyes. I did not say that I had recorded it without the faintest understanding of what it meant. This seemed, in the moment, an unnecessary qualification.

"The ridgeline," Holkos continued, "leads to a dry-season track that rejoins the main road one bēru north, near a waystation that Bel-iqisha's brother administers. The boy will be held there until he can be questioned in conditions that encourage cooperation. We have, I calculate, until the second hour after midday before the questioning begins in earnest. It is now one hour past dawn."

He was already walking toward the northern ridge.

VII.

He brought four of the hired men. He did not bring Kibri-Dagan. This was a calculation I understood only afterward: Kibri-Dagan served the official structure, and what happened at the waystation required the official structure to remain, for the moment, ignorant.

He did not tell the four men what we were walking into. He told them what they needed to know: there was a stone building; there were men inside; there was a boy who needed to come out. He spoke with the flat, practical tone of someone describing a cart that needed pushing, and the four men accepted this with the professional equanimity of men who are paid to accept such things. To Ilu-eriba specifically he said three additional words, quietly, as they shouldered their equipment. I did not hear them. Ilu-eriba nodded once, the brief nod of a man confirming a previous arrangement, and I understood that the quarter-hour conversation three evenings prior had not been wasted.

I was not told to remain behind. I did not ask for permission to come. I brought my tablet.

The path through the hills was not the kind that forgives inattention. The ground was loose shale in the steeper sections, the kind that announces your presence to anyone above you with the enthusiasm of a herald, and the sun was moving against us — we were walking east of south, into its face. Ilu-eriba kept pace with Holkos without being asked, positioning himself slightly ahead and to the left at the exposed sections. I observed this. It was not ordered. It was a professional reflex, the same way my own hand moved to steady the tablet when the path lurched downward.

Holkos did not speak during the ascent. He moved with an economy that I had seen before and that always surprised me — it was not the movement of a scholar or an administrator but of a man who had spent time, in some earlier portion of his career, in terrain that required it. His eyes were on the ridgeline. They did not move from it.

We came over the ridge at what I estimated was the hour before noon.

The waystation sat in the fold of two hills, a stone building of no particular distinction, the kind used in better seasons for sheltering animals. Even from the ridge I could smell it: goat dung and old lamp oil, a specific cheapness of oil cut with fish fat that made it burn with a thick, yellowish smoke. Two men were visible outside, seated on stones near the entrance. They were wearing the garments of local farmhands. They were watching the southern approach — the direction from which anyone following the caravan's route would come — and not the northern ridge from which we were descending.

I studied them from the ridge while Holkos crouched beside me and was very still. The two guards were young, not veterans. One had his spear laid across his knees rather than held upright — a man who expects no trouble adopts this posture, a man who has been on watch too long and has stopped believing that what he is watching for will arrive. The other was whittling something with a short knife. I noted this because it told me the knife was in his hand. I noted which hand: his left. I recorded both observations on my tablet in the compressed shorthand I use when there is no time for clarity, and I passed the tablet to Holkos.

He read it without expression. Then he looked at me for a moment in a way that I found difficult to interpret, and which I can only record accurately as: appraising.

"Good," he said, which was not a word he used often, and not a word I had previously understood to carry the weight it carried then.

He deployed the four hired men around the building's perimeter — one to each rear corner, Ilu-eriba to the eastern wall where a secondary door was visible in the stonework. He positioned himself and me at the front, which struck me as strange until I understood what he intended.

He walked out of the shadow of the ridge in full view of the two guards, at an unhurried pace, in the manner of a merchant who has taken a wrong turn and is mildly inconvenienced by it. He called out in the Aramaic trade dialect, with the flat vowels of a Harranite. "Is this the waystation on the Bel-iqisha road? I was told I might find shelter before the afternoon heat."

The guard with the spear across his knees stood up. The one with the knife kept whittling. Both of them looked at Holkos, and then at me — a scribe with a tablet, clearly a servant — and in their faces I saw the rapid arithmetic of threat-assessment performed by men who have been told to expect a rescue party and are instead presented with a merchant and his record-keeper. The arithmetic resolved in the direction of dismissiveness.

"No room," the first guard said. "Move on."

"I was told," Holkos said, still walking, closing the distance between them with the patient persistence of a man who has a legitimate purpose and is not accustomed to being refused, "that this station was administered by a representative of the Carchemish district. I have correspondence that requires a seal. I was told —"

"I said move on." The first guard raised the spear. He was close now — four paces. Three.

"Of course," Holkos said pleasantly, and stopped.

The guard had both hands on the spear. The second guard had looked up from his whittling. Neither of them was watching the corners of the building.

What happened next occurred in the space between two breaths.

The four hired men came around the walls. The first guard made the calculation that the spear gave him an advantage and lunged; Ilu-eriba, who had come around the eastern corner at a run, did something with his forearm and the shaft that I observed but could not have described technically, and the guard went down. The second guard — the one with the knife in his left hand, whose left-handedness I had recorded and Holkos had noted and Ilu-eriba had been quietly told about — stepped back and raised the blade.

He did not step back far enough. There was a wall there. He had forgotten the wall.

Ilu-eriba looked at him across three paces of ground and waited. The guard looked at four men, and at a wall, and at Ilu-eriba's expression, which was not threatening so much as simply patient, in the manner of a man who has already decided how this ends and is allowing the other party time to reach the same conclusion.

The knife came down.

"How many inside," Holkos said. It was not a question.

"Two," the guard said, after a moment that was almost dignified. "And the boy."

"Is the boy unharmed."

Something moved in the guard's face. "He hasn't been touched. We were told — we were told to keep him comfortable. Until—"

"Until what," Holkos said.

The guard was quiet.

"Until the man who sent you arrived to conduct the questioning himself," Holkos said. It was still not a question. "Bel-iqisha does not trust proxies for work of this delicacy. He would come in person. When."

The guard's silence was a different quality of silence from before. It had resistance in it, but also the shape of a man calculating the cost of that resistance.

"Third hour past midday," he said finally.

Holkos looked at the position of the sun. So did I. We had less than two hours.

"Your employer," Holkos said, "has constructed an elaborate mechanism to extract information from a boy who does not possess it. When you report this failure to him, convey from me that the waste offends me rather more than the malice." He paused. "He will not be coming. Stand aside."

But one of the two men inside had heard the commotion, and the secondary door opened before Ilu-eriba reached it.

The man who came through was not young and was not making a calculation. He had a short blade already drawn and the particular forward lean of someone who has decided that the only way through a situation is directly. He went for Holkos, which was the rational choice — the apparent leader, unarmed, the nearest target. I was between them and slightly to the left, and I did the only thing available to me: I put the edge of my writing tablet across the inside of his leading wrist with all the force I could manage.

It was not elegant. The tablet cracked. His wrist turned, the blade skittered sideways, and then Ilu-eriba's hand was on the back of his neck and it was over, and the man was on the ground, and I was standing with half a tablet and a feeling in my hands that I expect I will remember for some time.

Holkos looked at me.

"Your tablet," he said.

"It is damaged," I said. "I have a second."

"Good," he said, for the second time that day, and the word carried the same weight as before and something more besides.

The fourth man — the second inside — was seated on the floor when we entered, having observed through the doorway what had occurred and arrived at the correct conclusion without requiring it to be demonstrated to him personally.

VIII.

The building smelled worse inside than it had outside. A single oil lamp was burning on a ledge, the thick fish-fat smoke coiling upward into the low ceiling, and in the corner furthest from the door, on a mat that had been provided — they had, as the guard said, been keeping him comfortable — was Shamash-iddin.

He was pale in the way of someone who has not slept. His hands were not bound. He had been given bread, of which half remained uneaten. When the door opened he flinched with the full-body flinch of someone who has been waiting for a specific thing to happen and cannot help responding to every approximation of it.

Then he saw Holkos.

He did not speak at first. His lips moved in their habitual way — the way they moved when he was working through a difficult passage — but there was no tablet in front of him and nothing to decode. Then, from the corner where he sat, he said: "I couldn't tell them anything. I told them I only copy the marks. I don't know the marks. I told them that."

His voice was steady in the way that voices are steady when a person has been practising steadiness for some hours and is not certain how much more practice is left in them.

"I know," Holkos said. He crossed the room and crouched to the boy's level. "That is precisely why you are unharmed. And it is why we were able to find you where you are, rather than somewhere else." He said it simply, as fact, which was more comfort than reassurance would have been. "Can you walk?"

"Yes." A pause. "My lord — the tablets. The ones I copy. Are they — did I —"

"You did nothing wrong," Holkos said. "The tablets are safe. You are safe. The rest is not your concern."

Shamash-iddin's lips stopped moving. He allowed himself to be helped to his feet, and when we came out into the dust-heavy air of noon he raised his face to the light with the expression of someone who had been conducting a private negotiation with the next several hours of his life and had now revised the terms considerably upward.

I watched this. I felt something I have no precise word for in the language of records.

We left the waystation before the sun had moved a hand's breadth. Bel-iqisha, coming at the third hour past midday, would find four men in various states of reconsideration and a building containing nothing he had paid for. I thought about that as we descended the southern side of the ridge. My master, I noticed, did not look back.

IX.

We were back at the caravan before the third hour after midday. Shamash-iddin rode on one of the hired men's horses, still pale, still moving his lips in his habitual way, though he had no tablet. I sat beside him for some time and let him be quiet, which was what he needed, while Holkos dealt with Kibri-Dagan.

Kibri-Dagan received the account with the face of a man who has been handed a problem that none of his procedures address.

"Bel-iqisha," he said. "The administrator of Carchemish."

"The same."

"He cannot be — I cannot simply — there is a process, an inquiry, a report to be filed with —"

"Yes," said Holkos. "There is. And while that process unfolds over the next two or three seasons, Bel-iqisha will have ample opportunity to revise his arrangements, relocate his instruments, and ensure that nothing discoverable remains." He paused. "Or."

Kibri-Dagan waited.

"You allow us to pass through your checkpoint without the document difficulty. The caravan continues west on schedule. Bel-iqisha is informed — through channels I will arrange — that the boy remembered nothing useful, that the tablets were copied in a script only Holkos reads, and that the entire enterprise was a waste of effort. Bel-iqisha, being a practical man, will conclude that the risk is not worth the reward and will trouble us no further." Another pause. "You receive credit for the smooth passage of a sensitive household through a difficult zone. Your record shows competence. A future appointment in a less contested district becomes, perhaps, easier to obtain."

Kibri-Dagan looked at him for a long time.

"The boy," he said. "Is he —"

"He is well. He will remain well. He is also," Holkos said, "a sixteen-year-old child who was taken from his mat in the night by strangers, and who will remember this for the rest of his life without, I believe, requiring it to become an entry in an official report that will be read by exactly the people who ordered it done."

The silence that followed had texture in it — the sound of flies in the warm air, the shifting of animals, the creak of lashed cedar boxes settling.

"Move the caravan," Kibri-Dagan said finally, to no one in particular.

X.

That evening, at the night halt, I sat beside Holkos while he ate — an act of some novelty; he had not eaten since the previous day, and I had begun to consider whether I needed to manage the situation more directly.

"You knew," I said. "When Kibri-Dagan first told you. You already knew the shape of it."

"I suspected the shape," he said, with the precision that was its own form of honesty. "The Carchemish escort's behaviour had been wrong since Harran. Not wrong in a manner you would record. Wrong in a manner of small calibrations — they always positioned themselves between the scribes and the baggage." He was quiet for a moment. "The sealed letter three days past confirmed the rest. Bel-iqisha is not a subtle man. He is a small man in an uncertain posting with large ambitions and no genius. When I told him, through a carefully imprecise intermediary, that we were carrying correspondence of significant value, he responded exactly as a man of his type responds: immediately, clumsily, and with more confidence in his instruments than the instruments warranted."

I was quiet for a moment. "You told him."

"I gave him the shape of a thing worth taking," Holkos said. "I did not give him what he imagined he would find inside it."

"He came himself," I said. "To the waystation."

"Of course he did. A man who does not trust his proxies with the specifics will always come himself when the specifics matter." He considered. "He will have a long, empty afternoon. I expect he has already begun revising his understanding of the situation."

"He tried to have a child questioned," I said. I did not often say things like this to him. It was not my function.

He looked at me. The fire was behind him and his face was half in shadow, and what I saw there was not nothing, though it was not what the rest of us wore when we felt what we felt.

"Yes," he said. "He did."

We sat with that.

"The tablets," I said. "Shamash-iddin could not have decoded the cipher-key in them. He doesn't know there is a cipher-key. He copies them as astronomical observations."

"Correct."

"So Bel-iqisha risked everything for information a sixteen-year-old boy could not have given him regardless."

"Also correct."

"That is," I said, searching for the right word, "a tragedy."

Holkos considered this. "It is inefficiency," he said. "Which is a form of tragedy, I suppose. A man who had thought more carefully would have found a better approach or abandoned the effort entirely. The waste offends me more than the malice."

He finished his bread. He handed me the cup.

"You observed the guards well," he said. "The left-handed man with the knife. If I had not read your tablet, Ilu-eriba would have come around the wrong corner." A pause. "And the third man. You acted."

I looked at him. "My tablet is broken."

"You have a second tablet," he said. "You said so yourself."

It was, I understood, the closest he would come to the thing he meant. I have spent eighteen years learning to translate him.

"Drink something, Nerab. You've been running half the day."

I drank. I wrote down what I could before the firelight failed.

Here ends the account of the case of Shamash-iddin the scribe, as recorded by Nerab son of Adad-uballit, in the service of Holkos the Assyrian, in the hill country west of Carchemish, in the year that was among the last years any man could travel under Assyrian papers and expect them to mean something.

The caravan reached the Lydian sphere without further incident. Kibri-Dagan was appointed to an administrative post in the Amanus region the following year.

Bel-iqisha, arriving at the waystation at the third hour past midday, found four men and an empty room. He returned to Carchemish and, within six months, received word through certain channels that the boy had remembered nothing of use and the tablets were unreadable without a key that did not exist in written form. He accepted this, as practical men accept the failure of expensive plans, and did not repeat the error. He lived another seven years, and died, as small men in uncertain postings often do, of causes that require no commentary.

Shamash-iddin continued to copy the astronomical tablets. He never asked what was in them. He worked more quickly after that autumn, and with a precision that, if anything, exceeded what it had been before. Nerab recorded this without knowing what to make of it, and left it here for whoever reads this account to make of it what they will.
