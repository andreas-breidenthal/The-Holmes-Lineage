HOLKOS THE ASSYRIAN

~700 BC — Anatolia

Holkos read the fall of Nineveh before it happened — in the dispatches arriving from the eastern frontier, in the changing tone of the Babylonian correspondence, in the quality of the tribute reports from the empire’s periphery, in a hundred small signals that individually meant nothing and collectively meant everything. He began moving the family’s assets west two decades before the Medes and Babylonians arrived at the walls, converting property into portable wealth with the methodical efficiency of a man who has identified an endpoint and is working backward from it.

Nineveh fell in 612 BC. Holkos the Assyrian was in western Anatolia by then, his family intact, his wealth preserved, his library of copied texts — selected for portability and value — carried west in cedar boxes on the backs of donkeys. He settled in the Lydian sphere, near Sardis, bringing with him the accumulated learning of the Assyrian library tradition and the analytical habits of a man who had spent his career at the centre of the world’s most powerful intelligence apparatus. He was the last of the line to hold an Assyrian name. The world that name belonged to no longer existed.

The account that follows survives because it was recorded in full by Nerab the scribe, a man attached to Holkos’s household in Harran whose work was to copy and preserve correspondence, accounts, and observations with a diligence that exceeded the ordinary requirements of his trade. Nerab kept a continuous written record of the events that unfolded during the caravan’s passage west, and it is from his tablets that this narrative is drawn. He wrote as a witness, but also as one who asked questions of his master, shaping what he recorded through the limits of his understanding and the discipline of his craft.

The Record Of Nerab

Set down in the eighteenth year of his service, during the passage westward through the hill country beyond the Euphrates ford at Carchemish

The road from Carchemish to the Lydian sphere does not forgive the inattentive. This I knew before we set out. What I did not know — what none of us knew save perhaps Holkos himself, who seemed to have known it from the moment the caravan was assembled and the cedar boxes lashed to their animals — was that the road would require something more than attention before we reached the mountains.

I set this account down now, while the details remain clear and the smell of that particular stretch of dust still lives in my nostrils when I close my eyes. The other scribes in the household have urged me to omit certain portions. I have chosen not to. My master would say that a record with portions omitted is not a record but a decoration, and he would be right, as he is almost always right about things that do not require a human being to feel them.

The morning we lost Shamash-iddin, the road had been quiet for three days.

This is the detail that mattered. I did not understand it to be a detail at the time. I understood it to be a relief, the way a man understands the absence of rain to be pleasant without asking where the rain has gone.

We were seventeen days out of Harran, moving through the hill country where the empire's western hand had grown thin and uncertain, where the garrison posts were spaced too far apart and the local dynasts paid their tribute to whichever power seemed most present on any given season. The escort numbered twenty — eight of Holkos's own hired men and twelve provided by the local administrator at Carchemish, whose name was Bel-iqisha, and who had the eyes of a man perpetually calculating whether his loyalties required renegotiation. The caravan itself was forty animals: twelve bearing the cedar boxes of copied texts, the rest carrying the household's provisions, the commercial goods Holkos used to explain his movement to officials who required explanations, and the eleven members of the household staff who had survived the initial sorting that preceded departure from Harran.

Shamash-iddin was the youngest of the scribes. He was sixteen years old and had been in Holkos's household for fourteen months, assigned initially to copy inventory lists and correspondence of the routine kind, advancing by degrees to the copying of astronomical observations, which he performed with a precision unusual in someone his age. He was a thin boy with ink-darkened fingers and the habit of moving his lips slightly when he worked through a difficult passage. He had no enemies that anyone had identified. He had no debts. He had no attachments in the hill country that would explain a voluntary disappearance.

He was simply gone.

The escort captain — one Kibri-Dagan, a competent man of the professional type who followed procedure as a priest follows liturgy — informed Holkos at the third hour of morning. I was present. I watched my master receive the news with an expression that did not change, which I had learned to read as the expression he wore when a conclusion he had already formed had been confirmed.

"When was he last seen?" Holkos said.

"At the night halt. He took his meal with the other scribes."

"Which night halt."

Kibri-Dagan paused. "The one before last, my lord."

"So he has been missing since the previous night, and you are informing me now, as the caravan prepares to move."

Kibri-Dagan said nothing. The silence was its own answer.

I watched my master stand very still in the grey pre-dawn light, looking at nothing visible to the rest of us, which was his way when the machine behind his eyes was running at full capacity. He was a tall man, lean in the way that years of preferring thought to food will make a man lean, with a long jaw and eyes that had in them something of the quality of a military surveyor's instrument — the sense that distance was being measured precisely and without sentiment. He wore his Assyrian robes now only in private. On the road he dressed as a Harranite merchant, which suited him rather better than it should have.

"We do not move the caravan," he said.

Kibri-Dagan straightened. "My lord, the checkpoint at the next station —"

"Is two days hence. We have time. We do not move the caravan." He turned to me. "Nerab. Your tablet."

I had it already. I had learned not to be without it when he used that tone.

What followed was the alternation I had come by then to expect from him, though it never ceased to unsettle me. For the first hour he did nothing. He sat on a flat stone at the caravan's perimeter and appeared to be watching the hills to the north with the vacant attention of a man watching weather form. He declined food. He declined water. He allowed a fly to land on his hand and did not move to dispel it. The hired men watched him and said nothing, but I could see in the manner they avoided looking directly at him that they thought he was performing a religious observance, which was close enough to accurate that I did not correct them.

Kibri-Dagan, who was a man of practical temperament, came to me privately.

"The caravan must move," he said. "You understand this. The checkpoint requires our arrival within the window of the safe-passage document. If we are late, the document is void. If the document is void—"

"I understand," I said.

"—then we must explain ourselves to the checkpoint commander, who will want to know why we are late, and then he will want to know what we are carrying, and then—"

"I understand," I said again.

"Your master sits on a rock."

"Yes."

"Looking at the hills."

"Yes."

Kibri-Dagan looked at me with the expression of a man who is considering whether the people he works for are, in the final analysis, sound. "The boy has probably run," he said. "Boys run. Especially boys who are carrying important correspondence through hostile country and begin to understand what that means. He is on the road back to Harran by now, or he has found a farm and is offering his labour for bread. He will not be found on this road."

"Tell him that," I said, nodding toward Holkos.

Kibri-Dagan looked at my master on his stone. He decided not to.

Then, without transition or explanation, Holkos stood. The fly departed. He moved to the baggage area with a purposeful energy that seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere at once, the languor abandoned like a garment he no longer required. He began, methodically, to examine the ground around the place where the scribes had slept on the second night back, which the caravan had already passed through and which required us to walk back along our own route perhaps four hundred paces.

I followed, tablet ready. Kibri-Dagan followed at a distance, maintaining the expression of official skepticism that was, I had come to understand, his form of hope.

The ground told a story. I could see that it told a story the way I could see that a text written in Elamite told a story — the marks were clearly meaningful, arranged in an order that was clearly deliberate, in a language I did not speak.

Holkos spoke it for me.

"Three men," he said, crouching at the edge of the sleeping area where the scribes' mats had been laid. "Not from the caravan. You see the sole pattern here — this cut across the heel is a local cobbler's mark, the style worn in the garrison town we passed two days before Carchemish. Our hired men came from Harran. Kibri-Dagan's men came from Carchemish itself, which uses a different sole entirely." He moved forward perhaps ten paces. "They waited. You see where they stood — the ground is compressed but not scuffed; these were patient men, not nervous ones. Patient men are professional men. This was not opportunism."

"Bandits," said Kibri-Dagan, who had come close enough to hear. "The road has bandits. This is known. We should report to the checkpoint —"

"Bandits take everything they can carry and leave quickly," Holkos said, without looking up. "They took one boy and nothing else. One sleeping boy from a caravan with twenty armed escorts, which they navigated without alerting anyone." He moved again, following something invisible to me across the rocky ground. "These men knew exactly which sleeping mat to approach. They knew the boy's position in the camp. They had, I would estimate, a specific reason for wanting this particular scribe and no other."

I felt something cold move through me that had nothing to do with the morning air.

"He copies the astronomical tablets," I said slowly.

Holkos looked at me. It was the look he gave when I had arrived, unexpectedly and slightly late, at a conclusion he had been waiting for.

"He copies the astronomical tablets," he agreed. "Which contain, embedded in their observations, the cipher-key to the correspondence records of the household's commercial arrangements. Which records, if decoded by someone who understood what they were reading, would identify every asset we have moved west, every contact we have established in the Lydian sphere, and every arrangement that makes our continued existence in this part of the world dependent on not being examined too closely by any interested party."

He let that settle. Then: "The local administrator at Carchemish. Bel-iqisha. He provided the escort. He knew our composition and our route and the nature of our cargo — or suspected enough of it to want to know more. The men with the garrison-town soles are his instruments. The question is not who. The question is where they have taken the boy, and how quickly."

"But how," Kibri-Dagan said, with the careful tone of a man who is being educated against his will, "can you know they have taken him north? The ground tells you three men waited and departed. It does not tell you the direction."

Holkos stood and looked at him with something that I had learned to identify as patience at its most disciplined.

"The ground tells me the direction," he said. "But more useful: the caravan's water-carrier tells me the direction. He was awake at the second hour of last night — he has the bladder complaint I have been meaning to address — and he saw three men moving toward the northern ridge with a burden that was, in his words, like a rolled carpet that breathed. I spoke to him forty minutes ago while you watched me sitting on a rock, Kibri-Dagan."

Kibri-Dagan absorbed this. His expression did not change, but something behind it did.

"The ridgeline," Holkos said, "leads to a dry-season track that rejoins the main road eight miles north, near a waystation that Bel-iqisha's brother administers. The boy will be held there until he can be questioned in conditions that encourage cooperation. We have, I calculate, until the second hour after midday before the questioning begins in earnest. It is now one hour past dawn."

He was already walking toward the northern ridge.

I will compress the passage through the hills, partly because I was too frightened during much of it to observe with the precision my training required, and partly because some of what occurred there is not mine to record in full.

What I will say:

The waystation was a stone building of no particular distinction, used in better seasons for sheltering animals. It smelled of goat dung and old lamp oil — a specific cheapness of oil, the kind cut with fish fat that made it burn with a thick, yellowish smoke, and I cannot encounter that smell now without feeling the particular dread of standing outside that door in the dust-heavy air of noon.

Holkos had brought four of the hired men. He had not brought Kibri-Dagan. This was a calculation I understood only afterward: Kibri-Dagan served the official structure. What happened at the waystation required the official structure to remain, for the moment, ignorant.

Shamash-iddin was inside. He was frightened but unhurt — they had been methodical with him, softening him with hunger and isolation rather than injury, which was the professional approach and which Holkos had apparently also calculated, since he did not move with the urgency he would have moved with otherwise. The two men left to guard the boy made a brief resistance and then a practical one.

The man who had organised it was not present. He did not need to be. Holkos already knew who he was.

We were back at the caravan before the third hour after midday. Shamash-iddin rode on one of the hired men's horses, still pale and moving his lips in the way he did when he was working through something difficult, though he had no tablet. He had not spoken since we found him. I sat with him while Holkos dealt with Kibri-Dagan, and I let the boy be quiet, which was what he needed.

Kibri-Dagan received the account with the face of a man who has been handed a problem that none of his procedures address.

"Bel-iqisha," he said. "The administrator of Carchemish."

"The same."

"He cannot be—I cannot simply—there is a process, an inquiry, a report to be filed with—"

"Yes," said Holkos. "There is. And while that process unfolds over the next two or three seasons, Bel-iqisha will have ample opportunity to revise his arrangements, relocate his instruments, and ensure that nothing discoverable remains." He paused. "Or."

Kibri-Dagan waited.

"You allow us to pass through your checkpoint without the document difficulty. The caravan continues west on schedule. Bel-iqisha is informed — through channels I will arrange — that the boy remembered nothing useful, that the tablets were copied in a script only Holkos reads, and that the entire enterprise was a waste of effort. Bel-iqisha, being a practical man, will conclude that the risk is not worth the reward and will trouble us no further." Another pause. "You receive credit for the smooth passage of a sensitive household through a difficult zone. Your record shows competence. A future appointment in a less contested district becomes, perhaps, easier to obtain."

Kibri-Dagan looked at him for a long time.

"The boy," he said. "Is he—"

"He is well. He will remain well. He is also," Holkos said, "a sixteen-year-old child who was taken from his mat in the night by strangers, and who will remember this for the rest of his life without, I believe, requiring it to become an entry in an official report that will be read by exactly the people who ordered it done."

The silence that followed had texture in it — the sound of flies in the warm air, the shifting of animals, the creak of lashed cedar boxes settling.

"Move the caravan," Kibri-Dagan said finally, to no one in particular.

That evening, at the night halt, I sat beside Holkos while he ate — an act of some novelty; he had not eaten since the previous day, and I had begun to consider whether I needed to manage the situation more directly.

"You knew," I said. "When Kibri-Dagan first told you. You already knew the shape of it."

"I suspected the shape," he said, with the precision that was its own form of honesty. "The Carchemish escort's behaviour had been wrong since Harran. Not wrong in a manner you would record. Wrong in a manner of small calibrations. They always positioned themselves between the scribes and the baggage." He was quiet for a moment. "Bel-iqisha is Bel-iqisha. He is a small man in an uncertain posting with large ambitions and no genius. He is not worth pursuing beyond what I have arranged."

"He tried to have a child questioned," I said. I did not often say things like this to him. It was not my function.

He looked at me. The fire was behind him and his face was half in shadow, and what I saw there was not nothing, though it was not what the rest of us wore when we felt what we felt.

"Yes," he said. "He did."

We sat with that.

"The tablets," I said. "Shamash-iddin could not have decoded the cipher-key in them. He doesn't know there is a cipher-key. He copies them as astronomical observations."

"Correct."

"So Bel-iqisha risked everything for information a sixteen-year-old boy could not have given him regardless."

"Also correct."

"That is," I said, searching for the right word, "a tragedy."

Holkos considered this. "It is inefficiency," he said. "Which is a form of tragedy, I suppose. A man who had thought more carefully would have found a better approach or abandoned the effort entirely. The waste offends me more than the malice."

He finished his bread. He handed me the cup.

"Drink something, Nerab. You've been running half the day."

I drank. I wrote down what I could before the firelight failed.

Here ends the account of the case of Shamash-iddin the scribe, as recorded by Nerab son of Adad-uballit, in the service of Holkos the Assyrian, in the hill country west of Carchemish, in the year that was among the last years any man could travel under Assyrian papers and expect them to mean something.

The caravan reached the Lydian sphere without further incident. Kibri-Dagan was appointed to an administrative post in the Amanus region the following year. Bel-iqisha died of a fever before the season turned, which Nerab records here without comment, as his master would have wished.

Shamash-iddin continued to copy the astronomical tablets. He never asked what was in them.
