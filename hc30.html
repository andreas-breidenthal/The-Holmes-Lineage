AKAR-BAAL OF TYRE

~1180 BC — Tyre, Phoenicia

The Correspondence Archive Of Abimilki

The following accopreserved in my own hand, as I was present for the events described, and as I am the only party with cause to preserve them accurately — the official record being, as will become apparent, of a different character entirely.

I.

The young man arrived at the counting house before I did, which is a distinction I have never before been required to make about a client, as I myself arrive before the fish-sellers open their stalls and considerably before any respectable merchant would think of stirring. He was perhaps twenty-two, with the raw hands of a man who had worked a ship's deck before he inherited the counting house desk, and he was sitting on the stone step outside the door with the particular stillness of a person who has been awake all night deciding whether to come at all.

His name was Rib-Addi — not a name of consequence in Tyre at that time, though it would become one — and he was the youngest son of a Byblos ship-owner whose sudden death the previous season had left the family's Tyrian operation to whoever among his sons was foolish enough to accept it. Rib-Addi had accepted it. He was, as I came to understand, the sort of young man who accepts things.

I let him in. Akar-Baal was already there.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was that Akar-Baal was not working. He sat behind the cedar-wood table that served as his desk, his long fingers pressed together at their tips, his eyes fixed on the middle distance in a way I had come to recognise over two years of close association — the way a lamp looks when it is burning without quite illuminating anything nearby. A cup of date wine sat at his elbow, untouched. The carnelian beads that he had acquired from an Aramean contact three seasons prior hung from his left hand, clicking through methodically without any apparent awareness of the action. He had arrived, I understood, before I had, at something he had not yet been asked.

His attention, when it moved to Rib-Addi, had the quality of a man who has been waiting for confirmation of a suspicion.

"Sit," he said. And then, before the young man had fully arranged himself on the stool: "Your cargo bond falls due when?"

Rib-Addi blinked. "In twelve days. How did you—"

"You are the youngest son. You have taken a position your brothers did not want. You have been in Tyre for less than a full season — your robe is Byblos-cut, and the stitching at the hem is local repair, recent. You came here at dawn rather than to the council chambers, which means you have already been to the council chambers and been told, gently, that your problem is not quite a council problem. And your hands—" Akar-Baal tilted his head slightly, studying them from across the table— "have the particular tension of a man who has not slept, but who is not given to dramatics. Something with a deadline."

"Twelve days," Rib-Addi said. "Yes."

The matter, as it emerged over the next quarter-hour, was this: Rib-Addi's family had operated as a junior trading partner in the electrum trade, providing ship-space and cargo guarantees for several of the larger Tyrian merchant houses. The most significant of these arrangements was with the house of Hanno-Melqart, who shipped electrum south to the Egyptian factor Djedhor of Memphis. Rib-Addi's family guaranteed the cargo weights — a standard arrangement, essentially a form of insurance against transit loss — and collected a small fee for doing so. The bond was renewed each season.

The bond for the current season had not yet been renewed. Hanno-Melqart had delayed the renewal, citing administrative matters, for three months. The bond payment, which the family required to cover their own obligations at the northern docks, fell due in twelve days. Without the renewal, Rib-Addi could not meet the payment. He would be technically in default on three separate dock agreements, his family's position in Tyre would dissolve in a season.

Two days ago, a man Rib-Addi did not know had approached him at the harbour and suggested, in terms that were courteous and therefore more frightening than any threat, that the bond renewal was unlikely to proceed unless Rib-Addi withdrew from a secondary arrangement he had recently entered into — one that involved providing cargo guarantees for a Sidonite merchant who had raised concerns with the council about Hanno-Melqart's weights.

Rib-Addi had gone the following morning to the council. They had been sympathetic and unhelpful in roughly equal measure.

Akar-Baal had listened to all of this without expression. The carnelian beads had continued their count. When Rib-Addi finished, there was a silence of perhaps thirty seconds — long enough that I began to formulate something useful to say, which is my usual function in such silences — and then Akar-Baal set down the beads and said, to me rather than to the young man:

"How many seasons has Hanno-Melqart been trading electrum southward?"

It was not the question one would have predicted.

II.

I should say something here about Akar-Baal, since it will shortly become relevant: he had arrived in Tyre from the wreckage of the Hittite frontier carrying his father's trading contacts, the family name for reliability, and a reputation for resolving matters that the council preferred not to have resolved publicly. He had rebuilt the family's commercial position with the patient efficiency of a man who understood that the most durable reputation is the one that never needs to be stated. What the harbour did not fully know — what I came to understand only gradually, in two years of close proximity — was that behind the stillness was an appetite so precise and restless that the stillness itself was a kind of controlled pressure, the way water held behind a weir is not still at all but merely contained.

III.

The council formalised the commission that afternoon. The matter had first come to them three days prior in the form it usually takes: not as an accusation but as an anxiety. A Sidonite merchant had brought to the council's attention — with the careful informality of a man who wishes to cause damage without being seen to throw the stone — that Hanno-Melqart's weights had given imprecise readings when tested against a set of royal standard measures recently arrived from the Egyptian administration.

The council had not wished to investigate. Hanno-Melqart was the third generation of a respected family; his grandfather had helped finance the first expansion of the northern docks. But the council could not ignore a formal complaint, even a carefully informal one, and so the matter was placed before Akar-Baal.

He spent that first evening in what appeared to be inactivity. The beads clicked. The account tablets sat untouched before him. At no point did he appear to be reading anything.

At the close of that evening he said to me: "Bring me every letter that has passed between Hanno-Melqart's counting house and the Egyptian factor Djedhor of Memphis. Four seasons. And separately — the harbour master's cargo logs for incoming Egyptian vessels over the same period. Not the outgoing declarations. The incoming tallies."

I noted the distinction. At the time, I did not understand it.

IV.

The following morning I assembled what he had requested and set it before him in two separate stacks: Hanno-Melqart's correspondence with Djedhor on the left, the harbour master's incoming cargo logs on the right. He looked at the two stacks together for a moment — not at either one individually, but at both simultaneously, as if he were already reading the relationship between them — and then he began.

He worked through the correspondence first. I occupied myself with other records at the far end of the table, and for perhaps an hour the room held only the sound of tablets being set down and taken up, the click of the beads, the harbour noise through the window. Then:

"Here." He did not look up. "Come here."

I crossed to him. He held a tablet — Djedhor's reply to Hanno-Melqart's letter of the previous second season, the one in which Hanno-Melqart had offered a written explanation of a weight discrepancy, attributed to damage in transit.

"Read the final sentence."

I read it. Djedhor wrote that he considered the matter resolved and had recorded the account as balanced.

"Balanced," Akar-Baal said. He set the tablet down with a care that was, in itself, a form of emphasis. "Not 'settled.' Not 'closed.' Balanced." He picked up the carnelian beads. "A factor of the Egyptian administration — an administration that requires, by its own procedure, a formal board of inquiry for weight discrepancies above the specified threshold — accepts a private letter of explanation and records the account as balanced. Why?"

I gave the common answer: that Djedhor trusted the relationship, or that the discrepancy was small enough to absorb, or that Egyptian administrative procedure at present discouraged the pursuit of small claims.

Akar-Baal looked at me with the patience of a teacher who has heard the expected answer and is not surprised by it.

"Or," he said, "he had reason not to investigate. Because investigation runs both ways." He picked up the right-hand stack — the harbour master's incoming cargo logs — and placed them before me. "Now. Your family imports Egyptian linen?"

I blinked at the apparent change of subject. "My family? No — we are administrative, not commercial."

"I was not asking about your family. I was asking whether you, as a man who has spent two years drafting commercial correspondence in this city, know which houses import Egyptian linen."

"Hanno-Melqart's house. They are the primary buyers of fine grades."

He nodded once, as if this confirmed something he had already concluded, and opened the harbour master's log to the first of the four seasons in question. "Sit," he said. "I want you to read me the declared quantities of linen on each Egyptian inbound, and then the actual quantities recorded by the harbour master at the quay."

I sat. I read. Season by season, vessel by vessel, the declared quantity of fine Egyptian linen and the quantity actually counted off the ships by the harbour master's own tallies.

The declared quantities were higher. Consistently higher. By a margin that was, in any single season, small — small enough to attribute to counting variation, to damaged bolts rejected at unloading, to any number of honest explanations. But consistent. The same margin, season after season, vessel after vessel.

I read the last entry and set down the tablet.

"Hanno-Melqart's house," I said, "has been receiving short measures of linen."

"For three seasons," Akar-Baal said. "From the same trading partner to whom he has been sending short weights for the same three seasons." He was looking not at the tablets but at the middle distance, the beads moving with a rhythm that I had learned to read as the particular tempo of a conclusion arriving. "The accounts balance. Not because either party is honest, but because both parties are dishonest in precisely calibrated opposition. Each defrauds the other with such consistency that the net result, across the trading relationship, is a roughly accurate transfer of value — with only the Tyrian harbour and the Egyptian administration, which tax on declared weights and quantities, bearing the actual loss."

He said it with the quietness of a man who has just completed an equation and is reading the result from the slate.

I sat with this for a moment. The harbour knocked and creaked through the window.

"The word 'balanced,'" I said.

"The word 'balanced.'"

"He was not recording an emotional resolution. He was recording an arithmetic one."

"Yes." The beads went still. "Now I need the false weights. That is what the weight workshop is for." He rose and crossed to the door with a sudden energy that was startling after the long stillness — the movement of a man who has been motionless only because motion was not yet required. "Come. We have eight days remaining before the young man from Byblos is ruined, and I wish to hold the weights in my hand before I hold anything else."

V.

The weight workshop was a small room off the upper market, smelling of copper dust and oil, tended by a craftsman called Shapan who had calibrated the city's standard measures for twenty years and regarded any deviation from them as a personal affront. Akar-Baal spent an afternoon there. He emerged with clay dust on the hem of his robe and four small weights wrapped in linen — transit weights from Hanno-Melqart's counting house, borrowed on the authority of the council's inquiry.

He placed them on the table next to the council's standard set. The margin was small. Consistent. The same discrepancy on each of the four graduated sizes.

"A man who falsifies weights," he said, studying them, "falsifies them with a logic. These were not made carelessly. They were made to be defensible — small enough, in any single shipment, to attribute to casting variation. But the same variation, across four sizes, in the same direction, across four seasons—" He wrapped them back in the cloth. "That is not a casting error. That is a decision."

We returned to the counting house.

VI.

We returned, that is, to a counting house in which three tablets were missing.

They had been on the archive shelf where I had placed them the previous evening — Hanno-Melqart's correspondence for the second and third seasons, the very material I had read to Akar-Baal that morning. They were gone. The shelf held their absence with the neatness of a gap in a set of teeth.

Akar-Baal stood before the shelf for a long moment. In two years of close association, I had seen him surprised perhaps once. This was the second time, and it was brief.

"The shelf latch," he said.

I examined it. The cedar latch had a particular stiffness — it required a specific inward pressure before it would disengage. I had opened it hundreds of times. "It is not forced," I said. "It was opened by someone who knew the mechanism."

"Or who had time to learn it." He turned from the shelf. "Who has been in this building today?"

"Shapan's apprentice, returning the calibration records. The harbour master's clerk with the secondary tallies. And—" I paused. "A man I did not know, mid-morning. He said he was delivering a commercial query from the house of—"

"Describe him."

I described him. Heavily built. Forty years, perhaps. A diagonal scar below the left ear that I had noticed without registering the noticing.

Akar-Baal turned this over. "Not an apprentice thief sent to obscure the record. A man who knew precisely which tablets to take." He moved to the window, looking down at the harbour street. "Hanno-Melqart came himself, or sent a man with specific instruction. The second and third season correspondence." He paused. "Not the fourth. Not the harbour master's logs. Not the weight records."

He turned from the window.

"He took the wrong material," he said. "He knows we are looking at his outgoing weights. He does not know we are looking at his incoming linen. He has concealed the evidence of his own crime and left untouched the evidence of Djedhor's — which, in combination with everything else we hold, makes his concealment a confession." There was something very close to satisfaction in his expression, the specific quality of a man who has encountered an opponent moving pieces on a board and finds that each move confirms what he already knew. "He has also told me that the confrontation must happen tonight. He will not wait."

This was when I understood that the evening had a different character from what I had imagined.

VII.

That night, as I was leaving to make the arrangements Akar-Baal had specified, a man fell into step beside me on the upper market street. Large. Dock-build. He did not look at me.

"The weight commission is a small matter," he said, in the conversational tone of a man discussing harbour fees. "There are men in this city for whom it is easier if it remains small."

I said nothing.

"The young man from Byblos has obligations at the northern docks. It would be a pity if those obligations became complicated by association with an inquiry that goes further than it needs to."

He turned off at the next junction.

I told Akar-Baal within the hour. He listened without interrupting, and when I finished, he asked: "Were you frightened?"

"Yes," I said.

He nodded, as if this were the correct and useful answer. "Good. It means he is paying for inconveniences, which means he believes inconvenience is still cheaper than exposure." He was quiet for a moment. "He is miscalculating. We proceed tonight. But I need you to do one further thing before we bring him to the council chamber."

He told me what it was.

I went to the harbour master's offices and left a sealed clay envelope with the night clerk, addressed to the council's senior administrator, with instruction that it was to be opened only in the event that I did not return by the second lamp to collect it. Inside the envelope, on Akar-Baal's instruction, I had written a full account of the linen discrepancies, the false weights, the missing tablets, and the name of Hanno-Melqart.

It was, Akar-Baal had explained with the calm of a man describing a commercial arrangement, our terms. Not a threat — a structure. The sealed envelope did not need to be mentioned. It simply needed to exist, and Hanno-Melqart would, at the appropriate moment, be made to understand that it did.

VIII.

The smell of the council chamber at midnight is different from its smell at dawn: less salt, more oil, the particular staleness of a room that has been heated by lamps through the long day and is now cooling around their guttering light. Hanno-Melqart had been summoned for the hour of the third lamp. He arrived seven minutes before it, which told me something about the quality of his unease.

He was perhaps fifty years, heavy through the chest, with the hands of a man who had started his working life loading ships. He wore three gold rings and a linen robe of Egyptian manufacture. He smelled of cedar oil and, beneath it, something sharper — the particular alertness of a man who has decided, in advance, how this meeting will end.

He had not come alone. Two men had accompanied him to the building — waiting in the street outside with the studied idleness of men who are being paid to be nearby. I noted them as I held the door. Akar-Baal had noted them before I arrived; when I entered and met his eyes, the slight inclination of his head told me he had already accounted for them in whatever arithmetic he was running. I gave a small nod. We did not speak of it.

The linen bundle containing the false weights sat at Akar-Baal's left hand. At his right, the harbour master's cargo logs. Between them, the correspondence — all of it, including Djedhor's letter with the word balanced at its close.

He did not begin with the accusation.

"Your grandfather," he said, "established this family's relationship with the Egyptian administration before the disruptions." He set his fingers together at their tips. "I have been reading his correspondence. He was a man of remarkable consistency."

Hanno-Melqart settled back slightly, the posture of a man who has prepared for an attack and found that the opening gambit does not resemble one. "He was."

"Consistency is the rarest commercial virtue. More valuable than any single successful venture. A reputation for consistency compounds across generations in a way that profit cannot." A pause. "It is therefore a striking fact that a family which has maintained this reputation for three generations should require, in the fourth, a written explanation of weight discrepancy in transit — and that the Egyptian factor should accept that explanation without seeking verification."

"There was damage in—"

"The factor Djedhor did not investigate." Akar-Baal said it without raising his voice, and without accelerating. "He did not request a board of inquiry, as Egyptian commercial procedure requires for claims above the specified weight. He accepted a private letter. I have his reply before me." He lifted the tablet and read from it in the flat tone of a man reading a public notice: "He writes that he considers the matter resolved and has recorded the account as balanced."

He set the tablet down.

"Balanced," he said. "That word. Not 'settled.' Not 'closed.' I found it precise. I found myself asking what equation a factor of the Egyptian administration would consider balanced." He gestured to the cargo logs at his right hand. "I asked the harbour master for three seasons of incoming tally records. Specifically, the declared quantities of Egyptian linen against the quantities his men counted at the quay."

Hanno-Melqart's stillness changed quality. It was a small change, the way a moored hull shifts when the tide begins — not movement, but a different relationship to the water beneath it.

"Your house," Akar-Baal said, "is the primary buyer of fine Egyptian linen in this city. In three seasons, across four vessels, the harbour master's quay count runs short of the declared quantity by a consistent margin. Small per shipment. Considerable across three seasons." He opened the log and turned it so that the relevant columns faced Hanno-Melqart across the table. "Djedhor has been sending you short measures of linen. You have been sending him short weights of electrum. Each of you defrauds the other with such precision that the net result, across the trading relationship, is a roughly accurate transfer of value."

He picked up the linen bundle and unfolded it.

Inside were the four false weights — graduated, correctly marked, wrong by a consistent margin.

"These are from your counting house. The margin matches the linen discrepancy." He set them on the table between the two men. "The accounts balance. Not because either party is honest, but because both parties are dishonest in precisely calibrated opposition. The only party bearing the actual loss is the one taxing on declared figures — the harbour, and the Egyptian administration."

He said it with the same quietness with which he had said everything else. It was, to him, a mathematical observation.

The silence that followed was of a different quality from the silences preceding it. Hanno-Melqart looked at the weights on the table. His right hand moved to the second of his gold rings and turned it — slowly, once — and then his eyes lifted to the two men outside, visible through the high window as shadows on the street wall.

Akar-Baal did not turn to follow his gaze. "Before you consider whether the evening has a different resolution available," he said, with the unhurried tone of a man who has already considered it, "I should tell you that I sent a sealed account of the linen records, the weight discrepancies, and the missing tablets from my archive — tablets that a man answering your description removed this morning — to the harbour master's night clerk. It is to be opened and conveyed to the council's senior administrator at the second lamp, unless I collect it myself."

A pause.

"It is now past the first lamp."

Hanno-Melqart's hand stopped on the ring.

The two shadows on the street wall did not move. Whatever signal he might have given, he did not give it.

The room was very quiet. The rope from the harbour creaked. The hull knocked against its mooring stone, patient and irregular, like a man tapping a finger on a table while he waits for the other man to speak first.

Hanno-Melqart spoke first.

"What will the council be told?"

It was the question of a man who has understood that the ground has shifted and is now locating the new boundaries.

"The council asked me to determine whether the accounts with the Egyptian factor were accurate," Akar-Baal said. "I will report that the accounts, on thorough inspection, balanced."

I looked up from my tablets.

Hanno-Melqart looked up from the table.

Akar-Baal reached forward and gathered the false weights back into their linen cloth, folding it with the neat, deliberate movements of a man who is being precise about a mundane task. "The council's concern was the integrity of the trading relationship and the accuracy of the accounts. The accounts are balanced. The relationship has been, in its own fashion, in equilibrium."

"The taxes—" I began.

"Are a matter for the harbour master's records and the Egyptian administration's own auditors, who will find the same discrepancies I found and draw whatever conclusions their procedure requires. That is their function. It is not mine." He stood, carrying the linen bundle. "My function was to examine the accounts."

He looked at Hanno-Melqart for a moment — a look without judgment, without contempt, without warmth.

"Your grandfather was consistent because he had nothing to hide. It is a simpler position." A pause, precise as a cut. "I would encourage a return to it. The margin on false weights is very small. The margin on reputation — across generations — is very large."

He crossed to the door, then turned back — the movement unhurried, almost casual, in the way that only truly deliberate things appear casual.

"The young man from Byblos will receive his bond renewal within three days. This is a description of what will happen, now that the accounts have been examined and found balanced, and now that I hold the terms on which that balance rests."

He left. His sandals were quiet on the stone.

IX.

I sat with Hanno-Melqart in the aftermath. He was looking at the table. I was looking at my notes. The two shadows on the street wall were gone — I could not have said exactly when they had left.

After some time, he turned one of his gold rings around his finger — the slow, unconscious movement of a man revising his understanding of his own position — and stood without speaking.

I went to the door, and then, because I had been carrying the question since the upper market street: "Your man this morning. The one with the scar."

He paused.

"The tablets he took," I said. "Akar-Baal would like them returned to the archive. Undamaged. It is a courtesy, and I mention it because the sealed account at the harbour master's offices contains an itemised description of their removal, and their prompt return would serve the record more cleanly than their absence."

He looked at me — a long look, of a kind I do not encounter often, the look of a man recalibrating the category into which he has placed you. Then he nodded once, and left.

The tablets were on the archive shelf the following morning. I do not know by what arrangement.

X.

I went to the harbour master's offices and collected the sealed envelope before the second lamp. The night clerk handed it back without comment. I carried it home and set it on my own archive shelf, still sealed, where it has remained.

The bond renewal reached Rib-Addi's counting house two days later. I happened to be passing the harbour when he received it; his expression was that of a man who has been told a fever has broken — relief so complete it momentarily resembles its opposite.

I asked him whether he was curious about the resolution. He said he had understood there were accounts that needed examining.

"And they balanced?" he said.

"Yes," I said. "In their own fashion."

He accepted this. He was, as I have noted, the sort of young man who accepts things.

XI.

I asked Akar-Baal, the following morning, to explain his beginning — the point from which everything else had unfolded.

He was sitting with his carnelian beads and a cup of fresh date wine that, for once, he actually drank. Outside, the harbour was making its ordinary noise: rope, hull, the distant shouts of the first watch handing off to the second.

"Djedhor accepted the letter," he said. "An Egyptian factor, in an administrative culture that requires boards of inquiry for discrepancies above the specified weight, accepted a private letter of explanation. A man accepts a private letter instead of demanding his rights under procedure for one of two reasons: he trusts the relationship completely, or he has reason to avoid scrutiny himself."

He turned the cup in his hands.

"Hanno-Melqart is a fourth-generation trader whose grandfather's reputation does not extend to the current holder of the family name — there have been rumours in the Sidonite community for two seasons, which is why our Sidonite competitor brought the matter before the council. Rumours do not survive three generations without cause. Therefore Djedhor's acceptance of the letter was not trust. It was mutual protection." He clicked the beads once. "After that, it was a question of finding the cargo records and doing the arithmetic."

"The incoming linen," I said. "You asked me for the incoming tallies specifically, that first evening. Before you had seen any of the correspondence."

He looked at me with the mild expression of a man who has been asked to confirm something he considers self-evident. "An equation requires two sides. If the outgoing weights were short, and Djedhor had reason not to investigate them, then either Djedhor was simply negligent — which the administrative record does not support, his procedure in all other matters being correct — or he was receiving equivalent consideration from the same arrangement. The question was only what form the consideration took." A pause. "Your family imports linen from Egypt?"

"We are administrative," I said. "Not commercial."

"Yes. You told me that." He drank his wine. "I was noting that you answered without hesitation, which means the question registered correctly."

I sat with this for a moment.

"The removed tablets," I said. "You said he took the wrong material."

"He took the second and third season correspondence — the evidence of his own outgoing fraud, which he already knew existed. He did not touch the harbour master's incoming logs, because he did not know I was looking at the other side of the equation. He revealed, by the specific shape of his concealment, the specific limit of what he understood about the inquiry." The beads moved. "A man who knew the full scope of the investigation would have taken everything, or nothing. He took only what he feared, which was only half of what I held."

He looked up — the expression of a man who finds, in the explanation of a completed thing, something not quite displeasure and not quite satisfaction, but the particular quality of a mind that has finished one problem and is already restless for the next.

"The sealed envelope," I said. "If he had called the men in from the street."

"Then the envelope would have been found and opened, the council would have had a full written account before morning, and Hanno-Melqart would have found that the cost of the evening was considerably higher than the cost of the accounts." He set down his cup. "But men of his type prefer the option to be available rather than exercised. He had not yet moved from the calculation that inconvenience was cheaper than exposure. The envelope changed the arithmetic." He picked up the beads. "He is, in his own way, a consistent man. He merely applies his consistency to the wrong things."

There was, in this observation, something that might have been a dry note of professional sympathy — one precise mind acknowledging the waste of another, bent to inferior purposes.

XII.

The council record of this matter, filed in the administrative archive, reads as follows: The accounts between the house of Hanno-Melqart and the Egyptian factor Djedhor of Memphis were examined by commission. The accounts, on inspection, were found to balance. The inquiry is closed.

This is accurate. I wrote it myself, on instruction.

The full account I have written above for my own records, because someone should know what balance sometimes costs, and what a man sometimes sacrifices in the pursuit of something larger than a single verdict. The harbour requires equilibrium. Equilibrium requires, occasionally, a reckoning that cannot be public — and a man who understands that the most dangerous thing he can carry out of a dim room at midnight is not a weapon but precise knowledge, and the patience to hold it correctly.

Three seasons later, Hanno-Melqart's weights — I checked them myself — matched the council's standard to within the tolerance of honest casting. Whether from fear or from something he heard in that final comment about his grandfather, I cannot say. I suspect it was both, and that the proportions shifted over time, which is perhaps the best resolution one can reasonably expect.

The Egyptian factor Djedhor was removed from his position two seasons later by the Memphis administration, following an internal audit. His successor sent an introductory letter to the Tyrian council that was correct in all its forms and weights.

Akar-Baal read it, nodded once, and returned to his beads.

Abimilki, secretary, in the fourth year of the merchant council's reconstituted record.
