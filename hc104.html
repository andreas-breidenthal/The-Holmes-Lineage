ROBERT HOLMR

~1160 — Halstead Manor, Yorkshire

Robert Holmr was the man who made the family seat. The house at Halstead — a modest stone structure on rising ground above the valley, looking north toward the moors — was built in his time, on land the family had held since before the Conquest but never before formalised into a manor. It was Robert who established the household, organised the demesne, and created the permanent base from which the family would operate for the next three centuries and more.

He was a man of local consequence rather than national ambition — a justice in the manor court, a wool dealer, a neighbour whose opinion was sought in disputes because he was known to weigh evidence before pronouncing. He administered Halstead through the latter years of Henry II’s long reign, when the itinerant justices were bringing the king’s law to every corner of England and the local court system was being formalised into something more durable than it had been. Robert Holmr made himself a useful part of that system, and the reputation it gave him was, in its modest way, the foundation on which everything that followed was built.

The record that preserves this case is kept in the hand of Elias of Bridlington, a lay clerk attached to the itinerant justices. Elias was not a trained jurist but a careful observer, tasked with maintaining the roll of cases heard during the northern circuit. His entries are brief and functional, but in this instance he preserved enough detail of testimony and sequence that the reasoning may be reconstructed. Where his record becomes terse, it is supplemented here only where necessary to maintain the continuity of events as he set them down.

The Death of Martin Woolfold

Set down by Elias of Bridlington, Lay Clerk to the Itinerant Justices of the Northern Circuit, in the Thirty-Second Year of the Reign of King Henry the Second

The body was found at Prime, in the third week after Michaelmas, when the mist off the low ground had not yet burnt away and lay in drifts across the road like wool pulled loose from a fleece. A drove-boy bringing sheep down from the upland pastures near Etton found the man face-down in the verge, one hand stretched toward the ditch as though he had been reaching for something in the moment he fell, the other folded beneath him. The drove-boy, being ten years of age and possessed of more sense than most men twice his years, did not touch the body. He left his sheep to scatter and ran for Beverley.

By the time I arrived with Thomas Bigod, the under-sheriff's man, the body had been turned onto its back by someone — a passing peddler, it was later established, who had the decency to look at the man's face but the self-preservation to keep walking. The face, thus revealed, was known to half the market. Martin Woolfold, who dealt in raw wool from the upland farms between Halstead and the Wolds, who kept a stall in Beverley on Wednesdays and Fridays, who was owed money by seventeen men of the riding and owed money to perhaps ten.

Bigod looked at the body for a considerable time. He was a thorough man, in the manner of those to whom thoroughness is a substitute for thought.

"Robbed," he said at last. "Wool dealers carry coin. Someone knew it."

I noted that the man's purse remained on his belt. I noted, further, that his ring — a modest thing of pewter, but visible enough — remained on his finger, and that his boots, which were good boots and would have fit a man of similar build, remained on his feet. I said nothing of this to Bigod. It was not my place to say it.

The matter might have been settled there, as a robbery gone somehow wrong, or a robbery interrupted, and Martin Woolfold's death recorded as one of those misfortunes the road between market towns dispenses with democratic indifference. But Bigod, knowing that the itinerant justices were due in the riding the following week and that unsolved deaths on the king's highway were a thing the justices noticed with displeasure, sent word to Halstead. He sent it, specifically, to Robert Holmr.

I had not before encountered Robert Holmr. I had heard the name — one heard it, in connexion with the manor court, in connexion with wool dealings and boundary disputes and the quiet settling of local quarrels — and I had formed some impression of a solid, unremarkable man of local consequence. The impression was revised within the first hour.

He came at midmorning, when the mist had thinned enough that the road could be seen in both directions to a reasonable distance. He was not a tall man, but there was a quality of concentration in his movement that made him seem, at first encounter, larger than he was — as though all the space he occupied was fully inhabited, nothing wasted. His eyes were the grey of the sky over the moors when rain is coming, and they moved over the scene in the road with an attention that was, I wrote in my notes that evening, more like reading than looking.

He dismounted without ceremony and went directly to the body, which was laid now in the shelter of a hedgerow, covered with a carter's cloth while we awaited the coroner. He drew back the cloth, and what followed — I set it down precisely, as I witnessed it, trusting that the reader will find in it the same quality of compressed reasoning that I found both instructive and, in its implication, deeply troubling.

He examined the hands first. The left, the one that had been reaching toward the ditch. The right, the one that had been folded beneath the body. He turned the right hand over, and I saw what I had not noticed, which was a smear of blue-grey clay along the outer edge of the palm, under the fingernails. He said nothing. He then examined the boots, and the knees of the man's clothing, and the front of his coat, and I noticed that he was not merely looking but pressing, here and there, as though the fabric might tell him something it had not yet been asked.

He rose. He walked to the ditch and looked into it, and along it, in both directions. He walked back to the road and crouched and looked at the surface of the road itself, which was packed earth, damp from the mist, and which bore the marks of feet and hooves in a confusion that I would have thought unreadable.

He stood. He looked north toward the moors, then south toward Beverley. He appeared, for a moment, to be listening.

"He was not killed here," he said.

Bigod looked at him. "The body was found here."

"The body was found here. He was killed somewhere that has blue-grey clay." He held up a finger before Bigod could speak. "The clay under his nails is not road clay. Road clay here is red-brown. The blue-grey variety occurs, as far as I am aware, in one place within a half-day's walk of this road: the bank of the stream that runs along the northern edge of the Woolfold common." He paused. "Which is, I should note, also the boundary in dispute between Geoffrey Woulforde and the Abbey of Meaux."

The silence that followed this had a particular quality — the quality of a thing everyone in the vicinity had known but no one had been prepared to say aloud.

"Woolfold common," Bigod said carefully. "And Martin Woolfold—"

"Was Martin Woolfold of Halstead, cousin to the Woulforde family, and the man whose testimony regarding the position of the boundary stones before the flood of fifteen years ago was to have been heard before the itinerant justices—" Robert Holmr looked at me steadily— "next Wednesday."

I should set down here what I learned of the matter in the days following, so that the reasoning of what came next may be understood in its full sequence.

The boundary dispute between Geoffrey Woulforde and the Abbey of Meaux had been in process for seven years. The abbey claimed that the stream represented the true boundary and that the grazing land north of the stream had been granted to them in the time of the old king. Geoffrey Woulforde claimed that the boundary was marked by stones laid before living memory, that the stream had shifted in the great flood of his father's time, and that the abbey's claim was founded on a lie. The case had been referred twice and deferred twice. It was now, at last, to be heard.

Martin Woolfold had been a boy of fifteen at the time of the flood. He had been present, according to his own accounts repeated at market and in the alehouse and wherever wool dealers gather to speak of local matters, when his father had walked the boundary with the old abbey cellarer and agreed, by handshake, that the pre-flood position of the stones would be acknowledged on both sides regardless of where the stream now ran. This agreement had been informal. There was no written record. Martin Woolfold's memory was the only surviving evidence for it.

Geoffrey Woulforde stood to lose, should the abbey's claim succeed, approximately one hundred and forty acres of upland grazing. He was not a rich man. He was, by the account of those who knew him, a man who had invested his entire material condition in the outcome of this case.

His son, Aldric Woulforde, was twenty-three years old and had been in Beverley on the night before the body was found.

Robert Holmr went to the Beverley market on the Thursday. He went not as Robert Holmr of Halstead, whose face was known, but — and I confess I would not have believed this if I had not seen it — as a wool buyer from the east, who wore a different coat and carried himself with the slight forward hunch of a man unused to long distances, and spoke with the flat vowels of the fen country. I was with him, in my capacity as clerk, though he introduced me only as his servant and suggested I keep my mouth closed, which I did.

He spoke to three men at the wool market.

The first was Edmund Thatcher, a staple merchant who dealt with Martin Woolfold on a regular basis. From Edmund Thatcher, by indirection and apparent casual interest, he learned that Woolfold had been approached three weeks prior by a man wishing to purchase a quantity of the lower-grade fleece at well above the market price — an unusual transaction, conducted in private, and that Woolfold had declined it. The man who had made the offer had been well-dressed and young, and had given no name.

The second was a widow who kept a stall selling threads and ribbons near the wool-weighing station, who had eyes for everything that happened within twenty yards of her position and who remembered, because she remembered everything, that Aldric Woulforde had been at the market the previous Friday, in conversation with Martin Woolfold, and that the conversation had ended with Woolfold walking away quickly and Woulforde standing still with his hands at his sides in a manner she described as like a man trying not to strike out.

The third was a Flemish cloth buyer who occupied a room above the inn where Aldric Woulforde had stayed the night before the body was found. The Flemish buyer had not slept well. He had heard, in the night, a man leave the inn yard at an hour he estimated as two hours before dawn. He had heard the horse return at an hour he estimated as just before Prime. He had not thought it worth mentioning to anyone because he had assumed, reasonably, that it was the business of a man who wished to travel in the dark to travel in the dark.

Robert Holmr thanked him pleasantly, bought a length of cloth he did not need, and we left the market.

On the road back to Halstead he was, for a long time, silent. This silence was, I had begun to understand, of a different quality from ordinary quiet — it was the silence of a man doing something vigorous in a place I could not see. His horse walked; his hands held the reins; his eyes looked at the road ahead; and none of these things were, apparently, what occupied him.

"The offer of money for the fleece," he said at last. "You noted the significance?"

I admitted I had not, entirely.

"An attempt to purchase Woolfold's absence from the hearing, dressed as commerce and thus deniable. When Woolfold declined — and declined, I think, with some firmness, or perhaps even with the suggestion that he found the offer itself informative — the calculation changed. The testimony could not be bought. It could only be prevented." He was quiet for a moment. "The method chosen tells us something about the man who chose it. He went himself. He did not hire it done. That is either pride, or economy, or both. It is also the decision of a young man who has not yet learned that the thing you do yourself is the thing that leaves the most evidence of yourself."

"And the clay," I said.

"The clay tells us where he was standing when he did it. The road tells us which direction he came from and returned to, if one knows what marks a nervous horse makes when it is held too tightly. The time tells us that he was back in his room before the drove-boy found the body and raised the alarm." He glanced at me with something that was not quite amusement. "Write it down as I tell it to you. Write all of it. It is the sequence that will matter before the justices, not any single piece."

I will not set down the full hearing before the itinerant court, as it is a matter of the official record which speaks for itself in its own terminology. I will record only what was said at Halstead, in the hall of the Holmr house, the evening before.

Bigod was there, and his superior, the under-sheriff's deputy whose name I have been asked not to record, and Robert Holmr, and I. The matter of Aldric Woulforde was laid out in the sequence Robert Holmr had described to me on the road. Bigod listened. The under-sheriff's deputy listened more carefully. When it was done, the deputy asked the question that I had been waiting for him to ask.

"This is sequence and testimony," he said. "There is no witness to the act itself."

"There is no witness who saw the blow struck," Robert Holmr agreed. "There is a witness who can place him in Beverley the night before. There is a witness who heard a horse leave before dawn and return before Prime. There is a witness who can testify that Woolfold declined an offer of money made by a young man of the same description shortly before the hearing was to be held. There is clay on a dead man's hand that does not belong to the road where he was found. And there is the fact—" he paused, and I noticed that he allowed the pause precisely long enough— "that I examined the horse Aldric Woulforde rode into Beverley, which is stabled at the inn, and that the animal has a worn shoe on the right foreleg that leaves a mark I can show you in the road clay two miles north of where the body was found."

The under-sheriff's deputy looked at him for a moment. "You examined his horse."

"I was curious about the horse." A brief pause. "I am frequently curious about horses."

Aldric Woulforde was taken the following morning, before the court sat. He did not confess immediately, but he confessed on the second day, when the clay evidence and the horse's shoe were presented and when it became apparent that the Flemish cloth buyer had agreed, on reflection and with some encouragement from the court, to testify to what he had heard. He was tried before the itinerant justices, convicted on the evidence as laid, and hanged at York in the fortnight before Advent.

Geoffrey Woulforde, his father, was not charged. The boundary dispute was heard without the testimony of Martin Woolfold and decided, on the documentary evidence that remained, in favour of the abbey. He lost the hundred and forty acres. I do not know what became of him afterward.

The official record of the court credits the resolution of Martin Woolfold's death to the diligence of the under-sheriff's office and the operation of the king's justice in the northern riding. This is accurate. It is also, as a complete account, somewhat thin.

I have kept these notes separately, in my own hand, as a record of the sequence of reasoning that I believe should not be entirely lost. Robert Holmr of Halstead will not object to this; I asked him, on the road home, whether he wished me to record anything of the matter, and he said only that a careful clerk was worth more than a clever advocate, and that I should write down what I had observed with as much precision as I could manage, and not concern myself with what the official record required.

He said one other thing, which I have thought about since, and which I set down here because I think it is the truest thing in this account.

We were passing the place on the road where the body had been found — the verge was still disturbed, the ditch-grass still flattened where the peddler had moved him — and I said that I found it a melancholy thing, that a man had died to prevent a truth being told, and that in the end the truth he carried had died with him and made no difference to the outcome.

Robert Holmr looked at the verge for a moment as we passed.

"The truth he carried determined everything," he said. "It determined that he had to be killed. The testimony perished; the fact of its existence did not. A man who has no dangerous knowledge is not worth silencing." He turned back to the road ahead. "His death was the record of what he knew. I only read it."

Here ends the account of the death of Martin Woolfold as preserved in the notes of Elias of Bridlington, clerk. The official roll of the northern circuit for the thirty-second year of Henry II records the conviction and execution of Aldric Woulforde, son of Geoffrey of that name, for murder on the king's highway. No further notation.
