ILI-IDDINAM

~1900 BC — Haran / Babylon, Mesopotamia

His name was the mixed thing his generation was — Amorite and Akkadian wound together, the name of the new dynasty that was rising in Babylon grafted onto the family’s Mesopotamian trunk. Ili-iddinam moved south from Haran to Babylon in early adulthood, following the Amorite dynasty’s consolidation of power in the great city on the Euphrates, reading in its rise the same signal his ancestor had read in the rise of Ur: this is where the world is organising itself, and this is therefore where a man of ability should be.

Babylon under the early Amorite kings was a city in the process of becoming what it would be under Hammurabi — the legal and commercial capital of the known world, the place where the great synthesis of Sumerian tradition and Amorite energy was producing something genuinely new. Ili-iddinam arrived as a scribe and rose quickly to become a man of property and local consequence, having applied his family’s gifts to the trade dealings of a city in its rising influence with the careful calculation of someone who understood that timing — the reading of when a place or a situation is about to become what it will be — was the essential prerequisite of every other form of intelligence.

Among the tablets that record these years is one kept by Nanna-iddin, a junior scribe in the service of the same house that employed Ili-iddinam in his early years. Nanna-iddin wrote in the careful hand of one who must account for every mark before it dries in the clay, and his record survives because he was thorough in copying contracts and because, in one particular season, he was asked to observe more than he understood. His account, preserved in a bundle of administrative tablets, does not praise Ili-iddinam but records what was done, and what was seen, and what was asked.

The Account Of Nanna-Iddin

Set down in the third year of Ili-iddinam’s tenure in that house, during the reign of Sumu-la-El of Babylon

I had been at my tablet since the first light, copying a grain-loan contract for the third time because my first impression of the reed had been too shallow and the second too deep, when the gate-warden called up from the street below that a man was dead in the counting house of Imgur-Adad the merchant, and that his apprentice had run through the Zababa quarter screaming like a temple-slave who has seen an omen. The smell the morning wind carried was not the smell of the canal, which we knew, nor of the slaughter-yard two streets east, which we also knew. It was the smell of spilled cedar oil mixed with something beneath it — something that had no name I wished to give it then.

Ili-iddinam was on the roof.

He spent the first hour of every morning on the roof of our house, not in prayer, as the household assumed, but in what I had learned by then was a form of active stillness — his eyes moving across the rooflines of Babylon the way a man reads a tablet he has nearly memorised, checking for what has changed, what is missing, what has been impressed too hard and is beginning to crack. He was already descending the stairs when I reached the bottom of them, his outer garment thrown over one shoulder and his reed case under his arm, as if he had been waiting for the gate-warden’s voice rather than startled by it.

“Imgur-Adad,” he said, not to me, but to himself, in the particular tone he used when he was adding a quantity to a calculation he had already begun.

“You know him?” I asked.

He looked at me briefly — that look, which I had not yet learned to interpret, which seemed to measure not my question but the gap between what I had noticed and what I had not. “I know his contracts,” he said. “Come.”

The counting house of Imgur-Adad occupied the ground floor of a mudbrick building on the Street of the Seal-Cutters, wedged between a workshop that produced cylinder seals for the great temple administrators and a narrow house of no apparent commercial purpose whose owner, I would later learn, Ili-iddinam had noted with interest some months before. The morning light had not yet reached the street properly; the buildings on the eastern side threw long shadows that made the lane feel like the passage between two cliff faces, and the canal water that seeped beneath the western foundations had left a dark tide-mark along the base of every wall.

Imgur-Adad lay on his side behind his counting table, one arm extended as if reaching for the nearest of the three clay tablets that lay on the table’s surface, his seal-ring still on his finger, the lamp beside him burned down to cold oil and ash. The lamp was the first thing Ili-iddinam examined. He crouched beside it and placed two fingers above the wick without touching it, then turned to look at the small high window that admitted the only outside air.

“The window,” he said, “faces north. The lamp burned through the night. A man who burns a lamp all night in a sealed counting house is a man who was working when he did not expect to be interrupted.”

Ku-Nannum, the officer of the watch who had arrived before us and who resented, as I would learn all such officers resented, the presence of Ili-iddinam at any scene he was called to examine, looked up from his own inspection of the body with an expression I can only describe as professionally unimpressed. “He is dead,” Ku-Nannum said. “Whether he worked at night or day is no question for the judge. The question is who entered.”

“The question is who entered, yes,” Ili-iddinam agreed pleasantly, and said nothing further for some time.

He moved around the table with the slow precision of a scribe approaching a tablet he has been told contains an error — certain that the error is there, uncertain only of where the reed slipped. He examined the tablets on the table’s surface without touching them, reading them, I understood, as documents and as objects simultaneously. He examined the door and its bolt. He examined the merchant’s hands, which he turned over gently and held up toward the north window’s light. He examined the floor near the cedar-oil lamp, where a thin stain had spread and dried, and crouched there for a long time.

Ku-Nannum, whose patience was that of a man who has stood in administrative lines his entire professional life and developed his patience as a fortification rather than a virtue, cleared his throat with significant authority. “The apprentice says his master had enemies,” he said. “A rival grain-merchant, Ea-gamil, who brought complaint against Imgur-Adad before the assembly only last month. I am told the assembly found against Ea-gamil and that he departed in a state of rage that the assembly scribe will confirm.”

“Ea-gamil,” Ili-iddinam said, without looking up from the floor.

“He is the obvious—”

“Yes,” Ili-iddinam said. “He is obvious.” He straightened, and the quality of his attention shifted in a way that always made me feel I was watching a man emerge from deep water — the slow return of focus to the world that was visibly present rather than the world he had been examining inside his own reasoning. He looked at the three tablets on the counting table. “You have read these?”

Ku-Nannum had not read them, because Ku-Nannum was not a scribe. He said so with the dignity of a man who considered this a professional division of labour rather than a limitation.

“I ask,” Ili-iddinam said, “because they are the whole of the matter.”

What followed — and here I must confess that Ili-iddinam required me to be present, which at the time I understood as clerical utility and later came to understand as something else entirely, as the need that certain extraordinary minds have for a fixed point, a known quantity, a face that represents the ordinary intelligence of the world they must translate their findings into — what followed was an explanation that proceeded with the slow, deliberate patience of a teacher who knows the student has not yet earned the full answer but who intends to arrive there by stages.

He laid the three tablets before us on the table and indicated each in turn.

“Imgur-Adad was party to three contracts that remained unsealed as of yesterday morning,” he said. “This one — a silver loan to a ship-owner, Awil-Marduk, secured against his next shipment from the south — was due for its first interest payment in seven days. It required only the parties’ seals and could be closed at any time. This one —” he moved to the second tablet “— a grain-storage agreement with the temple of Ishtar, through their representative administrator, required the co-seal of that administrator, who was available only on appointed days, the next of which was three days hence. And this one —” the third tablet “— a property surety lodged by a man named Warad-Sin in exchange for Imgur-Adad’s guarantee of his ability to meet his obligations before the city assembly.”

He paused, in the manner of a man who is certain the next step is visible to his audience and is waiting, not unkindly, for them to take it.

I did not take it.

Ku-Nannum did not take it.

Ili-iddinam showed no surprise at this. “Warad-Sin’s surety,” he continued, “was contingent on a clause I will show you here —” and he touched the relevant line with the tip of his reed, not pressing, not marking “— which specified that the guarantee would become void in the event of Imgur-Adad’s death before the seal-breaking of the first commercial day following the deposit of the surety tablet. The surety was deposited four days ago. The first commercial seal-breaking of the day occurs at sunrise. Imgur-Adad died —” he placed the two fingers above the lamp’s dead wick again, this time looking at us so we would understand the gesture “— some hours before dawn.”

The room had changed. Not in fact, but in the way a room changes when the thing that is wrong in it has been named.

“Warad-Sin,” I said, and heard my own voice as if I was listening to a scribe reading aloud a contract he had not written.

“Warad-Sin,” Ili-iddinam confirmed, “had deposited a surety he could not pay. He had done so knowing that if Imgur-Adad guaranteed him before the assembly and he subsequently defaulted, Imgur-Adad would pursue him to the value of his house, his inventory, and the labour of his sons. He could not meet the payment that would become due in eighteen days. He could not withdraw the surety without disgrace and the loss of every credit relationship in Babylon. But if Imgur-Adad died before the morning’s seal-breaking, the clause rendered the surety void. It was, from Warad-Sin’s position, a very precisely timed problem.”

“Ea-gamil had more obvious reason,” Ku-Nannum said, but his voice had taken on the quality of a man who argues a position he is no longer certain of, out of professional obligation rather than conviction.

“Ea-gamil,” Ili-iddinam said, “has a reason that everyone in the Zababa quarter knows, which means it is a reason that someone who intended to commit this act would wish to be known. Ea-gamil was also, as I happen to know because I reviewed a tablet record of debts for another client of this house two months ago, recently extended a significant loan by the temple administrators — a loan which provides him every incentive to remain respectable, quiet, and outside the attention of the judge. Angry men who make threats before assembly secretaries are rarely the men who act. They have spent their anger in the spending of words.”

He picked up his reed case. He looked at the extended hand of Imgur-Adad, still reaching toward the nearest tablet, with an expression that was, for one moment, not entirely mathematical.

“Warad-Sin,” he said, “will have oil on his sandal. Cedar oil, from this lamp —” the dark stain near the table’s foot “— which was knocked in the struggle and which the killer did not notice because the room was dark and because a man who is killing for the first time does not notice what he has stepped in. You will find the sandal. You will find it has not been cleaned because he had no reason to anticipate scrutiny and because his mind, since last night, has been occupied with survival rather than evidence.”

Ku-Nannum was already moving toward the door.

They found the sandal.

They found Warad-Sin with it still on his foot, sitting in his own counting house three streets away, working through his own tablets with the furious concentration of a man trying to perform normal life until normal life becomes real again.

I know, because I was required to attend as a witness to the reading of the relevant contract clauses before the junior judge, and I saw his face when Ili-iddinam entered the room behind Ku-Nannum. There was no surprise in it. There was something worse than surprise — recognition, the particular recognition of a man who has understood, in the interval since the act, that somewhere in the city there is a mind that would have known to look at the tablets first, and who had spent three streets and three hours wondering who that mind belonged to.

Ili-iddinam said nothing to him. He spoke only to the junior judge, and only about contracts, and only in the dry, precise language of clause and counter-clause that was his natural tongue. Ku-Nannum received the public acknowledgment, as was appropriate. The junior judge’s record names the city guard’s diligence. The temple of Ishtar’s administrator, reviewing the case months later, complimented the efficiency of the system.

I kept my own account, as I was in the habit of keeping, in the margins of the administrative tablets I was copying at the time, because the clay dries quickly and because some things, if they are not pressed into the record in the moment, leave no mark at all.

The cedar-oil smell stayed in the Street of the Seal-Cutters for three days. I know because I walked past it, those mornings, and Ili-iddinam walked past it also, on his way to and from the roof, and never once looked toward it. He had, I had begun to understand, no further use for a problem once it was solved. The finished tablet went to the archive. The question that had animated him was gone, and what remained in its place was — for a day, perhaps two — a particular quietness in his face, a stillness that was not peace but the absence of sufficient occupation, a lamp in a room with nothing yet to illuminate.

Three days later a new client arrived from Nippur with a question about a missing grain shipment, and the stillness was gone as if it had never been.

I recorded that too.

Here the account ends. The tablets containing Nanna-iddin’s marginal notations were recovered from a private archive in the ruins near the old Zababa quarter. The main administrative tablets of the house survive in the standard temple and palace collections of the period. The cedar-oil stain on the Street of the Seal-Cutters is long gone, but the contracts remain.
