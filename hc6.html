ILI-IDDINAM

~1900 BC — Babylon, Mesopotamia

THE ACCOUNT OF NANNA-IDDIN

Set down in the third year of Ili-iddinam's tenure in that house, during the reign of Sumu-la-El of Babylon

I had been at my tablet since the first light, copying a grain-loan contract for the third time — my first impression of the reed too shallow, the second too deep — when the gate-warden called up from the street below that a man was dead in the counting house of Imgur-Adad the merchant, and that his apprentice had run through the Zababa quarter screaming like a temple-slave who has seen an omen. The smell the morning wind carried was not the smell of the canal, which we knew, nor of the slaughter-yard two streets east, which we also knew. It was the smell of spilled cedar oil mixed with something beneath it — something that had no name I wished to give it then.

Ili-iddinam was already on the stairs.

He spent the first hour of every morning on the roof of our house, not in prayer, as the household assumed, but in what I had learned was a form of active stillness — his eyes moving across the rooflines of Babylon the way a man reads a tablet he has nearly memorised, checking for what has changed, what is missing, what has been impressed too hard and is beginning to crack. Yet he was descending before the gate-warden had finished speaking, his outer garment thrown over one shoulder and his reed case under his arm, as if the news had confirmed something he had already half-expected rather than arrived as a surprise.

"Imgur-Adad," he said, not to me, but to himself, in the particular tone he used when he was adding a quantity to a calculation he had already begun.

"You know him?" I asked, hurrying to match his pace.

He looked at me briefly — that look, which I had not yet learned to interpret, which seemed to measure not my question but the gap between what I had noticed and what I had not. "I know his contracts," he said. "Come."

The counting house of Imgur-Adad occupied the ground floor of a mudbrick building on the Street of the Seal-Cutters, wedged between a workshop that produced cylinder seals for the great temple administrators and a narrow house of no apparent commercial purpose whose owner, I would later learn, Ili-iddinam had noted with interest some months before. The morning light had not yet reached the street properly; the buildings on the eastern side threw long shadows that made the lane feel like the passage between two cliff faces, and the canal water that seeped beneath the western foundations had left a dark tide-mark along the base of every wall.

The apprentice — a boy of perhaps fourteen seasons, whose screaming had subsided into a rigid, dry-eyed stillness that was somehow worse — sat against the outer wall, and would not look at the door.

"He was expecting a friend," the boy whispered to the damp bricks, his voice entirely hollow. "He told me to leave the lamp filled and go."

Ili-iddinam paused, his eyes registering the detail with a brief, sharp nod, before stepping over the threshold.

Imgur-Adad lay on his side behind his counting table, one arm extended as if reaching for the nearest of the three clay tablets that lay on the table's surface, his seal-ring still on his finger, the lamp beside him burned down to cold oil and ash. The lamp was the first thing Ili-iddinam examined. He crouched beside it and placed two fingers above the wick without touching it, then turned to look at the small high window that admitted the only outside air.

"The window faces north," he said. "The lamp burned through the night. A man who burns a lamp all night in a sealed counting house is a man engaged in deep work. Yet, as the boy outside has just told us, he was expecting a friend. He admitted this visitor without alarm, and was killed before the work was finished."

Ku-Nannum, the officer of the watch who had arrived before us and who resented, as I would learn all such officers resented, the presence of Ili-iddinam at any scene he was called to examine, looked up from his inspection of the body with an expression I can only describe as professionally unimpressed. "He is dead. The question is who entered. Do not waste your time on mere clerical waste, scribe."

"The question is who entered, yes," Ili-iddinam agreed pleasantly, and said nothing further for some time.

He moved around the table with the slow precision of a scribe approaching a tablet he has been told contains an error — certain that the error is there, uncertain only of where the reed slipped. He examined the door and its bolt. He examined the merchant's hands, which he turned over gently and held up toward the north window's light, reading the marks there with a focused attention that made me look away. He examined the floor near the cedar-oil lamp, where a thin stain had spread and dried, crouching there for a long time, not moving.

Then he went to the three tablets on the table.

He read them without touching them for some moments, moving only his eyes. Then, with the careful two-fingered grip of a man handling another's work, he lifted the third tablet and held it to the window's light, reading aloud — not for Ku-Nannum, I understood, but for me, because I could follow the clauses and because he needed a mind to speak them into.

"A property surety," he said, "lodged by a man named Warad-Sin in exchange for Imgur-Adad's guarantee of his creditworthiness before the city assembly." He moved his reed tip along the lines without marking them. "Here — clause seven. The guarantee of the surety-holder shall be considered void and without obligation in the event of the surety-holder's death before the seal-breaking of the first commercial day following the deposit of this tablet." He set the tablet down. He set it down carefully, as if it were fragile, which it was not. "The surety was deposited four days ago," he said. "The first commercial seal-breaking of the day occurs at sunrise. Imgur-Adad died —" he placed the two fingers above the lamp's dead wick again, this time looking at us so we would understand the gesture "— some hours before dawn."

The room had changed. Not in fact, but in the way a room changes when the thing that is wrong in it has been named.

Ku-Nannum, whose patience was that of a man who has stood in administrative lines his entire professional life and developed patience as fortification rather than virtue, cleared his throat. "The apprentice says his master had enemies. A rival grain-merchant, Ea-gamil, who brought complaint before the assembly only last month. The assembly found against Ea-gamil. He departed in a rage the assembly scribe will confirm."

"Ea-gamil has a reason that everyone in the Zababa quarter knows," Ili-iddinam said. "Which means it is a reason that a man who intended this act would wish to be known. Ea-gamil was also —" he paused, and I saw his attention briefly inhabit a different room entirely, some interior space where the records of two months' previous work were stored "— recently extended a significant loan by the temple administrators. Such a loan provides every incentive to remain respectable, quiet, and outside the attention of the judge. Angry men who make threats before assembly secretaries are rarely the men who act. They have spent their anger in the spending of words."

"Warad-Sin," I said, and heard my own voice as if I was listening to a scribe read aloud a contract he had not written.

"Warad-Sin," Ili-iddinam confirmed. "He deposited a surety he could not pay. He knew that if he defaulted, Imgur-Adad would pursue him to the value of his house, his inventory, and the labour of his sons. He could not withdraw the surety without disgrace. But if Imgur-Adad died before this morning's first seal-breaking, clause seven rendered the guarantee void." He looked at the dark stain on the floor, where the cedar-oil lamp had been knocked and had spilled in the struggle and had dried. "The killer stood here. He would not have seen what he stepped in. A room this dark, a mind this occupied with survival." He looked up. "We will not find Warad-Sin at his house."

"Then we have nothing," Ku-Nannum said.

"We have a tablet," Ili-iddinam said, "and we have a morning."

What followed was not, as I had half-expected, a pursuit through the streets of Babylon. It was more precise than that, and considerably more cold-blooded.

Ili-iddinam sent me.

He wrote a message himself, on a small, unbaked lump of clay, the surface still damp. He used the hastily scratched, cramped hand he reserved for private communications rather than the careful administrative script he used for everything else. He handed it to me and told me the address of the counting house of Warad-Sin, three streets west of the Street of the Seal-Cutters, and said I was to present myself as a runner from the temple of Ishtar's administrative office and deliver the tablet and await a reply.

The message, which I read as I walked — because I was young, and frightened, and it was my name on the tablet's surface should anything go wrong — said the following: The three tablets from the counting house of Imgur-Adad, deceased, are being conveyed this morning to the temple archive for official seal-breaking and inventory. The surety tablet lodged under the name of Warad-Sin will be presented to the assembly judge at the third hour.

I did not, at the time, understand what this would cause. I understood it as I stood in the doorway of Warad-Sin's counting house and watched his face read the message.

He was a broad man, built like a man who had done physical work before he had done commercial work, with hands that were thicker than a scribe's and eyes that were — I have searched for the word across the years since that morning — not afraid, exactly, but calculating in the manner of a man who has discovered he has been given one more move in a game he believed already finished. He read the damp tablet twice. He set it on the table. He looked at me with an attention that was not the attention of a man reading a runner.

"Who sent this?" he asked.

"The temple office, sir," I said. My voice, to my considerable relief, remained level.

"The temple office," he repeated, and then he stood, and in standing he picked up, from beside the table's leg and with the naturalness of a man reaching for a familiar tool, a cedar-wood club of the kind used by the guards of grain warehouses. Not raising it. Not threatening with it. Simply holding it, in the way that men who are accustomed to resolving problems with their bodies hold the instruments of resolution, and looking at me with those calculating eyes.

"You are not from the temple office," he said.

I will record, for the completeness of this account, that I did not run. I am not certain whether this was courage or the simple physical fact that my legs had stopped accepting instructions from my mind. I stood in the doorway, which is perhaps why I am still alive to set down these words, because a man who stands in a doorway is a man who is not entirely inside the room, and Warad-Sin, who was a calculating man even in extremity, understood that a runner dead in a doorway is a runner who has been seen from the street.

Ili-iddinam appeared at my left shoulder.

He had followed at a distance I had not perceived, with Ku-Nannum's two guards behind him, and he came into the doorway's frame and stood beside me and looked at Warad-Sin across the table with an expression that was entirely, composedly still — the expression, I understood later, of a man who has engineered every element of the present moment and is now simply watching it complete.

"The tablets," Ili-iddinam said, "are not being moved anywhere. The message was mine. I wished to know whether you would attempt to flee or attempt to destroy the evidence. You have done neither." He regarded the club in Warad-Sin's hand with the mild, attentive interest of a scholar encountering an unexpected variant. "That is informative."

Warad-Sin set the club down.

Not because the guards had moved — they had not, yet — but because he was a calculating man, and he had calculated, in the interval between one breath and the next, what the presence of Ili-iddinam meant. The recognition in his face was the same as a man who has been assembling a tablet in the dark and who, when the lamp is lit, sees that the impression he believed he had hidden is plainly visible to anyone with eyes.

"The cedar oil," he said, not as a question.

"Among other things," Ili-iddinam said. He lowered his gaze and pointed the tip of his reed stylus toward Warad-Sin's left foot. "The dark, unmistakable stain of it on the edge of your sandal, which you have not yet had the time or the light to scrape clean. And the clause itself, which told me everything except the name, and the name, which your loan's terms told me, and the lamp, which told me the hour."

Warad-Sin said nothing further. He sat down, with the heaviness of a man whose legs have made the decision that his mind has not yet reached, and he looked at the surety tablet on the table — the message that had not come from the temple, the trap he had walked into, the morning that had not gone the way the night was supposed to buy him — and he looked at it with an expression that was not grief and not defiance and not quite despair, but something that existed between all three, in the space where a man understands that the calculation he staked his life upon was seen and countered before he had finished making it.

Ku-Nannum's guards entered then. The rest belongs to the official record, which is thorough and accurate as far as it goes and which does not, at any point, mention Ili-iddinam by name.

I saw Warad-Sin's face when Ili-iddinam entered that doorway — the particular recognition of a man who has understood, in the interval since the act, that somewhere in the city there is a mind that would have known to look at the tablets first, and who had spent three streets and three hours hoping it belonged to no one who would act on it.

He had hoped wrong.

Ku-Nannum received the public acknowledgment, as was appropriate. The junior judge's record names the city guard's diligence. The temple of Ishtar's administrator, reviewing the case months later, complimented the efficiency of the system.

Ili-iddinam said nothing to Warad-Sin except what was necessary to complete the trap, and nothing to Ku-Nannum except what was necessary to hand the case to its proper authority, and nothing to me, that afternoon, except to ask whether I had noted down the relevant clause from the surety tablet before Ku-Nannum's men took it into evidence.

I had. He nodded, once, as if this confirmed something he had already assumed, and went to the roof.

The cedar-oil smell stayed in the Street of the Seal-Cutters for three days. I know because I walked past it, those mornings, and Ili-iddinam walked past it also, on his way to and from the roof, and never once looked toward it. He had, I had begun to understand, no further use for a problem once it was solved. The finished tablet went to the archive. The question that had animated him was gone, and what remained in its place was — for a day, perhaps two — a particular quietness in his face, a stillness that was not peace but the absence of sufficient occupation, a lamp in a room with nothing yet to illuminate.

But I have thought of that morning, across the years since, more than he likely has. I have thought of Imgur-Adad's extended hand and the tablet it could not quite reach. I have thought of Warad-Sin sitting in his counting house with a club beside his chair, running the numbers of his own survival with the same thoroughness he had run every calculation that had led him there. I have thought of the clause he had discovered — the precise, elegant contractual mechanism that had seemed to offer him a solution — and of how a solution that requires the death of the man who stands between you and ruin is not, in the end, the kind of solution from which a man returns unchanged. He had not returned unchanged. That much was visible.

Three days later a new client arrived from Nippur with a desperate question about a missing grain shipment. The lethargy vanished in an instant. Ili-iddinam sat forward, his eyes sharpening, his posture snapping taut like a hound catching a fresh scent. The particular quietness in his face was gone as if it had never been. The hunt had begun anew.

I recorded that too.

Here the account ends. The tablets containing Nanna-iddin's marginal notations were recovered from a private archive in the ruins near the old Zababa quarter. The main administrative tablets of the house survive in the standard temple and palace collections of the period. The cedar-oil stain on the Street of the Seal-Cutters is long gone, but the contracts remain.
