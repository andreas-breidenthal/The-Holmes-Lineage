ILI-IDDINAM

~1900 BC — Babylon, Mesopotamia

The Street of the Seal-Cutters

Translated from the Akkadian script,
as set down by the hand of Nanna-iddin

I

The boy came before the light.

He came running, which is not how boys come to the house of a scribe at the hour when the canal-birds are only beginning to stir, and he came alone, which is not how boys come when the news they carry is ordinary. I heard him strike the gate with both palms rather than the measured knock of a messenger, and I heard, beneath the cedar-and-damp smell of the early morning, something else — the smell of cedar oil, thick and spilled and wrong, as if a lamp had been overturned not in an accident but in a struggle, and beneath that a second thing I could not then name, something that clung to the boy's outer garment as he stood gasping in our doorway and told us, in a voice that had been hollowed out by what it had already said several times in the last half-hour, that his master was dead.

Ili-iddinam was already descending the roof stairs.

He spent the first hour of every morning above the city, not in prayer as the household supposed, but in what I had learned to recognise as a form of engaged waiting — his gaze moving across the rooflines of Babylon the way a careful scribe reads a tablet for the error he has been told is there, certain of its presence, uncertain only of where the reed had slipped. Yet he had heard the boy's palms strike the gate from three floors above, and he came down with his outer garment already thrown across one shoulder and his reed case under his arm, as if the sound had confirmed something he had been half-expecting rather than surprised him.

He stopped on the bottom step and looked at the boy.

"Your name," he said.

"Imgur-Sin, sir." The boy's voice was the voice of someone who has passed through terror into a country beyond it, where everything is very still and very clear.

"Your master's name was Imgur-Adad," Ili-iddinam said — not asking.

"Yes, sir."

"How long have you served him?"

"Four years, sir. Since I was ten."

Something shifted in Ili-iddinam's face — not softness, precisely, but an attention that was different from the attention he gave to documents. He looked at the boy for a moment, reading something in his stillness, his dry eyes, the way he held his arms slightly away from his body as though his own garments had become unfamiliar to him.

"He was a good master," Ili-iddinam said. It was not a question. It was the acknowledgment of a fact he had inferred and was confirming aloud so the boy would understand it had been seen.

Imgur-Sin said nothing. He looked at the ground and breathed carefully.

"When did you find him?"

"Before first light. I had come to fill the lamp for the day. He — he always wanted the lamp filled at first light. He said a counting house that begins the day in darkness begins it badly."

"He was particular about his lamps," Ili-iddinam said.

"Yes, sir. Very particular." The boy swallowed. "He had me polish the bronze of the stand every new moon."

Ili-iddinam nodded once, with an expression I had not seen on his face before — the expression, I thought then and believe still, of a man who is adding to a calculation not only a set of facts but a weight that facts alone cannot carry. He looked past the boy into the lightening street, where the canal-birds were beginning their morning argument and the cedar-oil smell hung in the air with the particular persistence of something that does not belong to a morning.

"Where is he?" he asked.

"The counting house. The Street of the Seal-Cutters."

"Show me."

II

I had passed the counting house of Imgur-Adad many times without looking at it with any care, as one passes the houses of men who have not yet acquired significance. It occupied the ground floor of a mudbrick building wedged between a workshop producing cylinder seals for the great temple administrators and, on its western side, a narrow house of no apparent commercial purpose whose owner Ili-iddinam had once mentioned, with a brevity that signalled interest, in connection with a dispute about a disputed land boundary some months before. The canal water that seeped beneath the western foundations had left a dark tide-mark along the base of every wall, and the morning light had not yet reached the street properly; the buildings on the eastern side threw long shadows that made the lane feel less like a commercial thoroughfare than like the corridor between two cliff faces, a place where the city's noise arrived late and muffled, and where the cedar-oil smell was strongest.

Ku-Nannum, the officer of the watch, had arrived before us. He stood just inside the doorway in the posture of a man who has learned, through long administrative experience, that the surest way to appear to be in command of a situation is to stand very still within it and say as little as possible until the situation resolves itself through the efforts of others. He regarded Ili-iddinam's arrival with the expression of a man who has been given a task he finds straightforward and presented with assistance he finds unnecessary.

"Natural causes," he said, before we had crossed the threshold. "Old man. Sealed room. The lamp burned out." He paused. "The apprentice's screaming was excessive."

Ili-iddinam did not respond to this. He stepped past Ku-Nannum into the counting house.

Imgur-Adad lay on his side behind the counting table, one arm extended as if reaching for the nearest of the three clay tablets arranged on the table's surface. His seal-ring was still on his finger. The bronze lamp beside him — polished, I noticed, to a high shine that was incongruous with the disorder of everything else — had burned down to cold oil and ash, its wick a blackened thread in a shallow pool of congealed fat. On the table's edge, placed with some care, stood a small clay figurine of a household god, the plaster of its face worn smooth by years of handling. It was the kind of thing a man touches without thinking, at the beginning and end of his working day, the way a man touches the door-post of his house. It had not been disturbed.

The first thing Ili-iddinam examined was the door.

He ran his fingers along the bolt and its housing, crouching to examine where wood met mudbrick, then stood and examined the door's inner surface at shoulder height. Then he moved to the body.

He turned the dead man's hands with a gentleness that I found I had to look away from, and held them up toward the small high window that admitted the only outside air — the window that faced north, away from the sunrise, so that the room had been in near-total darkness through the whole of the night. He looked at the hands for a long time. Then he lowered them with the same care, and turned his attention to the cedar-oil stain that had spread across the floor near the lamp's bronze stand and dried there in the night — a wide, irregular mark, still dark at its centre where the oil had pooled deepest, thinning at its edges to a transparency that made it almost invisible unless you were crouching at floor level, as Ili-iddinam was now, with the north window's grey light falling across it at an angle.

He crouched there without moving for a time that felt longer than it was. When he stood, he went directly to the three tablets on the table.

He did not touch them immediately. He read two from where he stood, moving only his eyes along the impressed lines with the rapid scanning of a man who reads cuneiform the way others read speech — without translating, without effort. These he dismissed. The third tablet lay with its inscribed face downward, as though it had been set down in haste or turned by a hand that did not wish it read. Ili-iddinam looked at this for a moment, his expression briefly occupied by something I could only describe as the look of a man who has found, in a list of expenditures, the single entry that has been deliberately recorded in the wrong column.

He lifted it with the careful two-fingered grip of a man handling another scribe's work, turned it, and held it toward the window. He read it aloud — not for Ku-Nannum, I understood immediately, but for me, because I could follow the legal clauses and because he required a mind into which to speak the implications.

"A property surety," he said, "lodged by a man named Warad-Sin in exchange for Imgur-Adad's guarantee of his creditworthiness before the city assembly." His eyes moved along the lines without the stylus. "Clause seven. The guarantee of the surety-holder shall be considered void and without obligation in the event of the surety-holder's death before the seal-breaking of the first commercial day following the deposit of this tablet." He set the tablet down. He set it down with a care that was entirely disproportionate to the clay's physical fragility. "This surety was deposited four days ago," he said. "The first commercial seal-breaking of the day occurs at sunrise. Imgur-Adad died —" he placed two fingers above the lamp's dead wick without touching it, and looked at us so we would understand the gesture "— some hours before that sunrise."

Ku-Nannum cleared his throat. He had the patience of a man who has stood in administrative lines his entire professional life, a patience that was less a quality than a form of armour against the requirement to think quickly. "The apprentice confirms the master had enemies. Ea-gamil, a rival grain-merchant, lodged a formal complaint before the assembly last month. The assembly found against him. He left in a rage. Three witnesses confirm the rage."

"Yes," Ili-iddinam said pleasantly. He had turned back to the cedar-oil stain on the floor. He was looking at it at an angle now, the north light raking across it, his reed stylus held loosely at his side. "Come here," he said, and since he was looking at the stain and not at either of us, I came, and Ku-Nannum, after a brief internal negotiation with his dignity, also came.

The stain was more than a spill. At its far edge, preserved clearly in the dried surface of the oil where it had thinned to near-transparency, was the ghost of a partial impression. Not a sandal-print, or not only that — the oil had been thin enough there that only the outer edge of a heel and the ball of a foot had left marks, but they were marks of a specific size and shape, and more significantly they showed a forward lean, weight pressed onto the toes, the posture of a man who has lunged rather than stumbled.

"A man knocked over in a fall does not lean forward," Ili-iddinam said. "A man knocked backward — which is what the position of the body would suggest, if this were a struggle between equals — would step backward. This man stepped forward. He was the aggressor." He straightened. "Furthermore, the precision of what was done here — the darkness of the room, the timing relative to the seal-breaking, the specific legal clause that required Imgur-Adad's death before the sunrise — this is not the work of a man who has spent his anger in the spending of words before the assembly secretary. This is the work of a man who has spent four days reading a contract he found his way into by necessity, looking for the escape it offers, and finding only one." He looked, briefly, at the small worn figurine of the household god on the table's edge. "Ea-gamil is angry. Warad-Sin is desperate. These are not the same condition."

Ku-Nannum received this in the manner of a man receiving a delivery he did not order. "Then we have the name," he said. "We find him."

"He will not be at home," Ili-iddinam said. "And finding him accomplishes nothing without the evidence to bind him. He knows what he left in this room. He does not yet know whether we know it."

He looked at me.

III

He wrote the message himself, on a small lump of unbaked clay, its surface still damp and carrying the faint composite smell — cedar oil and wet clay together — that I now recognised as the smell the boy Imgur-Sin had carried into our doorway in the morning dark, the smell of the counting house, absorbed into the boy's outer garment and carried through the streets. The second thing I had not been able to name at first light. I understood, when I saw Ili-iddinam working the damp clay, that he had named it before we had crossed the first street.

He used the cramped, rapid hand he reserved for private communications. He handed the tablet to me and gave me the address of Warad-Sin's counting house — three streets west, toward the canal — and told me I was to present myself as a runner from the temple of Ishtar's administrative office and deliver it and await a reply.

I read the message as I walked, because I was young and frightened and my name was not on the tablet but my face would be remembered.

The three tablets from the counting house of Imgur-Adad, deceased, are being conveyed this morning to the temple archive for official seal-breaking and inventory. The surety tablet lodged under the name of Warad-Sin will be presented to the assembly judge at the third hour.

I did not, as I walked those three streets, understand what this would produce. I had not yet learned to see, in the gap between what a message said and what it was designed to cause, the trap that Ili-iddinam assembled as instinctively as a weaver assembles the warp before the shuttle moves. I understood it only when I stood in the doorway of Warad-Sin's counting house and watched his face read the damp clay.

He was a broad man, built in the way of men who have done physical work before they have done commercial work, with hands thicker than a scribe's and a way of occupying space that suggested he had never needed to make room for himself. He read the tablet twice. He placed it on the table. He looked at me with the attention of a man who is not reading a runner but reading the runner's purpose, and in that attention I saw, beneath the calculation, something that was not fear, precisely, but its elder and more dangerous relation — the look of a man who has staked everything on a single outcome and who sees, in the arrival of this particular message at this particular hour, evidence that the outcome has not gone as the night was supposed to have purchased.

He stood. As he stood, he took up, from beside the leg of the table, with the naturalness of a man reaching for a tool he has placed close to hand because he anticipated needing it, a cedar-wood club of the kind used by grain-warehouse guards. He did not raise it. He held it the way men hold the instruments of resolution they hope they will not need to use, with a practiced looseness that is more frightening than threat, and looked at me with those calculating eyes.

"You are not from the temple office," he said.

I will set down, because this account should be accurate in every particular, that I did not run. I am uncertain, across the years, whether this was courage or the simpler fact that a man who stands in a doorway is not entirely inside the room, and that Warad-Sin — who was above all things a calculating man — understood that a runner dead in a doorway is a runner who has been seen from the street, and that the street, in the early morning, is not empty.

So I stood, and he stood, and the cedar-wood club did not move, and the counting house of Warad-Sin was very quiet.

Then Ili-iddinam appeared at my left shoulder.

He had followed at a distance I had not perceived — three streets through the early-morning lanes of the Zababa quarter, the interval between hunter and quarry held steady with a precision that I would later understand required not only patience but a complete indifference to being seen to hurry, which is itself a form of concealment. Ku-Nannum's two guards were behind him, and they stopped in the street, and Ili-iddinam alone came into the doorway's frame beside me and looked at Warad-Sin across the table.

His expression was the expression of a man who has set every piece of a game in motion and is now simply present for its conclusion. Not satisfaction. Not triumph. A kind of composed attention that was entirely, unervingly still — the stillness of a man for whom this moment holds no surprises, because he designed it.

"The tablets are not being moved," he said. "That message was mine. I required you to show me what you would do when you believed the evidence against you was already in the archive's hands." He regarded the cedar-wood club with the detached interest of a scholar encountering an instructive exhibit. "You did not flee. You did not destroy. You armed yourself and waited to discover who sent a runner at this hour, which tells me you are still calculating." He paused. "You have been calculating since you read clause seven of that surety four days ago, and you have calculated correctly at every step until the last one. The lamp — correct. The hour — correct. The door — correct. But you did not know about the stain."

Warad-Sin's eyes moved to Ili-iddinam's reed stylus, which Ili-iddinam had lowered to point — briefly, precisely — toward the man's left sandal. Along its outer edge, where the strap met the sole, a dark residue was visible. Cedar oil, dried but not yet cracked. The colour of the floor of Imgur-Adad's counting house at its centre, where the spill had been deepest.

"You stood in it," Ili-iddinam said, "in the dark, in a room where the lamp was burning its final hours. You did not feel it because cedar oil is warm when it is fresh-spilled. You did not see it because the room faced north and the light was wrong. You carried it out on your sandal, and the night's walking has dried it but not removed it." He looked up from the sandal to Warad-Sin's face. "The lamp told me the hour. The tablet told me the motive. The clause told me the name. And your sandal tells the assembly judge everything that remains."

Warad-Sin put the club down.

Not because the guards had moved — they stood still in the street, hands at their sides, waiting. He put it down because he was, above all things, a man who calculated, and he had now completed the final calculation, the one that had no favourable outcome — and what he saw, I think, in the stillness of Ili-iddinam's face, was not an adversary at all, but the mechanism by which every correct calculation he had made for four days had been made irrelevant, simultaneously, at this precise moment, by the one thing he had not seen in the dark.

He sat down. He sat with the heaviness of a man whose body has arrived at a conclusion before his mind, and he looked at the damp tablet on the table — the message that had not come from the temple office, the morning that the night was not supposed to have permitted — and his expression held grief and calculation and something between them that was neither, something that lives in the space where a man understands he has been thorough, and careful, and precise, and seen through entirely.

"My sons," he said. It was not addressed to anyone. It was the final entry in the accounting.

Ku-Nannum's guards entered. What followed belongs to the official record, which names the city guard's diligence, and the assembly judge's efficiency, and no one else.

IV

The cedar-oil smell stayed in the Street of the Seal-Cutters for three days. I know because I walked past it on those mornings on my way to and from the markets, and because the smell of cedar oil has never, in the years since that morning, been a neutral thing to me. Ili-iddinam also walked past it, on his way to and from the roof, and in three days I did not once see him look toward it. He had, I had begun to understand, no further use for a question once it was answered. The finished tablet went to the archive. The living problem that had animated him was gone, and what remained was the particular restlessness of a man whose occupation has been removed — a lamp in a room with nothing yet to illuminate, burning with nowhere useful to send its light.

I thought of Imgur-Adad's household god, standing undisturbed on the table's edge. I thought of the worn smoothness of its plaster face — the wearing of years of hands, morning after morning, a man touching it as he sat down to his work and as he rose from it, the private ritual of a man who believed in small consistences. I thought of Warad-Sin reading that surety tablet four days before, reading it again, reading clause seven a third time and a fourth, the slow crystallisation of what he had found there and what it would require of him. I thought of the word he had said at the end, which had not been a plea and had not been addressed to us — the two-letter word that was the only thing in his accounting he had not, in the end, been able to calculate around.

A man who finds in a legal contract the precise mechanism by which he can destroy another man and save his own household, and who acts on it — such a man is not a monster. He is something harder to look at than a monster: he is a man who solved his problem correctly, within the terms his desperation had set him, and was destroyed by the one thing he could not account for, which was the existence of a mind that would know to look at the floor. I have turned that over in the years since. I have not resolved it. I do not think it is the kind of thing that resolves.

Three days later a client arrived from Nippur — a grain merchant with a missing shipment and a face full of genuine, uncomplicated distress. The particular restlessness left Ili-iddinam's face as if it had been lifted from it. His posture changed, his attention sharpened, and he reached for his reed case with the focus of a man who has been given back his proper work.

I recorded that too.

Here the account ends.
The tablets containing Nanna-iddin's marginal notations were recovered from a private archive in the ruins near the old Zababa quarter. The main administrative records of the house survive in the standard temple and palace collections of the period. The household god of Imgur-Adad — a small worn figurine of painted plaster, the face smoothed almost featureless by handling — was catalogued in the inventory of the counting house and does not appear in the subsequent records. The cedar-oil stain on the Street of the Seal-Cutters is long gone. The contracts remain.
