HOLKOS OF SYBARIS

~610 BC — Sybaris, Magna Graecia

The Death Of Phormion Of Corinth

Being the account of Damaris of Sybaris, private clerk to the house of Kallikrates

I.

The night Phormion died, the harbour smell was wrong.

This is not a small thing in Sybaris, where the smell of the harbour is the smell of money, and the whole city has learned to read its variations the way other peoples read the sky before rain. That evening it carried something underneath the usual brine and cedar-pitch — something sweetish and organic, coming off the grain stores on the Krathis side where a shipment of Egyptian emmer had sat too long in the heat. It was the smell of things spoiling behind a prosperous surface, and I noticed it as I crossed the agora toward the house of Kallikrates, though I did not yet understand what it was telling me.

I was there to collect the evening's correspondence for copying. What I found instead was the beginning of a matter that occupied the house for eleven days and which I set down here because I was present throughout, and because I believe it should be set down.

I should say, since it bears on the shape of what followed: my work for Kallikrates placed me at the junction of the city's commercial life. I copied contracts, bill-of-lading records, correspondence between Sybarite merchant houses and their counterparts in Corinth and Miletus and the Sicilian cities. It was through this work that I had come to know Holkos — a Miletan by origin, arrived in Sybaris a decade before this account begins, who had built within those years a position of trust at the centre of the city's trade. He worked as a factor and assessor in the grain and textile routes, and the quality for which the better merchant houses retained him was the same quality that made him occasionally inconvenient: the willingness to say what the evidence required rather than what the client wished to hear. I had copied enough of his assessments to recognise the method, and I had been present at enough of his conversations with Kallikrates to understand that the method operated the same way in every domain it touched.

It was this same Holkos who had been a guest at Kallikrates's table on the night Phormion of Corinth ceased to be alive.

II.

Phormion had been in the city three months, representing interests in the Corinthian purple trade — several merchant houses seeking to renegotiate their arrangements with Sybarite dyers after a bad season had disrupted the previous year's contracts. He had conducted his negotiations with the patience and mild manner that Corinthians of the better sort bring to commerce: he appeared agreeable while conceding almost nothing. He was perhaps fifty, with the dry skin of a man who has spent decades between the coast and the counting-room, and he had made no obvious enemies in the city, which is often the most dangerous position for a man engaged in commercial negotiation. Obvious enemies know they are enemies. The other kind require study.

The dinner at which he died was hosted by Kallikrates himself, in the inner courtyard, and it was a small affair — seven guests, which is the proper Sybarite number for a dinner conducted at the middle level of formality, where the wine will be good but the entertainment will be conversation rather than performance. The guests were Phormion; Thrasyboulos of Akragas, a grain merchant whose dealings with the Sybarite houses were extensive and occasionally contentious; Mnasalkes, a Sybarite citizen of the second rank who had lately acquired, through a series of arrangements I had helped to record, a significant position in the dock-works on the northern quay; and four others of lesser consequence for what followed. Holkos was present as a guest rather than in a professional capacity, which was not unusual. Kallikrates valued his company, and Holkos, during those periods when the commercial season was between its peaks and his particular faculty had insufficient material to occupy it, accepted such invitations as a man accepts stimulation when the alternative is the particular restlessness that afflicts those whose minds cannot idle comfortably.

I arrived at the house as the third course was being cleared. I know this because I passed the kitchen slaves carrying the remains of the small birds — thrushes in the Sybarite fashion, cooked in sweet wine — and entered the courtyard as the lamps were being refreshed. The smell of fresh Laconian oil meeting the warm evening air had a green quality at first light, sharp and clean, before the heat of the flames softened it. It is against this smell that I situate everything that followed.

The fourth course was fish. The preparation was elaborate — the fish had been stuffed with herbs from the eastern trade, things brought over the portage road from the Ionian side, and the whole dressed with a reduction that the cook had been working since the morning before. It was during this course that Phormion of Corinth became unwell. He did not die at the table. He was assisted to an interior room at his own request, insisting that he had merely eaten too quickly in the heat, and he was dead before the lamps were extinguished.

I learned this the following morning, when I arrived to begin the day's copying and found the house in the particular stillness that follows a catastrophe that has not yet been named. Kallikrates met me in the entry himself — not a thing he did — and his face carried something I had not seen on it before. He was not a man given to visible distress; twenty years in the grain trade had produced in him a considerable capacity for composure under loss. What I saw now was not distress of the ordinary commercial kind. It was the face of a man who understood that something had happened in his house, under his protection, which he could not account for, and whose consequences he could not yet measure.

"You will have heard," he said.

"I have heard nothing yet," I told him. "I came for the copying."

He looked at me for a moment. "Then hear it now." He told me what had happened. A physician had examined Phormion that morning at Kallikrates's own summons, before any official was involved, and had identified the presentation of the death as consistent with the alkaloid preparations known to be obtainable from certain plants brought into Sybaris through the eastern trade. Kallikrates had sent for the city's investigating officer immediately after. But he had also sent for Holkos.

"Why Holkos?" I asked.

"Because Sosias will look for what he can prosecute," Kallikrates said. "Holkos will look for what happened." He paused. "They are not always the same thing. I need both, but I need the second more." He looked past me toward the courtyard. "Someone killed a guest in my house, Damaris. Under my hospitality. You understand what that means for every arrangement I have with Corinth — with every partner who knows he may one day be a guest here. I need to know who did it before it is decided for me by a process that may not care what is true."

I understood him. I set aside the copying and stayed.

III.

There was, in the work I had done in the preceding weeks, a thing I had noticed and not yet placed. This is the point at which I must be exact, because it bears on how the matter resolved.

In the bill-of-lading records I copied for Kallikrates's house — those relating to the eastern trade, the goods coming over the portage road from the Ionian side — there had appeared, sixteen days before the dinner, a transaction I had flagged in my own notes as unusual. It was recorded under the medicinal category, which is to say it was not recorded in useful detail, because medicinal imports occupied a classification the clerks processed without close notation. But the quantity was atypical for a personal purchase, and the purchasing party was not a physician or an apothecary. It was a name I knew from other records: a commercial name, not a medical one. I had made a note to query it and then had been occupied with other work and had not returned to it.

The purchasing name was Mnasalkes.

I did not yet understand the significance of this. I mention it here because Holkos, when he arrived at the house that morning, asked to see the recent eastern trade records before he asked anything else — before he saw the courtyard, before he spoke to Sosias, before he looked at the table. He sat with the tablets in a room off the main corridor for perhaps a quarter of an hour. When he came out, his expression had not changed in any way I could have described to another person, but he moved with a quality of focused attention that I had learned, over the preceding years, to associate with a mind that has found its direction and is now simply following it.

He did not say what he had found. He put the tablets back, thanked me, and asked to be shown the courtyard.

IV.

By the following morning, three things were known among those involved: that Phormion had died of something he ate or drank; that the physician's findings pointed to an alkaloid preparation of the kind obtainable through the eastern trade; and that his death was of significant material consequence to three of the seven guests.

Thrasyboulos of Akragas stood to benefit most directly. Phormion had been concluding arrangements that would have formalised a Corinthian position in the Akragantine grain corridor — a route that Thrasyboulos had held, through a series of agreements with intermediate parties, in something approaching monopoly control. With Phormion dead, the formalisation would not occur.

Mnasalkes had a different stake. He had borrowed substantially against anticipated returns from a contract that Phormion's principals were in a position to contest. The contest had not yet been initiated — Phormion had been in correspondence with Corinth about it — but its resolution against Mnasalkes would have been ruinous. I knew this because I had copied the relevant correspondence myself.

The third was Kallikrates. No one believed this seriously, but it was required to be considered, and I record it because accuracy demands recording what the process examined, not only what it concluded.

Sosias — the investigating officer, competent and perpetually harassed, his jurisdiction over commercial disputes extended to cover this matter in the absence of a senior official — arrived mid-morning with a theory already assembled. It had the virtues of speed and administrative cleanliness: Thrasyboulos had brought something to the table and found an opportunity to introduce it into Phormion's food during the natural movement between courses. He proposed to summon Thrasyboulos, examine the kitchen slaves under pressure, and present his findings to the council before the week was out.

Holkos, who had been standing at the courtyard's edge with the particular stillness he inhabits when a problem has fully occupied him — not the stillness of a man at rest, but the stillness of a man who is moving very fast inside — said nothing for a moment after Sosias finished. He was turning the small stone mortar in his hands, the one he carried in the periods between cases when the anise oil was the only thing that kept the restlessness within tolerable limits. He set it down on the balustrade with a sound that was precise enough to be deliberate.

Then he moved.

Not toward Sosias. Toward the table. He crossed the courtyard in six strides and stood at Phormion's couch with a sudden, focused energy that made the steward step back without knowing why. He crouched, looked along the surface of the couch at the angle a reclining man would have occupied, then stood and turned to look at the serving entry with an expression of sharp, almost hungry attention.

"The theory is coherent, Sosias," he said, without turning back. "It has only one difficulty, which is that it requires Thrasyboulos to have acted without knowing where Phormion would be seated." Now he turned, and there was something in his manner — the abruptness of a man whose mind has already arrived at a destination and finds the conversation lagging behind — that made even Sosias pause. "The fish course. The preparation is important. Look at the table as it was."

"There are seven positions," he said — not to us, but as a man confirming something to himself. He walked the full circuit of the table with the rapid, measuring attention of someone checking arithmetic, his gaze moving from couch to couch and then to the serving entry and back, and the circuit took no more time than it takes to draw a breath. When he stopped, he stopped at the pillar on the eastern wall and put his hand flat against it.

"A dinner of this formality distributes positions according to protocol," he said. "The host at the head, the most honoured guest to his right, the order descending clockwise. Not preference. Custom. Established custom that every guest at a dinner of this type expects, and which the host cannot deviate from without causing notice." He removed his hand from the pillar and turned to face us. "Who arranged the seating?"

Kallikrates indicated his steward. The steward confirmed: arranged according to protocol, status of the guests, not adjusted.

"Then Phormion was in the second position before he arrived." Holkos said it flatly. "Any person who has attended a dinner in this city, or spoken to anyone who has, knew that before they walked through the gate." He paused, and in the pause something shifted in the quality of his attention — sharpened, the way a blade sharpens. "Your theory requires Thrasyboulos to have seized a moment. But you don't seize a moment when you already know where your man will sit, what he will eat, in what order, and where every slave will be looking." He looked at Sosias directly for the first time since entering the courtyard. "You don't seize an opportunity. You build one."

The words landed with a quality of finality, like a bolt dropping.

He moved immediately to the serving entry — the narrow gap through which the kitchen slaves had brought each course — and positioned himself in front of it. From this angle, I could see what he was demonstrating before he said it: the structural pillar on the eastern wall cut the sightline between the second couch and this entry with quiet, architectural precision. Not dramatically. Not in a way that would be noticed during conversation and wine, when eyes move to faces and cups. But cut it was.

"Phormion's portion was the second served," he said. "Before the vessel completed its circuit. Before the majority of guests had received anything. At the precise moment when attention would be on the host, who had received first, and on the cook's performance — which was," he added, "by all accounts visually engaging. Elaborate. Designed to be looked at." He said this last sentence in a tone that made me wonder, suddenly, whether the elaborateness of the preparation had also been arranged. "The moment of maximum distraction. And from the second couch, you cannot see this entry without turning away from the spectacle." He stepped back from the pillar. "This was not a man who watched and waited. This was a man who drew the plans."

"Then Thrasyboulos arranged the dinner," Sosias said, with the emphasis of a man arriving at a conclusion he is prepared to act on.

"I have said nothing of the kind," Holkos said, with a sharpness that stopped Sosias mid-breath. "I have told you what the table tells me. The table tells me the crime required the dinner. Now." He turned to Kallikrates, and his voice had the quality of a man who already knows the answer but wants to hear it spoken aloud. "Who suggested it?"

There was a silence in the courtyard.

Kallikrates, once the question was posed to him directly by a man who had already demonstrated he understood the geometry of the table, answered without hesitation. The suggestion for the dinner had come from Mnasalkes, proposed three weeks before the evening in the course of a commercial conversation and framed as a social nicety — Phormion had been in the city long enough, Mnasalkes had said, that it would seem poor hospitality not to host him properly before his negotiations concluded. Kallikrates had agreed and made the arrangements accordingly.

Three weeks. This was what Holkos had meant by the opportunity being engineered in advance.

V.

It was on the third day that Mnasalkes became aware that the inquiry had turned toward him.

I know this because he came to me.

I was working in the records room off the main corridor — the same room where Holkos had sat with the eastern trade tablets — when the shadow fell across the doorway. Mnasalkes did not knock. He was a large man, broad through the chest and shoulders, with the deliberate economy of movement that belongs to men who have learned to make their size felt without the vulgarity of displaying it. He stood in the doorway for a moment before he spoke, and in that moment I felt, with a physical specificity I remember clearly, the smallness of the room.

"You copy the trade records," he said. Not a question.

"I do."

"The eastern shipments. The bill-of-lading records for the past month." He let that sit for a moment, then came into the room. Not aggressively — he never did anything aggressively — but with the natural authority of a man who has decided that a given space belongs to him. He moved to the shelves and stood before them with his hands clasped behind his back, and he was counting. I watched him count without moving my stylus.

"Records can be incomplete," he said, still looking at the shelves. "Commercial records especially. They require context. A clerk copying without full knowledge of the transactions may not be able to supply that context." He turned and looked at me with the expression of a man who is accustomed to people understanding him quickly. "It would be a serious matter if incomplete records led the investigating process toward a conclusion that was inaccurate. Serious for the investigation." He paused. "And potentially inconvenient for those involved in maintaining them."

He said this in the tone one uses for observations about the quality of the morning. But he was standing between me and the door, and he did not move.

"I copy what I am given to copy," I said. My voice came out steadier than I had expected it to.

"Yes," he said. "You do." He looked at me for a moment longer, in the particular way of a man who is satisfied that he has been understood, and then he walked to the door and through it without another word.

I sat without moving until I could no longer hear his footsteps. Then I went to find Holkos.

He was in the inner court, at the pillar on the eastern wall, with the untouched cup at his feet and the mortar in his hands grinding in the rapid, continuous way that meant his mind was working at a rate the rest of him was struggling to keep pace with. He had a fragment of wax tablet in his other hand and he was marking on it — not writing, exactly, but a series of compressed notations I could not read from the doorway. He did not look up when I arrived.

"He came to the records room," I said.

Now he looked up. The mortar stopped.

I told him everything: the counting of the shelves, the position in the doorway, the language about incomplete records and inconvenience. Holkos listened without a word, and as I spoke I saw his expression undergo a change that I had not seen before — not alarm, exactly, but a rapid recalculation, the face of a man who has been tracking one thing and has realised that a second element is moving faster than he had estimated.

"He stood between you and the door," Holkos said.

"Yes."

"And counted the tablets before he spoke to you."

"Yes."

He set the mortar down and stood. "He came to determine what you had copied and whether you understood it. The position in the doorway was not accidental." He said this in a voice that was quiet and very even, in the way that things said very quietly and very evenly are sometimes more alarming than things said at a volume. "He will have concluded that you are a competent clerk who works methodically and does not interpret what she copies. Whether that conclusion satisfies him depends on what he believes the records contain." He crossed to me. "Go back to the records room. Remove every eastern trade tablet from the shelves — all of them, not only the relevant ones, so that the removal is not itself informative — and bring them here. Do it now. Don't run. Don't look as though you are hurrying. And when you have done it, do not return to that room without me or Sosias present."

I went. I worked through it steadily, carrying the tablets in the ordinary fashion, in the ordinary number a clerk would carry, in the ordinary number of trips. On the second trip, passing through the outer corridor, I heard the front entry open. I stepped into the alcove beside the storage jars and waited.

It was Mnasalkes's voice. He was asking the steward, with perfect ease, whether Holkos was available on a commercial matter. The steward said he would enquire. I heard Mnasalkes settle into a waiting position — the minor shift of weight, the particular quality of silence that belongs to a man making himself comfortable for however long is required.

I finished the last trip with my back to the corridor and my arms full of tablets, and I did not look toward the entry.

When I set the final set down in the inner court, I found my hands were unsteady in a way they had not been during the work itself.

Holkos looked at the tablets and then at my hands. He said nothing about what he saw. He said: "Good."

VI.

The cook interview produced what Holkos had expected.

He spoke with the cook — I was present — and established that Mnasalkes had visited the house twice during the weeks preceding the dinner on commercial pretexts, and had on one visit spent time in conversation with the cook in the kitchen court while waiting for Kallikrates to become available. The cook had not thought this remarkable. Men wait in various parts of a house. Holkos thought it very remarkable indeed, and when the cook described which herbs had been laid out for preparation that day — because the fish preparation had been begun and set aside and returned to over the full three weeks — Holkos stood very still for a moment and then said, in the voice he uses when a proof closes: "Yes. That is where it went."

He explained it afterward. Mnasalkes had, during his time in the kitchen court, identified the specific preparation the cook intended and obtained a sample of the eastern herbs sufficient to determine which alkaloid compound would be most effective when introduced into that particular sauce base — because the compound he ultimately used, as the physician later confirmed when given the information Holkos provided him, required the acidic quality of the reduction to remain stable and active through the final heat. He had introduced it not into the sauce vessel itself, which would have poisoned everyone, but into the specific herb bundle reserved for the sauce, at a stage of preparation early enough that the compound had fully integrated. He had known the timing of the preparation because he had asked the cook, conversationally, when the cook intended to do the final work.

The medicinal purchase was the final element. The eastern trade record I had flagged and not returned to — the unusual transaction in Mnasalkes's name, sixteen days before the dinner, recorded under the medicinal classification without useful detail — was the purchase of the compound itself. Holkos had seen it in the tablets on the morning he arrived. I had seen it three weeks earlier without knowing what I was looking at.

VII.

The trap Holkos laid was simple and required only that Mnasalkes believe himself to be one step ahead of the inquiry.

It was constructed in two parts. The first was a message sent through Sosias's office — framed as a routine administrative summons, requesting that Mnasalkes attend the house of Kallikrates the following morning to provide a statement regarding the guest list and his role in proposing the dinner. Nothing in the message indicated the shape of what was known. It was precisely the kind of summons a man who had already come to examine the records and satisfied himself that they were incomplete would feel comfortable answering.

The second part was the arrangement of the room.

Holkos had asked Kallikrates to make available the same records room in which Mnasalkes had appeared to me. The eastern trade tablets were returned to their shelves, arranged exactly as they had been when Mnasalkes had stood in the doorway and counted them. On the central table, Holkos had placed a single tablet — one I had prepared under his direction — which contained a copy of the bill-of-lading entry for the medicinal purchase, rewritten in clean script with the detail that the original had omitted: the compound name, as the physician had supplied it, and the quantity. It was placed so that a man entering the room would see it from the doorway before anyone spoke.

I was to be in the room when Mnasalkes arrived. This was not a role I had volunteered for. Holkos explained it with the economy he applied to everything: "You are the clerk. Your presence is expected and unremarkable. Sosias and I will be visible, which will put him on his guard. You will be copying, which will not." He paused. "He has already visited you once to assess what you know. He will look at you when he enters, before he looks at us. I want to see his face at the moment he looks at the tablet."

"And if he decides that the most direct resolution involves harm to me?" I said.

Holkos looked at me with the careful attention he gave to any material variable. "He will not. The presence of Sosias forecloses it. What he will do is calculate whether the evidence on that tablet is what it appears to be, or whether it is a construction." He paused. "He is an intelligent man. The calculation will take him approximately the time it takes to walk from the doorway to the table."

I sat with my tablets and my stylus and I was not comfortable, but I was present.

VIII.

Mnasalkes arrived precisely at the appointed time, which was itself information. A man with nothing to fear arrives slightly late. A man managing a situation arrives at the exact moment he committed to, because the management of situations requires precision.

He saw me first, as Holkos had predicted. Then Sosias at the far end of the room. Then Holkos — and here he paused, barely perceptibly, because Holkos was not standing where a man receiving a visitor conventionally stands. He was at the shelves. Not examining them; simply present at them, in the way a man is present at a thing he has already assessed and found in order. It took Mnasalkes a fraction of a moment to understand why this was wrong, and in that fraction I saw something move behind his eyes.

Then he saw the tablet on the central table.

He crossed to it with a deliberateness that was itself a performance — the walk of a man who is approaching something at his own pace and on his own terms — and looked at it for a long time without touching it. The compound name. The quantity. The source, noted in Holkos's own hand alongside the corresponding bill-of-lading reference, cross-matched against the physician's testimony.

"This is a detailed document," Mnasalkes said.

"It is," said Holkos. He did not move from the shelves.

"The original entry recorded nothing of this kind." Mnasalkes looked up. His voice was level, and he was working to keep it that way, and only the quality of the effort showed. "A medicinal purchase, categorised without specificity. The clerks process such entries without notation. That is established practice." He put his hands flat on the table. "What you have here is a reconstruction. An interpretation. Applied to an ambiguous entry by a man who has decided, prior to the reconstruction, what the entry should mean."

"Possibly," Holkos said. He turned from the shelves and looked at Mnasalkes with the particular focus of a man examining something he has been curious about. "If that were the only evidence, you would be right to say so."

"It is the only evidence of the purchase," Mnasalkes said. "The rest — the kitchen visit, the herb bundle — these are the observations of a cook and a clerk. A cook who saw a man waiting in a kitchen court, which is unremarkable. A clerk who copied records she did not understand." He said this last without looking at me, which was itself precise — a man who wishes to diminish something does not honour it with attention. "You have a theory. You have given your theory the appearance of documentation. These are different things."

The room was very quiet. I was aware of the weight of the tablets under my hands.

Holkos was silent for a moment. I had seen him use silence before — the silence that arrives when he has reached the point in a sequence where the next step requires care. Then he crossed to the central table. He did not move quickly. He moved with the deliberate economy of a man who is in no hurry because the ground has already been prepared, and he set down, beside the existing tablet, a second one.

"The cook described the herb bundle," he said. "He described which herbs had been laid out on the day of Mnasalkes's visit to the kitchen court, because the preparation had been begun in stages across three weeks and he remembered the stages clearly. He described the bundle as it was on that day, and he described it again as it was found on the day of the dinner." He looked at Mnasalkes. "Between those two descriptions, one herb is absent from the bundle and replaced by another of similar appearance, from the same trade origin, with a different property. The substitution was identified by the physician when I put the question to him directly. The substitute herb is the delivery mechanism for the compound noted in the purchase record." He placed his finger on the second tablet. "This is the physician's testimony. The original is with Sosias's office."

For the first time, something in Mnasalkes's composure shifted. Not much. A tightening around the jaw — the physical correlate of a man who has played the move he prepared and found it answered.

"A physician's interpretation of a cook's description of a herb bundle," he said. "These are layers of inference."

"They are," Holkos agreed. "And on their own, still insufficient." He set down a third tablet. "Three days ago you came to this room. You stood in the doorway. You counted the tablets on these shelves before you spoke to Damaris, and when you spoke to her, you spoke to her about the consequences of records that were incomplete or misread. Then you left, and within the hour you returned to enquire whether I was available." He looked at Mnasalkes with an expression that had nothing in it of anger or triumph — only the flat, precise attention of a man completing an assessment. "The presence of Sosias prevents me from characterising that visit as a threat. I will characterise it as this instead: it is the action of a man who came to determine the state of the evidence against him. An innocent man, summoned to answer questions about a dinner he proposed, sends a message, or waits to be called. He does not come to count the records and speak to the clerk about what she does and does not understand." He let this sit for a moment. "When these three things are presented together to the council — the purchase, the physician's identification of the compound and the delivery mechanism, and your attendance in this room — they will not find layers of inference. They will find a sequence."

Mnasalkes looked at the three tablets for a long time. I watched him look at them with the attention of a man reading not the text but the argument — testing it, finding its joints, looking for the place to apply pressure. He found no such place, and I watched him find that he had found none, and something in his face changed in a way that was not dramatic. It was more like a door closing quietly than anything else.

"The compound," he said finally, and the word came out with a different quality from everything that had preceded it — not the level challenge of a man making his case, but the involuntary release of a man acknowledging something he had not intended to speak aloud. He stopped himself.

Holkos looked at him.

"The compound required the acid," Mnasalkes said, in the voice of a man who is past the point of managing what he says. "It had to be the reduction. Any other preparation and it would have been detectable. It was the only method that — " He stopped again. His jaw tightened. He had, in the space of two sentences, said more than he had said in the entire preceding exchange, and he knew it.

The room was very still.

"Yes," said Holkos, very quietly. "It was."

Mnasalkes straightened. The composure came back, or the shell of it — the practised surface of a man who has spent a career managing the presentation of himself under pressure. When he spoke again his voice was level. "I wish to speak with the council."

IX.

He was convicted eleven days after Phormion's death. The penalty that Sybarite commercial law provided for deliberate poisoning with malicious intent was substantial and effectively ended his commercial position in the city.

I asked Holkos, at some point during those eleven days — I cannot now fix exactly when — whether he had known from the beginning that it was Mnasalkes.

He was at the mortar. This was not unusual during the days after a case resolved, when the restlessness that a problem had been holding in check returned to him in force. What was unusual was the rate — the grinding was rapid, continuous, barely controlled, the motion of a man whose hands are managing something his mind has already finished with and cannot release. He had, I noticed, consumed a quantity of the oil that his physician would have described as injudicious. He looked up at me with eyes that had a particular quality of brightness in them — not warmth, exactly, but the lit-from-inside quality of a man who has been running at full capacity for eleven days and is only now beginning to feel it.

"I suspected Thrasyboulos initially," he said. "Because Thrasyboulos's motive was the most visible, and visible motives are where investigators look first. It is a structural weakness in the investigation of crimes: the most obvious suspect absorbs the inquiry's energy until something forces a redirection." He ground the anise with a vehemence that sent a thread of oil down the side of the mortar. "Sosias would have found Thrasyboulos a satisfactory answer, and Thrasyboulos had in fact done nothing. Mnasalkes's motive was less visible because it required reading the correspondence, and the correspondence was in my keeping, and I had read it." He set the mortar down with a sudden abruptness, as though deciding something. "The table told me it was not opportunism. It is remarkable," he added, with an energy that surprised me — he had leaned forward, both hands flat on the table, the mortar abandoned — "it is remarkable how much a table tells you if you are willing to look at it as though it is a document rather than a piece of furniture. The couch positions. The distances. The pillar. Every one of these is a fact, and facts do not rearrange themselves to suit the preferred theory. Sosias had a theory. The table had facts. The facts were not kind to the theory."

He pushed back from the table and moved to the window. This was the other thing about the days after a case: the stillness he maintained during it gave way to something close to its opposite — a restless, pacing energy, as though the concentration that had been held in a single point was now dispersing through his whole body.

"The herbs told me it required preparation," he said, to the window. "The preparation told me it required a visit. The visit told me it required knowledge of the kitchen's schedule, which told me it required a conversation with the cook. Once I had those elements assembled, the question of who had proposed the dinner was the only question that remained, and Kallikrates answered it without hesitation." He turned from the window. "The difficult part was the compound."

"The compound?"

"I had to determine which one would behave correctly in that sauce." The brightness in his eyes was very direct now. "I have some knowledge of these materials — their properties, their behaviours in different preparations. It is knowledge I acquired in Miletus, from sources that did not obtain it academically, and it is not knowledge I advertise. But it allowed me to understand what had been done, to confirm it with the physician, and then — " and here something very like pleasure crossed his face, the pleasure of a craftsman describing the finest stroke of his work — "to construct the document that would tell Mnasalkes exactly what was known. The detail on that tablet was accurate. But it was also exactly the detail that a careful man, testing the limits of the evidence, would reach for. When he told me the compound required the acid, he was telling me that he knew the compound well enough to have chosen it for that property. He had not intended to say so." He paused. "You were the instrument that confirmed he was testing the evidence rather than disputing it. When he came to the records room and counted the tablets, the question of what he knew became answerable."

He looked at me with the directness that he applied to all material variables, and I recognised, not for the first time, the particular quality of being regarded by him: accurate, undeceived, entirely without warmth.

"I am aware," he said, "that I placed you in a position of some exposure."

I said nothing.

"It was necessary," he said. "I had taken precautions that I judged sufficient." He picked up the mortar again, regarded it, set it back down. "Whether they were sufficient is a question I was prepared to have answered incorrectly. I judged the probability acceptable." He said this in the tone of a man who has reported a fact, which is what it was.

I found I did not know what to do with this information, so I recorded it instead.

X.

The harbour smell corrected itself within a week. The Egyptian emmer was identified as degraded and removed, the storing house aired, and the usual complex fragrance of Sybaris's prosperity reasserted itself over the agora and the waterfront — pitch and grain and olive oil and the faint salt undertone that in good weather carried all the way to the upper market. I crossed it on the morning after Mnasalkes's conviction and found it as it should have been.

I am a clerk. I record what I am given to record, and I record it as accurately as the distance from which I observed it allows. What I observed in this matter was a man who looked at a table where a person had died and saw, in its geometry, not a tragedy but a problem — and in the problem, a sequence that was patient enough to follow backward from its conclusion to its beginning.

But I also observed something else, which I have thought about since: Mnasalkes came to this room and looked at me across a table of records, and in that conversation he made clear, in the mildest possible terms, that a clerk who knew too much and said so might find that her knowledge became a difficulty. Holkos had known, before I told him, the shape of what Mnasalkes would do — had known that an intelligent man covering his tracks would come first to the records and then to the person who kept them. He had moved me out of that room and kept me out of it until the trap was set. He did not tell me any of this. He gave me instructions and waited for me to follow them.

I found that I was not entirely comfortable with the admiration this produced in me. He was not warm about it. He was, in some technical sense, admirable in a way that sits uneasily alongside the fact that I was the instrument he used to read Mnasalkes's face, and that he had judged the probability of my safety acceptable rather than certain, and had not seen fit to mention this until the matter was closed.

The world in which this happened was a world organised around the movement of goods and the management of agreements, and within that world men like Mnasalkes calculated that a clerk and a set of records were problems to be managed like any other. Holkos understood this better than Mnasalkes did. That is the difference. It is not a small one, but it is not entirely a comfortable one either. A man who sees the world as a sequence of facts to be correctly ordered will solve the problem in front of him and will accept, as a necessary cost, whatever the solving requires. I was a necessary cost. So, in a different sense, was Phormion.

The official record of Mnasalkes's conviction credits the diligence of the council's investigating process and the testimony of witnesses secured by Sosias's office. Holkos is not named in it. This is, as far as I can determine, how he preferred things.

I name him here because these are my records, kept under my authority, and because I believe accuracy requires it.

This account, in eleven tablet sections, was kept with the papers of the house of Kallikrates and passed to the archive of the Sybarite merchants' association upon the dissolution of that house. The association's records were partially recovered following the destruction of the city in 510 BC. These tablets are among the recovered sections.
