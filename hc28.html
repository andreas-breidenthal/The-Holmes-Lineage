HOLKOS OF WILUSA

~1240 BC — Wilusa, Anatolia

The Private Notes of Eutykhos

Set down in the third month of the siege, when the Greek fires could be seen from the eastern tower on clear nights.

I.

The door-boy Siku ran through the administrative quarter at the first hour of light with the look of a person who has seen something that cannot be unseen, and collided with me in the corridor outside the archive.

He had blood on neither hand, I noted — and then noted that I was already thinking in the manner Holkos had taught me, cataloguing the absence of things. Siku had no blood on him at all. Whatever he had found, it had not died fighting.

"Pramnos," he said. "On the floor. Between the big jars."

I followed him down.

The grain-stores of Wilusa occupied the lower level of the great citadel complex, cut into the living rock beneath the administrative quarter so that the temperature remained constant through both the killing heat of summer and the mountain cold of winter — the design of a man who understood that a city under pressure is a city that will eventually be fed or not be fed, and that the distance between those two outcomes is measured in what you keep and what you lose. It was, in every material sense, the heart of the citadel. Everyone who understood Wilusa understood this. Including, as it proved, the man who had decided to use it as a place to commit a murder.

Pramnos lay between the third and fourth pithoi — the great clay storage vessels that stood chest-high and held the winter barley. He was on his back. His arms were at his sides with a neatness that struck me immediately as wrong, with the precise wrongness of a thing that has been made to look like something it is not. A man does not fall with that arrangement. He is placed with it.

The oil lamp in the niche above him was burned entirely out, the wick cold and flat. There was no wound visible from the doorway. The room smelled of barley-dust and lamp-black and something else — a faint sharpness beneath the grain-smell, almost like the copper-tang from the smithy quarter two streets above us, but softer. More intimate. I could not name it. I registered it, and I could not name it, and I remembered it.

I should describe Pramnos, because Holkos always said you cannot understand a death without first understanding the life. He was thirty years old, or near enough. He had served the Chamberlain Arion-Bel for eleven years, risen through the lower administrative lists by a combination of competence with the tally-notch system and a remarkable memory for faces — every merchant, every porter, every soldier who had passed through the distribution point in a given week, recalled a month later when the accounts were being reconciled. He was not a man who made enemies. He was, if I am honest, the sort of man who disappears into the function he performs — present, reliable, essentially invisible.

The invisibility, as Holkos would later demonstrate, was the thing that got him killed.

The guard came. The guard fetched the duty officer — Telamon-Sur, a man built on the architectural principle of a storage jar himself: wide, solid, and not designed for subtlety. He looked at the body. He looked at the locked door, which Siku had opened with the morning key. He looked at the ceiling, for reasons I did not understand and which I do not believe he could have explained.

"The Greeks have found a way inside the walls," he said, with the certainty of a man who has located his answer before he has finished asking his question. "There will be an assassin in the lower city by now. Seal the gates."

The gates were sealed. The lower city was searched. No assassin was found. Telamon-Sur wrote his report anyway — a matter of enemy infiltration, circumstances consistent with Greek sabotage of our administrative personnel — and had it sealed for dispatch to the King's chancellery.

Then he did something that surprised me. He came to find me.

I was at my tallying-bench in the outer archive when he appeared in the doorway, and his expression, beneath the official granite, was the expression of a man who is beginning, quietly, to be afraid. Not of the Greeks. Of his own report.

"You knew Pramnos," he said. It was not a question.

"I worked alongside him for four years."

"And you saw — when you went down this morning — you saw nothing to contradict the obvious conclusion."

I looked at him. I understood the shape of what he was asking. He had already dispatched the report. If the report was wrong — if Pramnos had not died at Greek hands but at Wilusan ones — then Telamon-Sur had sent a false account to the King's chancellery under his own seal. In a besieged city, in the third month, with the judicial council already stretched across two capital cases and the King's advisors looking for evidence of administrative failure. He would not survive it professionally. He might not survive it at all.

"I saw nothing," I said carefully, "that I would be willing to swear to before a council."

He absorbed that.

"Send for Holkos," I said.

He looked at me as though I had suggested something mildly heretical. I looked back at him with the steadiness of a man who has made an accurate count and knows what the tally shows. He was afraid of Holkos, I understood — afraid of what a man who finds things out might find out. But he was more afraid of the chancellery. In Wilusa in the third month of the siege, the chancellery was the nearer danger.

He sent for Holkos.

II.

I had encountered Holkos perhaps a dozen times before that morning — at a distance, through the medium of other men's reports, which are always unreliable instruments. I had heard him described as brilliant and maddening in equal measure by Premnath the archive-keeper, who had worked with him on the matter of the missing temple inventory two years prior. Premnath described a man who did not sleep during a case, who forgot to eat, who spoke in rapid half-sentences that only resolved into meaning several exchanges after they were uttered. Between cases, Holkos was barely present — he would sit in the administrative quarter for whole afternoons doing nothing visible, or he would be found in the market studying the arrangement of goods on a trader's table with an intensity that made the trader nervous. He had a particular weakness for the coded tallies that Aegean merchants used to hide their actual cargo manifests from port inspectors — not from any commercial interest, but because, as he had apparently told Premnath, the puzzle was the only thing quiet enough not to bore me to madness.

He arrived at the grain-store an hour after Telamon-Sur had sealed the room and departed. He arrived alone. He had sent no message ahead. He simply appeared in the doorway — a lean man of perhaps fifty, in a plain wool chiton of undyed grey, good leather sandals, no jewellery except a single ring of Hittite bronze on the right hand that he wore not for display but for the edge of the seal-stone, which served him as a measuring tool. His face gave away nothing. He had long hands and a habit of stillness that was different from the stillness of calm — it was the stillness of something attending. His eyes were dark, and they moved in a way that suggested they were always, simultaneously, looking at two different things.

He looked at Pramnos for a long moment. Then he looked at me.

"You are Eutykhos."

"I am."

"You keep the distribution tallies for this store."

"Among others."

"You noticed the arms."

It was not a question. I had not said anything about the arms to anyone.

"Yes," I said.

"And the smell."

"Yes."

He looked at me for another moment with those simultaneously-attending eyes. "Good," he said, and crossed to the body, and began.

III.

He worked for a quarter of an hour without speaking, and I had the sense — watching him crouch over Pramnos with the composed attention of a man examining a particularly interesting piece of Bronze Age pottery — that the room had, for him, reduced to a set of questions that he was answering in sequence, and that the sequence was not the sequence I would have chosen or that Telamon-Sur had chosen, because it was not the sequence of a man who already knew what had happened.

He examined the hands — each finger separately. He examined the neck, tilting the head with careful hands, studying the skin. He examined the nose and the lips with a lamp he had brought himself — his own lamp, with oil that smelled different from the stores we used: lighter, cleaner, better suited to close examination. He pressed two fingers to the centre of the chest and held them there for a moment, as though counting something that was no longer there to count. He studied the hem of the robe — particularly the inner face near the right ankle, where there was a faint staining I had not noticed from the doorway.

Then he stood, crossed to the pithoi, and studied them in order — specifically the stoppers, the clay caps that sealed each vessel's mouth, and the surface of the clay around each stopper. He crouched and examined the floor between the vessels. He looked at the ceiling. He examined the door-socket, the groove worn into the rock where the door-bar rested, and he produced a piece of smooth bone from a fold of his chiton and measured the gap between the bottom of the door and the threshold stone, writing the figure with a small stylus he wore tucked behind his ear.

Then he looked up, above the fourth pithos, at the ventilation channel cut into the rock — and something in his expression shifted. Not much. A slight concentration, the way a man's face changes when a calculation completes.

"Bring me the tallies," he said. "All of them. Not just the recent dates."

I brought them. For the next hour he read them — clay tablets, the small ones used for daily distribution records — with a speed and concentration I found genuinely unsettling: a man for whom the world outside the tablets had ceased to exist. His lips moved slightly as he read. He arranged them and rearranged them. He put three aside. He found two more in the stack and put them with the three. He held his lamp close and studied the physical impression of the notches in a way that seemed to be about something other than their content.

A besieged city makes less noise than a free one. I had noticed this over the three months — less hammering from the smiths' quarter above us, fewer children in the streets audible even from this depth below ground. We had learned, all of us, to make less of ourselves. To shrink. Holkos, crouched over his tablets in the grain-store of a city that was slowly learning to be quiet, was the only thing in Wilusa that did not seem to be diminishing.

He came and stood beside me.

"Pramnos was not killed by a Greek," he said.

"No," I said. I had known it since I saw the arms. I had not said it to anyone until now, because Telamon-Sur had not asked and because I am, at heart, a man who keeps tallies rather than a man who volunteers conclusions he cannot yet prove.

"He was killed by a man who had access to this room before the door was locked for the night. The door was not forced and was not unlocked with the key — the bar-socket shows no fresh scoring on the lower edge, which would be present if the bar had been lifted from outside with a tool." He paused. "The smell you registered but could not name is hemlock oil — diluted and mixed with cedar, which is why it read as familiar without resolving into a name. It was introduced through the ventilation channel above the fourth pithos. Do you see the dark smear on the channel lip? That is from a ceramic vessel — lowered, then withdrawn. The hemlock dripped into the room after the door was barred for the night. Pramnos breathed it. He lay down, because the sedation came gently and he thought he was simply tired. Then the dose became sufficient." He looked at the body. "His arms are arranged because whoever killed him returned before Siku — opened the door, arranged the body, and left. They had a key. Not the morning key. The supervisory key. There are three. Do you know where each is held?"

I said I knew where two of them were.

"Then we already know something about who held the third. But first," he said, "I want to see the passage."

IV.

I did not know the passage he meant until he described it — the service corridor that ran behind the north face of the administrative quarter, cut from the same rock as the store-rooms, used in the old design for moving grain sacks before the main stairway was widened. Most of the people currently working in the citadel had no occasion to know it existed. I knew it only because I had once helped Premnath retrieve a misfiled archive box from the alcove at its eastern end.

I told Holkos this.

"Good," he said. "Then you know the way."

We went at the second hour of afternoon, after Holkos had sent word — through Telamon-Sur, who had returned by then and was managing his fear by occupying the outer archive with the concentrated stillness of a man trying very hard to look like furniture — that the full distribution records would need to be assembled for a formal accounting review, and that both distribution assistants should attend, along with the archive-keeper. The word he chose was accounting review. Deliberate, I think. An accounting review is a dreary thing. It does not put people on their guard the way an accusation does.

The north passage was accessed from a door in the outer wall of the administrative corridor — a door I had walked past uncounted times and registered as a store-cupboard. The bar was iron and heavy, but it moved in its socket with a smoothness that spoke of recent use. The interior was dark. Holkos lit his lamp.

The corridor was perhaps twelve paces long and smelled of old stone and cedar resin. That last detail he noted without a word — simply raised his head slightly, the way a dog lifts its muzzle, and moved the lamp in a slow arc along the wall.

The channel opening was in the floor of an alcove at the northern end. Below it, four feet down through solid rock, was the ceiling of the grain-store's inner room. The channel was six inches across — enough for a rope, and for a ceramic vessel on the end of a rope, and for the kind of patience required to drip hemlock oil through six inches of darkness into a room where a man was breathing.

Holkos crouched at the channel mouth and held his lamp low. He studied the stone lip. Then he sat back on his heels and turned the lamp toward the floor of the alcove itself, moving it in a slow, careful circle.

There was a heel-mark in the dust near the channel. Fresh — within the day, I would have said, from the sharpness of its edge. Holkos looked at it for a long moment, then looked at me.

"Someone has been here since dawn," he said. Not alarmed. Merely noting a fact. But he rose more quickly than I had seen him move before, and he moved to the door of the passage and set his back against the wall beside it rather than in front of it, which struck me as the instinct of a man who does not care to be silhouetted in a doorway when he opens it.

He opened it. The corridor beyond was empty.

"They are checking whether the evidence can be read," he said. "Which tells us they know it is there to be read. Which tells us they know what they did." He handed me the lamp and took out his bone measuring-tablet. "Hold that higher."

My hand, I will admit, was not entirely steady. I held it higher.

He measured the angle of the channel mouth against the alcove wall, writing quickly on the bone. Then he straightened, put the tablet away, and looked at me with those simultaneously-attending eyes.

"Now," he said, with the particular satisfaction of a man who has confirmed what he already knew and found the confirmation more interesting than expected, "we are ready for the accounting review."

V.

Telamon-Sur was in the outer archive when we returned. He had arranged himself behind the tallying bench with the expression of a man who has decided that proximity to official furniture lends him official weight. It did not, notably, but I gave him credit for the attempt.

Holkos glanced at him. "You will remain," he said. "I require a witness of sufficient authority to make the proceedings unimpeachable."

Telamon-Sur straightened almost imperceptibly. Sufficient authority was a thing he understood. What he had not understood, and what became apparent to me only as Holkos arranged the room — positioning Telamon-Sur at the bench where the tablets would be visible to him, placing himself at the centre, instructing me to stand near the door — was that he had been given a function in what was about to happen. He was not furniture. He was a fixed point. His dread of the chancellery was not an inconvenience to be managed; it was a mechanism Holkos intended to use.

Koros and Premnath arrived together, which told me something I filed away without examining yet. Koros was a broad man — not tall, but built with the density of someone who had spent years moving heavy things and had decided somewhere along the way that physical mass was its own form of argument. He had been pleasant enough in the ordinary run of the stores-work — quiet, methodical, apparently without edges. Looking at him now, I revised that assessment. The pleasantness had been a surface, worn smooth by practice, and what lay beneath it was harder and more watchful than anything I had seen from him before. He moved into the room the way a man moves into a space he is calculating.

Premnath moved differently. He was a slight man, dry as old papyrus, with the quality of stillness that archive-keepers develop over years of knowing things they are not asked and saying nothing they are not required to say. He had cultivated that stillness as a professional instrument, and it was a fine one. He took his position at the edge of the room with the composure of a man who has decided, in advance, what this meeting is and what his role within it will be. His eyes went first to the tablets stacked on the ledge, then to Holkos, then — briefly, carefully — to the door behind me.

"The accounting review," Holkos said, pleasantly.

He walked to the tablet stack. He selected three of the tablets from the upper portion — not the ones he had separated earlier; I noted this — and laid them on the bench before Telamon-Sur with a deliberateness that drew everyone's gaze.

"I have been examining the distribution records for the past twelve days," he said. "The discrepancy in the totals is small — four or five units per cycle — but it is consistent, and the twelve-day aggregate is sufficient to provision a vessel for an eighteen-day voyage." He paused, letting that establish itself. "More interesting than the discrepancy, however, is this."

He set his own lamp close to the tablets and leaned over them.

"The notch-pressure on the records from the past nine days is different on three of the tablets. Shallower. A slight inconsistency at the top of each vertical stroke, of the kind produced when a left-handed man adjusts his grip to cut notches that appear right-handed." He straightened and looked directly at Koros. "You kept the records for the past nine days. You are left-handed. The adjustment required to produce this inconsistency is characteristic and unmistakeable."

Koros said nothing. But his hands — large, capable hands, the hands of a man who moved grain-sacks — had flattened against his thighs.

"Furthermore," Holkos continued, in the same measured tone, "I have found, on the three tablets you falsified, evidence of a second set of marks. Beneath the notches you cut, in the clay, there are impressions from a previous set of records — records that were smoothed and recut. The original impressions show the true figures." He looked up. "The original impressions show you."

I knew, as he said it, that the second set of marks was not there. I had watched him with those tablets for an hour. He had found the notch inconsistency; he had found nothing beneath the clay, because there was nothing beneath the clay to find. The tablets Holkos had set before Telamon-Sur were not even the ones he had separated.

I kept my face still. This, I understood, was the accounting review.

Koros moved first, and he moved fast — not toward the tablets this time, but toward the door, toward me, because I was between him and the corridor and I am not a large man and I expect he had looked at me and made a calculation in the same way he had made all his other calculations. He was correct that I was not large. He was incorrect that I was the only obstacle.

Holkos had crossed the room in three strides before Koros reached me, and he did something with his forearm and the door-frame that I cannot entirely reconstruct because it happened very quickly and involved a degree of controlled violence that I had not associated with a man who had spent the last hour reading clay tablets. Koros hit the door-frame with his shoulder and staggered, and in the moment of staggering Telamon-Sur — to his lasting credit, and I do not think I would have predicted it — came off his bench and placed himself in the doorway with a solidity that was, for once, exactly the right quality for the moment.

Koros stopped. He was breathing hard. He looked at Holkos, then at Telamon-Sur blocking the door, then at Holkos again.

"Sit down," Holkos said. Not the pleasantry of the previous version — the instruction of a man who has already determined the outcome and is simply waiting for the room to catch up.

Koros sat.

In the silence that followed, I became aware that Premnath had not moved. He was still at the edge of the room where he had positioned himself on arrival, still with that practised stillness, and he was watching Holkos with an expression that was not, quite, the expression of a man watching something he does not understand.

"The marks beneath the clay," Premnath said, in a voice that was admirably level. "You will need to demonstrate those to the judicial council. The standard of evidence for a capital charge requires physical examination by the council's own record-keepers."

"It does," Holkos agreed.

"Then I suggest we allow the council to conduct that examination before any further characterisations are made." He looked at Telamon-Sur. "The duty officer will know that a formal accusation requires formal procedure."

He was good. I had always known he was competent — an archive-keeper's competence, thorough and careful and oriented around the preservation of form. He was using that competence now, and it was, in its way, impressive. He had assessed the situation, identified the weakness in Holkos's demonstration, and was attempting to apply procedural pressure to the one person in the room — Telamon-Sur — who was most susceptible to it. He had a voice, Premnath. He had a mind. And he had decided, in the seconds since Koros's lunge, that his best option was not silence but precision.

"You are correct," Holkos said, "that the marks beneath the clay will need to be verified." He moved to the original tablet stack — the ones he had separated earlier, which had been sitting at the end of the ledge throughout — and selected the three he had isolated in the morning. He placed them beside the others. "These are the tablets I set aside at the first hour of this morning. These are the ones that bear the notch inconsistency I described. I have examined their reverse faces, which are unworked clay. There are no impressions on the reverse. Which means the marks I described were not, in the physical sense, there to be found."

He said it without emphasis. A man reporting an accurate figure.

"But you are here," he said to Premnath, "to tell me that they should have been examined by the council before I made a characterisation. Which means you understand that I was making a characterisation about the tablets. Which means you know which tablets I was discussing. Which means you know what is on them." He paused. "You have not touched these tablets. You have not seen me examine them. You have been in this room for four minutes. The only way you can know that my characterisation is challengeable — that the marks I described might not be present — is if you know the process by which those marks were and were not made."

The silence had a different quality now.

Premnath's stillness did not break. But it changed — the change was small, the way the surface of water changes when something moves beneath it rather than above it — and I saw him understand, in the moment of that change, that the accounting review had never been about the tablets.

"The ventilation channel in the north passage," Holkos said. "You opened the passage door. You stood in the alcove at the northern end. You lowered a ceramic vessel through the channel and dripped hemlock oil, diluted with cedar, into the room below. You withdrew the vessel. You waited. Then you descended, unlocked the grain-store with the supervisory key, arranged Pramnos's body so that the scene would read as a man who had fallen rather than a man who had been placed, and returned to your quarters before Siku arrived." He looked at Premnath. "You were thorough. The detail of the cedar oil was well-chosen — it masked the hemlock and read as an ambient smell rather than a foreign one. The arrangement of the body was careful. You simply did not account for the fact that someone came to the passage after dawn, to verify that the channel showed nothing, and left a heel-mark in the dust. I measured the angle of the channel mouth against the alcove wall this afternoon and confirmed the drip pattern on the inner hem of Pramnos's robe — it is directional, consistent with withdrawal from the north, not from above. The drip is yours."

Premnath looked at him. He was still composed. But the composure had changed in the way composure changes when it is being maintained by will rather than ease.

"I have nothing further to say," he said, "without the council present."

"No," Holkos agreed. "You have nothing further to say at all." He turned to Telamon-Sur. "The supervisory key is the remaining piece. It was not stolen from the chamberlain's office, as your report suggested. I think you know this. I think you sent for me because you knew it, and because you understood that a report attributing the theft to a Greek assassin would not survive a council review if someone — anyone — examined the bar-socket and found no scoring." He let that sit for a moment, with the patience of a man who has laid a count before an administrator and is waiting for the administrator to confirm it. "The key question, from the perspective of your own position, is whether the official record reflects what the evidence will show, or whether it continues to reflect what you initially reported."

Telamon-Sur's face moved through three distinct expressions in quick succession, none of which he managed to conceal. He arrived at something that was not quite resolution but was adjacent to it — the expression of a man who has looked at two sets of numbers and identified which one will still be accurate in six months.

"I will amend the report," he said.

"Good," said Holkos. "I will require a copy of the amendment for my own record."

VI.

Koros was tried before the city's judicial council on the thirteenth day of the third siege-month. The charge: the murder of Pramnos and the falsification of the royal stores record. The evidence — the tallies, Holkos's analysis of the notch-pressure, the drip pattern on the robe hem and its directional geometry, the testimony of Siku regarding the arrangement of the body — was sufficient. Koros was convicted. The penalty, under Hittite administrative practice, was significant.

Premnath was tried alongside him as a knowing accessory. He said nothing at his trial that he had not said in the archive room — which is to say, nothing. He was convicted.

Arion-Bel was not tried.

I should be honest about this, because the record should be honest.

Arion-Bel had been chamberlain for sixteen years. He had managed the stores through three droughts, two coastal raids, and the grinding attrition of the siege's first months, during which the trading routes through the straits had closed one by one as the Greek fleet settled into its positions and the flow of Aegean goods that Wilusa had grown dependent on over two generations of prosperity simply stopped. He had watched the city's reserves shrink with the attention of a man who understood precisely what the numbers meant.

He had also, at some point in the months before Pramnos died, entered into an arrangement with a party outside the walls. Not the Greeks — that was Telamon-Sur's assumption, which tells you something about the limits of Telamon-Sur's imagination. The grain diverted by Koros went to a particular merchant family in the lower town whose routes ran east — overland, into the Hittite interior, along the road toward Hattusa. Arion-Bel was not a traitor in the manner that a duty officer might imagine, with gold and enemy plans. He was a man who had looked at the numbers and concluded, with the cold accuracy of a lifetime spent in their company, that the city was going to fall — not in a year, perhaps, but in three, or five — and who had begun, in the methodical way of a man trained to think in inventories, to arrange his family's future somewhere that would still exist after the citadel was rubble.

The grain was passage price. Not for him — not yet. For his wife. For his three daughters. The youngest was four years old.

Holkos presented his full analysis to Telamon-Sur in a private meeting to which I was not admitted but of which Holkos afterward saw no reason to conceal from me. He presented the evidence regarding Arion-Bel. He explained the arrangement with the eastern merchant family. He explained the motive.

Telamon-Sur listened. He was not a stupid man. He understood what he was being told, and he understood what would follow if he acted on it.

Wilusa in the third month of the siege was a city that required believing it could survive. The chamberlain was the man who managed that belief as much as the stores themselves. A chamberlain who has run the numbers and concluded the city will fall is a fact that cannot be released into a besieged city without consequences that dwarf any particular grain diversion.

Telamon-Sur wrote in his official report that the diversion had been perpetrated by Koros and Premnath, motivated by private gain. He wrote that the supervisory key had been stolen from the chamberlain's office and subsequently recovered. He wrote nothing about Arion-Bel.

I will note, for the record, that Telamon-Sur's own position was also not unaffected by this arrangement — the official report that now stood was the one he had dispatched before Holkos arrived, the one that had attributed the death to Greek infiltration. The revised account that reached the chancellery attributed the error to incomplete initial information, and the man responsible for that revision was Holkos, not Telamon-Sur. Some obligations, I have learned, are paid in silence.

The report went to the King's chancellery.

Arion-Bel continued to manage the stores for another eleven months. I watched him do it. He was, as I have said, a capable man. His family — his wife, his three daughters — left the city in the eighth month, joining a convoy of non-combatant dependents evacuated at the King's direction. They travelled east. I do not know where they arrived.

When the city fell, in its own time, Arion-Bel was not among those who departed.

VII.

Holkos said to me, when I asked him — about whether the resolution had been just — that justice was a word that covered several different calculations, and that the function of a just outcome was not always to punish every person who deserved punishment. Sometimes it was to remove the immediate threat, stabilise the structure, and leave certain matters for the judgment of time.

"The law," he said, "is a counting-board. It produces accurate answers, but only for the numbers you put into it. Arion-Bel put his family on a road east. The city gets its chamberlain. The grain-store gets its integrity restored. Pramnos gets two men convicted of his death." He was quiet. "The child is four years old."

I wrote it down. I write it down here. I do not say he was wrong. I say only that I am not certain he was right, and that the uncertainty is one of the things I find I cannot set aside, even now.

VIII.

On the morning after the trial, I found him in the lower grain-store again. He was standing at the fourth pithos, looking up at the ventilation channel. The body was long gone. The room had been cleaned. The morning distribution had already begun from the outer stores.

I asked him what he was looking at.

He said he was looking at a question he had not answered.

"Koros is a left-handed man who cut false tallies with his right hand," he said. "He is careful. He is patient. Nine days of meticulous, considered diversion. But the killing of Pramnos was rushed — a response to a sudden threat. Which means someone communicated that threat to Koros quickly, through a channel I do not know. Someone told him that Pramnos was about to speak. And someone told him about this corridor." He looked at the channel. "Koros managed distribution. He knew the stores. He did not know that passage. Someone with a longer reach than a distribution assistant showed him what it could be used for. And Premnath opened the door, yes — but I am not certain Premnath was the first to propose it."

He looked at me with those dark, simultaneously-attending eyes.

"There is a merchant in the lower city whose name I will not write here. He trades in cedar oil and dried fish and certain commodities that appear in no official manifest. He has been in Wilusa for three years, and his routes run in three directions simultaneously, which is not how a cedar-oil merchant's routes work. I have been watching him for two months, for reasons unrelated to this case. He is, I think, the man who told Koros about the channel, and the man who told someone — not Arion-Bel, but someone — that Pramnos was a problem." He paused. "A siege is a container. Things concentrate inside a container. That merchant is the edge of something larger — a web of arrangements that were in place before the Greeks arrived and will be in place, in some form, after this city is a memory. I would very much like to find where that web anchors."

He never did, to my knowledge. The city's end came faster than the patterns resolved. He departed — with his records and his family, in the manner of a man who had read his own analysis carefully — before the last season. He had watched the catastrophe build with the attention of a man whose survival depended on reading it correctly, and when the reading was complete, he acted on it.

I departed with him, or near enough. The records came too. These are among them.

Here the private notes of Eutykhos end on this matter.

The tally tablets referenced in the account, or copies of them, were recovered in the eastern administrative archive at the secondary site. The original store-room no longer exists. The city above it is a particular kind of ruin — the kind that has been rebuilt so many times over the centuries that the layers no longer speak to each other without patient reading. But the grain-channels cut into the rock below remain, as rock-cut channels tend to.

They are the most honest part of what survives.
