HOLKOS OF WILUSA

~1240 BC — Wilusa, Anatolia

Wilusa is the Hittite name for the region the Greeks called Ilios — the territory of Troy, on the north-western tip of Anatolia, where the Aegean meets the Propontis and the control of the straits means the control of the trade between two worlds. Holkos lived there at precisely the period when the tension between Mycenaean ambition and Hittite suzerainty over Wilusa was building toward the conflict that Greek tradition would remember as the Trojan War, and he watched it build with the attention of a man whose survival depended on reading it correctly.

The war, when it came, was not a single event but a decade of siege, raid, negotiation, and attrition that involved not only the Mycenaean forces and the defenders of the citadel but every trading family, every minor settlement, every person in the region with something to lose. Holkos of Wilusa was not a warrior. He was a man who read the region’s condition with the same methodical attention he brought to a merchant’s accounts, and who understood that the most valuable intelligence is always the intelligence that arrives before it is needed. He survived the fall by understanding what the evidence around him required him to understand, and he departed in time. He took with him his family, his records, and the accumulated intelligence of a lifetime spent watching a catastrophe assemble itself from its components.

The surviving account is attributed to a man named Eutykhos, a minor steward in service to the Wilusan chamberlain. Eutykhos kept tally of grain distributions and storehouse allocations, but he also maintained private notes on matters that drew his attention beyond routine accounting. His perspective is partial and sometimes shaped by his own position within the administrative structure, yet his record preserves the sequence of observations and actions that follow.

The Private Notes Of Eutykhos

Set down in the third month of the siege, when the Greek fires could be seen from the eastern tower on clear nights, and the sound of the lower city had already changed — less hammering from the smiths' quarter, fewer children in the streets below the wall. A city under siege learns to make less noise. It is one of the first things to go.

The grain-stores of Wilusa occupied the lower level of the great citadel complex, cut into the living rock beneath the administrative quarter so that the temperature remained constant through both the killing heat of summer and the mountain cold of winter. This was not an accident of architecture. The man who had designed them had understood that a city under pressure is a city that will eventually be fed or not be fed, and that the distance between those two outcomes is measured in what you keep and what you lose. The stores were the city’s true heart. Everyone who understood Wilusa understood this. Including, as it proved, the man who would later stand in the smaller of the two grain rooms with blood on the hem of his robe and a story that did not survive contact with Holkos for more than the length of a single morning.

But I am writing this in sequence, as it happened, because that is how Holkos asked me to set it down. The sequence matters, he told me. A man who reads the end first has already decided what the middle means. A man who reads the middle first sees what is actually there.

I will try to honour that instruction, though I confess the beginning is not a pleasant thing to revisit.

The body of Pramnos the younger steward was found by the door-boy Siku at the first hour of light, when the boy came to open the store-room for the morning distribution.

I should describe Pramnos, because Holkos always said you cannot understand a death without first understanding the life. Pramnos was thirty years old, or near enough. He had served the Chamberlain Arion-Bel for eleven years, having come up from the lower administrative lists through a combination of genuine competence with the tally-notch system and a remarkable memory for faces — he could recall every merchant, every porter, every soldier who had passed through the distribution point in a given week, and he could recall them a month later when the accounts were being reconciled. He was not a man who made enemies easily. He was, if I am to be honest, the sort of man who disappears into the function he performs — present, reliable, essentially invisible. The invisibility, as Holkos would demonstrate, was the thing that got him killed.

He was found lying on his back between the third and fourth storage vessels — the great clay pithoi that stood chest-high and held the winter barley. His arms were at his sides with a neatness that struck me immediately as wrong, as though he had been arranged rather than fallen. The oil lamp in the niche above him was burned entirely out, the wick cold and flat. There was no wound visible from the doorway. The room smelled of barley-dust and lamp-black and something else, something I could not name but registered as out of place — a faint sharpness beneath the grain-smell, almost like the copper-tang of the smithy quarter, but softer. More intimate.

Siku ran for the guard. The guard fetched the duty officer, a man named Telamon-Sur. Telamon-Sur looked at the body, looked at the locked door — which Siku had unlocked with the morning key — and said, with the certainty of a man who has found his answer before he has finished asking the question: The Greeks have found a way inside the walls. There will be an assassin in the lower city by now. Seal the gates.

The gates were sealed. The lower city was searched. No assassin was found. Telamon-Sur wrote his report anyway — a matter of enemy infiltration, circumstances consistent with Greek sabotage of our administrative personnel — and had it sealed for dispatch to the King’s chancellery. Then someone, and I do not know who, thought to send for Holkos.

I had encountered Holkos perhaps a dozen times before the morning he came down to the grain-store. My impressions of him had been formed at a distance and through the medium of other men’s reports, which are always unreliable instruments.

He was a lean man, perhaps fifty, with a face that gave away nothing. He dressed simply — a plain wool chiton of undyed grey, good leather sandals, no jewelry except a single ring of Hittite bronze on the right hand that he wore not for display but for the edge of the seal-stone, which he used as a measuring tool. He had long hands and a habit of stillness that was different from the stillness of calm. It was the stillness of something attending. His eyes were dark and moved in a way that suggested they were always, simultaneously, looking at two different things.

When he was engaged in a case — and I had heard this from Premnath the archive-keeper, who had worked with him on the matter of the missing temple inventory two years prior — he was a different creature altogether. Premnath described a man who did not sleep, who forgot to eat, who spoke in rapid half-sentences that only resolved into meaning several exchanges after they were uttered. Between cases, Premnath said, he was barely present. He would sit in the administrative quarter for whole afternoons doing nothing visible, or he would be found in the market, studying the arrangement of goods on a trader’s table with an intensity that made the trader nervous. He had a particular weakness for solving the coded tallies that Aegean merchants sometimes used to hide their actual cargo manifests from port inspectors — not because he had any commercial interest, but because the puzzle was, in his words, the only thing quiet enough not to bore me to madness.

He arrived at the grain-store an hour after Telamon-Sur had sealed and departed. He arrived alone, which was unusual. He had sent no message ahead. He simply appeared in the doorway, looked at the body for a long moment, and then looked at me.

“You are Eutykhos.”

“I am.”

“You keep the distribution tallies for this store.”

“Among others, yes.”

“Bring them.”

He did not look at me again after that. He crossed to the body and crouched beside it with the composed attention of a man examining an interesting piece of pottery, and he began to work.

I brought the tallies. They were clay tablets, the small ones used for daily distribution records, stacked in the archive box by date. I set them on the ledge near the door and waited, because it seemed clear that interruption would not be welcome and that my function, for the moment, was to be available.

Holkos examined Pramnos for perhaps a quarter of an hour without speaking. He examined the hands — each finger separately. He examined the neck, tilting the head with careful hands, studying the skin. He examined the nose and the lips. He examined the eyes, pressing the lids gently back and studying what was underneath them with the lamp he had brought — his own lamp, I noticed, not the wall-fixture, and the oil in it smelled different from the stores we used, lighter, cleaner. He pressed two fingers to the centre of the chest and held them there, as though counting something that was no longer there to count. He studied the hem of the robe — particularly the inner face of the hem near the right ankle, where there was a faint staining I had not noticed.

Then he stood, crossed to the pithoi, and studied them in order. He was looking at the stoppers — the clay caps that sealed the mouth of each vessel — and at the surface of the clay around each stopper. He crouched and looked at the floor between the vessels. He looked at the ceiling, which was bare rock. He looked at the door-socket, the groove worn into the rock where the door-bar rested. He took a piece of smooth bone from a fold of his chiton and measured the gap between the bottom of the door and the threshold stone, then wrote something on the bone with a small stylus he wore tucked behind his ear.

He crossed to the tallies.

For the next hour he read them — all of them, not just the recent dates — with a speed and concentration that I found genuinely unsettling. His lips moved slightly as he read, a habit I had seen in archive-keepers but never with that quality of absolute focus, as though the world outside the tablets had ceased to exist. He arranged them and rearranged them. He put three aside. He found two more in the stack and put them with the three. He held the lamp close and studied the physical impression of the notches in a way that seemed to be about something other than their content.

Then he came and stood beside me.

“Pramnos was not killed by a Greek,” he said.

“No,” I said. I had known this since I saw the arranged arms, but I had not said it to anyone.

“He was killed by a man who had access to this room before the door was locked for the night. The door was not forced and was not unlocked with the key — the bar-socket shows no fresh scoring on the lower edge, which would be present if the bar had been lifted from outside with a tool. He was killed with something introduced through the ventilation channel above the fourth pithos — do you see the dark smear on the channel lip? That is from a vessel, ceramic, that was lowered and then withdrawn. The smell you are trying to name is hemlock oil. Not pure hemlock — diluted and mixed with cedar, which is why you registered it as familiar but could not place it. It was dripped into the room through the channel after the door was barred. Pramnos breathed it. He lay down — you can see from the arrangement that he lay down voluntarily, which means the sedation came first, gently, and he thought he was simply tired. Then the dose became sufficient. He died quietly. His arms are arranged because whoever killed him came in at first light, before Siku, opened the door, arranged the body, and left. They had a key. Not the morning key. The supervisory key. There are three. Do you know where each of them is?”

I said I knew where two of them were.

He said, “Then we already know who held the third.”

Holkos had a way, when explaining his reasoning, of seeming genuinely puzzled by the need to explain it at all — as though the connections between facts were so visible to him that setting them out in sequence required a conscious act of restraint, the way a man of complete sight might have to think carefully before describing colour to a man who sees only shadow.

He had summoned me to the outer room of the archive by midday, where we could speak without the presence of the body. He had also summoned, with a quiet directness that suggested the request was not optional, the archive-keeper Premnath, and the two distribution assistants who had worked the previous day’s allocation under Pramnos.

He stood with his back to the ventilation screen, the light behind him, and he worked through it the way a weaver works a loom — each thread placed exactly, the pattern emerging from the placing.

“The tallies,” he said, “tell three stories. The first story is the one they are meant to tell: grain received, grain distributed, the balance carried forward. This story is correct in its general form. But the second story is visible in the gap between the weights recorded and the distribution figures from the last nine days. The gap is small — four or five units per distribution cycle, not enough to notice if you are reading the daily record in isolation. But if you read the twelve-day cycle as a unit, the total divergence from the inventory figure is sufficient to provision a small vessel for an eighteen-day voyage, or to supply a party of twenty men for six days at reduced ration.”

He paused. One of the distribution assistants, a man named Koros, had gone the colour of river clay.

“The third story,” Holkos continued, is visible in the physical character of the tallies themselves. The notch-pressure for the past nine days is different on three of the tablets — slightly shallower, the angle of the stylus slightly different. Pramnos used a particular grip — you can see it in his older work, a slight leftward lean on the vertical strokes. These three tablets were not cut by Pramnos. They were cut by someone copying Pramnos’s record style with considerable skill, but not his specific grip. Someone who is left-handed, in fact, cutting notches to appear right-handed, which requires a conscious adjustment of pressure that leaves a characteristic inconsistency at the top of each vertical stroke.” He looked at Koros. “You are left-handed.”

Koros said nothing. His mouth was open.

“Pramnos noticed,” Holkos said. “He was a man with a remarkable memory for detail — this is well attested. He noticed the discrepancy in the tally notches, or he noticed the physical disparity in the grain-store, or he noticed both. He confronted someone. Or he was about to. The timing of his death — at the precise point in the distribution cycle where the accumulated diversion would have become visible in the nine-day reconciliation — tells us this was not planned far in advance. This was a response to an immediate threat. Someone was warned that Pramnos was about to speak.”

He let that sit for a moment.

“Koros diverted the grain,” he said. “He did not act alone — the supervisory key is not his to hold, and the ventilation channel above the fourth pithos is not accessible from inside this building. It is accessible from the passage behind the north face of the administrative quarter. That passage key is held by the chamberlain’s office.”

He looked at Premnath, who had been standing very quietly at the edge of the group and whose quietness, I now understood, was of a different quality from the quietness of a man who is merely listening.

I should say something here about Arion-Bel, because the archive will record him as a loyal servant of the citadel and a capable administrator, and he was both of these things, and this is also not the complete account.

Arion-Bel had been chamberlain for sixteen years. He had managed the stores through three droughts, two coastal raids, and the slow, grinding attrition of the siege’s first months, during which the trading routes through the straits had closed one by one as the Greek fleet settled into its positions and the flow of Aegean goods that Wilusa had grown dependent on over two generations of prosperity simply stopped. He had watched the city’s reserves shrink with the attention of a man who understood precisely what the numbers meant.

He had also, at some point in the months before Pramnos died, entered into an arrangement with a party outside the walls.

Holkos explained this to me later, privately, when the formal proceeding was concluded. The grain that Koros had diverted did not go to the Greeks. It went to a particular merchant family in the lower town whose routes ran east — overland, into the Hittite interior, along the road toward Hattusa. Arion-Bel was not a traitor to the city in the way that Telamon-Sur had imagined, with Greek gold and Greek plans. He was a man who had looked at the numbers and concluded that the city was going to fall — not in a year, perhaps, but in three, or five, and that the fall would come from inside, from the slow arithmetic of consumption exceeding supply — and who had begun, in the methodical way of a man trained to think in inventories, to arrange his family’s future in a place that would still exist after the citadel was rubble.

The grain was being paid to the merchants as passage price. Not for him — not yet. For his family. His wife. His three daughters. The youngest was four years old.

Premnath had known. He had not arranged the killing — that was Koros, acting on the instruction that something needed to be done about Pramnos, an instruction Arion-Bel had given in terms carefully imprecise enough that the actual doing of it remained Koros’s decision and Koros’s act. But Premnath had opened the passage. He had provided the access. He had stood in the north corridor and listened to a man breathe himself to death through a ventilation channel, and then he had descended and arranged the body before Siku arrived.

He was a thorough man, Premnath. He had simply not accounted for Holkos.

Koros was tried before the city’s judicial council on the thirteenth day of the third siege-month. The charge was the murder of Pramnos and the falsification of the royal stores record. The evidence — the tallies, Holkos’s analysis of the physical record, the testimony of the door-boy Siku regarding the arrangement of the body — was sufficient. Koros was convicted. The penalty for the falsification of royal stores was, in Hittite administrative practice, significant.

Premnath was tried alongside him as a knowing accessory to both the falsification and the death. He was convicted.

Arion-Bel was not tried.

I should be honest about this, because the record should be honest.

Holkos presented his full analysis to Telamon-Sur in a private meeting to which I was not invited but of which I received an account from Holkos afterward, who saw no reason to hide it from me. He presented the evidence regarding Arion-Bel. He explained the arrangement with the eastern merchant family. He explained the motive — not Greek collaboration, but survival arithmetic, the calculations of a man who had managed a city’s stores long enough to understand the mathematics of an extended siege with perfect, merciless clarity.

Telamon-Sur listened. He was not a stupid man. He understood what he was being told, and he understood what would follow if he acted on it.

Wilusa in the third month of the siege was a city that required believing it could survive. The chamberlain was the man who managed the belief as much as the stores. His competence was not in question — indeed, his competence was precisely the problem. A chamberlain who has run the numbers and concluded the city will fall is a fact that cannot be released into a besieged city without consequences that dwarf the loss of any particular grain allocation.

Telamon-Sur wrote in his official report that the diversion of stores had been perpetrated by Koros acting in concert with Premnath, motivated by private gain. He wrote that the supervisory key had been stolen from the chamberlain’s office and subsequently recovered. He wrote nothing about Arion-Bel.

The report went to the King’s chancellery.

Arion-Bel continued to manage the stores for another eleven months. I watched him do it. He was, as I have said, a capable man. His family — his wife, his three daughters — left the city in the eighth month, joining a convoy of non-combatant dependents evacuated at the King’s direction. They traveled east. I do not know where they arrived.

When the city fell, in its own time, Arion-Bel was not among those who departed.

Holkos said to me, when I asked him about this — about whether the resolution had been just — that justice was a word that covered several different calculations, and that the function of a just outcome was not always to punish every person who deserved punishment. Sometimes it was to remove the immediate threat, stabilize the structure, and leave certain matters for the judgment of time.

“The law,” he said, “is a counting-board. It produces accurate answers, but only for the numbers you put into it. Arion-Bel put his family on a road east. The city gets its chamberlain. The grain-store gets its integrity restored. Pramnos gets two men convicted of his death.” He was quiet for a moment. “The child is four years old.”

I wrote it down. I write it down here. I do not say he was wrong. I say only that I am not certain he was right, and that the uncertainty is one of the things I find I cannot set aside, even now.

There is one more thing I should record, because I think it is the heart of what made Holkos different from the duty officers and the judicial councillors and the archive-keepers who surrounded him in that besieged city.

On the morning after the trial, I found him in the lower grain-store again. He was standing at the fourth pithos, looking up at the ventilation channel. The body was long gone. The room had been cleaned and the morning distribution had already begun from the outer stores.

I asked him what he was looking at.

He said he was looking at a question he had not answered.

I asked which question.

“Who told Koros that Pramnos was going to speak?” he said. “Koros is a left-handed man who cut false tallies with his right hand. He is careful. He is patient. He diverting grain over nine days is meticulous, considered work. But the killing of Pramnos was rushed. It was a response to a sudden threat — I said this, and I believe it. Which means someone communicated that threat to Koros quickly, and through a channel that I do not know.”

I asked if it mattered now, with Koros convicted and Premnath convicted and the trial closed.

“It matters,” he said, “because Koros did not have the idea of the ventilation channel. He managed distribution. He knew the stores. He did not know that corridor. Someone told him it existed and explained what it could be used for. Someone with a longer reach than a distribution assistant. And Premnath opened the passage door, yes — but I am not certain Premnath was the first person to suggest it to him.”

He looked at me. The dark eyes moved in that particular way.

“There is a merchant in the lower city,” he said, “whose name I will not write here. He trades in cedar oil and dried fish and certain commodities that do not appear in any official manifest. He has been in Wilusa for three years, and his routes run in three directions simultaneously, which is not how a cedar-oil merchant’s routes work. I have been watching him for two months, for reasons unrelated to this case. He is, I think, the man who told Koros about the channel, and the man who told someone — not Arion-Bel, but someone — that Pramnos was a problem.”

I asked what he intended to do about this.

“Nothing, yet,” he said. “I have no evidence that would satisfy a council. I have a pattern. A pattern is not a tally. But I intend to watch the pattern.” He looked back at the ventilation channel. “A siege is a container. Things concentrate inside a container. That merchant is the edge of something larger — a web of arrangements that were in place before the Greeks arrived and will be in place, in some form, after this city is a memory.” He was quiet for a moment. “I would very much like to find where that web anchors.”

He never did, to my knowledge. The city’s end came faster than the patterns resolved. He departed — with his records and his family, in the manner of a man who had read his own analysis carefully — before the last season.

I departed with him, or near enough. The records came too. These are among them.

Here the private notes of Eutykhos end on this matter.
The tally tablets referenced in the account, or copies of them, were recovered in the eastern administrative archive at the secondary site. The original store-room no longer exists. The city above it is a particular kind of ruin — the kind that has been rebuilt so many times over the centuries that the layers no longer speak to each other without patient reading. But the grain-channels cut into the rock below remain, as rock-cut channels tend to.
They are the most honest part of what survives.
