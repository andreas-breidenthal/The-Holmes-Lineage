# BOOK IV: THE FRANKISH & NORSE WORLD
## *~370 AD – ~1090 AD*
### Gothic Lands · Frankish Territory · Frisia · Vestfold · Yorkshire

---

### HULMAR THE GOTH
**~370 AD — Gothic territory / Frankish lands**

He was born into the great movement — the churning of peoples across the face of Europe that the Romans called, with characteristic understatement, the migrations, and that those who lived through it experienced as the dissolution of everything that had been fixed. The Visigoths were on the march, pressed from the east by the Huns and pressing in turn against the crumbling frontiers of Rome, and Hulmar moved with them — not as a warrior chieftain but as something the migrations needed equally and acknowledged less: a man who could assess a situation, read the intentions of strangers, and negotiate passage through hostile country without provoking the violence that was always, in those years, the easiest outcome.

His name was a Gothic rendering of the old name-element the family had carried westward from Constantinople — "Hulmar," cognate with the Norse "Holmr" that his descendants would bear, the sound shifting as the languages shifted around it. He settled eventually in the territory between the Rhine and the Meuse, where Gothic and Frankish and Roman remnants were beginning the long process of becoming something new. He left his son a position rather than an estate — which, in a world where estates could be taken in an afternoon, was the more durable inheritance.

### HULMAR OF THE RHINE
**~400 AD — Frankish territory**

The Rhine in 400 AD was not a boundary but a battlefield — or rather, a place where the idea of a boundary was being continuously tested and continuously failing. Hulmar of the Rhine grew up on its western bank in the generation that watched the river cease to mean anything, the Vandals and Burgundians and Alans crossing it in force in the winter of 406 and dissolving whatever remained of the Roman frontier in the west. He was a man formed by flux, which is to say he was a man who had learned, before he could fully articulate the lesson, that the world did not hold still and that those who survived were those who could read its movements in advance.

He worked as an interpreter and factor between the various peoples competing for the rich agricultural land of the Rhineland — a role that required not merely linguistic facility but the subtler skill of understanding what each party actually wanted beneath what they said they wanted, and finding the formulation that allowed both to believe they had prevailed. He was rarely wrong in his assessments, and his value to those who employed him lay precisely in that accuracy.

### HOLMAR OF BURGUNDY
**~430 AD — Burgundy, Frankish territory**

The Burgundians had settled in the Rhône valley and the territory that would take their name, and Holmar grew up among them — a family of Rhine German origin now embedded in a Burgundian political world that was itself embedded in the decaying Roman administrative structure of the south. The name was shifting: less Gothic now, more Frankish in its sound, the family adapting its identity as it always had to the prevailing culture without quite dissolving into it.

Holmar served the Burgundian court as a scribe and record-keeper, maintaining the kind of documentation — land grants, tribute records, the terms of agreements between petty kings and their subordinates — that allowed a polity to function as something more than organised plunder. He understood that written records were power of a particular and durable kind, that the man who held the documents often outlasted the man who held the sword, and he organised his life accordingly. His records were accurate and his copies were good and he died in his bed, which in fifth-century Burgundy represented a considerable achievement.

### HOLMAR THE FRANK
**~460 AD — Frankish territory**

By Holmar the Frank's generation, the family had completed its absorption into the Frankish world — the Gothic origins faded, the Rhine crossing two generations past, the name now sounding native to the country north of the Loire where the Franks were consolidating their hold on what had been Roman Gaul. He was a Frank in the way that his ancestors had been Romans and before that Greeks and before that Phoenicians: thoroughly, for as long as it lasted, and with one eye always on the horizon.

He administered land in the service of a Frankish noble house, a role that combined the functions of estate manager, legal officer, and intelligence agent — for the assessment of neighbouring estates, the loyalty of tenants, and the intentions of rival lords was not separable, in that world, from the daily business of keeping an account. He had his father's gift for reading documents and his grandfather's gift for reading men, and he applied both to the service of a family that was not his own with the professional detachment of someone who understood that competence was its own form of security.

- **Holmar of the Merovingians** — direct line; see next entry.

### HOLMAR OF THE MEROVINGIANS
**~490 AD — Frankish territory**

Clovis I converted to Catholic Christianity in 496 AD — an act of political calculation as much as spiritual conviction, aligning the Frankish kingdom with the bishops of Rome and the remaining Roman population of Gaul in a single stroke of extraordinary strategic intelligence. Holmar of the Merovingians was a young man in Clovis's court when the conversion occurred, and he recognised it for what it was with an admiration that was partly professional. Here was the gift applied at the scale of kingdoms: the reading of a situation — religious, political, demographic — and the identification of the single decisive act that would reshape it permanently.

He served in the Merovingian administration through the consolidation of the Frankish realm, managing the correspondence between the court and the ecclesiastical hierarchy that was now the kingdom's most important political relationship. He was a Catholic, as all sensible men became Catholic in that decade, and he wore the faith with the same comfortable practicality with which his ancestors had worn their successive identities — sincerely enough to function, without allowing it to obstruct his judgement.

- **Holmar of Austrasia** — direct line; see next entry.

### HOLMAR OF AUSTRASIA
**~520 AD — Austrasia, Frankish territory**

Austrasia — the eastern Frankish realm, centred on the Meuse and Rhine valleys — was the more Germanic half of the Frankish world, rougher and less Romanised than the south, and Holmar grew up in it with the adaptability that had characterised his family across a dozen cultures and five centuries. The Merovingian dynasty was at the height of its power and already, to a careful observer, beginning to show the internal fractures that would eventually produce the long crisis of the rois fainéants — the do-nothing kings — and the rise of the Carolingian mayors of the palace.

Holmar was a careful observer. He served as a legal officer in the Austrasian court, adjudicating disputes between the Germanic and Romanised populations according to the separate legal codes each community used — a role that required him to hold two incompatible systems of law simultaneously in his head and find, between them, outcomes that both parties could accept. He was, in the terminology of a much later age, a remarkably effective mediator. The secret, as he might have explained it, was simple: listen to what each man says, identify what he has not said, and find the fact he is most afraid of you discovering. The rest follows naturally.

- **Holmar of the Rhine** — direct line; see next entry.

### HOLMAR OF THE RHINE
**~550 AD — Rhine, Frankish territory**

The second Holmar of the Rhine was not a migrant like the first but a settled man — a man of a particular valley and a particular estate, rooted in the rich agricultural land between Cologne and the sea that had been his family's territory for three generations. The Frankish world in 550 was more stable than it had been in 400, more organised, more Christian, and considerably more violent in the specific, calculated way of a society that had invented feudalism and was working out its implications.

He managed the family's land holdings with the methodical precision that had characterised the line since the scribal schools of Nippur, recording every transaction, every tenancy, every obligation in documents that survived him by generations. He had a particular facility for detecting the falsified account — the altered measure, the understated yield, the inflated claim — that his estate workers quickly learned to respect, or rather to fear. Nothing was taken from him twice. He was a fair man, by the standards of his age, which is to say he was harder than mercy and kinder than the law required.

### HOLMAR NORTHWARD
**~580 AD — Rhine / Frisia**

Something in this generation turned toward the sea. It may have been a marriage into a Frisian trading family, or simply the pull that the North Sea coast has always exerted on men of restless and acquisitive intelligence — the sense that the horizon there is not a boundary but an invitation. Holmar Northward, as he came to be known in the family's oral tradition, left the Rhine valley of his father and moved north and west toward the coast, toward the flat salt marshes and the grey water and the Frisian merchants who were, in the late sixth century, in the process of building the most extensive trading network in northern Europe.

He arrived with nothing except the family's talent for assessment and the Rhine German's ingrained habit of keeping accurate records, and found that both were exactly what the Frisian trading houses needed. He settled among them, learned their language — which was not difficult, closely related as it was to the Germanic dialects he already knew — and made himself useful in the way that the family had always made itself useful: by being the most reliable reader of a situation in any room he entered.

*Children:*
- **Holmar of Frisia** — direct line; see next entry.

### HOLMAR OF FRISIA
**~610 AD — Frisia, North Sea coast**

The Frisian traders of the seventh century were the arteries of the northern world — their ships moving between the Rhine delta and the Thames, between the Seine and the Humber, carrying wool and wine and slaves and pottery and the thousand other commodities that a fragmented post-Roman Europe still needed to move between its scattered centres of production and consumption. Holmar of Frisia was born into this world and mastered it with the thoroughness of someone who understood that trade, at its root, is a problem of information — who has what, who needs it, at what price, and whether the man offering to broker the transaction can be trusted.

He could not always be trusted, in the way that all successful merchants cannot entirely be trusted, but he was consistently more honest than his competitors, which amounted to the same thing commercially and was considerably more comfortable to live with. He assessed cargoes with an accuracy that became locally legendary — running his hands over bales of wool in the dim holds of Frisian trading vessels and pronouncing on their quality, origin, and probable value with a confidence that was almost never wrong, and that he could not, when pressed, entirely explain. He simply knew. The family had always simply known.

*Children:*
- **Holmar of Jutland** — direct line; see next entry.

### HOLMAR OF JUTLAND
**~640 AD — Jutland, Denmark**

The Frisian trading routes extended north into the Danish peninsula, and Holmar of Jutland followed them — or was sent along them, as a young factor establishing the family's commercial connections in the markets of the north. He settled in Jutland, married a Danish woman of good family, and spent his adult life as a merchant of the middle rank: prosperous enough to be comfortable, obscure enough to be safe, well-connected enough to be informed.

He was a collector of information in the way that other men collected silver or land — systematically, with an appreciation for quality, and with a clear sense of what a given piece of knowledge was worth and to whom. The Danish chieftains of his acquaintance learned that Holmar knew things — about the movements of rival jarls, the reliability of trading partners, the temper of distant markets — and that what he knew was accurate. He became, in time, a man whose counsel was sought on matters well beyond the price of a shipment of amber, and who gave that counsel carefully, understanding that the value of a reputation for accuracy is destroyed the moment it is compromised.

- **Holmar of Vestfold** — direct line; see next entry.

### HOLMAR OF VESTFOLD
**~670 AD — Vestfold, Norway**

The western shore of the Oslofjord — Vestfold, the field of the west — was in the seventh century the heartland of a Norse culture that was gathering itself, slowly and not yet consciously, for the explosion that would send it across every ocean of the known world within five generations. Holmar arrived there from Jutland with his Scandinavian wife, his trading connections, and his family's accumulated talent for being useful to the powerful, and found in Vestfold a world that rewarded exactly those qualities.

He attached himself to the household of a local jarl as a factor and counsellor, managing the jarl's commercial relationships with the Frisian and Frankish traders to the south with a competence that soon extended to managing rather more than commerce. He read men well, read situations better, and had the additional faculty — rarer and more dangerous — of being able to tell a leader what he did not want to hear in terms that allowed the leader to believe the conclusion was his own. He died in Vestfold, Norse in everything but the deepest layer of his blood, and left his son a position at the heart of a world that was about to become very much larger.

- **Sigr Holmr** — direct line; see next entry.

### SIGR HOLMR
**~700 AD — Vestfold, Norway**

The name was fully Norse now — Sigr Holmr, victory on the flat water — and the man matched it. He grew up in the hall culture of Vestfold, learned the sea from boyhood, and combined his father's analytical intelligence with the physical courage and appetite for risk that the Norse world demanded of anyone who wished to be taken seriously within it. He was not primarily a warrior, though he could fight; he was primarily a reader of men and situations, and in the complex politics of the Vestfold jarls — the shifting alliances, the inheritance disputes, the careful calibration of tribute and loyalty that kept the peace between rival houses — that faculty was worth more than any number of swords.

He served as a skald of sorts — not primarily a poet, but a keeper of the oral record, a man who remembered the genealogies and agreements and precedents that governed the relationships between families. In a world without written law, such men were the law; their memories were the archive. Sigr Holmr's memory was, by the testimony of those who relied on it, impeccable — not merely retentive but analytical, capable of finding in the accumulated record of the past the precise precedent that illuminated the dispute of the present. He was consulted from considerable distances by people who needed to know what had been agreed, and by whom, and when.

### LEIFR HOLMR
**~765 AD — Vestfold, Norway**

Leifr Holmr was his father's student in the art of memory and his own man in the application of it — where Sigr had used the accumulated knowledge of precedent to resolve disputes, Leifr used it to anticipate them. He could trace an inheritance dispute three generations before it became one, identify the competing claims before either party had articulated them, and in several recorded instances approached the families concerned with a proposed settlement before either had thought to quarrel. This was not meddling; it was the recognition, arrived at early in his career, that a conflict prevented is worth considerably more than a conflict resolved.

He was a man of the assembly — the thing, the Norse gathering where matters of law and community were decided — and his standing there was formidable. He spoke rarely, which made his contributions conspicuous; and he was right with a frequency that, over years, became the basis of a reputation that outlasted him. When his son Ragnarr was born, the naming of the child was attended by half the prominent men of Vestfold, which tells you something about the position Leifr Holmr had built, and the expectations he had created for those who came after him.

### RAGNARR HOLMR
**~795 AD — Vestfold, Norway**

The Viking Age began, by the reckoning of those who lived through it, in the summer of 793, when the longships appeared off the coast of Lindisfarne and the monks of that holy island learned what the men of the north had become. Ragnarr was a young boy that summer, and he grew up in the world that followed — the generation for whom raiding was not a new and shocking thing but simply the condition of their age, the background noise of an expanding Norse world. He watched the expeditions depart from Vestfold across his entire childhood and youth, building from those observations the detailed understanding of what the raids were actually for, what they found, and what they left undone.

He did not go raiding himself — he was not that kind of man, and he understood his own value clearly enough to know that it was not enhanced by a sword. He stayed in Vestfold, managing the networks of intelligence and supply that the raiding expeditions depended on more than they admitted, and ensuring that his son grew up understanding both the world that was passing and the one that was arriving. He gave Sigurðr the Norse world at its height — and the analytical tools to survive whatever came next.

*Children:*
- **Sigurðr Holmr** — direct line; see next entry.

### SIGURÐR HOLMR
**~830 AD — Vestfold / Yorkshire, England**

Sigurðr Holmr crossed the North Sea with the great Danish armies of the 860s — the micel here, the Great Heathen Army that descended on Northumbria in 866 and took York before the English understood what was happening. He was not among the axemen at the gates; he was among the men behind the axemen, the ones who had studied the English kingdoms for years through the accounts of Frisian traders and returned raiders and had built, from those accounts, a detailed picture of where the resistance would be strongest, where the harvest was richest, and where a man of intelligence and patience might put down roots that would outlast the initial violence.

Yorkshire was his answer. He settled in the North Riding after the conquest of Jorvik, taking land that was offered to Danish settlers, and spent the remainder of his life building the foundation that his descendants would occupy for the next two centuries. He spoke English within a year of arrival — he had prepared for that too — and made himself useful to the new Danish administration with the facility that his family had always demonstrated for inserting itself into the mechanisms of power without drawing the attention of those who resented such insertions. The North Riding in 870 was a place of considerable danger for anyone who miscalculated; Sigurðr Holmr did not miscalculate.

### LEIFR HOLMR II
**~870 AD — Yorkshire, England**

Leifr Holmr II was the first of the line to be born in English soil, in the farmstead his father had established in the North Riding in the years following the Danish conquest of Jorvik. He came into the world as the Danelaw was consolidating — the Norse settlements taking root, the Danish administration organising itself, a new hybrid culture forming in the space between the conquerors and the conquered. He grew up bilingual, Norse at home and English in the market and the moot, moving between the two worlds with the ease of someone for whom cultural boundaries had always been permeable rather than fixed.

He was a farmer and a local arbitrator — a man of the thing, the assembly, as his great-grandfather had been in Vestfold, called upon by his neighbours to resolve the disputes that the Danelaw's uneasy cohabitation of Norse and English custom generated in abundance. Property disputes, inheritance claims, the conflicting obligations of men who owed service under two incompatible legal traditions — these were the problems that came to Leifr Holmr II, and he resolved them with a practical ingenuity that owed as much to his Norse heritage of oral jurisprudence as to the English common law that was slowly asserting itself across the North.

### RAGNARR HOLMR II
**~900 AD — Yorkshire, England**

He grew up in the years when the Danelaw was at its most confident and most vulnerable simultaneously — a Norse dominion that had sunk deep roots into the English north while remaining perpetually aware that the kingdoms of the south had not accepted its existence and would, when strong enough, contest it. Ragnarr Holmr II understood this dynamic with the clarity of someone who had been reading it since childhood, watching the growing power of the southern English kingdoms under Edward the Elder and his son Æthelstan with the attention of a man who knows that the outcome of a distant conflict will eventually determine the terms of his own life.

He managed the family's land with his father's methodical precision and added to it a political alertness — a habit of maintaining connections across the boundary between the Danish and English communities, cultivating relationships on both sides of a line that he suspected, correctly, would not always be where it currently stood. When the English kings began extending their authority northward in the early tenth century, the Holmr family was positioned to accommodate the change without catastrophe.

### SIGURÐR HOLMR II
**~930 AD — Yorkshire, England**

Sigurðr Holmr II lived through the gradual erosion of the Danelaw — the slow reassertion of English royal authority that Edgar's consolidation of a unified kingdom would complete in his lifetime, the Norse culture of the north receding year by year before the administrative and ecclesiastical machinery of a unified English kingdom. He adapted, as his family had always adapted, by making himself useful to whoever was consolidating power at any given moment.

His particular contribution was a facility with the documentary record — the land charters, the court rolls, the ecclesiastical records that the English administration used to govern in ways the Norse oral tradition had not required. He had taught himself to read Latin in middle age, at a point when most men of his background considered such learning either unnecessary or vaguely suspicious, because he had identified it as the key to the kind of influence his family had always sought — the influence of the person who understands the document on which everything else depends.

### ÞORFINNR HOLMR
**~960 AD — Yorkshire, England**

Þorfinnr Holmr bore a fully Norse name in a Yorkshire that was becoming, generation by generation, less Norse — the Danelaw's cultural distinctiveness fading under the pressure of English kingship, English law, and the English Church, which had been working steadily northward since the conversion of the Danish settlers had given it a foothold in the region. He was a man caught between identities and comfortable in neither, which may explain why he invested so heavily in the one identity that transcended both: the family's ancient role as the man who knew things.

He served in the household of a Yorkshire thegn as a steward and counsellor, managing the accounts and correspondence of an estate that sat precisely on the cultural boundary between the English and Danish communities. He heard cases in both languages, applied law from both traditions, and developed in the process a scepticism about the claims of any single system of rules to represent the truth of a matter that would have been familiar to his ancestors in the Merovingian courts of three centuries earlier. The truth, in his experience, was not in the law; it was in the facts, which preceded the law and frequently survived it.

### GUNNARR HOLMR
**~990 AD — Yorkshire, England**

Gunnarr Holmr grew up in the reign of Æthelred — not the later years of crisis and Danegeld, but the earlier decades when the administration inherited from Edgar the Peaceful still functioned with something of its predecessor's order, and when the North Riding enjoyed a period of relative stability that suited a man of Gunnarr's type: methodical, observant, trusted equally by both communities, without the sharp edges that periods of crisis tend to produce and periods of peace make unnecessary.

He worked as a wool assessor and market overseer in the North Riding — an official role in the local economy, responsible for certifying weights, resolving disputes between traders, and detecting the falsifications of measure and quality that were the endemic fraud of every medieval market. He was very good at it. He had, colleagues noted, a peculiar facility for identifying adulterated goods — wool mixed with inferior fibres, ale diluted beyond what custom permitted, grain that had been stored wet and dried artificially before sale — by examination alone, without needing to be told what he was looking for. He simply looked, and found it.

### HALFDAN HOLMR
**~1020 AD — Yorkshire, England**

The name Halfdan — half-Dane — was, by the time he bore it, almost an anachronism; the Danelaw had dissolved into Cnut's England, and then into the restored English monarchy after him, and the distinction between Danish and English in the North Riding was less one of culture than of memory. Halfdan Holmr was English in everything that mattered legally and Danish in the oral traditions of his household — the stories told at the fire, the names given to children, the particular cast of a family mind that had come, centuries ago, from Vestfold.

He served as a reeve — a local official responsible for the Crown's interests in a district of the North Riding — in the unsettled years after Cnut's death, when the succession lurched between Danish and English claimants before settling, temporarily, on Edward the Confessor. The position required constant negotiation between the administrative continuity that the local community needed and the political volatility that successive royal changes produced, a negotiation that Halfdan conducted with the family's characteristic facility for finding the position that was simultaneously principled and survivable.

### EADMUND HOLMR
**~1055 AD — Yorkshire, England**

The name Eadmund was Anglo-Saxon — a deliberate choice reflecting the family's recognition that the Scandinavian phase of their identity was ending and the English one asserting itself as the permanent condition. Cnut's Danish empire had not outlasted Cnut; England was English again, after a fashion, and the men of the North Riding were adjusting their self-presentation accordingly. His father had spent the last decade of his life watching the succession question grow more dangerous with each passing year — Harold Godwinson, Duke William of Normandy, and Harald Hardrada of Norway all casting their shadows over a throne that Edward the Confessor seemed unlikely to fill for much longer — and had raised Eadmund in the understanding that a reckoning was coming and that the family's survival would depend on reading it clearly. Eadmund Holmr was perhaps ten years old when Harold was crowned, and a young man of eleven when the world changed at Hastings in October 1066.

The Conquest was catastrophic for most English landholders in Yorkshire, and doubly so after the Harrying of the North in 1069-70, when William I's army systematically destroyed the agricultural infrastructure of the region, leaving a landscape of burned settlements and starving survivors that observers described as uninhabitable for years afterward. Eadmund Holmr survived it — the family's land survived, diminished but intact. What accommodations were made, what services rendered to the new Norman overlords, what was surrendered to preserve what remained, is not fully recorded. What is recorded is that the Holmr family held their land in the North Riding in 1086, when the Domesday survey was compiled, and that Eadmund presented himself to the commissioners with accurate accounts, answered their questions without hesitation, and was noted as a reliable witness. The entry stood. The name on it was already shifting toward the Norman form.

### WILLELM HOLMR
**~1080 AD — Yorkshire, England**

Willelm — the name William in its Norman form — was the name his father had given him as a declaration of the new order, and he grew up entirely within it: post-Conquest Yorkshire, Norman French in the courts and the castles, Latin in the Church, English in the fields, and a man of his type needed all three. The Holmr family's land was intact but their position reduced, and Willelm spent his adult life rebuilding the local standing that the Harrying had cost them, doing it the way the family had always rebuilt — through competence, through the careful cultivation of useful relationships, and through being the man in any room whose assessment of a situation proved, in time, to have been accurate.

He served in the local ecclesiastical administration — the Church in Yorkshire was reconstructing its infrastructure after decades of disruption, and men who could read Latin and keep accurate records were in high demand. He worked for the canons of York Minster as a chapter clerk, maintaining the documentary record of the Minster's extensive land holdings with a thoroughness that his employers found exemplary. He had a gift for finding discrepancies in accounts — errors of addition, transpositions of figures, and occasionally something less innocent — that kept the Minster's records cleaner than most of its neighbours. He was not suspicious by temperament; he simply noticed.

- **Robert Holmr** — direct line; see next entry.

### ROBERT HOLMR
**~1110 AD — Yorkshire, England**

Robert Holmr grew up in the reign of Henry I — the Beauclerc, the scholar-king, the man who brought a measure of administrative order to his father's conquest — and came of age in the chaos of the Anarchy, the civil war between Stephen and Matilda that tore England apart from 1135 to 1154 and reduced large parts of the country to the condition that the chroniclers described, with unusual precision, as a time when God and his angels slept. Yorkshire was not the worst of it, but it was not untouched, and Robert Holmr spent the middle decades of his life managing an estate through conditions of radical uncertainty.

He managed it well. He had a Norman name and an English temperament and his family's imperturbable habit of reading the situation as it actually was rather than as he might have wished it to be, and in the Anarchy that combination was worth more than allegiance to either claimant. He supported order where it existed, kept his head down where it did not, and emerged from the civil war with the estate intact and his reputation for sound judgement enhanced by the simple fact of having survived a period when a great many men of property had not. He was, in the understated fashion of his age and position, a man whose evidence settled cases — whose word, in the local courts that resumed their function under Henry II, was accepted without question.
