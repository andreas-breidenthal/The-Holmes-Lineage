MARCUS-HOLKOS

~460 BC — Rome, Roman Republic

He came to Rome from Athens during the early Republic — a Greek in a city that had not yet decided whether to admire or distrust the culture it was in the process of surpassing. The praenomen Marcus was adopted as a practical accommodation, the kind of small self-reinvention that his ancestors had been performing since Nabi-ilum took an Egyptian wife in Memphis seven centuries before. He brought with him what Greeks of his generation carried as a matter of course: a facility with argument, a respect for evidence, and the habit of following a chain of reasoning wherever it led regardless of whether the destination was convenient.

He settled in the Aventine district, worked as a translator and factor between the Greek trading community and the Roman merchants who were beginning to reach into the eastern Mediterranean, and made himself, within a decade, indispensable to both. Rome in 460 BC was a city of perhaps thirty thousand people with the appetite of a million; Marcus-Holkos understood its hunger and positioned himself at the point where it intersected with the Greek world’s supply. He was the first of the line to bear a Roman name. He would not be the last.

What follows is drawn from the surviving record of Gaius Scribonius, a minor Roman scribe attached to a warehouse registry on the Aventine. His account, preserved in fragments on wax tablets later recopied to papyrus, records not only what was seen but what was asked, though it must be remembered that his eye was trained for accounts rather than consequences, and that his memory often lingers where his understanding did not. It is through his record that this matter endures.

The Affairs Of Demetrios The Milesian

The morning it began was the kind that Rome produces in the last days of November, when the river smell comes up from the Tiber in layers — first the cold mud, then the rot beneath, then something older that has no name in either tongue I know. The lamps in the registry had been burning since before first light because Publius Arvina, the senior partner of the Arvina-Cornelius grain syndicate, had arrived before the door was unbarred and stood in the street making a sound like a man who has swallowed something that disagreed with him.

I remember the quality of his waiting. He stood absolutely still, which is not a thing large men usually manage, and his stillness had the quality of controlled fury — the kind that has been held in the hand all night and has grown warm from the grip.

I was the second man through the door. The first had been Marcus-Holkos, which surprised me, as he had no business at the registry that morning that I knew of. He was simply there, as he had a habit of being — standing at the far end of the long table where the day's tallies were laid out, reading the surface of the wood rather than anything written on it, with the look of a man waiting for something he expected to arrive shortly. He wore his usual Greek himation in a shade of undyed wool that the Romans around him tended to find vaguely suspicious, as though the failure to dye cloth signalled a failure of civic commitment. His hair was dark, his eyes were the particular grey-green of deep harbour water, and he was at that hour in what I can only describe as the languor — that loose-limbed, heavy-lidded state he inhabited between problems, which those who did not know him regularly mistook for stupidity.

Publius Arvina came in behind me and filled the room with his displeasure.

"The Milesian has defrauded us," he said, dispensing with greeting in the manner of men who believe that social convention is a luxury affordable only to those without genuine grievances. "Twelve medimnoi short on a delivery of four hundred. I have the weights. I have the tally. The wax seals are intact. The Milesian is a thief."

The Milesian in question was Demetrios of Miletus, who had been trading grain through the Aventine for eleven years without, to my knowledge, a single complaint against his measure. He was a careful man — careful in the specific way of Greeks who have built a reputation in a foreign city and know exactly what it weighs and what it costs to repair. I had processed three years of his transactions myself. His weights were always consistent. His tallies were meticulous. He wrote his figures in a small, even hand that suggested a mind that liked everything in its place.

Lucius Marius arrived within the hour. He was the quaestor's man for this district, a compact, efficient Roman of forty with the bearing of someone who had done this particular task — the investigation of commercial fraud — so many times that the individual cases had begun to blur together into a single composite accusation. He wore his authority the way comfortable men wear old shoes: without thought, because the fit was long established.

"The law is clear," Lucius Marius said, examining Arvina's complaint with the expression of a man reading something he has already understood. "Short weight is fraud. The tally seal is the magistrate's guarantee. If the seal is intact and the weight is wrong, the merchant is liable." He looked at the bronze weights arranged on the table — eight of them, each stamped with the registry mark, each supposedly certified. "These were sealed here?"

"Three days ago," I said. "I sealed them myself."

"Then I will send men to the Milesian's warehouse this morning. He will be brought before the quaestor before the second hour."

"If I might," said Marcus-Holkos.

He had not moved from his position at the far end of the table. He said these words in the mild, slightly distant tone he used when he was already three steps ahead of the conversation and was waiting, with imperfect patience, for the others to arrive.

Lucius Marius looked at him. "You might not. This is a registry matter. You are a translator."

"I am a translator," Marcus-Holkos agreed pleasantly. "Who has spent six years rendering Greek commercial practice into Roman accounting convention and Roman accounting convention into Greek commercial practice, and who has therefore had considerable opportunity to observe the points at which the two systems do not quite coincide." He straightened, and the languor fell away from him the way a cloak falls when the pin is released — suddenly, completely, revealing something different underneath. "May I examine the weights?"

Lucius Marius looked at Arvina. Arvina, to his credit, made a gesture that meant: let him, if only so we can proceed sooner.

What followed occupied perhaps twenty minutes, though I could not have reconstructed it sequentially if my life required it. Marcus-Holkos moved around the table with an energy that seemed disproportionate to the smallness of the objects under examination. He picked up each bronze weight in turn, held it at different angles to the lamp, set it down, picked up the next. He asked me to bring the original certification tablet — the wax impression made when the weights were first stamped. He asked Arvina when precisely he had noticed the discrepancy. He asked what the weather had been like on the day of delivery, whether the grain had been moved in the morning or afternoon, how many men had been on the receiving dock.

Arvina answered these questions with the tolerance of a man who believes he is indulging nonsense in the short term to achieve justice in the long term.

Then Marcus-Holkos set down the seventh weight and was silent for a moment.

"Gaius," he said, using my name with the Greek casualness that I had learned to read as a signal of concentration rather than familiarity, "when you stamped these weights three days ago, what tool did you use?"

"The registry punch. The iron one, with the consular mark."

"And where is it kept?"

"In the iron box, under the left counter. Chained."

"And who has access to that box?"

I opened my mouth and then closed it. "The senior clerk. Myself. And—" I stopped.

"And the registry's commercial agent," said Marcus-Holkos. "Who is employed, I believe, by the Arvina-Cornelius syndicate."

The room became very quiet. The lamp smell was sharp — cheap oil, the kind the registry purchased in bulk, with a slightly rancid undertone that I had stopped noticing years ago but now noticed.

Lucius Marius said, slowly, "Explain."

Marcus-Holkos set the seventh weight next to the eighth and indicated the bottom face of each.

"Look at the stamp," he said. "Not the mark itself — the depth of the impression. All six weights sealed on the day Gaius certifies have an impression depth consistent with a single strike from a fresh punch held at a consistent angle. The mark is crisp. The edges are sharp." He touched the seventh weight. "This one's impression is slightly shallower on the left edge, and the right edge shows a minute doubling — a second strike, fractionally misaligned. As if the punch was applied a second time, by a different hand, or at a different angle, or both."

"That might be a flaw in the original striking," Lucius Marius said.

"It might be," Marcus-Holkos agreed. "If this weight were otherwise identical to its fellows. But it is not." He picked up the seventh weight and placed it in the palm of his hand, then held it beside the fifth, which was certified for the same unit of measure. "Feel the difference."

Lucius Marius picked up both. A Roman official's face is trained for inscrutability, but something moved behind his eyes.

"The seventh is lighter," he said.

"By approximately one-fortieth of its declared measure. Small enough to evade casual examination. Large enough, multiplied across four hundred medimnoi, to account for nine of your twelve missing units, Publius Arvina." Marcus-Holkos set both weights down with a precise click. "The seventh weight is not the weight that I sealed three days ago. It is a substitute — close enough in appearance to pass inspection, but newly re-stamped with a copy of the registry punch to give it the appearance of official certification."

"A copy," said Lucius Marius.

"A copy made from a mould taken from the original punch, I would imagine, and cast in a slightly inferior bronze — the colour is marginally warmer, which is visible in direct lamplight and invisible in warehouse shadow." He turned to Arvina with the expression of a man completing an arithmetic problem. "The discrepancy was not introduced at the point of weighing. It was introduced after the transaction was complete and the weights were returned — during the interval when they passed through your syndicate's agent before being brought here as evidence of Demetrios's fraud."

Arvina said nothing for a long moment. Then he said: "That is a considerable accusation."

"It is a considerable observation," Marcus-Holkos said. "The accusation is yours. Or rather, the accusation you made is now returned to its origin, and what you choose to do with that is a matter between you and the quaestor." He looked at Lucius Marius. "I would suggest that the commercial agent's lodgings repay examination. Mould-casting requires equipment. Equipment leaves traces."

Lucius Marius found the equipment. He found, additionally, a set of correspondence between the syndicate's agent — a man named Gnaeus Tuccius, a Roman freedman of middling ambition and poor judgment — and a rival trader who had reason to want Demetrios discredited and his Aventine contracts freed up for re-allocation. The scale of the scheme was, in the end, modest: a private grievance, a corrupt clerk, a copied punch. Not the infrastructure of serious crime. The small, mean architecture of ordinary malice.

Gnaeus Tuccius was brought before the quaestor. Demetrios was not.

I asked Marcus-Holkos, afterward, how he had known to look at the stamps rather than the tallies. Everyone else — myself included — had assumed the question was about the numbers.

"The numbers were consistent," he said, in the tone he used when explaining things he found self-evident. "Consistent records are either accurate or comprehensively falsified. Demetrios's tally matched the syndicate's tally at every point except the final weight. That degree of consistency is not the signature of a man who cheats — it is the signature of a man who is careful. Careful men do not make isolated, detectable errors. When you find an isolated, detectable error in a careful man's work, you look for the point at which someone else touched it."

"The stamp," I said.

"Everything has a history of contact," he said. "Bronze remembers hands. The question is whether you know how to read the memory."

He was already, I noticed, returning to the languid state — the loosened posture, the half-focused gaze, as though the conclusion of the problem had withdrawn something essential and left him briefly hollow. He stood by the counter, turning one of the weights over and over in his fingers without looking at it, like a man who has finished a meal and cannot quite remember the taste.

I record this as accurately as I am able. I have training in accounts, not in consequences, and there were aspects of that morning I did not fully understand until considerably later. I record that Marcus-Holkos received no formal recognition from the quaestor's office. The official record of the Tuccius case credits the registry inspection and Lucius Marius's investigation. This seems to me both unjust and, somehow, entirely in keeping with the man — as though public credit were simply another piece of information he had decided had no place in his attention.

Demetrios sent a jar of Milesian wine to the address on the Aventine that I provided, on his behalf, because Marcus-Holkos would not take a fee.

Whether Marcus-Holkos drank it or used it for something else, I cannot say. I suspect the latter. He had, I had begun to notice, an extensive and somewhat alarming interest in the chemistry of fermentation that had little to do with the pleasures of drinking.

Thus closes this account. The tablets are accurate to the best of my knowledge. The wax is my own.

— G.S.
