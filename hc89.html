HOLMAR OF VESTFOLD

~670 AD — Vestfold, Norway

The western shore of the Oslofjord — Vestfold, the field of the west — was in the seventh century the heartland of a Norse culture that was gathering itself, slowly and not yet consciously, for the explosion that would send it across every ocean of the known world within five generations. Holmar arrived there from Jutland with his Scandinavian wife, his trading connections, and his family’s accumulated talent for being useful to the powerful, and found in Vestfold a world that rewarded exactly those qualities.

He attached himself to the household of a local jarl as a factor and counsellor, managing the jarl’s commercial relationships with the Frisian and Frankish traders to the south with a competence that soon extended to managing rather more than commerce. He read men well, read situations better, and had the additional faculty — rarer and more dangerous — of being able to tell a leader what he did not want to hear in terms that allowed the leader to believe the conclusion was his own. He died in Vestfold, Norse in everything but the deepest layer of his blood, and left his son a position at the heart of a world that was about to become very much larger.

Among those who preserved his work was Hrefna, a woman attached to the jarl’s hall whose task was to maintain accounts of disputes, agreements, and judgments. She kept her records on vellum, written with ink made from soot and resin, stored in a chest lined with oiled cloth to protect them from the damp air that came in from the fjord. Her record of Holmar’s work is fragmentary, shaped by the demands of her position and her own limited time to observe events in full, but it preserves the essential sequence of what occurred.

The Accounts of Hrefna

Set down in the year of Our Lord seven hundred and eleven, though the hall does not yet number its years in that fashion.

The fjord breathed that autumn as though it were a living thing preparing to die. I had noticed this before — the way the water goes grey and flat in the weeks before the first ice, how the smell of it changes from salt-and-fish to something older and colder, like wet stone in a cave that sees no sun. The seabirds stopped their noise. The pine forest across the water became a dark smear, indistinct, as if the world were quietly erasing itself at its edges.

It was into this dying light that the gifts began to arrive from Thorvald Rannulfsson.

I should say something of Holmar, since it is his work I am recording and it is his work alone that makes these events worth the vellum.

He came to us from Jutland, or rather he came to Vestfold from Jutland by way of a merchant career and a Norse wife and the kind of reputation that precedes a man like smoke precedes a fire. He was not a large man, nor a particularly imposing one. There was nothing about his appearance at the feast-bench that would cause you to remember him before the man beside him. He had the pale eyes common to men of his region, the kind that seem to be measuring something even when the rest of his face is entirely at rest. His hands were a trader’s hands — quick, careful, accustomed to weight and texture and the detection of what was not where it should be.

What he had that other men lacked was the faculty of reading a situation the way I read a column of figures: finding the internal logic, noting where the numbers did not add up, and working backward from the discrepancy to discover what had been taken, or hidden, or never entered in the first place.

Jarl Sigmund valued this. He valued it the way a man values a good blade — not always understanding how it works, but knowing when it is needed.

Holmar’s vice, if I am to be honest in my account, was the riddles. When there was no case to occupy him, no pattern to untangle, he grew impossible — restless in a way that expressed itself not in movement but in a terrible stillness, a coiled irritability that would spend itself on whoever was nearest. He collected carved wooden puzzles. He sat for hours working a single knotted cord. When Sigmund’s hall was peaceful and the trade accounts balanced and no man had a grievance requiring thought, Holmar was at his worst. He would sit by the long fire with his knees drawn up and stare at the smoke-hole as though the answer to some unarticulated question might eventually be written there in ash.

It was in one such period of dangerous quiet that the gifts from Thorvald Rannulfsson began.

The first came by a young man in a good cloak — not magnificent, but good, which told you something in itself. A chest of Frankish glassware. Six pieces, not broken, which meant they had been packed with uncommon care and carried by men who were told the consequences of carelessness in terms they understood. The accompanying words, spoken by the young man in the formal cadence used for such occasions, expressed Thorvald’s admiration for Jarl Sigmund’s wisdom, his pleasure in their continued good relations, and his hope that the coming season would bring prosperity to both their households.

Sigmund received this warmly, as he received everything warmly, and ordered the young man fed and given a sleeping place of honour.

I recorded the gift in the accounts, as I record all gifts. It was my second winter doing so, and I had by then a sufficient sense of patterns to notice that this was early for such a sending. Thorvald and Sigmund exchanged gifts at the great seasonal feasts, as all neighbouring jarls did. This was neither feast-time nor the anniversary of any agreement between them. I noted the irregularity, then noted that I had noted it, and returned to my other work.

Three weeks later, a second gift. Two hunting dogs of a breed I did not recognise, lean and long-legged, with the intelligent eyes of animals that had been trained rather than broken. A man came with them to demonstrate their working, and spent two days in the hall, very pleasant, very open, eating our food and sleeping under our roof and asking questions that were each, individually, entirely natural, and which together formed a shape I could almost see but could not quite name.

It was the second gift that made Holmar sit up.

That evening, Holmar came to me.

“Show me the gift records for the last four months. Not just Thorvald’s. All of them.”

I showed him. He was silent for a long time, turning the vellum sheets carefully.

“The proportions are wrong,” he said finally.

“Thorvald is a man who gives exactly as much as he has received, and slightly less,” Holmar said. “If you look at the record of exchange between his household and ours over the past six years, you will see that he has never initiated a gift of this quality except before a request or before a conflict.”

“There has been no request,” he said. “So we are looking at preparation for a conflict.”

He told Sigmund.

Sigmund listened, and said he would consider the matter and act as seemed wise.

Sigmund did nothing.

Three weeks later, Thorvald made his move. It came not through open action, but through Ragnhild — Jarl Sigmund’s wife.

The form of Thorvald’s action was elegant. He had approached the families connected to Ragnhild — her kin, her fosterage ties — and through them secured agreements that bypassed Sigmund entirely. When the move came, it came as established fact, impossible to undo without breaking bonds Sigmund could not afford to break.

I found Holmar at the account-chest the morning after.

“Tell me what I missed,” he said.

I told him of Ragnhild’s gift-giving — the quiet network she maintained, visible only if one knew where to look.

“It was invisible to me,” he said. “Because I was not looking at the right records.”

“I was watching the accounts of jarls,” he said. “I did not read the movements between households.”

“I had the right pattern. Wrong half of the archive.”

Some weeks later, Ragnhild came to the account place.

She said: “Does he understand now?”

I said I believed he did.

“He reads every written thing,” she said. “But there is a great deal that is never written.”

She left. I recorded this also.

Here ends this account. What is recorded here is what the formal documents do not contain.
