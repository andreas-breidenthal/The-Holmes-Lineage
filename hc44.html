HOLKOS-NABU

~760 BC — Nineveh

Nabu, god of writing and the archive, stood at the center of scribal culture across Babylon and Assyria, and Holkos adopted the name with the understanding that it marked not only profession but responsibility. In the Neo-Assyrian imperial administration, where governance depended upon the movement of language across regions and authorities, Holkos served as a scribe and translator within the chancelleries that coordinated correspondence between the Assyrian court and its western territories. He worked among documents written in multiple languages, each carrying implications that extended beyond the words themselves.

His role placed him within the chain through which policy was transmitted and interpreted. He did not alter messages, but he was responsible for ensuring that what was said in one language was accurately rendered in another — a responsibility that required not only linguistic knowledge but an awareness of how much meaning could move in the space between languages without anyone noticing the displacement.

The record of his work was maintained by a scribe named Bel-ibni, who served within the same administrative environment and whose responsibilities included the copying and preservation of official correspondence. Bel-ibni was attentive to detail but limited to recording what passed through his hands. He did not interpret beyond what was necessary for his task. His account preserves the sequence of events that follows.

The Account Of Holkos-Nabu

Recorded by Bel-ibni, Keeper of Correspondence, in the Reign of Tiglath-Pileser, King of Assyria

The morning the body of Kibri-Adad was discovered, the air in the chancellery still carried the smell from the previous night — cedar oil from the lamps that had burned until the third watch, and beneath it the particular sharpness of freshly worked clay, the mineral cold of tablets that had not yet dried. It is a smell I have come to associate with long evenings of careful work. After that morning, I found I could no longer separate it from what I saw when we entered the inner room.

I record this account because Holkos-Nabu asked me to, and because I have since come to understand that what he did in those days deserves to exist somewhere other than in his own silence. The official record of the chancellery will say only that Kibri-Adad, Senior Translator of the Western Correspondence, died of a stroke brought on by the exertion of his duties. The guard-captain, Sharrukku, will receive commendation for his handling of what the administration will call a confusion of the night watch.

None of that is true. I was there. This is what happened.

Kibri-Adad was found by the water-carrier just after dawn, slumped against the wall of the inner tablet room with his stylus still in his hand and a look on his face that the water-carrier described, when she had recovered enough to describe anything, as peaceful. I will say only that it did not look peaceful to me. A man who has been struck at the base of the skull with sufficient force to stop his heart does not look peaceful. He looks interrupted.

Sharrukku arrived within the quarter-hour, assessed the body with the practiced indifference of a man who had seen enough death in the field campaigns not to be disturbed by its appearance in an administrative building, and immediately sent men to find Urdu-Marduk, the junior translator who had been the last person confirmed to enter the inner room the previous evening.

I went to find Holkos-Nabu.

He was in his usual place at the far end of the outer hall, sitting cross-legged on his reed mat with three tablets arranged before him and his eyes closed. He had been this way since before my own arrival that morning. I had assumed he was composing. In retrospect, I think he had been waiting.

“Kibri-Adad,” I said.

“I know,” he said, without opening his eyes. “I have been sitting here for some time wondering when you would come.”

“Sharrukku is questioning Urdu-Marduk.”

He opened his eyes then. They were the color of river water in the dry season — pale brown, almost amber — and they moved across me with the same quality of attention I had seen him direct at a disputed tablet, taking inventory of something I could not identify.

“Urdu-Marduk,” he said, and his mouth held the name for a moment as though tasting it for information. “No. Come. Bring your stylus.”

He did not go to the body first. This struck me as unusual, and I said so as we walked through the outer hall, past the rows of junior scribes who had begun to arrive and now stood in uncertain clusters, speaking quietly.

“The body will tell me what stopped,” he said, moving ahead of me with the particular energy that sometimes came over him, a sharpness of motion that was entirely unlike the stillness of the mat. “The tablets will tell me what was happening when it was stopped. The sequence matters more than the conclusion.”

He went to the draft shelf.

In our chancellery, there is a procedure for the composition of diplomatic dispatches. A senior translator prepares the initial draft in Akkadian. A junior translator produces a working version in the target language — in this case, Aramaic, for the correspondence with the western governors. The senior translator reviews and corrects. The corrected draft is then sealed and copied by the archive scribes. At each stage, the tablet passes through specific hands, and the passage is noted in the duty register.

Holkos-Nabu pulled the duty register from its shelf and read it in the way he read everything important — standing completely still, his lips not moving, his face showing nothing.

Then he set it down and began moving along the draft shelf, lifting tablets and turning them in the thin morning light that came through the high window slots.

“Bel-ibni,” he said. “How many men in this building can read Aramaic?”

I considered. “Kibri-Adad. Urdu-Marduk. Yourself. And — ” I paused. “Nabu-shum-iddina.”

“Nabu-shum-iddina,” he repeated. He set down the tablet he was holding and picked up another. “Who is responsible for the seal matrix.”

“Yes.”

“Come here.”

I came. He held the tablet so I could see its surface — the draft Aramaic version of the previous night’s dispatch, prepared by Urdu-Marduk. It was covered in small correction marks.

“These corrections,” I said. “Kibri-Adad reviewed it.”

“Kibri-Adad reviewed something,” Holkos-Nabu said. “Look at the marks. Here, and here. The stylus pressure is consistent — same hand throughout. But here —” he moved his finger to a section in the lower third of the tablet “— the angle changes. Slightly. A man correcting another man’s work moves with a particular kind of authority. A man writing his own corrections moves with a different weight. He is not questioning. He is instructing himself.”

I looked more closely. “I don’t see it.”

“You see it. You are not yet looking in the right direction.” He set the tablet down. “Kibri-Adad did not write these corrections. They were written after his review was complete, and then the draft was returned to the shelf to wait for the morning archive. Whoever added these corrections knew they would be copied into the final sealed dispatch before anyone compared them to Kibri-Adad’s seal.”

“What do the corrections change?”

He was already moving toward the body.

Kibri-Adad lay as the water-carrier had left him, which is to say as whoever had placed him there had arranged him. Holkos-Nabu knelt and studied the dead man’s hand — the one holding the stylus — for a long time without touching anything. Then he looked at the floor around the body. Then at the wall. Then at the small oil lamp that sat in its bracket above the tablet niche, which had burned down entirely and gone cold.

“He worked late,” I said.

“He was brought here after he died,” Holkos-Nabu said. He stood. “The lamp burned from the evening lighting to its natural end, which would have been the second watch. The body was arranged here sometime after that. Before the third watch, or the night duty scribe would have seen something when he made his rounds.” He paused. “Kibri-Adad died somewhere else in this building. He was moved to suggest that he had simply fallen where he sat.”

He looked at the stylus in the dead man’s hand. His expression was the one I had come to recognize as the moment before he said something that would rearrange everything I thought I understood about a situation.

“The stylus is in his right hand,” he said.

“Kibri-Adad wrote with his right hand.”

“Yes. But Kibri-Adad, when he finished working for the evening, always cleaned his stylus before setting it down. He was, as you know, meticulous. The man who put this stylus in his hand did not know that, because Nabu-shum-iddina manages seals and matrices, not drafts. He has seen Kibri-Adad work, but he has not watched him finish.”

I was quiet for a moment. “You are certain it is Nabu-shum-iddina.”

“I am certain of the sequence. Nabu-shum-iddina controls the seal matrix for outgoing dispatches. He is the only man in this building, other than Kibri-Adad and Urdu-Marduk, who can read Aramaic — and Urdu-Marduk prepared the original draft honestly, which means the corruption had to be introduced at the sealing stage, where Nabu-shum-iddina has sole authority. Kibri-Adad, reviewing a late copy, discovered the alteration and confronted someone. That someone could not be Urdu-Marduk, because the alteration was not Urdu-Marduk’s work.” He paused. “Also, there is clay on the floor of the seal room. Fresh clay. Not from a tablet. From a foot that walked through the clay trough and did not notice. Nabu-shum-iddina wears sandals with a distinctive binding — a double strap, wider than standard — which leaves a mark I have seen on three surfaces between this room and the seal chamber.”

He said this last part as though it were the most straightforward observation in the world, and perhaps to him it was.

Sharrukku, when Holkos-Nabu presented this account, was silent for long enough that I began to count the sounds of the building around us — a door hinge in the outer hall that needed oil, the low murmur of the junior scribes still clustered near the entrance, the drip of water from the courtyard cistern.

“Urdu-Marduk,” the guard-captain said finally.

“Has done nothing wrong,” Holkos-Nabu said. “He wrote what he was asked to write. He is frightened and he should be released.”

“And the dispatch —”

“Has not yet been sealed and sent, because the death interrupted Nabu-shum-iddina’s work.” Holkos-Nabu produced the draft tablet from beneath his arm — I had not seen him take it from the shelf, which was characteristic. “When you ask him about the corrections in the lower third, you will know everything by the way he looks at this before he looks at you.”

Sharrukku looked at Holkos-Nabu for another moment. “What did the corrections change? In the dispatch.”

Holkos-Nabu met his gaze evenly. “Troop movement instructions for the western garrison. Specifically, the withdrawal schedule. The original Akkadian draft called for the garrison to hold its position through the grain season. The Aramaic version, as corrected, would have told the western governor that withdrawal was immediate — that he was being abandoned.” He paused. “Someone in the western territories was paying for that information, or for that confusion. A garrison that believes it has been abandoned behaves differently from a garrison that knows it is held.”

The guard-captain was quiet. Somewhere in the building, a door closed.

“You will find,” Holkos-Nabu added, “that Nabu-shum-iddina’s household has recently acquired a quantity of silver that is inconsistent with his salary. He keeps it in a sealed jar beneath the third step of his interior staircase. He believes the irregularity of the hiding place is sufficient concealment. He has a talent for seals, not for hiding things.”

Nabu-shum-iddina was taken before the second hour. He looked at the draft tablet before he looked at Sharrukku, exactly as Holkos-Nabu had said he would. The silver was under the third step.

He was convicted and the matter was settled at a level above my own knowledge, in rooms where I have no access. What the official record says, I cannot tell you, because I have not been permitted to see it. I know only that Sharrukku received a commendation and that the western dispatch was rewritten and resealed by Holkos-Nabu himself, working alone in the inner room that same afternoon with the door closed and the lamp burning.

I asked him, afterward, whether it troubled him — that the man who would be credited with the resolution was the one who had spent the morning arresting the wrong person.

“Sharrukku is competent,” Holkos-Nabu said. He was back on his mat, the three tablets rearranged before him in a new configuration I didn’t recognize. His eyes were already half-closed. “Competence deserves its credit. What he cannot do is see past the first line of the document. That is not his function. It is mine.”

“And if I write this account —”

“Write it accurately,” he said. “The only dishonesty I cannot tolerate is one that corrupts the sequence. The sequence is everything. Everything else is politics.”

He closed his eyes.

In the outer hall, the junior scribes had returned to their work. The door hinge needed oil. The cistern dripped. The smell of cedar and wet clay settled back into the corridors of the chancellery like something that had only briefly been disturbed, and intended to remain.

Thus recorded by Bel-ibni, Scribe of the Western Correspondence, in the thirty-first year of the great king. The tablet from which this account derives was discovered among the Kuyunjik archive deposits and is held in partial translation. The name Holkos-Nabu does not appear in any official administrative register of the period. The case of Kibri-Adad is recorded in the duty log only as a vacancy, filled the following month.
