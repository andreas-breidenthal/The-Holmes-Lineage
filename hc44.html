HOLKOS-NABU

~760 BC — Nineveh, Assyria

The Account Of Holkos-Nabu

Recorded by Bel-ibni, Keeper of Correspondence, in the Reign of Tiglath-Pileser, King of Assyria

I.

I have served in the chancellery of the western correspondence for eleven years. In that time I have copied treaties, recorded the movements of armies, and transcribed the private instructions of men whose names appear on no monument. I am a scribe of moderate gifts and, I think, sound judgment. I mention this only so that the reader may understand how thoroughly Holkos-Nabu could make a man of moderate gifts and sound judgment feel that he had never in his life paid proper attention to anything at all.

The morning the body of Kibri-Adad was discovered, the air in the chancellery still carried the smell from the previous night — cedar oil from the lamps that had burned until the third watch, and beneath it the particular sharpness of freshly worked clay, the mineral cold of tablets that had not yet dried. It is a smell I have come to associate with long evenings of careful work. After that morning, I found I could no longer separate it from what I saw when we entered the inner room.

I record this account because Holkos-Nabu asked me to, and because I have since come to understand that what he did in those days deserves to exist somewhere other than in his own silence. The official record of the chancellery will say only that Kibri-Adad, Senior Translator of the Western Correspondence, died of a stroke brought on by the exertion of his duties. The guard-captain, Sharrukku, will receive commendation for his handling of what the administration will call a confusion of the night watch.

None of that is true. I was there. This is what happened.

II.

The morning had already begun badly before Kibri-Adad's body was discovered.

A messenger had arrived at dawn from the western road — a young man from the garrison relay, his sandals grey with three days of dust, his breathing still rough from the final descent into the city. He asked, with an urgency that bypassed the usual protocols entirely, to speak with the senior translator immediately. I told him Kibri-Adad had not yet arrived. He looked at me with the expression of a man who has run a long way and found the gate locked, and said that the governor of the western territories was demanding confirmation of the garrison's orders, because a dispatch had reached him — he produced a sealed clay tablet from the pouch at his hip — that contradicted everything he had previously been told.

The garrison, he said, believed it was being abandoned.

I understood what this meant, though the messenger perhaps did not understand the full weight of what he carried. A garrison that believes itself abandoned does not hold its position. It negotiates. It fragments. It becomes, in the space of a fortnight, something that an enemy — or a governor with ambitions — can walk through without resistance. The western territories are not a comfortable frontier. The men stationed there know it. If they believed the king had withdrawn his support, they would not wait to confirm the error. They would begin the calculus of survival, and that calculus does not favour discipline.

I had taken the messenger to wait in the outer hall. I was still trying to decide what to do when the water-carrier's cry came from the inner room.

III.

Kibri-Adad was found slumped against the wall of the inner tablet room with his stylus still in his hand and a look on his face that the water-carrier described, when she had recovered enough to describe anything, as peaceful. I will say only that it did not look peaceful to me. A man who has been struck at the base of the skull with sufficient force to stop his heart does not look peaceful. He looks interrupted. I am not a physician, but I have eleven years of working alongside men who came back from the field campaigns, and I have enough acquaintance with the physical facts of violence to note the lividity of the skin and the unnatural angle of the neck, and to understand with reasonable certainty that Kibri-Adad had not died where he lay.

I knew Kibri-Adad well enough to know that the stylus in his hand was wrong in a second sense beyond the merely anatomical. He was the most meticulous man in the chancellery — a quality that his junior translators found exhausting and that I had always privately admired — and among his fixed habits was the cleaning of his stylus before he set it down at the end of any working session. He did not set down an unclean stylus. He had not done so in eleven years. The stylus in his dead hand had not been cleaned. Whoever had placed it there had not known that about him, which meant it was someone who had observed Kibri-Adad at work but had never stayed to watch him finish.

There was something else I noticed, standing in the doorway while Sharrukku's men moved around the body. Near the entrance, where the clay floor met the stone threshold, there were marks I did not immediately understand — a trail of smeared clay leading inward from the corridor, and at its edge, visible only because the morning light from the high window slot struck it at a flat angle, the impression of a sandalled foot. The binding pattern was unusual: a double strap, wider than the standard issue worn by the guard or the archive scribes. I stepped back to let one of Sharrukku's men pass and filed the observation in the part of my mind that holds things whose significance has not yet declared itself.

Sharrukku arrived within the quarter-hour, assessed the body with the practiced indifference of a man who had seen enough death in the field not to be disturbed by its appearance in an administrative building, and immediately sent men to find Urdu-Marduk, the junior translator who had been the last person confirmed to enter the inner room the previous evening.

I passed Urdu-Marduk in the corridor outside the records room, before Sharrukku's men had fully secured him. He was a slight young man from the southern territories, twenty-two or twenty-three years of age, not yet a year in the chancellery, who still addressed every senior scribe with a formality he had not learned to modulate. He was standing with his back against the wall and his hands pressed flat to the plaster, and he was not making the sounds of a man who is frightened. He was making the sounds of a man who has passed through fright into the cold country beyond it, where the mind begins to understand, with horrible clarity, the shape of what is coming.

I should explain what was coming. Sharrukku had already, I learned from a junior scribe who had been near the guard-room, sent word to the palace administrator. A summary tribunal convened by the palace administrator, in a case touching diplomatic correspondence, could reach its conclusions and execute them within the same morning. Urdu-Marduk was twenty-two years old. He had been the last person in the room. He had prepared the Aramaic draft. To a summary tribunal, that sequence would constitute a narrative that required no further elaboration.

"Bel-ibni," he said, when he saw me. Just the name. The way a man says the name of a plank he is hoping will hold.

I had no authority to intervene in Sharrukku's inquiry and I told him so, honestly, because he deserved honesty rather than comfort. But I also told him I would find Holkos-Nabu, and his expression when I said it — the particular loosening of something that had been rigid with the effort of not collapsing — told me that even at the junior translation desks, the name carried weight.

I went to find him immediately. There was perhaps an hour before the administrator arrived.

IV.

He was in his usual place at the far end of the outer hall, sitting cross-legged on his reed mat with three tablets arranged before him and his eyes closed. I should explain something about Holkos-Nabu, which is that he did not, at first encounter, appear to be a man who commanded particular notice. He was spare in build, somewhere between thirty and fifty in the way that thin, contained men sometimes are, with eyes the colour of river water in the dry season — pale brown, almost amber — that moved across a tablet, or a room, or a man's face with the same unhurried precision. He had come to the chancellery seven years earlier from somewhere in the eastern territories, speaking Akkadian, Aramaic, and two other languages I had never been able to identify, and he had declined every administrative appointment offered to him on the grounds that titles created obligations that interfered with observation. When he worked, he worked with a concentration so absolute it bordered on discourtesy. When he was still, as he was now, he was the stillest thing in any room he occupied.

I had assumed he was composing. In retrospect, I think he had been waiting.

"Kibri-Adad," I said.

"I know," he said, without opening his eyes. "I have been sitting here for some time wondering when you would come."

"Sharrukku has sent for the palace administrator. Urdu-Marduk will go before a summary tribunal. I think there is an hour, perhaps less."

He opened his eyes. The amber in them, in that light, was very clear.

"Then we will not waste it," he said, and rose from the mat with a single fluid motion — the stillness replaced in an instant by that sharpness of movement that always startled me, however many times I had seen it, as though two different men inhabited the same body and took turns. "Come. Bring your stylus. And tell me about the messenger. And the ring."

I had not mentioned the ring. I told him about both as we walked — the dust-grey messenger and the garrison's desperate dispatch, and the carnelian ring I had seen on Nabu-shum-iddina's hand the previous evening when he was leaving as I arrived for the late records: well-cut stone, expensive setting, not the sort of acquisition a keeper of seal matrices makes on his salary.

He listened without interrupting. When I had finished, he said, "The governor of Hatarikka's grain factor passes through the city twice in the season. Does Nabu-shum-iddina have any connection to that trade?"

"His brother deals in storage contracts," I said. "Southern district."

"Yes." He said it as though I had confirmed something that had been waiting for confirmation, not as though I had provided new information. "That is the channel. Three years of it, I would estimate. Long enough to be careful. Not long enough to have stopped being tempted by the visible demonstration of what he was earning."

The ring, in other words. The ring was the temptation winning over the caution, and Holkos-Nabu had reasoned the entire architecture of it from the mention of a piece of carnelian jewellery I had thought barely worth noting.

He moved ahead of me with that particular energy — not hurried, but absolutely purposeful, the walk of a man who knows where the load-bearing walls are and is already planning what comes after.

V.

He did not go to the body first.

"The body will tell me what stopped," he said. "The tablets will tell me what was happening when it was stopped. The sequence matters more than the conclusion."

He went to the draft shelf.

In our chancellery, there is a procedure for the composition of diplomatic dispatches. A senior translator prepares the initial draft in Akkadian. A junior translator produces a working version in the target language — in this case, Aramaic, for the correspondence with the western governors. The senior translator reviews and corrects. The corrected draft is then sealed and copied by the archive scribes. At each stage, the tablet passes through specific hands, and the passage is noted in the duty register.

Holkos-Nabu pulled the duty register from its shelf and read it the way he read everything important — standing completely still, lips not moving, face showing nothing.

Then he set it down and moved along the draft shelf, lifting tablets and turning them in the thin morning light from the high window slots.

"Bel-ibni. How many men in this building can read Aramaic?"

I considered. "Kibri-Adad. Urdu-Marduk. Yourself. And — " I paused. "Nabu-shum-iddina."

"Nabu-shum-iddina," he repeated. He set down the tablet he was holding and picked up another. "Who controls the seal matrix."

"Yes."

"Come here."

I came. He held the tablet so I could see its surface — the draft Aramaic version of the previous night's dispatch, prepared by Urdu-Marduk. It was covered in small correction marks.

"These corrections," I said. "Kibri-Adad reviewed it."

"Kibri-Adad reviewed something," Holkos-Nabu said. "Look at the marks. Here, and here. The stylus pressure is consistent — same hand throughout. But here —" his finger moved to a section in the lower third of the tablet "— the angle changes. Slightly. A man correcting another man's work moves with a particular kind of authority. A man writing his own corrections moves with a different weight. He is not questioning. He is instructing himself."

I looked more closely. "I don't see it."

"You see it. You are not yet looking in the right direction." He tilted the tablet toward the window light and held it there. "Look also at the line itself. The marks in the upper portion — Kibri-Adad's genuine corrections — are made with a broad scribal stylus, the standard tool of any translator in this building. But the alterations in the lower third are finer. Thinner. Made with something narrower than a scribe carries." He set the tablet down. "What instrument in this chancellery leaves a line that precise?"

I thought about it. The seal-keeper's tools came to mind — the fine bronze instruments Nabu-shum-iddina used when working directly on a matrix, where the impression surface was small and the line had to be exact. I had seen them at his belt often enough, a set of narrow-tipped styluses quite unlike anything a translator would use. I did not yet connect this thought to anything useful. I merely held it.

"I don't know," I said.

"You will," Holkos-Nabu said, and moved on.

"Kibri-Adad did not write these corrections," he continued, setting the tablet down. "They were added after his review was complete, and the draft was returned to the shelf to wait for the morning archive. Whoever added them knew they would be copied into the final sealed dispatch before anyone compared the corrections to Kibri-Adad's seal."

"What do they change?"

"Troop movement instructions." His voice was level, but there was something behind his eyes — appetite, is the only word for it, the look of a man who has found the load-bearing wall and now understands exactly what brought the building down. "The withdrawal schedule for the western garrison. The Akkadian original calls for the garrison to hold through the grain season. The Aramaic version, as corrected, tells the western governor that withdrawal is immediate — that he is being abandoned. A garrison that believes itself abandoned fragments. A frontier that fragments opens." He set the tablet down with a care that was itself precise. "The governor of Hatarikka has been paying, through a grain factor, through a brother's storage contracts, for a ring of carnelian and the destruction of a garrison. Kibri-Adad found the correction and went to confront the man who made it. He did not come back."

He took the tablet from the shelf and turned it once in his hands.

"Now," he said. "The body."

VI.

Kibri-Adad lay as the water-carrier had left him — arranged, as Holkos-Nabu immediately confirmed, by the same hands that had killed him. He moved to the clay marks near the entrance without my directing him to them, crouched over the sandal impression for a time, and straightened.

"You saw this," he said.

"When I first came in. I didn't know what to make of it."

"A double binding. Wider than standard. The clay is from the trough outside the seal chamber — fresh, from last night, not this morning's traffic. The man who carried Kibri-Adad here walked through that trough in the dark without noticing. He would have been careful in daylight." He looked at the trail leading to the body's position. "He was careful about a great many things. Not about his sandals."

He turned to the dead man's hand.

"The stylus is in his right hand," he said.

"Kibri-Adad wrote with his right hand. But —"

"He always cleaned it before setting it down," Holkos-Nabu said, nodding once. "Yes. The man who placed this stylus in his hand did not know that, because he has seen Kibri-Adad work but has not watched him finish. He manages seals and matrices, not drafts. The final gestures of a translator's day were invisible to him."

He looked at the lamp in its bracket above the tablet niche, burned entirely down and cold.

"The lamp burned from the evening lighting to its natural end — the second watch. The body was moved here after that, before the third watch, or the night duty scribe would have seen something during his rounds. Kibri-Adad died somewhere else in this building, and was arranged here to appear as a man who had simply fallen where he sat." He held up the draft tablet. "And this was returned to the shelf, with its alterations, to wait for the morning archive. By which point the dispatch would have been sealed, copied, and travelling west, and whatever Kibri-Adad had discovered would have died with him."

He looked at me then with that particular purposefulness.

"There is perhaps an hour before the administrator arrives," he said.

"Yes."

"Then we have exactly enough time. Go to Nabu-shum-iddina. Tell him Sharrukku requires his expert assessment — that a forgery has been alleged and the guard-captain needs the seal-keeper's professional opinion on whether the Aramaic draft shows signs of tampering by Urdu-Marduk. Tell him to come to the inner room immediately, and tell him to bring his instruments. Tell him nothing else."

I understood then the shape of what he was constructing. He was not inviting Nabu-shum-iddina to hear an accusation. He was asking Nabu-shum-iddina to authenticate his own crime.

"And if he refuses to come?"

"Then his refusal is its own answer, and we take the tablet to Sharrukku without him." The amber eyes were steady. "But he will come. He needs to know what has been found and what has not. The only way to know that is to be in the room."

VII.

I found Nabu-shum-iddina in the seal chamber.

He was a large man, heavyset in the way of those who had been strong in youth and since traded muscle for settled authority. His face was square and deliberate, with the kind of stillness that is not calm but control — a man accustomed to keeping things behind his expression. He was at his bench when I entered, though he was not working. His hands were flat on the surface before him in an attitude that struck me as carefully neutral, like a man who has decided in advance what his hands will be doing when the door opens.

At his left hip, on a leather loop attached to his belt, hung his working instruments — a set of three narrow-tipped bronze styluses of varying fineness, the tools he used when cutting or verifying seal matrices, where precision was essential and a standard scribal stylus too broad for the work. They were, I noticed now with a particular attention I had not brought to them before, exactly the kind of instrument that would leave a thinner, more precise line than anything in the translators' hall. I had seen them there for fourteen years without thinking about them. I thought about them now.

I glanced at his feet. The sandals were the ones from the previous evening. The double binding, wide-strapped and distinctive.

"Sharrukku requires your assessment," I said, and delivered the message as Holkos-Nabu had framed it — the allegation of forgery, the need for expert opinion on the Aramaic draft, the urgency. I watched his face as I spoke. The surface of it did not change, which told me a great deal, because the message I had delivered was one that should have prompted, in an innocent man, some expression of surprise.

"Of course," he said. His voice was smooth. He took his instruments from the belt loop — his working habit, I understood, reaching for the tools before the task was fully formed — and followed me down the corridor without hurrying, because he understood, as I had already noted, that the most dangerous thing he could do was to appear to hurry.

Holkos-Nabu was in the inner room when we arrived, standing at the draft shelf with his back to the door. The tablet with the altered corrections was on the shelf before him, visible, positioned as though it were simply one among several. Sharrukku was not there.

Nabu-shum-iddina's eyes went to the tablet immediately. Then to the empty room. Then, with the controlled deliberateness of a man recalibrating, to Holkos-Nabu's back.

"Where is Sharrukku?"

"Occupied." Holkos-Nabu did not turn. "I have asked him to give us a few minutes for the preliminary examination. Your professional eye on this tablet, before Sharrukku's less trained one, will help me frame the question correctly." He stepped aside from the shelf, clearing the space before the tablet. "Please. I need you to tell me whether, in your expert assessment, the corrections in the lower third of this draft were made by the same hand as the corrections in the upper portion."

The room was very still.

Nabu-shum-iddina looked at the tablet. I watched him understand, in the space of perhaps two seconds, the full geometry of the trap: that the tablet he was being asked to authenticate contained corrections he had made himself, in a hand he could not claim was identical to Kibri-Adad's without examining marks he knew were his own, and that whatever answer he gave — whatever he said, or refused to say — would be heard by the man who had already worked out the sequence.

He did not touch the tablet.

"I am a keeper of seal matrices," he said, and his voice had shed the smoothness and acquired something else — not panic, but a cold precision, the voice of a man shifting to a different strategy. "I have no expertise in handwriting assessment. That is the work of translators. If there is a question of forgery, it should be examined by Urdu-Marduk." He let a beat pass. "Or, perhaps, by you." He turned to Holkos-Nabu, who had still not faced him, and something entered his voice that was not quite an accusation but was shaped like one. "You read Aramaic. You were in this building last evening — I saw you at the late hour, Bel-ibni has confirmed as much. You have no title, no rank, no standing in this administration. You are a foreigner who has spent seven years making himself useful enough not to be removed. And you are now alone in a room, with a tablet that has been alleged to contain a forgery, constructing an inquiry that Sharrukku has not authorised."

He turned to me then. His eyes were very calm. "Bel-ibni. You have served this chancellery for eleven years. You understand what it means to make an accusation against a man of standing without evidence. You understand what happens to the men who make such accusations." He held my gaze for a moment. "Is this what you want to be part of?"

I will say this for him: he was good at it. The words were precisely chosen. For one moment — a genuine moment, not a short one — I felt the ground shift. Holkos-Nabu was a foreigner without rank. I was a scribe of moderate standing. Nabu-shum-iddina had administered the seal matrix of this chancellery for fourteen years. And we were alone in a room, with a tablet, and Sharrukku was elsewhere.

The moment stretched.

Then Holkos-Nabu turned around.

He had the tablet in his hands. He had moved to take it from the shelf at some point during Nabu-shum-iddina's speech, and neither of us had seen him do it, and he held it now with the particular quality of stillness he brought to everything that mattered, and he said, very quietly:

"The corrections in the lower third were not made with a scribal stylus. They were made with a finer instrument — one capable of a narrower, more precise line. An instrument of the kind used by a man who works daily on seal matrices, where a broad tip would ruin the impression." He tilted the tablet toward the window light, exactly as he had done during the investigation, so that the line of the alterations was visible. "The instrument you carry at your left hip. The one you brought with you into this room."

Nabu-shum-iddina's left hand closed around the tool at his belt.

It was, I think, a reflex rather than a decision. The closing of the hand around the thing that had been named. And it was enough.

"There it is," Holkos-Nabu said, simply.

What followed happened quickly. Nabu-shum-iddina moved — not toward Holkos-Nabu, but toward me, and he moved with the directness of a large man who has made a calculation and acted on it before the calculation was fully complete. The tool in his left hand was not a weapon, but his right hand was free, and I had time to understand that Kibri-Adad had also been in a corridor with this man when his options had run out, and that the resolution had been a physical one.

I put my back against the wall and had nowhere useful to go.

Holkos-Nabu crossed the room.

I do not entirely know how to describe what he did, because it did not look like the action of a man who fights — it looked like a continuation of the same purposeful movement he applied to everything, as though subduing a killer were simply the next item in the sequence. He stepped into Nabu-shum-iddina's path rather than away from it, caught the larger man's extended right arm at the wrist with both hands, and applied to the joint a pressure that was not violent in any dramatic sense but produced an immediate and total cessation of forward movement. Nabu-shum-iddina made a sound — sharp, involuntary — and stopped.

They stood like that for a moment: Holkos-Nabu spare and still, Nabu-shum-iddina immobilised by what appeared to be a minimal and precise application of knowledge about how a wrist works when it is taken against its own momentum.

"The tablet is already in my possession," Holkos-Nabu said, from a distance of perhaps six inches from the larger man's ear. His voice was entirely unchanged. "The instrument in your hand has been identified. Sharrukku is twenty yards from this door." He released the wrist. He stepped back. He held the tablet up, clearly, in the light from the window slots. "There are two remaining questions. Where is the silver? And what is the name of the man in Hatarikka who gave the instruction?"

Nabu-shum-iddina stood very still, his hand at his side, looking at the floor.

"Beneath the third step of my interior staircase," he said, in the voice of a man from whom something has finally, irrevocably, gone out. "A sealed jar. The name is the governor's grain factor — Arad-Adad. He came to me three years ago through my brother's contacts." He paused. "Kibri-Adad came to me rather than Sharrukku. He thought we could contain it between us. He was trying to protect the chancellery from the political consequence."

"He was a careful man," Holkos-Nabu said, "who was careful about the wrong things."

He walked to the door and called for Sharrukku.

VIII.

Sharrukku listened. He looked at the tablet, at Nabu-shum-iddina, and at Holkos-Nabu, in that order, with the expression of a man who has been handed a complete accounting he did not prepare and is deciding how to sign it. The silver was under the third step. Urdu-Marduk was released before the palace administrator arrived. What the administrator was told, I do not know, because I was not in that room.

I know only that Sharrukku received a commendation, and that the western dispatch was rewritten and resealed by Holkos-Nabu himself, working alone in the inner room that same afternoon with the door closed and the lamp burning.

IX.

I asked him, afterward, whether it troubled him — the commendation going to the man who had spent the morning arresting the wrong person.

"Sharrukku is competent," Holkos-Nabu said. He was back on his mat, the three tablets rearranged before him in some configuration I didn't recognise. His eyes were already half-closed. "Competence deserves its credit. What he cannot do is see past the first line of the document. That is not his function. It is mine."

"And if I write this account —"

"Write it accurately," he said. "The only dishonesty I cannot tolerate is one that corrupts the sequence. The sequence is everything. Everything else is politics."

I thought of Kibri-Adad, who had understood the value of silence and had applied it one moment past the point at which silence had stopped being safe. I thought of Urdu-Marduk against the wall with his hands flat to the plaster, saying only my name. I thought of the garrison at the edge of the western territories, whose men had stood to their posts that morning believing that their king had forgotten them — not knowing that a scribe was dead, that a seal-keeper had been taken, that a translation had been corrected back to its truth. They had known only that a message had come, in the proper form, bearing the proper seal, saying they were alone.

The message telling them otherwise was already travelling. Holkos-Nabu had written it in the inner room with the door closed, in the same careful hand that had found in the angle of a stylus, the impression of a sandal, and the involuntary closing of a man's fist around a tool he should not have recognised, the complete shape of his guilt.

I do not know whether what he did constitutes justice, in any sense that the courts of this city would recognise. I know only that it was accurate, and that accuracy, for men standing at a frontier waiting to learn whether they have been abandoned, is a form of mercy that no monument will record.

Thus recorded by Bel-ibni, Scribe of the Western Correspondence, in the thirty-first year of the great king. The tablet from which this account derives was discovered among the Kuyunjik archive deposits and is held in partial translation. The name Holkos-Nabu does not appear in any official administrative register of the period. The case of Kibri-Adad is recorded in the duty log only as a vacancy, filled the following month.
