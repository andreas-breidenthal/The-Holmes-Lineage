WILLIAM HOLMR

~1140 — Yorkshire, England

William Holmr was born in the worst years of the Anarchy and grew up in the peace that Henry II imposed upon a country exhausted by civil war — the Plantagenet settlement, with its systematic reconstruction of royal justice and administrative order, providing precisely the kind of environment in which a man of his particular gifts could find productive employment. The family estate in the North Riding had come through the chaos intact, thanks to his father’s careful management, and William inherited both the land and the reputation that went with it: a family known for accurate accounts, reliable testimony, and the uncommon quality of meaning exactly what they said.

He made himself useful to the new Plantagenet administration in the way the family had always made itself useful — by being the most reliable reader of a situation in whatever room he was in. He served as a local assessor under the early Assize system that Henry II was extending across England, evaluating land valuations and witnessing charters with the scrupulous precision of a man who understood that the document, once sealed, became the reality it described, and that errors in documents had consequences that outlasted the men who made them.

The account that follows is preserved in the report of Sir Geoffrey of Stainforth, a royal justice travelling the northern circuit in the service of Henry II. Geoffrey was not a scribe by training, but a justice accustomed to dictating findings to his clerk, whose notes were later recopied into a circuit record. The original clerk’s hand is visible in portions of the surviving manuscript, particularly where corrections were made in the margins. Geoffrey’s record is more concerned with outcome than method, yet in this case he preserves enough of William’s reasoning that the sequence may be followed with care.

The Circuit Record of Sir Geoffrey of Stainforth

Transcribed from the clerk Osbert's notes into fair copy at York, in the year of Our Lord 1167. Certain passages are expanded from marginal notation.

The autumn had come hard into the North Riding that year, driving rain off the moors in curtains so thick that a man could lose sight of his horse's ears. I had been riding the circuit for six weeks when we came down into the valley where the manor of Thornby stands — a grim hall of new-cut stone sitting in old mud, with a smell of wet sheep so pervasive that even the candles seemed to burn it. The hearth in the hall smoked badly. The rushes had not been changed since summer. These things I note because the atmosphere of the place imposed itself upon every proceeding, as if the house itself were reluctant to yield up its secrets.

The matter had been brought to my attention by the steward of Thornby, one Aldwin Foss — a compact, careful man with a face built for concealing things, who wore his authority like a cloak slightly too large. He had presented the formal complaint in proper form: that one Godric Thatcher, tenant of the lower furlong, had understated the value of his holding in the recent assessment to reduce his payment to the Crown. The sealed return, Aldwin said, showed a valuation of eleven shillings; the true worth, he maintained, was closer to sixteen. Godric was brought in with his hands still dirty from the field, his expression that of a man who has understood, suddenly and completely, that innocence is not the same thing as safety.

I would have proceeded in the ordinary fashion — questioned the parties, examined the document, reached a finding — had it not been for William Holmr.

He had attached himself to my circuit at Northallerton, where he had been acting as assessor for the previous fortnight, and he travelled now at my invitation because I had found his eye useful in two prior cases where the numbers in submitted charters had seemed somehow incorrect without my being able to say precisely why. He was a lean man, not tall, with long fingers that were always moving slightly, as if working through some calculation. He had his father's reputation and, I suspected, his father's habit of listening to rooms rather than people — of treating every space he entered as a document to be read.

During Aldwin's complaint, William sat to my left and said nothing. He was looking not at the steward but at the parchment Aldwin had submitted as evidence: the sealed return itself, sitting flat on the table between us.

When Aldwin finished, I asked whether he wished to add anything.

William said, "May I hold the document?"

I said he might.

He did not read it. He turned it over. He held it at an angle to the window, where what poor grey light came through the clouds struck it obliquely. He pressed a fingertip very lightly against the surface, once, in the middle of the sheet. He set it down.

"We should hear the tenant," he said.

Godric Thatcher's account was simple and consistent: he had appeared before the assessor at the proper time, given his valuation as eleven shillings, and the assessor had written it down. He had no skill with letters himself and had trusted the process as a man must trust a process he cannot verify. He had never seen the sealed return.

After Godric was taken aside, Aldwin asked smoothly whether the tenant's word was to be preferred over a sealed document.

"A sealed document," William said, "is not sealed air. It is parchment — animal skin, scraped, dried, and treated. It remembers things."

He rose and carried the return to the window, holding it so that the light lay across the surface.

"When a mark is made on parchment," he said, "pressure is applied. The instrument does not merely deposit ink — it presses the fibres. Even when the ink fades, the impression in the fibres does not."

On the sill, in the winter light, the earlier figures could be seen beneath the newer ones. Eleven shillings, overwritten.

There was a silence of some duration.

"The difference between eleven shillings and sixteen," I said finally, "is five shillings."

"Which, multiplied across a sufficient number of tenants," William said, "represents a considerable income."

What followed need not be set down in detail. Aldwin Foss denied everything with considerable fluency. His rooms were searched. Further altered returns were found. The pattern was consistent: a lower figure recorded at assessment, a higher figure entered into the return, and a margin taken between.

That evening, when the hall had fallen quiet, I asked William how he had known before the document was raised to the light.

"A freshly altered document sits differently than an aged one," he said. "The ink resists differently. The surface has a stiffness where it has been reworked. It is a small thing."

"And why were you looking for it?"

"The steward submitted the complaint," he said. "Not the assessor. A man watches the thing that can betray him."

I have recorded this matter in more detail than is perhaps strictly required for a circuit report. The formal summary records what was determined. This records how it was determined. The difference between them is the difference between law as record and law as practice.

The law is a document. William Holmr treats it as parchment: a material that remembers everything pressed into it, if one knows how to read it.

Geoffrey of Stainforth, Justice in Eyre, northern circuit.

York, Feast of Saint Cuthbert, 1167.
