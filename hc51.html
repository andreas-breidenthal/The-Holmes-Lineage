HOLKOS-SOKRATES

~550 BC — Athens, Greece

The name was a tribute and a recognition — Socrates himself was not yet born, would not be born for another eighty years, but the quality that the name would eventually designate was already present in this man, had been present in the family for generations, and was identified here, for the first time, with the Athenian tradition of rational inquiry that was reaching its early flowering in the decades of Peisistratos and his sons. Holkos-Sokrates was his father’s student and his city’s most exacting questioner — a man who could not encounter a confident claim without wanting to understand the basis of its confidence, and who found, on examination, that most confident claims rested on foundations that could not bear the weight of a serious question.

He was not always welcome at dinner. He was not, by his own account, always pleasant to argue with. But he was honest — genuinely, uncomfortably honest — about the difference between what he knew and what he believed, between what the evidence supported and what he wished it supported, and that honesty, in a city that was developing the institutions of democratic deliberation, was both necessary and rare.

His younger companion, Nikandros, son of a shipwright, served as scribe to this account. He was not a philosopher and did not pretend to be. He recorded what he saw and what he heard, sometimes with understanding, sometimes without it. He writes here as witness, not as interpreter, and acknowledges that his account is limited by what he could perceive and by what he was able to ask at the moment. The following record is drawn from his notes and preserved with his voice intact.

The Account Of Nikandros

Set down here so that what was truly observed may not be lost beneath what was officially declared.

The smell of the palaestra in high summer is not easily described to someone who has not spent time near one. It is the oil, first — the heavy Attic olive oil the athletes use on their skin before they wrestle, which soaks into the dust of the floor and the clay of the walls over years until the walls themselves seem to sweat. Then beneath that, the sharper smell of effort and heat, which is honest enough. And beneath that, if you know what to look for, the smell of ambition, which is not honest at all and which has no distinct quality of its own but can be sensed by its effects on those who carry it.

I had noticed the smell long before Holkos-Sokrates said anything about it. But I had not, as he would say, observed it. There is a difference.

It was the second hour after sunrise when the trainer Prokleides came to find us. We were sitting, as we often sat in those dead morning hours before the city properly woke, in the shade of the colonnade on the south face of the agora, Holkos-Sokrates watching the pigeons with an expression of faint contempt and I attempting, without success, to interest him in a bowl of olives.

"You are eating," he said, not to me but apparently to the pigeons, "without having first established what hunger is. You act on instinct. It must be restful."

"The olives are getting warm," I said.

"Everything gets warm and then cold. This is the primary complaint of all philosophy and I refuse to engage with it before the third hour." He was in one of his grey moods, which was when he was most dangerous — not because he became cruel, but because he became very still, and when Holkos-Sokrates became still he was like a bowstring under tension: the stillness was not rest but readiness. I had learned to watch for what would release it.

What released it was Prokleides, who arrived at a pace slightly faster than dignity allowed, with the look of a man carrying a problem he did not know how to put down.

Holkos-Sokrates saw him before I did. The stillness changed. Something came into his eyes — not warmth, exactly, but attention, which in him served the same function.

"A gymnasium man," he said quietly, to me. "The calluses on the palms from the leather straps, the tan stopping exactly at the collar — and he is distressed, but not afraid. He has not been threatened. He has been inconvenienced. There is a difference in how a man holds his shoulders." He paused. "Also, I know Prokleides. He is the trainer at the palaestra of Menandros near the Dipylon gate. Come. The olives can wait."

"They really cannot," I said, but I followed.

The story Prokleides told, as we walked toward the Dipylon gate with the morning traffic of the city pushing past us on all sides — the water-sellers, the early merchants, the slaves with their amphorae — was this:

A young man named Lykias, son of Demetrios, had been an athlete at the palaestra for three years. He was seventeen, a wrestler of moderate talent and serious application, the son of a leather-worker who had stretched himself financially to pay the fees, because a son who competed in the games brought honour to a family and opened doors that leather-working alone could not. Lykias had been, by all accounts, a diligent and inoffensive presence.

Two days ago, a bronze strigil had gone missing from the undressing room — not an ordinary strigil, but one belonging to Chairephon, son of Aristokrates, which had been a gift from his father and was engraved with the family dedication to Apollo. Chairephon was twenty, the son of a prosperous man of middling noble blood, and he had made, publicly and loudly, the accusation that Lykias had stolen it.

His evidence was this: he had seen Lykias near the undressing room at the relevant hour. Three other young men had confirmed they had seen Lykias in that area. The strigil was gone. Lykias had denied it, but denial was what a thief would say, and Lykias was, after all, the son of a leather-worker, and such boys sometimes looked at beautiful bronze things and felt they deserved them.

Lykias, under the weight of this public accusation before the other athletes, had fled the palaestra the previous morning and had not returned home. His father Demetrios had come to the palaestra at dawn today, a small man grey with worry, asking after his son, and Prokleides — who did not, I thought, believe the accusation entirely, but found himself without tools to challenge it — had heard that Holkos-Sokrates sometimes asked useful questions, and had come to find him.

Holkos-Sokrates listened to all of this without interrupting, which was unusual. His method was normally to begin questioning almost immediately. But he let Prokleides finish, and then he said only one thing before we reached the palaestra gate:

"Tell me about Chairephon and Lykias. Not the accusation. Before the accusation."

Prokleides hesitated. "They trained together."

"And?"

Another hesitation. "There was some — friction."

"Over what?"

"A place on the team for the Panathenaic games. Chairephon believed he had earned it. The selection went to Lykias."

Holkos-Sokrates made a small sound. Not quite satisfaction — he was not satisfied by anything before he had completed his examination — but recognition. As if a word he had been searching for had finally presented itself.

"Good," he said. "Now we can begin."

The palaestra of Menandros near the Dipylon gate was a modest establishment, as such places went — a square of beaten earth surrounded on three sides by a colonnade, with the undressing room and the oil-stores and the small shrine to Hermes occupying the fourth side. At the hour we arrived, a handful of young men were going through exercises in the sun, though with less than their usual focus; the drama of the last two days had unsettled the ordinary business of the place.

Chairephon was there. He had, I noticed, the particular bearing of a young man who is accustomed to being taken seriously and was currently taking himself very seriously indeed. He stood in the shade of the colonnade with two companions, and when Prokleides brought us to him, he looked at Holkos-Sokrates with the polite condescension of wealth encountering an unfamiliar person of uncertain status.

"You are the questioner," Chairephon said. "Prokleides said you might come. I am happy to speak with you, though I don't know what question remains. The matter is clear enough."

"Is it?" said Holkos-Sokrates pleasantly. "Then you will not object to my examining it. A clear matter survives examination. Only an unclear matter fears questions."

Chairephon smiled, a little stiffly. "Ask."

"The strigil. Describe it for me."

"Bronze. Eight fingers in length. My father's name engraved on the handle, and beneath it, the dedication — Aristokrates son of Lysimachus, to Apollo of the Gymnasium."

"And it was in the undressing room."

"On the bench beside my clothing. I had oiled and scraped before my exercises and left it there — I often do. When I returned, it was gone."

"And you immediately suspected Lykias."

"I saw him near the room."

"Near," said Holkos-Sokrates. "This is an interesting word. Where were you when you saw him?"

A small pause. "Coming from the exercise floor. Toward the room."

"So you were approaching the undressing room from the exercise floor, and you saw Lykias — where, exactly?"

"Near the entrance to the room."

"Coming out of it, or going in, or standing outside it?"

The pause this time was longer. "Standing near the entrance."

"Standing," said Holkos-Sokrates. "Not moving. He was not emerging from the room with the strigil in his hand. He was standing near the entrance."

"He had been inside."

"Had he? Did you see him come out?"

"He must have."

Holkos-Sokrates tilted his head. He had a way of doing this — a slight tilt, very precise, as if he were adjusting an instrument — that I had come to recognise as the moment when he had found the first loose thread. "Chairephon," he said, with the same pleasant tone he had maintained throughout, "you have just told me two different things. First you said you saw him near the room. Now you say he must have been inside. Which of these is something you observed, and which is something you concluded?"

Chairephon's jaw tightened slightly. "He was seen near the room. The strigil was missing. The conclusion is reasonable."

"The conclusion may be reasonable. But a reasonable conclusion and an observation are different things, and I am trying to understand which you have. What did your companions see?"

The companions were named Alkias and Sosias — young men of similar background to Chairephon, which is to say sons of middling prosperous families who exercised at the same palaestra and moved in the same social orbit. They had been, from the beginning of this examination, standing slightly behind Chairephon in the manner of witnesses who had agreed on their testimony and were waiting to deliver it.

Holkos-Sokrates spoke to them separately, which they had not expected. He asked Prokleides to take Alkias to the far end of the colonnade, and he spoke to Sosias first, and I stood beside him as I always did, as witness and record.

"Where were you when you saw Lykias near the undressing room?" he asked Sosias.

"At the water basin. By the north column."

Holkos-Sokrates walked to the north column and looked at the entrance to the undressing room. Then he walked back. "How far is that? Thirty feet? Forty?"

"Perhaps."

"And Lykias was — standing? Walking?"

"Standing. Looking around."

"Looking around," said Holkos-Sokrates. "In which direction?"

Sosias frowned. This was clearly not a question he had prepared for. "I — toward the exercise floor, I think."

"Toward the exercise floor. So he was standing near the entrance to the undressing room, looking toward the exercise floor, not toward the undressing room."

"He may have just come out."

"He may have. Or he may have been waiting for a friend, or looking for the trainer, or watching a particular exercise. You did not see him enter or leave the room. You saw him standing near it, looking in another direction."

Sosias said nothing. The silence was enough.

Alkias, when questioned, proved even less certain. He had been at the shrine of Hermes when he saw Lykias — further even than Sosias. He had seen a figure near the undressing room. He believed the figure was Lykias. He had not, under examination, seen the figure's face clearly. He had assumed, after Chairephon announced the theft, that the figure must have been Lykias.

"Must have been," said Holkos-Sokrates softly, as he walked back toward me. "They are all explaining what must have been, Nikandros. Not what was. The testimony has already been shaped by the accusation. They saw something consistent with Lykias being there, and then the accusation told them what they had seen." He paused. "This is how false memory grows. It is not lying, necessarily. It is the mind filling gaps with what the conclusion demands. The mind is very efficient at this. It is not always helpful."

He looked across the palaestra to where Chairephon stood watching us.

"Now," he said, "let us speak to the steward."

The steward of the palaestra was an old man named Theoros, who had served Menandros for twenty years and had the particular dignity of those who have maintained a household or an institution long enough to become part of its fabric. He was oiling the hinge of the storeroom door when we found him — a specific sound, the iron voice of that hinge, which would trouble me for some time afterward whenever I heard a gate in need of oil — and he looked at us with the calm of someone who was not accustomed to being questioned by strangers and was not certain he approved of the innovation.

Holkos-Sokrates did not ask him about Lykias first. He asked him about the palaestra.

He asked about the routine of the place — who arrived when, who used which part of the facility, how the undressing room was typically occupied and at what hours. He asked about the storage of valuables, whether athletes kept items in the room during exercises or brought them back and forth. He asked about the oil — who maintained the supply, where it was kept, who had access.

Theoros answered these questions with the precision of a man who had organized a physical space for two decades and knew its rhythms. And then Holkos-Sokrates asked something that made the old steward's expression shift, just slightly — not guilt, exactly, but discomfort.

"The bronze strigil of Aristokrates — had you seen it before? Had it been left in the undressing room previously?"

"Chairephon brought it most days," Theoros said carefully.

"And was it ever misplaced before?"

A pause. "Once. Several weeks ago. It was found in the oil store."

"In the oil store," said Holkos-Sokrates. "How did it come to be there?"

"I — could not say."

"Theoros." Holkos-Sokrates's voice did not change in tone, but something in it shifted to a different register — gentle still, but no longer casual. "I am not an official. I have no power to prosecute anyone. A young man is missing from his home and his father is grey with grief. I am asking questions because questions are the only instrument I possess, and I am asking you this particular question because I think you know the answer to it, and I think the answer may matter."

The old steward looked at the gate hinge. He looked at his oilcloth. He was, I thought, making a calculation about what could be kept quiet and what could not, and concluding that the calculation had already been made once and had not served him well.

"Chairephon left it in the oil store himself," he said finally. "Three weeks ago. He forgot it — he had been talking with the others and he left it on a shelf when he went to take oil for his skin. I found it and gave it back to him the next morning."

"He was careless with it before."

"He is careless with his things. He has money enough that carelessness has never cost him."

"And when the strigil was reported missing two days ago — did you look in the oil store?"

The silence that followed was the most informative silence of the entire morning.

"Show me," said Holkos-Sokrates.

The oil store was a small room adjacent to the undressing room, smelling of the heavy Attic olive oil that permeated the walls and the air and the wooden shelves along three sides. On the lowest shelf, behind an amphora of ordinary training oil, tucked into a back corner in a manner entirely consistent with being set down absent-mindedly and forgotten, was a bronze strigil eight fingers in length, with the name of Aristokrates son of Lysimachus engraved on the handle, and beneath it, the dedication to Apollo.

Holkos-Sokrates looked at it for a long moment without touching it. Then he said, "Did Chairephon know it was here?"

Theoros said quietly, "I cannot say what Chairephon knew."

"But you knew."

Another long pause. "I found it yesterday evening. After the accusation. I — I did not know what to do with the knowledge."

"You knew a boy had been publicly shamed and had fled his home."

"Chairephon's father is a patron of this palaestra. He provides—"

"I understand what he provides." Holkos-Sokrates's voice, for the first time that morning, carried something colder than neutrality. "I am not asking you to explain what he provides. I am asking you to understand what that explanation costs."

He picked up the strigil and turned it in his hands. Then he turned to me. "Nikandros — observe this. The strigil is not stolen. It is here, where it was left, by the same carelessness that left it here three weeks ago. The accusation of theft is not supported by any testimony that would survive a serious question. Three witnesses saw a young man standing near a room and concluded, after being told a theft had occurred, that he must have been the thief. Not one of them saw him enter or leave the room. Not one of them saw the strigil in his possession." He set the strigil down. "A man announced that he had been wronged. He chose, as the person responsible for the wrong, someone he had reason to dislike. The witnesses saw what they expected to see and then reported it as evidence. And a boy is now somewhere in this city, frightened, having done nothing."

He said it plainly, as if describing the composition of a metal or the arrangement of stars. No anger, which I found somehow more disturbing than anger.

We returned to the exercise floor. Prokleides was there, and so was Chairephon, who had the manner of someone who has been waiting through an examination longer than he expected and has begun to feel uncertain about its direction.

Holkos-Sokrates walked to the centre of the exercise floor, so that the young men at their practice and Prokleides and Theoros and Chairephon's two companions were all roughly within hearing distance. He did not raise his voice. He simply spoke at a volume that carried.

"Chairephon. Your strigil is in the oil store, where you left it."

The quality of silence that followed was unlike the previous silences. This one had weight in it — the weight of the two companions suddenly finding reasons to examine their feet, the weight of Prokleides's carefully neutral expression, the weight of the young wrestlers pausing at their exercises.

Chairephon's face went through several arrangements before settling on one. "I — it must have been moved there. After—"

"By whom? The strigil was there yesterday evening, before which time Lykias had already been accused and had already fled. Who moved it? Theoros found it and told no one, which was wrong of him, though understandable given the pressures of his position. The strigil was not in Lykias's possession. It was never in Lykias's possession. You saw him near the undressing room — near which many people stand throughout the morning — and you announced him a thief." He paused. "You had a reason for this. You lost your place on the Panathenaic team to him in fair competition, and you experienced that loss as an insult, which is natural enough in a young man of your background. What is not natural, or should not be natural, is announcing a man's son a thief before his training companions on the basis of proximity and dislike."

Chairephon said nothing. He was, I noted, not yet ashamed — he was calculating. I could see the calculation. There was a social architecture around him that the accusation had never truly threatened, because Theoros had stayed silent and because a leather-worker's son has less recourse than a prosperous man's son when accused.

Holkos-Sokrates looked at him for a long moment. Then he said, more quietly, "Prokleides will need to find the boy Lykias and bring him back, and there will need to be some public correction of what was said about him. How that correction is framed, and what your role in it is described to be, are things you can still, to some degree, influence. A man who discovers he was mistaken is in a better position than a man who maintained a mistaken accusation knowing it to be mistaken. I am explaining this to you as clearly as I can, because I find obscurity unhelpful."

Here Nikandros's narrative continues with a postscript, added some days later:

Lykias was found by his uncle on the third day, sheltering in a pottery yard across the city where he had been sleeping among the unfired amphorae. He was cold and had eaten very little and he was, according to his father, not yet fully able to believe that the strigil had simply been found, that it was over, that he could return.

Prokleides made a public announcement at the palaestra that the strigil had been discovered in the oil store and that the matter of the accusation was closed. The word mistake was used. The word Chairephon was not used in connection with the word false, though every person present understood the connection to be made. Chairephon's father made a donation to the shrine of Hermes at the palaestra, the purpose of which was understood by everyone and explained by no one.

Lykias competed in the Panathenaic games that autumn. He did not place in the wrestling. But he competed, and his father was there to watch, and a leather-worker in the crowd of Athens is as entitled to his pride as any other man.

I asked Holkos-Sokrates, the evening after the strigil was found, whether it satisfied him.

"Satisfied?" He was in one of his vivid moods now — the stillness entirely gone, some interior machine running at a pace I could not follow. He had been posing himself riddles all afternoon and answering them with the appetite of a man who has been hungry for days. "Satisfaction is for men who have reached a final conclusion. I have not. The strigil is found. That particular false claim is dismantled. But Chairephon made the claim in full confidence that it would hold — that confidence is interesting. He believed the architecture of his social position would bear the weight of an unsupported accusation. He was almost right. He would have been right, if Theoros had not been honest and if I had not asked questions in an order he did not anticipate." He picked up a small stone and turned it in his fingers. "What this means is that there are a great many accusations, in this city and every city, that are never questioned in that order. The strigil is found. The larger question is not."

"And what is the larger question?"

He set the stone down. "How many confident claims," he said, "are resting on foundations they have never been asked to name?"

I wrote it down. I did not have an answer. I suspected, and still suspect, that he did not either, and that this was precisely why it interested him.

— Nikandros, son of Theophrastos, in the archonship of Hippias, at Athens.
