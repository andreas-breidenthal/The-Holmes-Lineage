THOMAS DE HOLMES

~1240 — Yorkshire, England

Thomas de Holmes inherited from his father a settled estate and a local reputation for fair dealing, and spent the middle decades of the thirteenth century holding both intact through a period of considerable political turbulence. The Barons’ Wars of 1264–65 — the conflict between Henry Ⅲ and Simon de Montfort that convulsed English political life and drew every landowner of consequence into one camp or another — presented him with the dilemma that had faced his ancestors at every comparable crossroads: which side, and when.

He chose, as Yorkshire gentry tended to choose, the path of local administration over baronial adventure — presenting himself as a servant of order rather than a partisan of any faction. He sat as one of the keepers of the peace, administered the local court, and maintained the estate with careful attention to accounts, tenants, and the condition of the land itself. He measured his success not in alliances made but in stability preserved, noting rents, repairs, and disputes with the same steady hand that his father had used in earlier records. Where others spoke in terms of cause and loyalty, Thomas kept to what could be recorded, verified, and upheld.

The record of his actions survives through the account of Brother Elias of Whitby, a Benedictine scribe assigned for a time to record legal and administrative matters in the North Riding. Elias was a man trained in the keeping of monastic records — accustomed to copying charters, noting tithes, and preserving the wording of grants with exactitude. He approached Thomas’s affairs with a different discipline than his predecessor, less concerned with the subtleties of testimony and more attentive to the material conditions in which documents and claims were produced. His record forms the basis of the account that follows.

The Account of Brother Elias of Whitby

A note upon this record:
I am Brother Elias, formerly of the scriptorium at Whitby, set to work in the North Riding at the direction of the Prior during the years of the great contention between the King and the Earl of Leicester. My office was the keeping of records — the copying of charters, the noting of proceedings, the preservation of what was said and witnessed before the commission. I had not, before my time with Thomas de Holmes, thought of record-keeping as anything other than a craft of patience. I have since revised that opinion. What follows I have set down as faithfully as I am able, though I have exercised the discretion that any honest scribe must exercise when the full truth, plainly stated, would serve no living man and injure several. The official record of this matter credits the commission with its resolution. That is the official record. This is the other one.

The morning of the fourteenth day of October came in cold and grey, with a mist off the low ground that settled into the yard of the assay house and did not lift until well past Terce. I remember the smell of it — wet stone, and beneath that the sharper smell of the tallow lamps that the groom had left burning all night, a pale and greasy light still guttering when we arrived. Someone had sent word to the manor before dawn.

The dead man was Walter Croft, sub-clerk to the commission, a careful and unremarkable functionary of perhaps forty years who had spent his career in the careful and unremarkable service of whoever held the commission's seal. He had been found face-down across the trestle table in the lower room of the assay house, which served on occasion as a place of temporary record-keeping when the main hall was occupied with baronial business. His neck had been broken. A cup lay on its side near his right hand. The candle in the iron holder had burned entirely to its socket and gone out.

Two men from the commission's guard were already standing in the yard when Thomas arrived. I was behind him, my writing box under my arm, unsure whether my presence was required or merely assumed.

Thomas did not look at the guard. He looked at the ground of the yard.

"The gate," he said.

"My lord?"

"The hinge on the inner gate. It was oiled within the last week. Do you hear it? There is no sound. But the outer gate —" He paused, and we all heard it: a faint, dry complaint of iron on iron when the wind moved the upper hinge. "The outer gate has not been attended to since midsummer at the earliest. Note it, Elias. The inner gate was recently silenced."

I noted it, though I could not yet see why I should.

Inside, Thomas stood in the doorway of the lower room for what felt like a considerable time before he moved. He had a habit, which I had observed over several months, of becoming very still at the beginning of things. During the waiting times — the weeks between sessions, when the commission's business was slow and the petitioners few — he was given to a restless irritability that made the hall uncomfortable and the servants wary. He would spend hours examining samples of soil from disputed field-boundaries, or re-reading account rolls that had already been copied and filed, or constructing small models from wax and splints to demonstrate to himself the geometry of some contested fence-line. I had concluded that the difficulty was simply that his mind, when it had nothing sufficient to occupy it, turned against itself. But here, now, it had found its quarry, and the stillness was the stillness of absolute concentration.

"Come in," he said at last. "But stay by the wall."

The commission's captain of guard was a man named Aldred — competent, well-meaning, and entirely certain of what had happened.

"It's the factions, my lord," he said, when Thomas had completed his first survey of the room. "Croft certified the Fenwick transfer yesterday afternoon. Fenwick lands go to the priory by this transfer, which means the de Montfort men lose a tenancy they've been depending on for provisioning. Everyone in the Riding knows it. I'd look first at—"

"You would look first at the most convenient explanation," Thomas said, without turning from the table. "Which is always the least likely to be correct, because convenient explanations are generally constructed after the fact by minds that have already decided what they wish to find."

Aldred said nothing. He had the expression of a man who has been corrected often enough to have made his peace with it.

Thomas was examining the documents on the table. There were three: the transfer instrument itself, a folio of parchment, sealed with Croft's seal and with the commission's seal; a secondary notation sheet in Croft's own hand; and a smaller piece, torn at one edge, which appeared to be a fragment of a letter.

"Elias," he said. "Come here. You know Croft's hand."

"I do. I have worked alongside it for eight months."

"Read me what is on the notation sheet."

I came forward and read. It was, at first appearance, entirely routine — a record of the parties present at the transfer, the consideration paid, the date, the witnesses. I said as much.

"The date," Thomas said. "Read it precisely."

"Die Jovis, post festum Sancti Edwardi—" I stopped.

"Thursday," Thomas said. "After the feast of Saint Edward. The feast of Saint Edward the Confessor is the thirteenth of October."

"Yesterday," I said.

"Yesterday was Thursday. Yes. And Croft wrote this notation yesterday, at the time of the transfer. Now." He pointed to the transfer instrument itself, the formal document bearing both seals. "Read the date on the instrument."

I looked. "Die Mercurii—" I stopped again. Wednesday. The day before.

"The instrument is dated the day before the transfer it records," Thomas said. He was not speaking to me now, but thinking aloud, in the manner I had come to recognise. "Which means either the instrument was prepared in advance and the date was assumed — a common enough informality — or the instrument was dated falsely, to place the certification before a particular event that occurred on Wednesday evening. Elias. What occurred on Wednesday evening that would affect a transfer of the Fenwick lands?"

I thought. "The Earl's commissioner arrived in Northallerton on Wednesday evening. There was word that he carried a general order suspending private land transfers in the disputed tenancies."

"If the Fenwick transfer was dated Wednesday," Thomas said, "it predates the suspension order and is therefore valid. If it is dated Thursday, correctly, it may fall under the suspension and be voided." He was quiet for a moment. "Croft knew this. Look at his notation sheet. He noted the correct date, Thursday, in his own record — because Croft was a careful man, and he did not falsify his own notes. But someone wanted the instrument to carry Wednesday's date. And Croft — having certified it and sealed it with Wednesday's date at someone's instruction — wrote Thursday in his notes, perhaps as his own private evidence of what had actually occurred."

"Then the factions—" Aldred began.

"The factions," Thomas said, "had every reason to want this transfer to stand, or to fail, depending on which faction you preferred. But the factions did not kill Walter Croft. A faction does not go to the trouble of silencing a gate hinge."

He spent another hour in that room. I will not pretend I followed every step of it. I am a scribe, trained in the orderly reproduction of what is placed before me; Thomas was doing something that resembled, from the outside, a kind of conversation with objects that could not speak.

He examined the cup. It had contained wine; the smell was still present, and a dark ring marked the interior. He held it to the remaining light from the doorway, turning it, and then set it down without comment. He looked at the candle-socket, and at the pool of hardened tallow beneath it, and said, to no one in particular, "Eight hours, perhaps nine. A full night candle." He looked at the position of Croft's body — not disturbed, I noted, as he had instructed Aldred's men to leave it — and at the angle of the fall, and at the condition of Croft's hands, which were unmarked. No struggle. The man had not known what was coming until it was done.

Then he crouched and looked at the floor beneath the table, on the side away from where Croft had sat.

"Elias. The lamp in this room."

"The hanging lamp? Yes."

"What fuel does it burn?"

I looked. "It appears to be rendered fat — the common sort. You can smell it."

"Quite so. The common sort, used throughout the assay house, throughout the yard, throughout every outbuilding. The smell is consistent." He reached under the table and produced, carefully, a small piece of rush with a darkened tip — a rushlight, the cheap and common kind used in cottages and lesser establishments. "Except that someone sat under this table, in the dark, burning a rushlight, and waited. Why would you sit under a table burning a rushlight when there is a hanging lamp burning above you?"

"Because," I said slowly, "you did not wish to be seen from the doorway."

"Because you arrived before Croft, let yourself in through the inner gate — which you had recently oiled to silence, because you knew you might need to pass through it quietly — and concealed yourself to wait. You did not light the hanging lamp because light under the door would have alerted Croft that someone was inside. You used a rushlight below the level of the table's edge, sufficient to see by, invisible from outside." He straightened. "This was not a crime of passion or politics. This was a crime of preparation."

"Then who—" Aldred said.

"The fragment," Thomas said, and returned to the torn piece of letter on the table. He did not read it aloud immediately. He read it twice, to himself, and then he was still for a long moment, and when he spoke again his voice had the quality of a man completing an arithmetical sum.

"The Fenwick transfer conveys land to the priory," he said. "But it does so at a consideration of twelve marks. Twelve marks is considerably below the value of the land. The priory acquires it cheaply. Why?"

"A gift," Aldred offered. "Piety."

"Geoffrey Fenwick is not a man noted for piety. He is noted for debt. Twelve marks is not a gift; twelve marks is the settlement of an obligation — a private obligation that it would not suit either party to record in the usual form. Now." He held up the fragment. "This is part of a letter. The other part was burned — you can see the ash in the corner of the room; Croft attempted to burn something when he realised what was coming, or perhaps he attempted to burn it earlier and was interrupted. What remains reads, in part: ...the matter of the mill cannot be known at Michaelmas if the transfer stands as dated. I have spoken with H and he is prepared to move the question to the spring assizes if you will confirm... The letter is unsigned. But the hand — Elias."

I looked at the fragment. It was a clear, practised hand, the hand of a man accustomed to writing his own correspondence rather than dictating it. I knew it.

I said the name.

"Yes," Thomas said. "Henry Mauger. Who holds the mill-rights that adjoin the Fenwick lands, and who stands to lose those rights if the transfer to the priory stands, because the priory will contest the mill-boundary that Mauger has held informally for twenty years, and the priory, unlike Fenwick, has the resources and the canonical standing to pursue the contest to its conclusion."

The room was quiet except for the wind against the shutter.

"Mauger needed the transfer voided," Thomas said. "But he could not void it through the commission — the instrument was sealed. He could not rely on the suspension order, because the instrument was backdated to Wednesday. He could not challenge Croft's notation openly, because to do so would reveal the false dating, which would expose both him and Fenwick to consequences neither would welcome. So he needed the instrument itself — the sealed transfer — to disappear. Croft would not give it to him. So Croft had to disappear first."

What followed I will compress, because it is the architecture of the conclusion that matters, not the lengthy process of Aldred's resistance and eventual capitulation to what the evidence required.

Thomas sent me to the mill that same afternoon with a short letter to Mauger requesting his attendance at the assay house to assist the commission's inquiry, on the grounds that his mill-boundary question touched upon the documents in Croft's possession. The letter was politely worded and suggested no accusation. I delivered it myself, and I observed Mauger read it, and I observed the quality of the pause before he agreed to come.

He arrived that evening, at the hour of Vespers. The mist had returned.

Thomas had arranged the room with a care that I recognised, by then, as deliberate. The documents were laid out on the table. The rushlight stub had been placed where it could be seen. The cup was positioned where Mauger could not easily avoid looking at it. Aldred and one of the guard stood against the wall, not obviously present.

Thomas let Mauger speak first about the mill boundary, at some length, and listened with apparent patience, and asked several questions about the dates of Mauger's informal tenure, and the nature of his dealings with Fenwick, and whether he had any recent correspondence with Croft on the matter.

"None," Mauger said. "I had no dealings with Croft directly."

"That is interesting," Thomas said, "because a fragment of a letter in your hand was found among Croft's documents, mentioning the mill question and a conversation with a man whose name is abbreviated to H. Would you tell me who H is?"

The pause was longer this time.

Thomas did not fill it. He waited with the particular patience of a man who knows that silence, properly maintained, eventually produces more than any question.

When he did speak, it was not to accuse. It was to lay out, in sequence, each element of what the room had disclosed — the oiled gate hinge, the rushlight, the false date on the instrument, the notation sheet with its honest Thursday, the ash in the corner, and the letter fragment. He spoke of each one in the measured tone of a man reading an account roll, as though he were simply recording what the evidence stated, rather than indicting anyone. He made it, in that way, feel inevitable. As though the room itself had written it down, and he was merely reading aloud.

Mauger's composure lasted until the rushlight.

He had not known the rushlight had survived. That was visible in his face. He had counted on the darkness under the table to make its presence impossible to find.

The rest was, as they say, a matter of procedure.

Henry Mauger was taken before the commission. The official proceeding — which I copied, and which exists in the enrolled records of the North Riding — states that he was identified as the killer of Walter Croft on the evidence of a letter fragment and witness testimony regarding his recent presence in the area of the assay house. He was convicted and hanged before Martinmas. The record credits the commission's diligence.

There are two matters that the official record does not contain, and which I set down here because a record that omits them is not, in my judgment, a complete record, whatever its procedural sufficiency.

The first: the question of Geoffrey Fenwick and the false dating of the transfer instrument. Thomas brought this to my attention privately, after the proceedings, and asked what I thought should be done with it. The instrument, correctly, should have been voided and Fenwick examined for his part in the fraudulent dating. Thomas sat with this question for some time.

"Fenwick did not kill anyone," he said at last. "He falsified a date on a document to protect a debt settlement that should not have been entered in that form. If we pursue it, the priory loses a legitimate transfer that was otherwise honestly transacted. Several of the priory's dependent tenants lose a degree of protection they currently have. The commission spends the winter in a proceeding that will produce nothing but noise and a modest fine, during which the actual question — who governs these lands and under what terms — remains unsettled." He paused. "Or we note that the instrument is sealed and enrolled and Croft's killer is already convicted, and we proceed."

We proceeded. I have noted it here because it seemed to me that someone should.

The second matter concerns Thomas himself, and his state in the weeks that followed. The great turbulence of the Barons' Wars moved through the North Riding that winter in the form of commissioners and counter-commissioners, contradictory orders, and a general atmosphere of administrative uncertainty that produced, for the commission, an extraordinary volume of routine business. Thomas sat through all of it with the steady, attentive patience that I had observed since my arrival. He was fair. He was thorough. He read every document presented to him.

But I noted that he had begun, in the evenings, to spend considerable time in the records room, re-reading old account rolls from decades previous — not for any apparent case, but for the texture of it, I believe, for the sensation of following a chain of reasoning through time. I brought him candles without being asked. I said nothing about it, because it was not my place, and because I had come to understand that this was his version of what the rushlight under the table had been for Mauger — a way of occupying a mind that would otherwise grow ungovernable.

The difference, of course, being that Thomas read his old rolls to preserve order, and Mauger had sat in the dark to destroy it.

That, I have always thought, is the essential distinction. It is not, in the end, the quality of the attention. It is the direction in which it is applied.

Thus ends the account of this matter, as I have been able to set it down. If any reader of this record should find it wanting in legal precision, I would observe that legal precision was the work of the enrolled proceedings, which any clerk of the court may consult. My work was the other kind of precision — the kind that requires you to look at a rushlight stub and understand what it means that a man sat alone in the dark, and waited.

— Frater Elias, Whitby, anno Domini MCCLXV
