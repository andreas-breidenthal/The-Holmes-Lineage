HOLMAR OF BURGUNDY

~435 AD — Burgundy, Frankish territory

The Burgundians had settled in the Rhône valley and the territory that would take their name, and Holmar grew up among them — a family of Rhine German origin now embedded in a Burgundian political world that was itself embedded in the decaying Roman administrative structure of the south. The name was shifting: less Gothic now, more Frankish in its sound, the family adapting its identity as it always had to the prevailing culture without quite dissolving into it.

Holmar served the Burgundian court as a scribe and record-keeper, maintaining the kind of documentation — land grants, tribute records, the terms of agreements between petty kings and their subordinates — that allowed a polity to function as something more than organised plunder. He understood that written records were power of a particular and durable kind, that the man who held the documents often outlasted the man who held the sword, and he organised his life accordingly. His records were accurate and his copies were good and he died in his bed, which in fifth-century Burgundy represented a considerable achievement.

The account that follows is preserved in a codex fragment copied by a later Burgundian archivist named Sigibert, who appended marginal notes correcting and clarifying earlier entries. Sigibert did not possess Holmar’s ability to perceive irregularities at a glance, but he possessed a careful hand and a respect for precision. His notes reveal both the strength and the limitation of his position: he recorded what was written and what he was told, but his conclusions lagged behind his observations, and where he suspected error he annotated rather than resolved it. The result is a layered record in which the original hand and the later commentary can be distinguished.

The smell of the Rhône in autumn was a particular thing. Not the clean coldness of the river in flood, which carried the honest mud of Alpine meadows and the cold mineral clarity of snowmelt, but the thick-banked autumnal odour of low water and rotting reed, of the fish-traps left too long, of the silt in which the previous season’s smaller catastrophes had come to rest and begun their slow decomposition. Holmar had lived within a quarter-mile of the river for the better part of his forty years, and he had ceased to notice it as other men ceased to notice the sound of their own heartbeat—but on the morning that Theudebald’s man came hammering at the gate in the grey hour before lauds, the river smell rose with particular intensity through the fog, as though the water itself had something to announce.

He lay still for a moment after waking, staring at the underside of the thatch. He could hear three things: the hammering, which was rhythmic and suggested a man who had not yet exhausted his patience; the breathing of his copyist Ansgar in the outer room, which had shifted from the long regularity of deep sleep into the shallow irregularity of a man pretending not to be awake; and beneath both of these, half-heard, the particular creak of the river gate three streets over, which swung on an iron hinge that had not been oiled since Midsummer and announced each tide-fluctuation with a sound like a cat in dispute with a larger cat.

It was the gate-hinge that told him the hour more accurately than any water-clock. Low tide. Therefore shortly before the second hour of morning. Therefore Theudebald’s man had ridden through the night, which meant that Theudebald considered the matter urgent. Whether Theudebald’s assessment of urgency was reliable was a separate question.

He rose, wrapped his heavy wool cloak over his sleeping-tunic, and opened the door before the hammering could begin its fourth sequence.

The man on the step was young and mud-covered to the thigh and his horse, visible at the gate-post, stood with its head low and its sides still heaving in the lamplight. The man began to speak.

“Lord Theudebald—”

“Has sent you without waiting for morning,” Holmar said, “which means the matter concerns the court, and he fears what daylight will bring. Whether he fears it will bring an inquiry or an escape I cannot yet say. Come in. Ansgar—” he raised his voice only slightly— “stop pretending. Heat water.”

The sound of Ansgar moving, with great dignity and no acknowledgment of having been asleep, came from the outer room.

It is necessary to explain the positions of the parties as Holmar understood them by the time he had heard the messenger’s account and consumed the better part of a flask of the thin Burgundian wine he kept for morning necessities, which was not good wine but was warm and did the work required of it.

The land in question lay some four miles east of Lugdunum, toward the hills—a villa property of perhaps two hundred iugera, good river-bottom soil producing grain and some wine-grapes and a stand of managed woodland that provided both timber and grazing. It had been the property of the Carantii, a Gallo-Roman family that had survived the transition from imperial administration to Burgundian rule by the reliable method of making themselves useful to each successive power: they kept their land records accurate, paid their levies without argument, and attended the court at the appropriate festivals without drawing attention to their religious opinions, which were their own business. The father of the present Carantius had served as a minor fiscal assessor; Carantius himself had no court position but maintained good relations, and was not the kind of man who made enemies.

The document that had destroyed him was a land grant, or rather a confirmation of grant, drafted eighteen months prior and bearing the seal of Gundobad’s court—the kind of document that confirmed existing ownership against competing claims, a legal fortress built in parchment and wax. Carantius had paid the clerk’s fee, received his copy, and considered the matter settled.

The competing claim had been filed six weeks ago by Richomer, lord of Ambria, a Burgundian of the second generation whose holdings lay directly adjacent to the Carantii property on its eastern margin. Richomer was ambitious in the way of men who have enough but have calculated that more is possible. He had submitted a document of his own: a grant from an earlier date, predating the Carantii confirmation, which purported to assign the villa property to his family line in partial settlement of a military obligation. The seals were in order. The dating formula was correct. The signatures of two now-dead court witnesses appeared at the bottom.

The junior clerk accused of having falsified the Carantii confirmation to obscure Richomer’s prior claim was named Wulfo. He was nineteen years old, the son of a minor Burgundian landowner, and had been employed in the chancery for fourteen months. He had been, until four days ago, considered competent. He was now in a locked room in Theudebald’s hall, having been found in possession of—or rather, having had found in his possession—a stylus of the particular bronze alloy used for the wax-tablet drafts of formal documents, with traces of a composition of oak-gall and iron that matched the ink used in the Carantii confirmation.

The night officer of the court, a Burgundian named Hrodmund who had the permanent expression of a man whose dignity was never quite adequately recognised, had declared the matter resolved. Wulfo had tampered with the document at the instruction of Carantius or for his own purposes. The Carantii confirmation was void. The earlier grant took precedence. Richomer’s claim stood.

Hrodmund had made this determination on the afternoon of the previous day and had sent to Theudebald to confirm it. Theudebald, who held the position of court chamberlain and who was, among his other qualities, a man who had worked alongside Holmar for eleven years and had developed in that time a reliable sense of when a conclusion arrived too smoothly, had not confirmed it. Instead he had sent his man south through the October fog.

“He is right to be uncertain,” Holmar said, setting down his flask. “Tell me one thing before we go. Who had custody of the original parchment copy in the interval between its drafting and its submission by Carantius?”

The messenger looked at him with the blankness of a man who had not been asked this question before.

“That,” Holmar said, more to himself than to the messenger, “is what I will need to establish.”

He nodded to Ansgar, who was already folding a clean piece of linen around Holmar’s case of instruments with the efficient competence that Holmar relied upon and occasionally acknowledged.

They arrived at the court hall at the third hour, while the fog was still absolute and the torches in the courtyard burned with their flames bent sideways by the river-wind, each one trailing its own thin column of oiled smoke that rose and was immediately swallowed by the grey. The smell of the lamp-oil—the cheaper variety, fish-based, that the court used for outdoor illumination and which carried a distinct marine undertone—mixed with the fog and the river and the smell of horses and made the courtyard air into something almost solid.

Hrodmund met them at the door with the expression of a man who had expected his conclusions to have already become the foundation of further events, and found instead that the foundation was being examined.

“The matter is resolved,” he said. He was a large man with a careful beard, and he wore his court position the way certain men wore new clothes—with a slight stiffness that suggested he had not yet entirely forgotten the effort required to acquire them.

“The matter is interesting,” Holmar replied. “Which is a different category. I require the original wax tablets, the parchment copy of the Carantii confirmation, the stylus you found on the boy, and the earlier grant submitted by Richomer. And the room where the documents are kept. And light.”

Hrodmund’s expression suggested that the list was longer than he had anticipated.

“I also require,” Holmar added, with a mildness that Ansgar had learned to recognise as the tone he used when he expected significant resistance, “that Carantius be brought from wherever he has been sent, and that Richomer be summoned from wherever he currently is, and that neither of them be informed of what the other is doing. And Wulfo. I will want Wulfo.”

“Wulfo is secured.”

“Unsecure him sufficiently to bring him here. He can be re-secured afterward if necessary.”

The document room was small and cold and smelled of the particular combination of dust, old parchment, and the wax used to seal document-chests that Holmar had always found slightly comforting, in the way that any smell associated with years of satisfactory work becomes comforting. Ansgar lit the lamps—two oil-lamps of the better quality, steady-flamed—and Holmar laid the documents on the reading-desk in the sequence he required.

The wax tablets first.

A wax tablet of the kind used in the Burgundian chancery was a practical instrument with a specific physical logic. The wooden frame held the wax surface. A stylus made impressions. If you smoothed the wax and reimposed it, you could reuse the tablet—but you could not, if you knew what to look for, eliminate the ghost of what had been written before. Wax retained memory the way certain silts retained the outlines of fish that had died in them.

The tablets bearing the draft record of the Carantii confirmation were three in number. Holmar examined them under lamplight at an oblique angle, tilting each one until the surface caught the flame from the side and the shadow-topography of the impressions became visible.

What he saw on the second tablet confirmed what he had already suspected before he entered the room.

“Ansgar,” he said. “Come here.”

Ansgar came. He was a solid young man from a Rhine German family, methodical and observant, and Holmar valued his observations precisely because they represented what an intelligent person without specialised knowledge would see.

“Tell me what you notice.”

Ansgar studied the tablet for a long moment, tilting it as Holmar had done. “There are two sets of impressions,” he said finally. “The upper ones are clear. The lower ones—underneath—are fainter. As though the tablet was smoothed and reused.”

“Good. Now tell me what the lower impressions say.”

Another silence. Ansgar was not a rapid reader of the older script-forms, but he was careful.

“It appears to be a land measurement,” he said. “Numbers. A boundary description.”

“It is a draft of a different document entirely,” Holmar said. “Specifically, it is a draft of Richomer’s grant—the earlier document, the one that predates the Carantii confirmation by three years. This tablet was used to draft Richomer’s grant, then smoothed and reused to draft the Carantii confirmation.”

He let that sit in the air of the small room for a moment.

“Is that significant?” Ansgar asked.

“Administratively, it is merely careless. Chronologically, it is impossible.”

He turned to face Ansgar directly, which he only did when he intended to explain something and wanted to observe whether the explanation had taken root.

“Richomer’s grant is dated three years before the Carantii confirmation. If Richomer’s grant is genuine and predates the Carantii confirmation, then the tablet used to draft it should have been smoothed and reused before the Carantii confirmation was drafted—which would mean the lower impressions, the earlier document, would be the Richomer grant, and the upper impressions, the later document, would be the Carantii confirmation. And this is indeed what we see. The lower impressions are the Richomer grant; the upper impressions are the Carantii confirmation.”

Ansgar nodded. “So the tablet is consistent.”

“The tablet is consistent with the dates as Richomer claims them. Yes.” Holmar set the tablet down. “But tablets are reused constantly; the consistency proves very little by itself. It would be easy to arrange. A scribe creating a false document with a false earlier date would be careful to use an old tablet for his draft. Now look at the parchment.”

The parchment copy of the Carantii confirmation was a good-quality document, calf-skin rather than sheep, which indicated a paying client who wanted permanence. The ink was of the standard court composition—iron gall with a small addition of lamp-black for density—and the hand was the formal chancery script that Holmar had himself helped to standardise across the court’s documents ten years prior.

He examined it first with the naked eye, then brought one of the lamps within three inches of the surface and tilted the parchment until he was reading it in near-raking light.

Ansgar waited.

“There are two texts here,” Holmar said after several minutes.

“I see only one,” Ansgar said carefully.

“You see one because only one was meant to be seen. But parchment, like wax, retains memory. When you scrape a parchment surface to remove text, you thin the skin. When you write new text over the scraped area, the underlying surface is slightly different in texture from the surrounding material—more absorbent in some places, slightly shinier in others, depending on the quality of the scraping. Under raking light, these differences become visible.”

He tilted the parchment further and Ansgar leaned in.

“The area around the date and the third boundary clause,” Holmar continued. “Do you see the slight difference in surface? Here—and here.”

Ansgar was quiet for a moment. Then: “It looks slightly different. I might not have noticed without looking for it.”

“Most people would not notice. That was the intention. The document as it left the chancery was correct. Carantius paid his fee, received his confirmation, and the text assigned him the correct property with the correct boundaries. At some point after it left the chancery, two elements were altered: the date formula—adjusted to appear more recent, so that Richomer’s supposedly earlier grant would seem to predate it—and the boundary clause, which was modified to show a slightly different eastern margin, one that could be argued to be ambiguous against Richomer’s claim.”

“Who altered it?”

Holmar set the parchment down with the care he gave all documents. “That,” he said, “is the question that Hrodmund believes he has already answered. Let us examine the stylus.”

The stylus found in Wulfo’s possession was bronze, of good quality, with the particular degree of wear at the point that indicated regular use. The traces of ink—iron gall, as specified—were visible in the grooves near the working end.

Holmar looked at it for a long time. Then he looked at the parchment again, specifically at the altered areas.

“Ansgar,” he said. “When you alter text on parchment—when you scrape and rewrite—what instrument do you use for the scraping?”

“A knife. Or a pumice stone, for finer work.”

“And for the rewriting?”

“A quill. Or a reed pen if the text is rough-draft only.”

“A stylus is for wax. For impression. Not for ink on parchment.” He held up the bronze instrument. “This is not the instrument that made the alterations on this document. The alterations were made with a quill cut to the court’s standard nib, loaded with iron-gall ink. This stylus may have ink on it because it was used to draft something on a tablet and then dipped carelessly, or because someone placed the ink there deliberately. But it was not the instrument of the falsification.”

He set it down.

“Furthermore,” he said, and now his voice took on the slightly quieter quality that Ansgar had learned meant he was approaching the point of the exercise, “Wulfo had custody of this document for six days, during the initial drafting phase. After Carantius paid and the document was sealed and entered into the court’s register, custody passed out of Wulfo’s hands. The register shows it was collected by Carantius on the day after sealing. But Carantius did not take it immediately home. He lodged it for safekeeping with the court archivist for a further three weeks while he settled a family matter that required his travel. During those three weeks, access to the archive chest required the key held by the duty chamberlain.”

He looked at Ansgar.

Ansgar said, carefully: “Theudebald is the court chamberlain.”

“Theudebald has been chamberlain for six years and is the most meticulous record-keeper I have worked with since I took my own position. But the duty key is not always held by Theudebald himself. It rotates to the officer of the court. And in those three weeks—” he had the archive duty-roster before him now, a document he had requested before leaving the building and which Ansgar had carried under his arm since— “the officer of the court who held the key was Hrodmund.”

The name settled in the room like a stone into water.

“And Richomer is Hrodmund’s wife’s brother,” Holmar said. He did not say it as a revelation. He said it the way he might note that the river was on their left as they walked north—a geographical fact that organised the landscape. “Which I knew before I entered this room. Which I expect Theudebald also knew, and which is why he sent for me rather than confirming Hrodmund’s conclusion.”

He assembled them in the hall in a specific order that was not accidental. Hrodmund he positioned nearest the door—not to block escape, but because a man near a door can more easily imagine departure, and a man who imagines departure reveals his assessment of his own position. Carantius he placed to the left, in the chair. Wulfo—pale, bewildered, still showing the marks of a night in a locked room—he placed standing, in the centre, facing Hrodmund. Richomer he had not summoned. Richomer was a problem for a later stage.

Theudebald sat to one side and said nothing. He had the quality, rare in powerful men, of being able to observe without needing to visibly participate.

“Wulfo,” Holmar said. “You prepared the initial draft of the Carantii confirmation on tablets, transcribed to parchment, and presented it for the seal. Is this accurate?”

Wulfo, who had the dazed quality of a young man who has spent the night discovering that the world is different from what he understood it to be, said that it was.

“After sealing and registration, did you have any further contact with the document?”

“No. I—no. I completed the registration and then Carantius came for it and I recorded the collection and that was—that was all I did.”

“I believe you,” Holmar said.

The quality of silence in the room changed. Hrodmund, near the door, went very still.

“The document was altered after it left Wulfo’s hands,” Holmar continued, and now he addressed Theudebald rather than the room generally, which was a deliberate choice—Theudebald was the authority, and the performance was for the authority’s benefit. “The alteration required access to a locked archive. It required a knife or pumice and a quill and ink matching the court standard. It required enough time to work carefully, because the work was done carefully—I nearly missed the surface-change in the date formula, which is the more important falsification. And it required knowledge of Richomer’s intention to file a competing claim at some future date, so that the alteration would be useful when it was finally deployed. The stylus found on Wulfo is a false planting. The iron-gall on its tip is consistent with his ordinary work but is not consistent with the method used to alter the document, which did not use a stylus at all.”

Hrodmund said: “This is a reading of impressions by a man who—”

“The archive duty-roster,” Holmar said, and Ansgar placed it on the table, “shows that during the three weeks in which the Carantii confirmation was in archive custody awaiting collection, the officer of the court holding the duty key was yourself on fourteen of twenty-one days. Richomer is your wife’s brother. You knew what grant he intended to file. You needed the Carantii document to appear weaker than it was.” He paused. “I think you also placed the stylus on the boy, or arranged for it to be placed. I cannot prove that specific act. I do not need to.”

The hall was quiet except for the river sound—the gate-hinge, very distant, marking the hour in its unreliable way.

Hrodmund looked at Theudebald. Then he looked at the door. Then he appeared to reach a calculation, and the calculation appeared to come out in favour of a different approach.

“I acted in accordance with the interests of Burgundian land-right,” he said, and there was something almost dignified in the attempt. “The Gallo-Roman families hold land by old imperial titles that—”

“You acted in accordance with your family’s interests,” Theudebald said. It was the first time he had spoken. “Which is a different category.”

Holmar walked home in the thinning fog, which by the mid-morning hour had begun to shred along its upper margin into a pale winter sky. Ansgar walked beside him and was, for a time, quiet—a quality Holmar valued in him more than he had ever explicitly said.

“Hrodmund will be stripped of the duty-key position,” Ansgar said, eventually. “What else?”

“What else depends on Theudebald’s judgment. The case against Hrodmund is clear but the case against Richomer is more complicated—he filed a document, and though I believe the document is also falsified, proving that requires access to the original witnesses, two of whom are dead. Richomer will argue that he received the document in good faith. Perhaps he did. It is not impossible that Hrodmund created the entire scheme without his knowledge.”

“Do you think that’s true?”

Holmar considered the fog-remnants over the river. A cormorant was standing on the far bank with its wings extended, which was a habit of the bird’s for drying its feathers, and which Holmar found he watched every time he saw it with a slight admiration for the creature’s total commitment to a practical necessity.

“I think it is probably not true,” he said. “But I also think that the destruction of Richomer—who is a Burgundian lord with three villages and thirty armed dependants—over a land-dispute with a Gallo-Roman family who will keep their land regardless of how the matter resolves, would create a local faction-problem that would concern the court for the next five years. Theudebald knows this.”

“So Richomer goes free.”

“Richomer loses his claim. That is not nothing. The Carantii keep their property. Hrodmund loses his position and, I expect, some land.” He paused. “There is a version of justice that would pursue every thread to its conclusion regardless of the fabric it unravels. And there is a version that preserves the fabric while cutting out the damage it can reach. The court practices the second version, as courts must. I am not the court.”

Ansgar was quiet again for a moment. “Is that what the law is for? Preserving the fabric?”

“The law is for predictability,” Holmar said. “Predictability allows people to plan their lives. Absolute justice, pursued without limit, makes everything unpredictable—because a perfectly just inquiry may destroy ten innocent people in the course of exposing one guilty one, and no one can know in advance who will be destroyed. I am not comfortable with this. But I am a record-keeper, not a philosopher, and I have found that the difference between those two professions lies precisely in whether you can live with unresolved questions.”

He stopped at the gate of his own house and looked back at the river, where the cormorant had closed its wings and plunged.

“What I know,” he said, “is that Wulfo is not in a locked room. Carantius has his land. The document has been corrected and re-registered. And Hrodmund will not hold a duty-key in this court again.” He opened the gate. “Record it correctly, Ansgar. Put down what was done and what was found and who was exonerated. The rest—what was considered and what was set aside—you can leave to the margins, where such things generally live.”

Sigibert’s Final Note: I have copied the above from Holmar’s own account, dictated over two mornings in the winter that followed these events. I add only the following: Holmar continued in the chancery’s service for a further nine years, after which age and a chest ailment retired him to a house near the river that he had purchased with his savings, where he lived four years more, dying, as his family tended to do, in his bed and at a reasonable hour, with his documents in order.

I have annotated where I found error or obscurity. Where I have found neither, I have tried to restrain myself from the archivist’s common temptation to annotate regardless, which is the vice of those who understand the importance of records without possessing the kind of mind that knows what to put in them and—more importantly—what to leave out.

His eyes, even in age, were remarkable. He noticed everything. He chose, very carefully, what to write down.
