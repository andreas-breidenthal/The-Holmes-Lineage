HOLKOS THE YOUNGER

~1300 BC — Hittite Anatolia

THE RECORD OF NIKOMACHOS

Set down in the season of the Pleiades rising, in the year that the great king's grain-ships came late to the coast.

I.

There is a smell particular to the trading post at Arziya that I have never encountered anywhere else along the western roads. It is cedar oil and rotting barley and the particular sweat of men who have walked a long way in leather sandals and then been made to wait. The smell was in my nostrils on the morning Tiwatali came through the gate, and it has lodged itself in my memory alongside everything that followed, so that I cannot think of one without the other.

The trading post squatted at the junction of two dust-roads like a man sitting down in the middle of an argument — stubborn, graceless, and impossible to go around. Its walls were the colour of old bone. The gate-hinge, a great bronze pin in a bronze socket, had been singing the same flat, mournful note since before my employment, and it sang that morning as the factor Pulli-ziti's wife was shown through it into the courtyard.

Her name was Tiwatali.

She crossed the courtyard with the contained efficiency of a woman who has decided that if she stops moving she will have to feel something she cannot afford to feel. She wore her best travelling wrap — I noticed this because clerks notice what does not fit, and a woman in her best wrap visiting a trading post on a working morning has dressed for a petition, not a transaction. Her sandals were dusty from the road into the village, not from the path between her house and the market. She had walked some distance before she came here. Her hands, when she clasped them in the formal manner of address, were steady, which is not the steadiness of a woman at peace. It is the steadiness of a woman who has been practising.

She carried, tucked into the fold of her wrap at the left shoulder — the position where a Luwian woman of her station would carry a document of personal importance — a sealed clay tablet, small enough to be a message. She did not offer it. She did not mention it. She spoke to Arnuwanda-rabi about her husband's absence and accepted his promises of enquiry in the manner of someone who has already received one promise and watched it fail.

Arnuwanda-rabi heard her out with the expression he wore when he was calculating whether a cargo was worth its risk. He thanked her for coming and promised enquiries. When she was gone he called for wine, looked at his hands for a moment in the way a man does when he is constructing something in his mind and wishes to appear to be doing nothing, and told me to find Holkos.

But Holkos had already found her.

I did not know this until I went to his room and found it empty, the gum vessel cold, and a knucklebone set precisely in the centre of his sleeping mat — the signal he used to indicate he had left deliberately and intended to return. I went back to the courtyard. He was standing in the shadow of the granary wall, watching Tiwatali as she passed through the gate. He was not looking at her grief. He was looking, I understood later, at everything else.

When she had gone through the gate and the bronze hinge had made its sound, he turned to me.

"You were present for the interview," he said.

"I was."

"She brought a message." He was still looking at the gate. "Tucked at her left shoulder, sealed. She has had it for some days — the clay has dried completely, which in this season takes three days at minimum. She did not produce it for Arnuwanda-rabi."

"Perhaps it was personal," I said. "A family matter."

"If it were a family matter, she would have left it at home. She brought it here because it is relevant to why she is here, and she did not produce it because she has decided, correctly, that Arnuwanda-rabi cannot be trusted with it." He turned from the gate. "Someone sent her a message after her husband failed to return. Someone told her that the matter was in hand and would be resolved. She waited on that promise for several days before concluding it would not be kept and coming here herself." He began walking toward the main building. "The question is who sent it, and whether they believed it when they sent it, or whether it was sent to buy time."

I said: "You conclude all of that from a tablet tucked in her wrap."

"I conclude that she received a message, that it was relevant to her husband, and that she has decided it is evidence rather than correspondence. The rest follows from the arithmetic of the road — twelve days gone, three days each way, eight days sufficient for return. She did not come to us until today, which means something or someone delayed her. The message is what delayed her." He paused at the door. "Bring me the manifest for the Hattena consignment. And bring me the document seal from Pulli-ziti's road chest."

II.

He was a different man with the manifest in his hands. The languor fell off him like a cloak dropped to the floor. He read it three times — I counted — and on the third reading his finger traced the column of weights and stopped.

"Nikomachos," he said. "You prepared this document."

"I did."

"These ingots were weighed by you personally?"

"Weighed and counted."

"Fourteen ingots at standard Hittite mine-weight. Good. And yet —" he turned the tablet toward me and pointed — "the declared value for customs passage at the garrison gate is entered here as the value of twelve ingots."

I looked. He was correct. I had not noticed it. I said so.

"Because you prepared the weight column," he said, not unkindly, "and someone else prepared the value column. The hands are different — here, look at the stroke of the numeral four. Your fours close at the top. These do not." He set the weight column beside the value column. The difference, once he had named it, was unmistakeable. "Who enters the customs values?"

The answer, and I felt it land on me like cold water, was Arnuwanda-rabi himself.

Holkos set the manifest down and picked up Pulli-ziti's road seal — the small cylinder of fired clay that factors use to authenticate documents before departure. He turned it in his fingers. "The weight column bears this seal. The value column does not. Pulli-ziti authenticated what he could see. He could see fourteen ingots. He could not see what Arnuwanda-rabi would write in the value column after he had gone." He set the seal carefully beside the manifest. "These two objects, together, tell the complete story of what occurred. I want you to remember that I am setting them side by side now, in this room, before we have gone anywhere or spoken to anyone. Everything that follows will explain them. Nothing that follows will be necessary to prove them."

I understood, later, why he said this. At the time I only noted it.

He picked up the seal again. "Tell me what you know of Arnuwanda-rabi's accounts with the garrison posts. Outstanding balances. Deliveries that did not quite match their declarations."

I told him about the previous dry season — a delivery to the Hattena garrison supply officer that had come back short, and correspondence that Arnuwanda-rabi had handled himself rather than through me, which was unusual. I had filed it and thought no more of it.

"How large a shortfall?"

I did not know precisely.

"Two ingots of standard weight," he said. "That would be my estimate, given the arithmetic of what he required to settle it quietly." He paused. "Tell me what is the standard penalty, under Hittite garrison administration, for a merchant factor found to be transporting undeclared goods."

I knew this because such things were my business. Detention, pending investigation. The factor held as surety for the discrepancy, since the factor was the responsible party at the garrison gate.

"Pending investigation," Holkos repeated. "Which might last how long?"

"Until the merchant principal appears to answer for the discrepancy. Or pays the bond. Or —" I stopped.

"Or the merchant principal fails to appear," Holkos said, "in which case the factor continues to wait." He looked at the two objects on the table — the manifest and the seal — for a moment longer. "Pulli-ziti was chosen because he is honest. A dishonest factor might have read the manifest closely enough to understand what he was carrying. An honest man trusts the house." He stood. "Get your sandals. We are going to Hattena."

Before we left, he went to Arnuwanda-rabi.

I was present, seated in my corner of the receiving room with my clay and my stylus. Arnuwanda-rabi received us with the wariness of a man who has something to protect but is not yet certain it is threatened.

Holkos inquired, with perfect courtesy, whether there were any outstanding matters between the house of Arnuwanda-rabi and the garrison administration at Hattena that might bear on Pulli-ziti's journey.

Arnuwanda-rabi said there were not.

But he did not look at Holkos when he said it. He looked at me — a brief, directed glance, the kind a man casts at a wall he expects to hold. A man with nothing to conceal looks at the person who has asked him the question. A man managing a secret looks first at the point where the secret is most exposed.

I was, apparently, that point.

Holkos thanked the merchant for his time.

Outside, he said: "Did you observe where he looked?"

"At me," I said.

"At you. Not at the man asking the question. He is not uncertain about his own story — he has told it to himself many times and it is smooth and complete. He is uncertain about you. You prepared the manifest. You are the element of the situation he does not fully control." He walked for a moment in silence. "He will want to speak to you before we leave. Do not let him do so alone."

He was correct. Arnuwanda-rabi came to my room that evening.

He carried his own lamp and sat without being invited. He spoke in the quiet, reasonable tone that powerful men use when they want a lesser man to understand that their interests are, at this moment, aligned. He said that Pulli-ziti had, over the years, made certain irregularities in his accounts — nothing actionable, but the kind that accumulate into a pattern and become significant in certain circumstances. He said he had always protected Pulli-ziti from consequences out of consideration for the man's family. He said that any inquiry conducted carelessly risked surfacing material embarrassing to everyone involved. He said that a clerk associated with such an inquiry might find himself associated with its complications.

He said all of this pleasantly, looking at the lamp.

Then he wished me a good night and left.

I sat for some time afterward. I will not pretend I felt no fear. He controlled my employment, my references, and the goodwill of every merchant on the western roads who respected his name. He was not making idle conversation.

I told Holkos at the gate the following morning.

Holkos listened without interrupting. When I had finished, he said: "He is frightened. A man confident of his position does not visit his clerk in the evening." He adjusted his pack. "He also confirmed something I required confirmation of."

I asked what.

"He knows that the manifest is the mechanism," Holkos said. "He went to you because you prepared the weight column. He did not go to Pulli-ziti, who is absent, or to his own records, which he controls. He went to the one element of the document that is not in his hand. That tells me he understands exactly where his exposure lies." He looked at me. "It also tells me what lie he will tell, if given the opportunity to tell one. Remember that."

I asked whether I was in danger.

He looked at me carefully. "You are in discomfort," he said. "There is a difference, though I understand it does not feel like one." He paused. "Stay close to me on the road. And bring Pulli-ziti's seal. I want it with us."

III.

The road to Hattena ran through a landscape the gods had assembled in a harsh mood. The hills were the colour of old bronze and offered no shade, and the dust of the road coated the inside of the mouth so that every breath tasted of the road itself. We travelled with a small merchant party going east, which gave us the cover of numbers.

We were a day out from Arziya, in the dry country where the hills pressed close to the road and left it no room to breathe, when Holkos said, quietly and without breaking stride: "The two men on the ridge to our right have been matching our pace since the last waypost. They are not merchants."

I looked. He was correct.

"Arnuwanda-rabi," I said.

"He sent word ahead last night by private route. He has contacts on this road — every licensed merchant of his standing does. He does not know specifically what we are doing, but he knows we are going to Hattena, and he knows that is not good for him." He walked for a moment. "The question is whether he sent word to have us delayed, or to have us stopped entirely."

The difference between those two possibilities occupied me uncomfortably for the next quarter-hour.

When we rounded a bend and found three men across the path, I had my answer.

They had chosen their ground: a narrowing between two rock faces where the merchant party was compressed and evasion eliminated. The leader was a broad man with the particular stillness of someone accustomed to violence, carrying a copper-headed staff in his right hand with the ease of long practice.

The merchant party stopped. Their muleteer began backing his animals. Holkos did not stop. He kept walking — not toward the three men but at an angle, toward the rock face on the left — and as he walked he pulled his travelling cloak from his shoulders and folded it over his left forearm. He looked, I realised, nothing like himself. Something in the set of his back had changed: a slight rounding of the shoulders, a different weight in the step, a heaviness that was not his.

He spoke to the leader in Luwian — but not in his western Luwian, not the dialect he had worn for years until it sat in his mouth without effort. He spoke in something more guttural, from further inland, with the rhythm of the upland traders who came through Arziya in the spring.

He said — and I followed this imperfectly, and reconstructed the rest from what he explained to me later — that he was a hired surveyor of the road, employed by the provincial administrator of Sarduwanda, and that the administrator was expecting his report on the condition and security of this corridor by the end of the month, and that the presence of men on the ridge had already been noted in his tablet and would appear in the report unless there was a reason for him to describe them otherwise.

He said the administrator's name. It was, I discovered later, genuinely the name of the man responsible for this section of road — a man whose interest in the activities of private parties along his corridor would be unwelcome to anyone conducting the kind of business these three men appeared to be conducting.

The leader looked at him for a long time. I was standing motionless against the rock face, aware of my own heartbeat.

Then the leader looked at the ridge, where his two companions were visible. Then back at Holkos. Then at me — I was, apparently, convincingly a surveyor's clerk.

He stepped aside.

Holkos walked through without hurrying. I followed. When we had rounded the next bend, he unfolded the cloak from his arm and resettled it.

"The administrator's name," I said. "Was that a gamble?"

"A calculated one. Men working for Arnuwanda-rabi on private hire are not men who want official scrutiny. The administrator's name is the name they most want not to hear." He paused. "It required that they be hired men, not Arnuwanda-rabi's own people. His own people would know whether I was lying. Hired men cannot be certain."

"And if they had been his own people?"

"Then we would have had a different kind of conversation." He looked at me. "You said nothing and stood still. That was correct. I was relying on it."

I did not find this as reassuring as I believe he intended it.

IV.

The garrison at Hattena was a place of administrative severity. Its walls were new and its commander, Piyamaradu-abi, was a Hittite of the imperial school who wore his authority like armour and kept his records with a precision I recognised as a fellow sufferer of the scribal profession. He received us in a room smelling of fresh clay and lamp-oil and the cedar resin used in imperial document-chests.

Holkos presented himself as a factor of the same trading house on a routine verification of delivery records and asked, with the bored efficiency of a man who has done this a hundred times, to confirm the receipt of fourteen copper ingots, standard weight, delivered some twelve days past.

Piyamaradu-abi's scribe consulted the tablets. Twelve ingots received and documented. A discrepancy of two had been noted. The factor present at delivery had been held pending clarification from the merchant principal, whose bond remained unpaid.

"The factor's name?" Holkos asked.

The scribe read it. Not Pulli-ziti. A Luwian name I did not recognise.

Holkos did not blink. "And where is this factor currently?"

The holding room, the scribe said. Where he had been for twelve days.

Outside Piyamaradu-abi's room, waiting for the paperwork, Holkos explained it quietly. "A transcription error at the gate — the garrison clerk wrote a Luwian name by ear, and Pulli-ziti's name sounds enough like the name in the record to an ear not attentive to Luwian phonetics. Probably accidental. But it made him unfindable. I asked to see the physical record of all detainees held during the relevant period rather than the name index. The date of arrival matched."

"How did you know to do that?"

"Because a man searching for 'Pulli-ziti' in garrison records and finding nothing would assume he had never arrived. I assumed he had arrived and was simply misfiled. The distinction matters." He paused. "Arnuwanda-rabi has a debt at Hattena — from that previous shortfall you remembered. Rather than settle it, he used this shipment: declared only twelve ingots, so the garrison took the two undeclared ingots against his debt as a silent settlement, while the factor at the gate became responsible for the discrepancy he knew nothing of. Pulli-ziti was the lock on the transaction. The merchant is insulated, the debt is cleared, and the record is a tangle no one wishes to unpick."

"It is monstrous," I said.

"It is arithmetically elegant," Holkos said, and I think he meant it as a condemnation, though his voice gave nothing away. "The most dangerous schemes are the ones that use an honest man's honesty against him. They are difficult to see because the honest man's behaviour looks like ordinary behaviour right up until the moment it has served its purpose."

V.

The holding room was dry and low, lit by a single lamp smelling of old fish. Pulli-ziti was seated on a mat in the corner. He looked, as men look who have been confined without explanation for twelve days, simultaneously older and somehow reduced, as though the waiting had taken something from him that would require time to grow back.

He recognised me. I will not describe what passed across his face when he understood that someone had come for him, except to say that I recorded it in my memory and have not forgotten it.

Holkos managed the paperwork with the precision of a man who has thought through every step in advance — documentation establishing the discrepancy as a clerical error in the value column, the full declared weight fourteen ingots, the bond paid from his own funds. Piyamaradu-abi was satisfied. The record would show a clerical error resolved in good order. It would not yet show Arnuwanda-rabi's name.

On the road home, when I told Pulli-ziti about Arnuwanda-rabi's visit to my room, he was quiet for a long time. Then he said: "He came to the clerk's room. At night. While I was sitting in a garrison holding cell."

"Yes," I said.

"That is not a man who made a mistake and hoped it would not be found," Pulli-ziti said. "That is a man who has done this kind of thing before and knows exactly how to protect himself while he does it."

Holkos, across the fire, said nothing. The fire made the sound fires make in dry country — a light, papery crackling, as though consuming something that has already given up.

"What do you want from this?" Holkos asked, eventually.

Pulli-ziti thought. "My wages. My name back. And I want him to answer for it — not to me, but to the men he trades with. The men whose opinion matters to him."

"Yes," Holkos said. "That is what we are going to arrange." He paused. "There is something I must explain to you about the manifest. I need you to understand it precisely, because I am going to ask you to be present when I use it."

He explained about the seal. About the two columns and the two hands and what their difference proved. I watched Pulli-ziti follow it — the gradual, dawning recognition of a careful man understanding exactly how he had been used, and what, specifically, the use had depended on.

When Holkos had finished, Pulli-ziti said: "He will say it was my error. Or Nikomachos's. He will say the seal proves nothing, that we are both his employees and our word is worth nothing against his."

"He will say exactly that," Holkos agreed. "He will say it in front of witnesses. And the seal will say otherwise."

VI.

We were back in Arziya by the evening of the fourth day. Tiwatali was at the gate — not waiting, precisely; she was speaking to a donkey driver about load weights, which appeared to be how she organised her time when she needed to be somewhere without admitting she was waiting. She saw us before we saw her, and crossed the courtyard in the flat, rapid way of someone who has been making herself busy so as not to stand still.

I watched her take her husband's face in her hands and look at him as you look at something you have been considering how to live without.

Holkos did not watch. He had already turned toward the trading house. "Tomorrow morning," he said to me. "I will need Pulli-ziti, Tiwatali, and the two eastern partners. Send word to the partners tonight — tell them Arnuwanda-rabi requests their presence on a matter of the spring consignments. Use the house messenger. Do not use his seal."

I said: "And Arnuwanda-rabi?"

"Tell him only that there is a question regarding the Hattena delivery and that the partners have been asked to attend as witnesses to an account reconciliation. A routine matter. Give him no further detail."

"He will prepare a position."

"Yes," Holkos said. "He will. I am counting on it."

That evening I went to Tiwatali. I told her plainly what had been done to her husband, who had done it, and what I believed Holkos intended. I told her also what Arnuwanda-rabi had said to me in my room. She listened without expression until I had finished.

Then she said: "Tell me what time."

VII.

The receiving room the following morning held: Arnuwanda-rabi, his two eastern partners — merchants of standing with their own relationships to the garrison administrators along the road — Pulli-ziti, Tiwatali, and myself. Holkos entered last and closed the door.

On the table he placed three objects: the manifest, the garrison receipt showing twelve ingots received, and Pulli-ziti's document seal. He placed them in a row with the careful neutrality of a man setting out items for inspection rather than accusation, and he sat down.

Arnuwanda-rabi looked at the objects. He looked at his partners. He looked at Pulli-ziti. He did not look at me, which I noted.

"A question has arisen," Holkos said pleasantly, "about the Hattena consignment. The garrison received twelve ingots. The manifest records fourteen weighed and counted. There is a discrepancy of two in the value column. I would like to understand how it arose."

He said nothing more. He waited.

Arnuwanda-rabi had prepared his position, as Holkos had known he would. He spoke with the smooth confidence of a man presenting a settled matter. He said that the value column discrepancy was a clerical error — regrettable, now resolved — and that the clerk responsible for preparing the document had confused the customs rate with the unit count. He gestured, briefly, toward me.

"The weight column and the value column," he said, "were both prepared by Nikomachos."

In the silence that followed, I heard the gate-hinge make its flat sound in the courtyard.

Holkos picked up the seal. He placed it against the weight column of the manifest, where Pulli-ziti's impression appeared beside each entry. Then he placed it beside the value column, where no impression appeared at all.

"The weight column," he said, "bears Pulli-ziti's seal. The value column does not. Pulli-ziti authenticated what he could see and count before departure. He sealed fourteen ingots at standard weight because he weighed them himself. The value column was completed after he left, in a different hand. You will observe that the stroke of the numeral four differs between the columns — here, and here." He set the tablet between the two partners. "Nikomachos did not prepare the value column. His fours close at the top. These do not."

The older partner — a Luwian from the coastal cities, precise in his accounts — leaned forward and looked. He looked for some time.

Arnuwanda-rabi said: "The seal proves only that Pulli-ziti authenticated the weight. It does not prove —"

"It proves," Holkos said, "that the weight and value columns were prepared by different hands. It proves that the factor responsible for the consignment sealed what he could verify and could not have sealed what was entered after his departure. It proves that you prepared the value column, because you are the man in this house who enters customs values, as your clerk will confirm and as your own records will show." He looked at Arnuwanda-rabi without hostility, in the manner of a man completing an arithmetic demonstration. "The garrison detained Pulli-ziti for twelve days on the basis of a discrepancy that was engineered by the same hand that enters customs values in this house. There is a previous shortfall between this house and the Hattena supply officer — two ingots of standard weight, outstanding since the dry season — which the two undeclared ingots in this consignment precisely settled."

The younger partner set down his cup. The older partner was still looking at the two columns.

Arnuwanda-rabi's jaw was steady. He was a man of genuine composure and I gave him that, even then. "These are assertions," he said. "The garrison is satisfied. The bond is paid. There is no formal complaint."

"There is no formal complaint," Holkos agreed. "There does not need to be." He paused. "The seal and the manifest will be held in safekeeping. The caravan master on the eastern road knows the story of the Hattena debt — I confirmed it from him on the journey, and he is a man who talks. Piyamaradu-abi is a precise administrator who will, in time, notice that the supply officer's outstanding account was settled by exactly two ingots at the moment a factor from this house was held on a discrepancy of exactly two ingots. He is the kind of man who notices such symmetries."

He let that sit.

"What I am offering," Holkos continued, "is the opportunity to resolve the matter privately, among people who are already in this room. Pulli-ziti's wages for the period of his detention, paid in full. His name restored in the house records without annotation. The bond amount returned to me by the end of the month." He gathered the manifest and the seal and held them. "These remain with me. What you do with the knowledge that they exist is a matter for your own judgement."

Arnuwanda-rabi looked at his partners.

The older partner looked back. In his expression was something I recognised from years of watching merchants at the moment they calculate a final price — not sympathy, not judgement, but the clean arithmetic of a man determining what something costs and whether he is willing to pay it. What he was calculating, I understood, was how much of his own reputation sat alongside Arnuwanda-rabi's, and whether it was worth defending the connection.

The calculation did not take long.

"Resolve it," the older partner said, to Arnuwanda-rabi. Quietly. Not as a suggestion.

In the silence that followed, Tiwatali had not moved or spoken. She had stood beside Pulli-ziti since we entered, with the quality of stillness that is not peace but something harder — the stillness of a woman who has already paid the cost of this moment in full and is waiting only for the ledger to reflect it. She met no one's gaze in particular. She simply remained present in the room, with her husband alive beside her and twelve days of his absence carried in her face, and that was enough. That was, in the end, the weight that settled it.

Arnuwanda-rabi looked at her last. And looked away.

"The bond will be paid," he said.

VIII.

Outside in the courtyard, the gate-hinge sang its flat mournful note as the morning's first caravan moved through. Tiwatali stood in the light for a moment. Then she looked at Holkos.

"He will find another way," she said. "Men like him always do."

"Yes," Holkos said. "But the men in that room will remember what they saw this morning. He will trade with them for the rest of his life knowing that they remember it. That is not nothing."

She considered this. "No," she said. "It is not nothing." She looked at him for a moment more, with the measuring quality that seemed to be how she looked at everything — determining its real weight against its declared weight. "Thank you," she said.

Holkos inclined his head. She turned and went back to her husband.

IX.

In the days that followed, Arnuwanda-rabi paid the bond before the month was out and Pulli-ziti's wages in full. He became scrupulous about manifest tallies in a way he had not been before. He developed, as men sometimes develop under the right kind of pressure, a careful interest in his own moral reputation.

Pulli-ziti chose to remain in employment at the trading house, because the work was known and the road was familiar and he had a family to keep. The atmosphere between merchant and factor was the atmosphere of two men who understand each other completely and trust each other not at all, which is a stable enough arrangement for commerce.

Holkos went to his room after the meeting and let the gum go out and sat by the window for the rest of the morning with a clay tablet of riddles from the eastern cities — the activity he retreated to when the world had given him nothing sufficiently interesting to think about. When I brought him the midday bread he looked up and said: "The seal was in the room from the first morning. I asked you to bring it before we had gone anywhere or spoken to anyone. Everything the trap required was in the evidence. Nothing needed to be added."

I said: "You knew what he would say. That it was my error, or Nikomachos's."

"I knew what he would say because he told me what he would say," Holkos said, "when he came to your room. He came to you because you prepared the weight column. The defence he intended was the one his visit implied: that the whole document was yours, that you were responsible for both columns, that his own hand was never on it. He walked into a room this morning and said exactly that." He set the tablet of riddles down. "The seal made it false. He knew the seal existed. He forgot that I had asked you to bring it with us."

He was quiet for a moment.

"The man was chosen for his honesty," he said. "That is the part of it that will stay with me."

X.

He had come east on the trade routes as a young man, drawn by an appetite that the west had not been able to satisfy — a hunger for the place where different worlds pressed against each other and produced, in the friction, complexity that neither empire had anticipated. He had learned Luwian until it sat in his mouth without effort, and Hittite well enough to argue prices with imperial scribes, which is a high standard. He had built, over years, a position that was both commercially productive and endlessly interesting to a man of his gifts. Every transaction was a negotiation between different ideas about value and obligation. Every negotiation was a chance to read the men across the table more precisely than they were reading him.

I understood, by the end of that season, that this was not ambition as most men practise it. It was something closer to appetite — a hunger for complication, and for the particular satisfaction of finding the point where a complicated thing resolves.

He had told me once that he had stayed east because home had not offered him sufficient complication. I had taken it for a joke.

I no longer took it for a joke.

XI.

It is not a story about a great battle or a king's justice or the will of the gods. The gods, as far as I could observe, took no interest in it. It is a story about a tally, and a name wrongly written, and a man who could read the difference between what was declared and what was true.

It is also a story about what happens to an honest man when the world decides that his honesty is a convenient mechanism. The world does this more often than it admits. It does it quietly, in the value columns of manifests, in the silence of receiving rooms, in the slow processing of complaints by men who depend on the same network as the man being complained about.

And it is a story about a seal — a small cylinder of fired clay that a factor pressed into a document before departure because he was an honest man who authenticated only what he could see and count. He did not know, when he pressed it, that he was leaving evidence. He did not know, when he was sitting in a garrison holding cell, that the evidence was sitting in his own road chest waiting to be carried to Hattena and back. He did not know, when he finally came home, that the thing that freed him was the mark of his own hand.

That seems to me worth recording.

—Nikomachos, clerk Arziya trading post, western roads In the year the grain-ships came late
