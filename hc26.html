HOLKOS THE YOUNGER

~1300 BC — Hittite Anatolia

The trade routes between Mycenaean Greece and the Hittite empire ran east across the Aegean, through the cities of the western Anatolian coast, and deep into the Hittite heartland — a commercial artery that the two great powers of the age maintained with the pragmatic recognition that the goods each wanted were in the possession of the other. Holkos the Younger followed those routes east in early adulthood, drawn by the same appetite for the frontier of the known world that had taken Milqart-yaton to Minoan Crete two centuries before.

He settled in western Anatolia, in the zone where Mycenaean and Hittite cultural influence overlapped and the sharp edges of both empires were softened by the necessities of trade. He learned Luwian, the language of the western Anatolian populations, and enough Hittite to function in the imperial administrative context, and built a position as a factor between the two worlds that was both commercially productive and, for a man of his particular gifts, endlessly interesting. Every transaction was a negotiation between different assumptions about value, obligation, and trust; every negotiation was an opportunity to read the men on the other side of the table more precisely than they were reading him.

The account that follows was preserved by Nikomachos, a clerk attached to the Hittite-licensed trading house. He was not a philosopher nor trained in the subtleties of inquiry, but he possessed a careful hand and a habit of recording what he saw in the margins of shipment tallies and correspondence tablets. It is through his record that the sequence of events has come down, though his own understanding of what he witnessed shaped what he chose to note and what he left unsaid.

The Record Of Nikomachos

Set down in the season of the Pleiades rising, in the year that the great king’s grain-ships came late to the coast.

There is a smell particular to the trading post at Arziya that I have never encountered anywhere else along the western roads. It is cedar oil and rotting barley and the particular sweat of men who have walked a long way in leather sandals and then been made to wait. The smell was in my nostrils on the morning Arnuwanda-rabi summoned me, and it has lodged itself in my memory alongside everything that followed, so that I cannot think of one without the other.

The trading post squatted at the junction of two dust-roads like a man sitting down in the middle of an argument — stubborn, graceless, and impossible to go around. Its walls were the colour of old bone. The gate-hinge, a great bronze pin in a bronze socket, had been singing the same flat, mournful note since before my employment, and it sang that morning as the factor Pulli-ziti’s wife was shown through it into the courtyard.

Her name was Tiwatali. She was a small woman made of hard decisions, with the kind of stillness about her that speaks not of peace but of someone accustomed to waiting for disaster. She told Arnuwanda-rabi that her husband had left twelve days past with the consignment of copper ingots for the garrison post at Hattena, and had not returned. Hattena was three days’ journey. He should have been back in eight.

Arnuwanda-rabi heard her out with the expression he wore when he was calculating whether a cargo was worth its risk — a slight compression of the lips, a lowering of the brows. He thanked her for coming and promised enquiries. When she was gone he called for more wine and told me to find Holkos.

I had known Holkos the Younger for four years by that morning, long enough to have ceased being surprised by him and not long enough to have ceased being unsettled. He was perhaps thirty years of age, or perhaps older — his face was one of those faces that the Fates seem to have assembled from the oldest features in their workshop, all blade and precision, so that he had always looked forty and would likely look forty when he was sixty. He was Achaean by birth and tongue, though he spoke Luwian without an accent and Hittite well enough to argue prices with imperial scribes, which is a high bar. He had come east on the trade roads as a young man, as men of appetite sometimes do, and had stayed because the west could not offer him sufficient complication.

His room at the back of the trading house was a place of organised disorder. He kept cedar tablets stacked in sequences only he understood. He kept samples of pigment from six different workshops arranged by colour on a cedar shelf. He kept a collection of knucklebones — not for gambling but for some private system of calculation I never penetrated — and he kept, on the windowsill, a small clay vessel from which came, at intervals, a thin curl of smoke, for he burned a particular resinous gum when his mind was restless, claiming that the smell cleared the passages through which thought must travel. When his mind was not restless — when a problem had presented itself that he judged worthy of his attention — he needed no gum. He needed nothing at all except the problem.

He was seated on his sleeping mat in the posture of absolute stillness that I had learned to associate with active thinking rather than idleness when I knocked. The gum was burning. Not a good sign, I thought, for the urgency of the matter.

I told him about Pulli-ziti.

He did not move for a moment. Then he said, without opening his eyes, “What was in the consignment?”

I told him: copper ingots from the coastal smelters, bound for the garrison at Hattena, fourteen ingots of standard weight, documented in the manifest I had myself prepared.

“And Arnuwanda-rabi sent Pulli-ziti alone?”

“With two pack-bearers. Neither has returned.”

“The pack-bearers’ names.”

I gave them. He opened his eyes.

“Show me the manifest.”

He was a different man with the manifest in his hands. The languor fell off him like a cloak dropped to the floor. He read it three times, which I knew because I watched him, and on the third reading his finger traced the column of weights and stopped.

“Nikomachos,” he said. “You prepared this document.”

“I did.”

“These ingots were weighed by you personally?”

“Weighed and counted.”

“Fourteen ingots at standard Hittite mine-weight. Good. And yet —” he turned the tablet toward me and pointed — “the declared value for customs passage at the garrison gate is entered here as the value of twelve ingots.”

I looked. He was correct. I had not noticed it. I said so.

“Because you prepared the weight column,” he said, not unkindly, “and someone else prepared the value column. Who enters the customs values?”

The answer, and I felt it land on me like cold water, was Arnuwanda-rabi himself.

Holkos set the tablet down on his collection of knucklebones with the precise care of a man placing a valuable object somewhere he will not lose it. “Tell me,” he said, “what is the standard penalty, under Hittite garrison administration, for a merchant factor found to be transporting undeclared goods — that is, goods present in a shipment but absent from the customs declaration?”

I knew the answer because I was a clerk and such things were my business. Detention, pending investigation. The factor himself held as surety for the discrepancy, since the factor was the responsible party at the garrison gate.

“Pending investigation,” Holkos repeated. “Which might last how long?”

“Until the merchant principal appears to answer for the discrepancy. Or pays the bond. Or —” I stopped.

“Or the merchant principal fails to appear,” Holkos said, “in which case the factor continues to wait. Yes. Get your sandals. We are going to Hattena.”

Before we left, he did one other thing. He went to Arnuwanda-rabi.

I was present for this interview, seated in my usual place at the corner of the merchant’s receiving room with my clay and my stylus, as though taking notes, which I was. Arnuwanda-rabi received us with the wariness of a man who has something to protect but is not yet certain it is threatened.

Holkos inquired, with perfect courtesy, whether there were any outstanding matters between the house of Arnuwanda-rabi and the garrison administration at Hattena that might bear on Pulli-ziti’s journey.

Arnuwanda-rabi said there were not.

Holkos studied him for a moment with those grey eyes that always reminded me of water in which something was moving underneath the surface. He thanked the merchant for his time.

Outside, he said to me: “He sweated on the left side of his neck only. A man who is warm sweats evenly. The left-side pattern appears when the body is braced for a blow from a specific direction and cannot decide whether to flee or receive it.”

“You learn a great deal from sweat,” I said.

“Sweat and manifest tallies,” he agreed. “The body cannot make false entries.”

The road to Hattena ran through a landscape that the gods had assembled in a harsh mood. The hills were the colour of old bronze and offered no shade, and the dust of the road coated the inside of the mouth and the back of the throat so that every breath tasted of the road itself. We traveled with a small merchant party going east, which gave us the cover of numbers and the misery of their company, for one of them sang.

Holkos was silent for most of the journey. This was not unusual. He had the capacity to be present without being reachable — to sit at a campfire and watch the flames while his mind was entirely elsewhere. On the second evening he asked me to describe Pulli-ziti from my own observation.

I described him: a careful, quiet man of middle years, more fluent in Luwian than in the administrative Hittite the garrison would use, reliable in his accounts, personally honest in the way that men are honest when they have decided that honesty is more economical than its alternative.

“Personally honest,” Holkos said. “And yet he did not notice the discrepancy in his own manifest.”

“He trusted the house.”

“Yes. That is a kind of honesty with a particular vulnerability.” He was quiet for a time. “What does Arnuwanda-rabi owe at Hattena?”

I did not know. He nodded as though my not knowing confirmed something.

The garrison at Hattena was a place of administrative severity. Its walls were new and its commander was a man named Piyamaradu-abi, a Hittite of the imperial school who wore his authority like armour and kept his records with a precision I recognized as a fellow sufferer of the scribal profession. He received us in a room that smelled of fresh clay and lamp-oil and the particular cedar resin used in imperial document-chests.

Holkos presented himself as a factor of the same trading house on a routine verification of delivery records. He asked, with the bored efficiency of a man who has done this a hundred times, to confirm the receipt of fourteen copper ingots, standard weight, delivered some twelve days past.

Piyamaradu-abi’s scribe consulted the tablets. Twelve ingots received and documented. A discrepancy of two had been noted in the declaration. The factor present at delivery had been held pending clarification from the merchant principal, whose bond against the discrepancy remained unpaid.

“The factor’s name?” Holkos asked.

The scribe read it. Not Pulli-ziti. A Luwian name I did not recognize.

Holkos did not blink. “And where is this factor currently?”

The holding room, the scribe said, where he had been for twelve days.

They had changed his name in the records. This was the thing Holkos explained to me quietly, outside Piyamaradu-abi’s room, while we waited for the necessary paperwork to be located. “A deliberate error in transcription at the gate,” he said. “The garrison clerk who processed him wrote a Luwian name by ear — Pulli-ziti sounds enough like the name in the record, to an ear not attentive to Luwian phonetics. It may have been accidental. Probably it was. The important thing is that it made him unfindable. A man searching the garrison records for ‘Pulli-ziti’ would find nothing.”

“How did you know to look past the name?”

“I didn’t know. I asked to see the physical record of all detainees held during the relevant period, not the index of names. The date of arrival matched.” He paused. “Arnuwanda-rabi has a debt at Hattena — I confirmed it last night from the caravan master, who knows the gossip of every post on the road. A commercial debt to the garrison supply officer, probably from a shortfall in a previous delivery. Rather than pay it, he used this shipment: declared only twelve ingots for customs purposes, so that the garrison took the two undeclared ingots against his debt as a silent settlement, while the factor standing at the gate became responsible for the discrepancy he knew nothing of. Pulli-ziti was the lock on the transaction. As long as he remained here, Arnuwanda-rabi was insulated — the factor takes the consequence, the merchant is uninvolved, and by the time anyone enquires, the debt is settled and the record is a tangle no one wishes to unpick.”

“It is monstrous,” I said.

“It is arithmetically elegant,” Holkos said, and I think he meant it as a condemnation, though his voice gave nothing away. “Pulli-ziti was chosen because he is honest. A dishonest factor might have inspected the manifest closely enough to understand what he was carrying.”

The holding room was a dry, low chamber cut into the garrison’s inner wall, lit by a single oil lamp whose wick needed trimming and smelled of old fish. Pulli-ziti was seated on a mat in the corner. He looked, as men look who have been confined without explanation for twelve days, simultaneously older and somehow reduced, as though the waiting had taken something from him that would require time to grow back.

He recognized me. I will not describe what passed across his face when he understood that someone had come for him, except to say that I recorded it in my memory and will not forget it.

Holkos managed the paperwork with Piyamaradu-abi with a precision that was itself a kind of performance. He produced documentation from the trading house — documents he had had me prepare before we left, on the authority of his own position as factor, which was technically sufficient — establishing that the discrepancy arose from a clerical error in the value column, that the full declared weight was fourteen ingots, and that the bond was paid. The bond amount he paid from his own funds, noting in a private aside to me that he would recover it through means he did not specify.

Piyamaradu-abi was satisfied. The administration had its closure. The official record would show a clerical error resolved in good order, a detainee released on proper authority. It would not show Arnuwanda-rabi’s name in any compromising connection.

As we left, the commander walked with us to the gate. He remarked that the matter seemed straightforward, in hindsight.

“These things often do,” Holkos said, “when one finds the thread.”

The gate-hinge at Hattena made no sound at all, which I noted, because I was already noting things by then.

On the road home, Pulli-ziti walked between us and said little for the first day. On the second he began to speak, quietly at first and then with more force, about what he wished to do in the matter of Arnuwanda-rabi. He used words that I will not record because they do not reflect well on anyone, and the sentiment behind them was understandable enough not to require the words.

Holkos listened to all of it without comment. On the second evening he said: “What do you want from this?”

Pulli-ziti thought. “My wages. My reputation. My name back in the records.”

“Those are recoverable,” Holkos said. “And Arnuwanda-rabi?”

“I want him to answer for it.”

Holkos was quiet for a while. The fire we were sitting beside made the particular sound that fires make in dry country — a light, papery crackling, as though it is eating something that has already given up. “I am going to tell you,” he said at last, “something that you may not wish to hear. Arnuwanda-rabi is known to the garrison supply officer at Hattena, who is known to the provincial administrator, who is known to three other administrators along this road. The commercial network in which he operates is older than either of us and will outlast both of us. If you bring a formal accusation, the accusation will be processed by men who depend on the network functioning. It will be processed slowly. You will be a Luwian factor with no patron and a record that shows a customs discrepancy, however resolved. He will be a licensed merchant with imperial documentation and twenty years of relationships.”

Pulli-ziti stared at the fire.

“What I can do,” Holkos continued, “is ensure that the right people know what he did. Not as an accusation. As information held by men who will use it when it becomes useful to them. Arnuwanda-rabi will know they know. He will spend his remaining years of trade in the knowledge that the ground is not solid. That is a different kind of justice from the one you want. It is the only kind available.”

I watched Pulli-ziti make his decision. I recorded it later in the margin of a shipping tally, because I had no other paper, and that is where I found it when I wrote this account.

He said: “Tell them.”

Tiwatali was at the gate of Arziya when we came back. Not waiting, precisely — she was speaking to a donkey driver about load weights, which was the kind of thing she apparently did with her time — but she saw us before we saw her, and she crossed the courtyard in the flat, rapid way of someone who has been making herself busy so as not to stand still.

I watched her take her husband’s face in her hands and look at him as you look at something you have been considering how to live without.

Holkos did not watch. He had already turned toward the trading house.

He did not, in the days that followed, explain to me in specific terms what he had communicated to whom, or how. Arnuwanda-rabi continued to operate his trading house. He did not dismiss Pulli-ziti, who — this surprised me — chose to remain in employment there, because the work was known and the road was familiar and he had a family to keep. The atmosphere between merchant and factor was, by my observation, the atmosphere of two men who understood each other completely and trusted each other not at all, which is a stable enough arrangement for commerce.

What changed was harder to describe. Arnuwanda-rabi began to settle his accounts promptly. He became scrupulous about manifest tallies. He developed, as men sometimes develop in middle age, a careful interest in his own moral reputation.

I do not know what Holkos said to him when they met privately, which they did once, and briefly, in the receiving room that smelled of cedar oil, and which I was not invited to attend. I know only that Holkos came out wearing the expression I had learned to associate with a calculation that has resolved correctly — not pleasure, exactly, but the quiet satisfaction of a mechanism that has performed its function.

He went to his room and let the gum go out and sat by the window for the rest of the afternoon with a clay tablet of riddles from the eastern cities, which was the activity he retreated to when the world had given him nothing sufficiently interesting to think about.

I recorded what I had seen, in the margins of my tablets, in the small careful script I use when I am writing for later rather than for now.

It is not a story about a great battle or a king’s justice or the will of the gods. The gods, as far as I could observe, took no interest in it. It is a story about a tally, and a name wrongly written, and a man who could read the difference between what was declared and what was true.

That seems to me worth recording.

—Nikomachos, clerk
Arziya trading post, western roads
In the year the grain-ships came late
