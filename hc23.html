HOLKOS OF CORINTH

~1390 BC — Corinth, Greece

THE MATTER OF THE DEATH OF ARKELAOS THE RHODIAN

Set down by Pythodoros, assistant to the scribe Menekles, in the administrative record of the Corinthian crossing, in the reign of the wanax, in the season of the early harvest. I have appended my own observations where the record of Menekles leaves gaps, though I confess that my hand reaches more readily toward meaning than my master's did, and I have tried, where I noticed this fault, to pull it back.

The body was already drawing flies when we reached the lean-to, and Holkos of Corinth — manager of the crossing, the man whose system of records had made the isthmus legible to the palace administration for eleven years — crouched beside it in a single fluid motion, as if the decision had been made several steps before he arrived.

I followed at a distance that I told myself was professional.

The lean-to faced east, and the morning light came in flat and hard off the Saronic, carrying the salt-edge that always distinguishes it from the softer, pine-dark smell of the Corinthian Gulf to the west. Between these two olfactory worlds lies the strip of land across which everything must be carried — the isthmus, the crossing, the narrow neck that Holkos managed with a precision that the palace administration found both indispensable and faintly irritating. He was not tall. He was lean in the way men who forget to eat during a working problem become lean — not discipline, but simple inattention to appetite. His hair was very black, very straight, bound for practical reasons he would explain at considerable length if asked. His eyes were that particular grey-brown that catches light at unexpected angles, and this morning they were reading the ground around the body with the focused attention of a man who does not yet know what he is reading but is confident he will.

He was studying the dead man. The dead man was Arkelaos of Lindos, a Rhodian copper merchant, and he had been found at first light by a water-carrier who had taken him initially for a sleeping man, until he observed that sleeping men do not have the back of the skull laid open in the manner that Arkelaos's skull had been laid open.

Menekles, the official scribe of the crossing, had come to Holkos's door before dawn. I know this because I was already at the log-house when they arrived together, and Menekles had the look of a man who has dressed in haste and thought in worse haste still — his face grey, his usual composed procedure entirely absent. This was not a bureaucrat managing an irregularity. His name was on every consolidated record in that log-house. His authority guaranteed the integrity of every tablet in it. Whatever had happened in the storage lean-to had happened on ground that was, in every legal and administrative sense, his. He had not sent a messenger for Holkos. He had gone himself, at that hour, in that state. I noted this. It seemed important, in the way that things noted at the wrong moment often prove to be.

By the time we stood at the edge of the lean-to, Menekles had assembled his theory, as was his habit. He stated it with the confidence of a man who has found a solution that resembles a solution he has applied before.

"The cargo-workers. The Rhodian argued the weight of his consignment yesterday evening. There were words."

"There are always words," Holkos said, without looking at him. He was looking at the blood. It had dried in an uneven spread, faster at the edges. "The blood dried here. He was not moved after death." He turned his head and examined the earth floor carefully, then the open face of the lean-to, then the weighing-house some thirty yards distant, whose door stood open on its leather hinges. Then he rose and crossed to the weighing-house without explanation.

I followed, as was my practice.

The weighing equipment was stored on wall-pegs inside the door: the pan-balance arms, the sets of standard stone weights in their graduated pouches, and the wooden mallet used to break apart cargo-bundles that had been sealed with river clay. The mallet's handle hung on its peg. The iron head that should have been fitted to it lay on the ground beneath, separated. It had not fallen — the fit was tight, designed against exactly that — but had been pulled free and set down, or thrown down, and not replaced.

I looked at it. I said nothing. I did not yet understand what I was looking at.

Holkos said: "Note the mallet, Pythodoros." He said it in the tone he used when he intended me to record something, and so I did record it, on the wax tablet I carried for running notes. Then he turned and walked back toward the lean-to.

"The argument over the Rhodian's consignment," he said to Menekles, as we fell in beside him. "How long did it run?"

"Most of the late afternoon. Near a quarter of it."

"Who managed the records during that period?"

"Dexios. My second assistant." Menekles paused. He said the name with the slight hesitation of a man who does not yet know what weight to attach to it. "He was the one who told me of the argument this morning. He said it was heated."

Holkos said nothing. He walked. But the quality of his silence had changed, and after eleven years I knew enough to notice that. Menekles, who had known him for rather fewer, shifted his weight and looked at me. I offered him nothing, having nothing to offer.

We spent the next hour on inquiries that were not, on their face, remarkable. Holkos spoke with the water-carrier who had found the body — a slow, deliberate conversation about where the man had been during the night, who else had been in the storage area, whether he had heard anything. He spoke with two of the cargo-workers, asking them not about the argument but about the sequence of the previous day's transfers. He asked about Arkelaos specifically: had anyone spoken with him after the argument was settled? Had the Rhodian gone to the storage lean-to alone?

It was from the second worker — an older man, Thessalos, who had worked the crossing for twenty years and knew most of what passed along it — that I heard two things I noted without understanding.

"The Rhodian was careful about his copper," Thessalos said. "More careful than copper warrants. I have carried copper ingots from a dozen merchants and none of them watched the unloading the way he did. He wanted to supervise it himself." He scratched the side of his jaw. "There was a thing, when we were stacking the ingots in the lean-to. A couple of them — two, maybe three — rang wrong when we knocked them with the mallet checking the stack. Hollow, like. And for all that hollowness, they felt... heavier than they ought, for mere copper. I thought it was a bad casting, or perhaps a trick of the clay seal. Didn't think to mention it."

"You were right not to trouble yourself," Holkos said pleasantly. "And the other thing?"

Thessalos looked at him. "I hadn't said there was another thing."

"You were about to."

The old man considered this with the equanimity of someone accustomed to being read. "Yesterday, he changed. He left it alone — left the cargo alone, walked to the argument at the weighing-stone and didn't come back. Strange, for a man who'd been watching those ingots like they were his children."

Holkos thanked him and said nothing further.

As we walked toward the log-house, I remarked on it — the hollow ingots, and the changed behaviour.

"He reached an arrangement," Holkos said. "Or he believed he had." He pushed open the log-house door and went to the reading-stone. "Bring me the tablets for yesterday's transfers."

There were seven tablets. He laid them in the order of their sequence numbers and studied them. This is the system he had introduced eight years before — not indexed by merchant name, as had been the practice, but by time-position in the day's sequence, each entry keyed to its place in the consecutive record. He had explained its purpose to me once, with the patience he reserved for things he considered foundational: under the old system, a missing transaction simply did not appear, and the absence could not be detected. Under his system, a missing transaction left a wound in the record that announced itself. I had thought this theoretical at the time. It had seemed like the kind of elegant solution that exists primarily to demonstrate the elegance of its inventor.

The Rhodian's entry was sequence eleven. The entry before it was sequence nine. The entry after it was sequence twelve.

There was no ten.

"A scribal error," Menekles said. "A missed number in the sequence."

"In eleven years, a sequence number has been missed on three occasions," Holkos said. "Each time, the error shows on the line itself — the number is written and then corrected, or the line is drawn through. There is no such correction between nine and eleven. This was not a slip of the hand. The number ten was omitted from the sequence deliberately, and the entry following it written as twelve to imply that ten had already passed."

"To hide a transaction," I said.

"To hide the absence of one. Whatever occurred — or failed to occur — during that position in the sequence has been removed from the record." He looked at Menekles. "Who writes the sequence numbers?"

The question fell into the room like a stone into still water.

The sequence numbers were written by the supervising scribe on duty. For the late sequence — transfers eleven through fifteen, covering the late afternoon and early evening — the hand was Dexios's.

Menekles's face, which had worn the resolute expression of a man proceeding by established method, became something else. He had signed the consolidated daily record. His name was on everything in that log-house. If the record had been deliberately falsified by one of his own staff, and the falsification had concealed a murder, then the question of what Menekles had known and when he had known it would not stay theoretical for long. He was a careful and methodical man whose procedures were impeccable. Impeccable procedures, I was learning, offer imperfect shelter.

"I see," he said, very quietly.

Holkos was already moving. "One further inquiry," he said. "Who can tell me about the cargo-ships at Lechaion?"

We went to the western harbour. The harbourmaster there was a talkative man named Damon, and he required only a small degree of encouragement to mention that a ship had arrived late the previous evening — a coastal runner from the eastern ports, which had not yet completed its arrival formalities. Its captain had sent a message to the crossing-administration that morning, asking when to expect delivery. Not of the Rhodian's copper, Damon added, with the particular relish of a man who has retained an interesting detail: the captain had specified a *small* consignment, separate from the main cargo, but of such singular value that it was to be brought to him *personally*, not through the usual channels, and secured in his private, below-deck store-room, before the copper was moved on.

Small, and separate. Not copper, then. Copper is neither small nor delivered separately from itself.

"He is expecting something to come to him," Holkos said, as we walked back up from the harbour. "Something that has not arrived. Something of such value that it must be delivered personally, and hidden below deck. He will wait a little longer before he grows anxious."

"The hollow, heavy ingots," I said. "Worked gold, then. Cast inside them."

"Precisely." The faint particular satisfaction was in his voice — the specific relief of a man who has been carrying an asymmetry and has now set it down. "Thessalos told us: a man who watched his cargo like it was his children, who then suddenly abandoned it. Arkelaos reached an arrangement with someone at the crossing — by which the worked gold would be extracted here and passed to the Lechaion ship, to be hidden in its bilge-timber, in exchange for some consideration. Then something went wrong. Either Arkelaos changed his mind, or he had already extracted the gold himself and intended to cross with both the legitimate copper and its precious contents. And the argument over weights was real, but it was not about weights."

"And the mallet," I said.

"Was the instrument of the argument's resolution. Dexios met Arkelaos at the lean-to while the cargo sat unsupervised and the transfer sequence was paused. He struck him from behind. Then he returned to the log-house and closed the record, omitting the tenth position to remove all trace of that gap — without realising that the omission was itself a trace, more legible than the thing it concealed."

"And then he came to Menekles this morning," I said, "and planted the seed."

"He is a man of genuine intelligence," Holkos said, without inflection. "Which makes this necessary."

What followed I will set down plainly, because the plainness of it was not, in the end, an absence of drama. It was drama of a different register — and then, briefly, it was the other kind.

Holkos sent me to bring Dexios to the log-house, merely asking him to come because a question had arisen about the afternoon's records. Dexios came agreeably. He was the kind of man who moves through institutional life on the energy of agreeableness — not servile, but perpetually oriented toward the smooth resolution of minor conflicts, anticipating small preferences, being useful before being asked. It is a social gift of real value. It is also, I now understood, a very good mask.

He came in. His eyes went to the tablets on the reading-stone, arranged in their sequence. Something happened in his face — a movement lasting perhaps as long as it takes a bird to pass a window — and then it was gone, resolved back into agreeableness.

Holkos had been standing at the side of the room. He now moved to the reading-stone and stood behind the tablets with his arms loose at his sides, facing Dexios. He said nothing. The silence extended past the point of comfort, past the point where a man of Dexios's social instincts could resist filling it — and Dexios, to his credit, resisted. He waited. But the waiting cost him something, and the cost was faintly visible in the set of his jaw.

Menekles stood to the right of the door. I was to the left of it, which I had not arranged and which I later understood had been arranged for me.

The door was the only way out.

"Tell me about the Rhodian," Holkos said, finally.

Dexios told him — competently, the same account he had given Menekles. The argument over weights. The heated words. The general ill-feeling.

"How long did the argument last?"

"Perhaps a quarter of the afternoon."

"And the transfers?"

"Were held. Nothing could proceed while the Rhodian was contesting his assessment."

"So the sequence paused. For a quarter of the afternoon. And yet I find no marginal notation in the record to account for that pause — no comment on the delay, no explanation of the gap between nine and eleven. That would be standard practice, Dexios. You know that better than most. Where is your notation?"

A pause. Brief, but present.

"I must have neglected it."

"You neglected it," Holkos said, "because a notation would require you to specify the duration of the pause and account for your own whereabouts during it. And you cannot account for your whereabouts. Because your whereabouts were the east storage lean-to." He did not raise his voice. He did not move. "Where you met Arkelaos of Lindos. And where you used the head of the wooden weighing-mallet — which Pythodoros and I observed this morning hanging separated from its handle on the peg in the weighing-house — to strike him from behind."

Dexios's agreeableness did not dissolve gradually. It stopped. One moment it was present; the next it was simply gone, as a lamp goes out, and what remained was a different thing entirely — the face of a man performing rapid, cold calculations about what remained available to him.

He looked at the door.

In the same moment, his right hand moved to his belt — a worker's belt, with a short-bladed tool-knife on it for cutting cord and seal-clay, the kind every man at the crossing carries without anyone thinking of it as a weapon, because it is not a weapon, until the moment that it is.

He took one step.

Menekles moved into him — not heroically, not with any great force, but with the simple, deliberate placement of a careful man who has decided where he will stand and intends to remain there. Dexios's shoulder struck him, and Menekles absorbed it and did not give ground. I came from the left, which was where Holkos had put me, and the three of us occupied the doorway in a way that made its dimensions suddenly very clear to everyone in the room.

Dexios stopped.

He looked at Menekles — this careful, methodical, impeccably procedural man, standing in a doorway with his feet set — and something in his calculation reached its result. His hand left his belt. His shoulders dropped by a fraction.

"The mallet head," he said, and his voice had changed — quieter, stripped of its social warmth. "You cannot place it in my hand."

"I don't need to place it in your hand," Holkos said. His voice had not changed at all. "I need to place you at the lean-to during the pause in the sequence, with the record falsified under your supervision, and the only alternative explanation — a genuine scribal omission — ruled out by eleven years of practice. The ship's captain at Lechaion, Dexios, who sent a message this morning asking after a small, valuable consignment — the worked gold that was cast inside those heavy, hollow ingots — will confirm the arrangement that brought you to the lean-to. He has been waiting since last night for it to arrive, expecting it to be brought to his private store-room below deck, perhaps even hidden in the bilge-timber. He does not yet know why it has not. He will tell us, when Menekles's guards bring him, that this arrangement was for you to extract the precious cargo here, before the copper moved on, and deliver it to its final, true destination."

The calculation ran its final course and arrived nowhere useful.

"Send for the guards," Holkos said to Menekles. "The case is yours to close."

Here I must allow myself the interpretive latitude I promised to restrict, because what happened after belongs to a record that only I will make.

What Holkos had deduced, the ship's captain at Lechaion confirmed. It was indeed worked gold he expected, fashioned to fit inside the hollow copper ingots—the very gold whose unusual weight and hollowness Thessalos had noted. The captain further detailed the arrangement for its secure transfer to his private store-room below deck, a place that, when examined by the palace administration's investigator, proved to be a clever compartment within the bilge-timber. This craft of concealing precious metals within seemingly common cargoes was known at certain eastern ports, a shadowy network extending along the trade-routes, with facilitators at various crossing-points who arranged for palace-assessed cargoes to shed their hidden contents before reaching their recorded destinations. Dexios, it was confirmed, had been recruited to it four years before, which explained his appointment, his agreeableness, and the careful usefulness that had kept him from scrutiny.

Menekles's name was not among the network's men. This was established, and it was not a small thing to establish. The palace administration's investigator, when he came, went through the consolidated records with the kind of meticulous attention that Menekles had spent a career deserving from his superiors and receiving instead from his subordinates, which is the particular injustice of the competent. He found them without irregularity except in the one position that Dexios's hand had touched. Menekles received this finding with the composed relief of an honest man who has spent several days not knowing whether honesty would be sufficient — which is, I think, the most honesty can ever guarantee, and perhaps enough.

Dexios was remanded to the palace authority and removed from the crossing.

The official record credits Menekles with the identification of the responsible party and commends the systematic value of the sequential log in exposing the irregularity. Menekles accepted this with the dignified discomfort of an honest man receiving credit he knows is not entirely his. He mentioned Holkos by name in a private communication to the wanax's administrative officer.

Whether that communication was recorded or discarded, I cannot say. I record it here. That is the only jurisdiction I have.

One matter remains, which bears on nothing official, and which I append because it has stayed with me.

The morning after Dexios was taken, I found Holkos in the log-house, not working. He was sitting at the reading-stone with an old tablet in his hand — worn clay, covered in a script I did not know, not our syllabary, not the standard trade-forms. He turned it slowly in the light from the east window, which was a good window for morning light.

"A riddle," he said, before I asked. "From the Cyprus trade. I have had it three months."

I sat down across the reading-stone from him. "And Dexios?" I said. "The network still operates. Other crossing-points, other facilitators."

"Yes."

"We have resolved one case and left the larger thing untouched."

He set the tablet down. He looked at it. "We have cut one thread, Pythodoros. The loom remains." He said it without satisfaction and without distress, in the tone of a man noting an observable fact about the world — that it is larger than the portion of it any single man can reach, and that this is not a counsel of despair but simply a description of the terms.

I thought of Arkelaos of Lindos, lying in the lean-to with his knees drawn up, his cargo of hollow ingots stacked in the dark beside him. I thought of Menekles, who had dressed in haste and gone to a man's door before dawn because he understood that some irregularities cannot be resolved by procedure alone. I thought of a network of men at crossing-points across the trade-routes, their threads intact, their records smooth, their agreeableness undisturbed.

And I thought: Holkos will find another thread. Not from any ambition to unravel the whole loom — he has no such ambition, or if he has it he has never once mentioned it to me — but because another thread will eventually present itself as a problem, and a problem is, for him, its own sufficient reason.

"Do you expect to solve the riddle?" I asked.

He picked up the tablet again. The light moved on it as he turned it.

"The riddle," he said, "is not the point. The point is that it is not yet solved."

He put it down, took up the day's first tablet, and began to work.

This is the account of the matter of Arkelaos of Lindos, set down truly and completely, according to the observation of Pythodoros, in the season of the early harvest.
