HOLKOS OF CORINTH

~1390 BC — Corinth, Greece

Holkos — the Greek word for that which is drawn, the ship hauled over the Corinthian isthmus from one sea to the other, the thing that crosses between worlds by the application of sustained effort rather than by any natural ease of passage. The name was not given lightly in a trading family. It described a function and declared a character: the man who moves things between places that cannot otherwise be connected, who applies his knowledge of both shores to the problem of the crossing, who understands the isthmus not as an obstacle but as an opportunity.

His father had chosen the isthmus city with the strategic foresight that had guided the family’s migrations across a millennium: the junction between the two seas was where the next chapter of the world was being written. Holkos of Corinth managed the overland transfer of cargoes at the crossing — the narrow neck of land where ships and their goods were hauled from the Saronic to the Corinthian gulf to avoid the dangerous voyage around the Peloponnese. Working under the authority of the Mycenaean palace administration whose trading network extended to the isthmus, he assessed the cargoes, organised the labour, and maintained the records of every consignment that passed through his hands with the precision of a man who understood that the document was the transaction and the transaction was the relationship. He was the last member of the lineage to use a Greek name that retained the Semitic name-element in any recoverable form; his son would be Greek, and his son’s son more Greek still, and the old Semitic thread would recede entirely into the family’s memory, present only in the archive that was their truest inheritance.

The account that follows is preserved by Menekles’ successor, the scribe Pythodoros, whose notes were appended to the Corinthian administrative record after the matter was resolved. Pythodoros was more reflective than precise, and where the earlier entries lean on observation, his hand occasionally reaches toward interpretation, though he repeatedly corrects himself when his inferences outpace the evidence.

The Matter Of The Death Of Arkelaos The Rhodian

Set down by Pythodoros, assistant to the scribe Menekles, in the administrative record of the Corinthian crossing, in the reign of the wanax, in the season of the early harvest. I have appended my own observations where the record of Menekles leaves gaps, though I confess that my hand reaches more readily toward meaning than my master’s did, and I have tried, where I noticed this fault, to pull it back.

There is a smell to the isthmus that no man who has worked it ever forgets, and that no man who has merely passed through it can adequately describe. It is the smell of two seas at once — the salt of the Saronic sharper, carrying the mineral edge of the eastern trade, the Corinthian Gulf to the west softer and darker, faintly of pine resin where the timber ships discharge. Between these two olfactory worlds lies the strip of land across which everything must be carried, and in the carrying, the smells mix and do not resolve. The oil that keeps the wooden sledge-runners greased against the packed earth of the crossing-track — rendered from fish-heads and old marrow-bones, a vile compound that works admirably — sits beneath everything else like a persistent bass note. In the heat of summer it rises, and the crossing-track becomes a lane of particular stench that the workers no longer notice and that visitors remark upon without exception.

It was into this smell that I, Pythodoros, descended on the morning the body was found, following at a careful distance behind Holkos of Corinth, who walked as he always walked on a matter of business: quickly, with his chin slightly forward, his narrow eyes already reading the ground before his feet had reached it.

I should say something of Holkos before I record what he did, because this account will be consulted by those who did not know him, and the things he did will seem, without the man himself before you, either miraculous or invented. He was not tall. He was lean in the way that men who forget to eat during a working problem become lean — not from discipline but from simple inattention to appetite. His hair was very black and very straight and he kept it bound for practical reasons he would explain at length if asked, and which I will not reproduce here. His eyes were the particular grey-brown that catches light at unusual angles, and when he was thinking — which was most of the time — they fixed on nothing visible to anyone else. When he was not thinking, he was insufferable in a different way: he would sit for half a day in the small room he kept behind the weighing-house, staring at the ceiling, occasionally asking me to read aloud the tallies from the previous month’s transfers, which he would then correct from memory without consulting the clay tablets themselves. This was how he rested his mind. It was not restful to observe.

He had no vice that I ever discovered, unless you counted the riddles. He collected them. He paid for them. Sailors off the eastern ships would bring him wordplay in Ugaritic or in the strange clipped trade-dialect of the Cyprus ports, and he would spend an evening with them and emerge satisfied or dissatisfied and in either case wanting no company for the rest of the night. I believe the riddles served the same function for him that wine serves for other men — they occupied a portion of the mind that, left empty, became destructive. He had once described it to me: the brain is a storage vessel, Pythodoros, and if you put nothing in it, it begins to consume the vessel itself. He had said this with great seriousness. I had thought he was making a philosophical remark. He was not. He was explaining his own architecture.

He was thirty-eight years old, or thereabouts. He had been managing the crossing for eleven years. There was no man in Corinth who knew more about the movement of cargo across the isthmus, and there was no man in Corinth to whom the palace administration was more reluctantly grateful — reluctantly, because he was almost always right when they were wrong, and institutional memory for such things is reliable.

The body of Arkelaos of Rhodes had been found by a water-carrier at first light, beneath the easternmost storage lean-to where the offloaded cargo waited to be assessed and tallied. He was lying on his side with his knees drawn up, which the water-carrier had taken at first for a sleeping man, except that sleeping men do not have the back of the skull laid open in the way that Arkelaos’s skull had been laid open. The instrument had been heavy and had been swung from above and behind. There was a great deal of blood on the packed earth floor. The blood had dried. This meant the night, or the early part of it.

Menekles had sent for Holkos. Menekles was the official scribe of the crossing and thus, by the logic of the palace administration, the officer responsible for irregularities occurring on palace-administered ground. He was a careful and methodical man whose procedures were impeccable and whose imagination was, through no fault of his own, limited. I do not say this unkindly. Menekles had trained me and I owe him my formation. But I observed, over the years I served him, that he approached a problem by selecting the solution that most resembled a problem he had solved before, and then fitting the available evidence to that solution. This works admirably for most problems. It does not work for the problems that Holkos was called to address.

Menekles had already formed his view by the time we arrived. He stated it to Holkos as we stood at the edge of the storage lean-to, the body before us, the morning light coming sideways through the open east face of the shelter.

“The cargo-workers,” Menekles said. “The Rhodian was a difficult man. He argued the weight of his consignment. He accused the weighing-crew of shortchanging him. There were words yesterday evening.”

“There are always words,” Holkos said, without looking at him. He was looking at the body. “The crossing-workers argued with three other merchants yesterday, by my count. Do you propose to examine them all?”

“The argument with the Rhodian was the most heated.”

“Heated arguments do not produce corpses. What produces corpses is motive, opportunity, and the momentary suppression of the instinct against killing. The workers had the first and possibly the last. What did they not have?”

Menekles waited.

“Opportunity,” Holkos said. “Or rather — they had opportunity in abundance, which amounts to the same problem as having none at all. When every man has access to the scene, the fact of access tells us nothing.” He crouched. He crouched with a sudden fluency that was strange in a man who had been standing still — he went from stillness to crouched examination in a single motion, as if a decision had been made at some level beneath conscious thought. “The blood has been dry for some hours. It dried unevenly — faster at the edges, which means it spread as it dried. The spreading stopped here.” He indicated a boundary with one finger, not touching the earth. “The body was not moved after the blood dried. He died here, or within this distance of here.”

He studied the skull. He studied the entry of the wound without touching it. He looked at the packed earth around the body and then looked at the ground outside the lean-to. Then he stood.

“Walk me to the log-house,” he said. “I want the transfer record for yesterday.”

The log-house was a small structure of mudbrick adjacent to the weighing-house, where the clay tablets of the daily record were kept before they were sealed and dispatched to the palace. The tablets were indexed in the way Holkos had introduced eight years before — a system of his own invention, which Menekles used faithfully without ever being entirely comfortable with its logic. It organized transactions not by the identity of the merchant, as had been the practice, but by time-position in the day’s sequence, each entry keyed to the sequence of transfers rather than to the names of the parties. This, Holkos had explained when he introduced it, meant that gaps in the sequence were visible. Under the old system, a missing transaction simply did not appear, and the absence could not be detected. Under the new system, a missing transaction left a wound in the record that announced itself.

I had thought this theoretical when he explained it. I had not imagined it would be consequential.

He asked for the tablets for the previous day’s transfers. There were seven. He laid them on the flat reading-stone in the order of their sequence numbers and studied them for what seemed like a long time but was probably not. Then he turned the fourth tablet to face me and Menekles, and he put his finger on a section of the clay.

“The Rhodian’s cargo,” he said.

The entry was there. Arkelaos of Rhodes. Copper ingots, twenty-two talents assessed weight, passed from the Saronic side, received into storage for eastern re-dispatch. The tally was complete and formally correct.

“What do I look at?” Menekles said.

“The sequence number of this entry,” Holkos said. “And then the sequence number of the entry before it, and the entry after it.”

Menekles looked. I looked. The tablets were numbered at the upper left edge in the standard form. The Rhodian’s entry was sequence eleven. The entry before it was sequence nine. The entry after it was sequence twelve.

There was no ten.

“A scribal error,” Menekles said immediately. “A missed number in the sequence.”

“In eleven years of the new system,” Holkos said, “a number has been missed in the sequence on three occasions. On each occasion the error appears on the line itself — the number is written and then corrected, or the line is drawn through. There is no such correction on the line nine, which runs directly into the line eleven. This was not a slip of the hand. The number ten was omitted from the sequence entirely.” He turned to look at us both with equal patience. “Which means either that someone destroyed the tenth tablet — which would show as a gap in the physical stack — or that someone was supervising the sequence closely enough to omit a number on purpose and write the subsequent entry as twelve to imply that ten had already passed.”

“To hide a transaction,” I said, before I had thought about whether to speak.

“To hide the absence of one,” Holkos said. “The tenth position in the sequence was the transfer immediately before the Rhodian’s cargo was received. Whatever occurred during that transfer — or whatever occurred instead of a transfer — has been removed from the record.”

“Who writes the sequence numbers?” Menekles asked.

The question fell into the log-house like a stone into still water, and we all felt the ripples.

The sequence numbers were written by the supervising scribe on duty. Yesterday, the late sequence — transfers eleven through fifteen, covering the late afternoon and early evening — had been recorded under the hand of Dexios.

Dexios was Menekles’s second assistant. He had been with the crossing administration for four years. He was a careful copyist and a plausible man of around twenty-five, with a round face and an agreeable manner. He had, as Menekles now recalled with some difficulty, reported this morning that the crossing-workers had argued violently with the Rhodian.

He had been, in other words, the man who planted the seed that had become Menekles’s theory before Holkos had arrived.

What followed was of a different character. I will not dress it as more dramatic than it was, because the plainness of it was itself a kind of drama.

Holkos sent me to bring Dexios to the log-house without explanation or ceremony, merely asking him to come because a question had arisen about the afternoon’s records. Dexios came agreeably, as he did everything. He was the kind of man who moves through institutional life on the energy of agreeableness — not servile, but perpetually oriented toward the smooth resolution of minor conflicts, anticipating small preferences, being useful before being asked. It is a social gift of real value. It is also a very good mask.

He came in. He looked at the tablets on the reading-stone. Something happened in his face that lasted perhaps as long as it takes a bird to pass a window — and then it was gone, resolved back into agreeableness.

Holkos had been standing at the side of the room. He now moved to the reading-stone and stood behind the tablets, which meant he was standing facing Dexios, with the tablets as a kind of altar between them. He said nothing for long enough that even Menekles shifted his weight.

“Tell me about the Rhodian,” he said finally.

Dexios told him, competently, the same account Menekles had given us — the argument over weights, the heated words, the general ill-feeling.

“How long did the argument last?”

“Perhaps a quarter of the afternoon.”

“And during the argument, what was occurring with the transfers?”

“They were held. No transfer could proceed while the Rhodian was contesting his assessment at the weighing-stone.”

“So the sequence paused.”

“Yes.”

“For a quarter of the afternoon. And in a sequence of fifteen transfers recorded yesterday, how many occurred during that pause?”

Dexios’s mouth opened slightly. He recovered. “None. The pause—”

“Would appear as a gap,” Holkos said pleasantly, “between two otherwise consecutive sequence numbers. As the gap between nine and eleven appears on this tablet. But you told Menekles the argument lasted a quarter of the afternoon. Surely you would have noted the pause in the record — a marginal notation, a comment on the delay, something to explain the gap to anyone reviewing the sequence. It would be standard practice. Where is your notation?”

There was no notation.

“You did not note it,” Holkos said, “because a notation would require you to specify the duration of the pause and account for your own whereabouts during it. And you cannot account for your whereabouts. Because your whereabouts were the east storage lean-to, where you met Arkelaos of Rhodes while his cargo sat unsupervised, and where you struck him from behind with the wooden weighing-mallet whose head I observed this morning was missing from its handle in the weighing-house, though the handle remains hung on its peg. A detail I noticed. A detail you did not think to remove.”

Dexios said nothing.

“What I cannot read directly,” Holkos continued, and his voice had the quality of a man who is completing an arithmetic in a ledger rather than accusing another man of murder, “is the why. I can speculate. The Rhodian was carrying copper ingots. Twenty-two talents assessed weight. But the Rhodian was known at the eastern ports as a man who carried other things inside copper ingots when the arrangement benefited him — valuables of a kind that do not appear on palace transfer records, and for which the copper is ballast and concealment both. Someone at the receiving end of those hidden goods arranged for their recovery at the crossing, before the cargo reached its eastern destination. You were their man here. The Rhodian objected — perhaps he wanted more, perhaps he refused the arrangement, perhaps he had already removed the concealed material himself and was trying to cross with both the legitimate cargo and whatever was hidden in it. And the argument over weights was real, but it was not about weights.”

He stopped.

The log-house was very quiet. Somewhere outside, a donkey was being loaded and was expressing its opinion of this.

“There is a ship at Lechaion,” Holkos said, “which docked late yesterday and which has not yet completed its arrival formalities. It was waiting. I think if Menekles sends guards to Lechaion now, he will find that ship’s captain is expecting something to be brought to him this morning. Something that is no longer available, because the man who was supposed to bring it is standing in this room.”

He turned to Menekles. “Send for the guards. The case is yours to close.”

Here I, Pythodoros, must depart from strict account and allow myself the interpretive latitude that I promised to restrict. Holkos will not thank me for it. He does not read these administrative appendices.

The ship’s captain at Lechaion was taken with a quantity of worked gold concealed in the bilge-timber, consistent with the kind of hollow ingot-work the Rhodian had been known to employ. There was, behind this, the shadow of an arrangement extending along the trade-routes — a network of facilitators at various crossing-points who arranged for palace-assessed cargoes to shed their hidden contents before reaching their recorded destinations. Dexios had been recruited to it four years ago, which explained his appointment to the crossing, his agreeableness, and the precision with which he had managed to be useful without ever being scrutinized.

Holkos, when I observed to him that the network itself remained operative despite our resolution of the case, said only: we have cut one thread, Pythodoros. The loom remains. He said it with neither satisfaction nor distress, in the tone of a man noting that the tide is going out, which it always does and always will.

Dexios was remanded to the palace authority and removed from the crossing. The official record credits Menekles with the identification of the responsible party and commends the systematic value of the sequential log — Holkos’s system — in exposing the irregularity. Menekles accepted this with the dignified discomfort of an honest man receiving credit he knows is not entirely his. He mentioned Holkos in a private communication to the wanax’s administrative officer. Whether the mention was recorded or discarded, I cannot say.

I record it here. That is the only jurisdiction I have.

One matter I will append, though it bears on nothing official.

The morning after Dexios was taken away, I came to the log-house and found Holkos sitting with a piece of worn clay in his hand — an old tablet, not one of ours, covered in a script I did not recognize. He was working at it slowly, in the way that other men sharpen tools: methodically, without great pleasure, as necessary maintenance.

I asked him what it was.

He said it was a riddle. From the Cyprus trade. He had had it three months and had not solved it.

I asked if he expected to.

He turned it in his hands. The light came through the east window, which was a good window for morning light, positioned as it was above the reading-stone.

“The riddle,” he said, “is not the point. The point is that it is not yet solved.”

He put it down, took up the day’s first tablet, and began to work.

This is the account of the matter of Arkelaos of Rhodes, set down truly and completely, according to the observation of Pythodoros, in the season of the early harvest.
