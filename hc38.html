HOLKOS THE ARBITER

~940 BC — Athens, Greece

An Account of the Arbiter Holkos

In the Archonship of Aischylos, Son of Nikodemos

I.

Demetrios of the Alkmaion deme walked through the door of the council hall like a man who had been carrying something heavy for a very long time and had finally decided to put it down on someone else's floor. I looked up from my tablet. I had thought the morning's session complete. I set my stylus down and reached for it again almost immediately, because there are men whose arrival announces that the day is not finished with you, and Demetrios was one of them.

He was perhaps fifty, broad through the shoulder, with the stillness of a man who has been angry long enough to make peace with the weight of it. Beside him stood his son Kleitos, younger, less contained. They were followed by a third figure I did not immediately place — stocky, grey-bearded, calloused hands — who entered at a careful distance, as if proximity to the other two carried a social risk he had not fully calculated. Behind all three came a fourth man I noted without yet weighing: broad as a grain sack, perhaps thirty, with a flat, attentive face that gave nothing away and small eyes that moved continuously around the room in the manner of a man professionally accustomed to counting exits.

On the far side of the hall, already seated and already watching the door, were Euthymios and his elder daughter Phila. Euthymios was thin in the way of a man not eating well, and his eyes had the particular quality of someone waiting for a blow whose direction he cannot determine. His daughter sat very straight and looked at nothing.

The agora baked outside. The smell of it — dung and rancid oil and the iron-sweet drift from the bronze-workers' quarter — pressed through the hall's open side and thickened in the still air. It was the kind of heat that strips men of patience, and every person in that room had already been stripped.

Sophranos was summarising the dispute for the record when Holkos arrived.

I had served as Sophranos's attached scribe for four years before I first encountered the Arbiter, and by the time of this account I had followed him through a dozen matters of varying darkness: a poisoning arranged to resemble fever, a forgery of testament documents detected by the wrong weight of the clay, a question of merchant cargo and deliberate miscounting that I still do not entirely understand. He was a lean man, angular, with the economy of movement that belongs to someone whose body is merely a conveyance for something operating at a different register. He arrived with fine grey dust still on the lower hem of his robe from the drainage dispute he had been examining that morning in the Kerameikos quarter, took a seat at the end of the council bench without being invited, and looked at the room with the focused attention of a man reading a document for evidence of alteration.

He looked at Demetrios for a long moment. Demetrios did not quite know what to do with being looked at in that fashion.

"The dispossession was not sudden," Holkos said. Not a question.

Demetrios said, "How could you —"

"Because you are angry rather than surprised. Surprise produces agitation. What you are carrying is settled fury, which is what accumulates in a man who has watched a loss compound and had no instrument with which to address it." He paused. "Your hands. The calluses are recent — the particular wear of a man re-marking a boundary himself, driving stakes into ground that kept returning the wrong answer." His gaze moved to the grey-bearded figure who had entered behind them. "And you are the surveyor. You produced a report you believe to be accurate. Yet you are watching Demetrios as though you expect him to call you a liar."

Timon said nothing.

"Which is because you are not entirely certain he would be wrong," Holkos said. "Which is interesting."

He told Sophranos he would like to visit the boundary before the afternoon light went. And then, as if it were an afterthought — it was not — he asked when the council next sat to consider land disputes.

Sophranos said: at dawn, two days hence. After that, any claim in active contest reverted to prior registration for resolution.

Holkos nodded. "Then we have until dawn the day after tomorrow," he said, to no one in particular, in the tone of a man setting a lamp in a window before a long night.

II.

We walked south of the city for perhaps half an hour into the low hills where the two properties met. The land was dry scrub cut through by rows of olives, some of them ancient, their trunks twisted into the postures of exhausted patience. The boundary was marked by six stones, each the height of a man's knee, set at intervals along a rough east-to-west line across a shallow slope. The two families walked apart from each other without being instructed to. The heat pressed down without apology.

Holkos walked the line once without speaking. Then again, more slowly.

At the third stone from the western end he stopped and crouched, and I moved close enough to see what had arrested him. The earth at the stone's base was darker than the ground an arm's length away — not dramatically, but distinctly, in the way that soil recently turned and re-compressed holds moisture differently from earth that has been still for years. There was a faint pressed line at the surface, a discontinuity, as though the ground had been lifted and replaced without perfectly matching itself. I noted it. I did not yet know what to make of it.

Holkos looked up at me. "What do you observe?"

"The soil is darker at the base. Recently disturbed."

"Yes. Now look east. The stones at the far end."

I looked. Even from that distance the difference was apparent: the earth around the eastern stones sat harder and more uniform, the colour consistent all the way to the base. Undisturbed, or so it appeared.

"The eastern stones look settled," I said. "The western ones do not."

He rose. "That is the correct observation," he said, quietly, so only I could hear, "and the wrong conclusion."

He took from his robe a short iron stylus — not for writing but for probing — and worked it into the earth at the base of the fourth stone, then the fifth. I watched his wrist. At approximately one hand's depth I saw it check — a slight resistance, then a change. He withdrew the stylus. The lower portion carried a finer, looser earth than the crust above: a different consistency, the texture of soil that had been turned and had not fully re-compressed.

He handed me the stylus. "The third stone. The western one."

I probed. The stylus met ordinary resistance at the surface, and the earth on it when withdrawn was uniform throughout — the same density top to bottom, undisturbed all the way through.

I understood the difference before he spoke it. The western stones had been disturbed recently and the entire column of earth was still loose. The eastern stones had been disturbed earlier — long enough for the surface to harden — but beneath that crust the settling was incomplete. Both sets had been moved. At different times, in opposite directions.

I said this to Holkos. He did not smile — he rarely did — but something in his expression acknowledged that I had arrived somewhere useful.

"The western stones were shifted outward," he confirmed, "away from the Euthymios property. The eastern stones, when they were moved — two seasons ago, perhaps three — were shifted inward, compressing the apparent boundary and costing Demetrios land. Then when Demetrios noticed the loss and engaged a surveyor, the western stones were moved outward to make the whole displacement appear a single act committed by Euthymios." He straightened and looked at Timon. "You measured the stones against each other. You found the western ones displaced and concluded they favoured Euthymios. You were correct as far as you proceeded. You did not probe the eastern stones because they appeared settled and your commission was to find the displacement, not to interrogate the baseline."

"That is what I was engaged to assess," Timon said, stiffly.

"I know. The error was in the commission, not in you." Holkos looked at both families. "Neither of you did this. A third hand moved these stones, in two stages, to set you against each other." He let that rest. "I am not yet prepared to name that hand. But I will be. Before dawn the day after tomorrow, I will be."

He turned toward the city — then stopped.

Something at the southern end of the slope had caught his attention. He stood for a moment with his hand raised against the sun, and I followed his gaze to where the hill's contour curved inward and the scrub gave way to a denser growth of reed-grass and tamarisk. Even from that distance the ground had a different colour there — darker, richer, the particular deep green that tells a man water finds its own level and does not forget where.

I walked toward it without being asked. The land dipped slightly at the southern end, forming a shallow basin perhaps twenty paces across, and at its centre the soil was dark and faintly damp even in the height of summer. A collection point. Rainwater from the upper slope would run south and gather here; even in dry years there would be moisture enough, channelled correctly, to support an olive grove three times the size of either family's present planting. The two strips of contested land, added to what lay already on either side of them, would share this basin as a common resource. Separately the parcels were adequate. Combined around this southern collection, with the water managed properly, they would constitute something considerably more valuable than the sum of their individual parts.

I noted all of this in my tablet and said nothing of it until Holkos joined me.

"Southern end," I said. "The water collects here."

He was already looking at it with the expression he wore when something had confirmed rather than surprised him.

"Yes," he said. "The southern collection basin." He stayed for a moment, then turned north again toward the city. "I need to look at the registration documents. And I need to look at them before whoever filed the relevant prior claim learns I am looking."

III.

The archive was beneath the council hall — a low-ceilinged room that held the heat like a closed hand, its shelves packed with clay tablets in the dense, apparently random order of a system comprehensible only to those who had built it. Which meant it was comprehensible to me, and to very few others.

We were not alone in the hall above when we descended to the archive. The broad young man who had entered behind Demetrios's party that morning — the one with the flat face and the counting eyes — was seated on the steps of the portico as we passed, doing nothing with the deliberate quality of someone who has been instructed to do nothing and is performing the instruction conscientiously. He looked at us without appearing to look at us, which is a thing that requires practice.

I mentioned this to Holkos as we descended the archive stairs.

"Yes," he said, without elaboration.

Holkos told me what he was looking for: the original partition document from the archon before last, the secondary registration establishing the eastern boundary reference points, and — this he added with the precision of a man who had already arrived at a conclusion he had not yet shared — any prior land claim filed under the Brauronian deme registrations within the last three years.

He left me to find these things, which was the correct allocation of labour. I knew which shelves held which tenures, which scribes had worked under which archons, the particular way the tablets from the previous generation were stacked with the older documents to the inside where the weight would not crack the edges. Holkos sat on the archive room's single stool with his elbows on his knees and his eyes on the middle distance, which was his way of being somewhere else entirely while his body remained present.

I found the partition document first, then the secondary registration. Then I began working through the Brauronian deme records, and it was here that I encountered something that arrested me long enough that I set the other tablets down.

The Brauronian deme holdings were recorded in order of registration date. As I worked through them, I began to see a shape. Three separate properties, registered at intervals spanning nine years, each one adjacent to the land now under dispute. The first was filed under the previous archon. The second two years later. The third — the most recent — shared a boundary with the eastern edge of the Euthymios grove, and another with the northern edge of the Demetrios strip, and had been filed four years ago by a man whose name appeared on all three registrations: Lykides of the Brauronian deme.

I held the three tablets up in the lamplight. Picture it as Holkos later described it to me: three rectangular clay tablets, each one representing a property, arranged around a central gap the way three fingers curl around an empty palm. The first tablet — the oldest, filed nine years prior — lay to the west of the disputed land. The second, filed seven years ago, lay to the east. The third, filed four years ago, pressed against both the northern edge of the Demetrios strip and the eastern edge of the Euthymios grove simultaneously. The gap at the centre of these three encircling properties was precisely the contested boundary land. Separately, each of Lykides's parcels was a subsistence plot, too small and dry for serious agriculture. Together, with the disputed land completing the circuit, they would surround and share the southern collection basin I had stood beside that afternoon — the basin whose dark soil and reed-grass spoke of water even in the height of summer. Every registration had been filed at an interval too regular for coincidence, too patient for opportunism.

"Holkos," I said.

He was beside me before I had completed the word, which told me he had already been watching.

I showed him the arrangement. He took the three tablets, held them with both hands, and looked at them for a long time with the blank expression that meant he was not thinking of nothing but of everything simultaneously. Then he set them down, one beside the other, and pointed without speaking to the space between them — the gap that the disputed boundary land would fill, if acquired.

"A consolidated agricultural holding," he said. It was not a question. "The Alkmaion strip under olives, the eastern grove, and these three properties together — they share the water collection at the southern end. I saw the basin this afternoon." He was not looking at the tablets anymore but at something only he could see. "Separately they are subsistence plots managed by families with no excess capital. Together, irrigated from the southern basin and properly managed, they would support an estate three times the value of any individual parcel." He set the three Brauronian tablets down with the deliberateness of a man closing an argument. "He has been building toward this for a decade. The falsification of the boundary — both movements — was the final instrument. Let both families destroy each other through litigation, or have both found dishonest, and the prior claim resolves the contested land in his favour. He acquires the centre. The whole shape completes."

"You knew this before we came to the archive," I said.

"I suspected it. The geometry of the stones was too deliberate for a simple theft. A simple theft takes land. This scheme" — he looked at the arranged tablets — "was designed to take a future. That requires a man who has been thinking about that future for a very long time." He picked up the secondary registration. "Now let me see the forgery."

I showed him what I had already seen in the secondary registration: the eastern reference points written in a different hand. The pressure lighter. The land-measure notation curving the wrong way at the foot of the third character.

He looked at it. "Professional," he said.

"Yes. A trained scribe."

"A trained scribe with archive access who processed property documents between families and the city record." He set it down. "There is a merchant named Kratinos who handles exactly this class of transfer. He has done so for fifteen years. He is, from what I know of him, a man who makes careful arrangements and does not expose himself unnecessarily." He was quiet for a moment. "He will be afraid. He has been afraid since Demetrios filed the complaint. He is the kind of man who makes practical calculations, which means he is now calculating whether his arrangement with Lykides is more dangerous than the alternative."

He stood. "Leave these tablets with Sophranos. All of them — the partition, the secondary registration, the three Brauronian records. Go to him now, directly, and tell him they are archive materials relevant to the boundary dispute and that they are to be held in his personal custody until the hearing at dawn." He picked up a small wax writing board from the shelf — the kind used for temporary notations, the surface re-usable — and held it out to me. "Before you go: transcribe, on this, the specific errors in the secondary registration. The pressure differential. The curvature of the notation. The shelf location and registration mark of each Brauronian tablet. Everything a man would need to reconstruct the argument before Sophranos without the physical documents." His eyes were steady on me. "Because we will not have the physical documents tonight."

I looked at him. "Where will we be tonight?"

"At a meeting," he said. "I will tell you what to write to Kratinos."

As we climbed the archive stairs I noted that the portico steps were empty. The broad young man with the counting eyes was gone.

IV.

I will not pretend I was entirely easy with what Holkos proposed. I was not asked whether I was easy with it.

The message to Kratinos was precisely worded: the archive records had been examined, the interpolated registration was known, the prior claim under the Brauronian registrations had been located. Holkos was prepared to offer Kratinos a means of separating his fate from that of the man who had commissioned the forgeries. This offer would expire at dawn. The terms required discussion tonight, at a location of Kratinos's choosing, with no other party present. The final line — Holkos dictated it with a pause before it — read: I have sent a copy of this message to no one. I recommend you confirm the same before you respond.

Which was true. He had sent a copy to no one. He had also — he explained this to me, walking to Sophranos's house with the tablets under my arm — correctly anticipated that Lykides would have a man watching Kratinos, and that by the time Kratinos received this message, Lykides would already know its contents.

"You are sending the message to Lykides," I said. "Through Kratinos."

"I am sending it to both simultaneously. Kratinos will want to accept. Lykides will want to intercept." He walked without slowing. "A man who intercepts a message and adds his own line to it has told me several things. He has told me he knows what the message contains, which confirms his connection to Kratinos. He has told me he is willing to meet, which means he believes the meeting gives him the advantage. And he has told me he wants the transcription." He glanced at the wax board I was carrying alongside the tablets. "Which we will bring him."

"And he will believe it is the only record."

"He will believe it is the only record he cannot refute, because the tablets are with Sophranos. A transcription on wax" — he let this settle — "can be smoothed. Clay cannot be unbroken. He will want the wax board because destroying it appears to solve his problem. This is the error I need him to make."

I deposited the tablets with Sophranos, who received them with the calm of a man who has learned not to ask Holkos for explanations before they are offered. Then I sent the message to Kratinos and returned to the council hall and did not eat anything, my knees having commenced their private argument and my stomach having declined to participate in the evening's proceedings.

Kratinos's reply came two hours after dusk. It named a colonnaded court near the potters' district — enclosed, known to the property merchants' guild, deniable. The second hour past midnight. And, added in a heavier hand that was not Kratinos's, a single line:

Bring the transcription. Come alone. The merchant will not be the only one present.

Holkos read this line twice. Then he looked at me with the expression he wore when a mechanism had engaged exactly as he had designed it to.

"He intercepted Kratinos's reply," I said.

"Added his instruction and sent it on." He folded the message. "He wants the wax board and my presence in an enclosed space at midnight. He believes this solves two problems at once." He rose from the bench. "We should go. I need to give certain instructions before we arrive."

"Instructions to whom?"

"To Demetrios and Euthymios. And Timon. And Sophranos's guard."

I record that the next hour was spent in a series of conversations I was not always present for, conducted by Holkos with the efficiency of a man who has planned a thing completely and is now merely implementing it. What I can say is that by the time we set out through the dark city toward the potters' district, there were people in motion who had not been in motion before, moving toward a courtyard they had been told to enter from the northern colonnade and to remain in the shadow of the eastern pillars until they heard one word.

I asked what the word was.

"Precisely," he said.

I asked why that word.

"Because I am unlikely to use it in any other context, and almost certain to use it in this one." He walked without slowing. "Lykides is going to tell me I am wrong about something tonight. Not because he is uncertain — he knows I am right — but because a man who has spent a decade being the only person who knows the full shape of his own scheme cannot resist contesting the account given of it by someone else. It is a vanity native to a particular kind of intelligence. He will correct me. And the correction will be the trap."

"What will you be wrong about?"

He said nothing for three paces.

"The water basin," he said. "I saw it this afternoon at the southern end of the slope. I will describe it as being at the northern end. A man who has merely acquired adjacent properties over the years has no reason to know the precise hydrology of land he does not yet own. But a man who has surveyed and planned a consolidated estate for a decade — who has staked his life's work on the water that collects there — knows it to the inch. He will not be able to hear me place it at the wrong end of the slope and say nothing."

He walked on.

"Whether he says nothing," he added, "is the only thing tonight I am not entirely certain of."

V.

I will say plainly that the walk through the dark city to the potters' district was the least comfortable hour of my professional life to that point, and that my knees renewed the private argument they had begun in the archive and had not since resolved. The street altars threw small circles of lamp-light that made the dark between them heavier by contrast. The city was not silent — cities never fully are — but the sounds were the wrong kind: a dog somewhere distant, the creak of cooling wood, and once, a burst of laughter from behind a closed gate that faded as quickly as it had come and left the silence heavier than before.

Holkos walked at his usual pace, which gave the impression of a man to whom the city's mood was a matter of purely academic interest. I have since come to understand that this impression was entirely deliberate.

The colonnaded court was a rectangle of stone perhaps twenty paces by thirty, open to the sky, its four sides lined with pillars that threw angled shadows in the moonlight. In the middle of the night it was empty to appearance, the shadows between the pillars dense enough to conceal figures that I knew, without being able to see them, were present in the eastern colonnade.

We entered from the southern passage and stood in the open centre and waited. The night air carried the smell of cooling stone and something acrid from the kilns nearby. I held the wax board under my arm and was aware of it in the way one is aware of a thing that someone intends to take. I was also aware — because I had been thinking about it since we left the council hall — that the broad young man with the counting eyes had last been seen on the portico steps that afternoon and had not been accounted for since.

Lykides did not make us wait long.

He came through the northern passage with the bearing of someone who has spent his life occupying rooms by right rather than invitation. He was perhaps fifty-five, broad through the chest, and dressed with the understated precision of a man who considers display to be a form of weakness. Behind him came the careful-eyed older figure I had not seen before, whose function was clearly to observe and to remember. And then, half a step behind and to the right, the broad young man himself — the one who had sat on the portico steps counting exits — now positioned in the way a man positions a tool he expects to use. He had not moved to stand between us and the southern passage by accident. When he looked at me there was nothing in his face that resembled hesitation.

Lykides looked at Holkos. He looked at me. He looked at the wax board under my arm with the brief, controlled attention of a man confirming the presence of something he has already planned around.

"The Arbiter," he said. Measured. The voice of a man who has learned to treat affect as a resource to be spent deliberately.

"Lykides of the Brauronian deme," Holkos said. "You have three properties arranged around this disputed land. You filed them at intervals over nine years. The most recent shares a boundary with the eastern grove and another with the northern strip."

Lykides looked at him with the assessing patience of a man waiting for the point.

"You have been assembling this holding for a decade," Holkos continued. "The disputed land is the centre. Without it your three properties are subsistence parcels. With it — and with access to the water collection I observed this afternoon at the northern end of the upper slope — you have an estate worth four times any individual parcel. The consolidated holding was the objective. Both families' ruin was the instrument."

He said all of this in the same tone he had used to describe the soil at the boundary stones — the tone of a man describing a thing he has already finished examining. Not a question. Not an accusation awaiting confirmation. A completed account.

Lykides was quiet for a moment. He had the quality of a man finding his footing on ground that has shifted slightly, and not wishing to show the adjustment.

"You are describing a land acquisition," he said. "There is nothing criminal in acquiring adjacent properties over time."

"There is nothing criminal in the acquisitions," Holkos agreed. "The crime is in what you did to accelerate the final step." He paused. "The prior claim you filed under the Brauronian registrations was built on interpolated reference points in the secondary boundary registration. The interpolation was made by a trained scribe with archive access who handles property document transfers. The same hand that added the eastern reference points to the secondary registration made the original filing of your prior claim." He looked at Lykides steadily. "Kratinos has been before Sophranos since the eighth hour of the evening."

Something moved in Lykides's face. Not fear — I do not think he was a man who experienced fear as most men do — but a rapid, precise recalculation. He covered it within the space of two breaths.

Then he said: "You said the water collection at the northern end."

The court was quiet. The broad young man had not moved, but I felt his attention shift — the slight realignment of a man who has received a signal.

"Yes," Holkos said. "The northern end."

"The water collection is at the southern end of the upper slope," Lykides said, with the precision of a man correcting an error he cannot help but correct. "The northern drainage runs off the hill entirely. It is the southern collection basin that makes the consolidated holding viable. Your geography is wrong."

The court was silent.

"Precisely," said Holkos.

The word struck the night air like a stylus on fresh clay.

The broad young man moved in the same instant — not toward the colonnade but toward me, directly, with the speed of a man who has been waiting for permission and has now received it. He covered the distance in three strides and had my arm above the elbow before I could do anything of use, and his other hand drove for the wax board with a force that made it a question of pure grip. There was a moment — I will record it honestly — in which I was genuinely uncertain whether the wax board and I were going to remain in the same location, and in which the southern passage, conveniently behind him, represented an exit I could not reach. He was not large in the way that is merely impressive. He was large in the way that forecloses argument.

What happened next I can only partially account for, because events arrived faster than a scribe is accustomed to processing.

Demetrios was the first through the eastern colonnade. He had been instructed to remain in the shadow until the signal, and he had obeyed for precisely as long as it took the broad young man's hand to close on my arm — which is to say he had obeyed right up until the instant when his year of quiet fury find a target it could justify. He crossed the courtyard in four strides with the absolute authority of a man whose patience had been an imposition from the beginning, and what he did when he arrived was not a blow but something more decisive: he took the broad young man's wrist in both hands — the particular grip of someone who has spent fifty years on land — and applied to it a leverage that the broad young man, for all his advantages, found it simpler to yield to than resist. The wax board remained in my possession. My arm was released with an abruptness that suggested the negotiation had been brief.

The broad young man turned to re-engage, and it was at this point that Sophranos's guards came through the southern passage in sufficient number to render the question academic.

From the eastern colonnade, Kleitos followed his father. And then Euthymios, his daughter's hand at his arm. And Timon the surveyor, grey beard catching the moonlight, with the expression of a man grateful to be on the correct side of an accounting. They spread into the courtyard and Lykides stood among the witnesses and understood, visibly, that every exit from the room had been addressed in advance.

But first, for the two or three breaths before Demetrios crossed the courtyard, I had seen something in Lykides's face at the word Precisely that I want to set down accurately. It was not fear and it was not shock. It was recognition — the precise, cold recognition of a man who understands that the mechanism has closed, that the decade of planning and the careful patience and the managed distances have all been rendered into evidence by a single corrected fact about which end of a slope retains water in summer. He had known the southern basin the way a man knows the face of something he intends to own. He had been unable to hear it mislocated and say nothing. The vanity of the complete schemer — Holkos had named it in the dark street — had been more powerful than a decade's discipline, and for one unguarded moment in that colonnaded court it had shown itself plainly.

Then the guards arrived, and his face was his own again.

"The prior claim will be set aside," Holkos said. "The boundary stones will be restored to the positions of the original partition. The case between the two families resolves without prejudice to either." He paused. "And you will answer before the council for conspiracy to defraud two households and corrupt the city's archive." The same absence of drama. The same quality of settled fact. "When Sophranos asks you to speak, speak accurately. The testimony available to him is considerable. Kratinos has already shown you what accurate testimony from a frightened instrument looks like."

Lykides was taken that night. He went with the specific dignity of a man for whom the manner of going is the last remaining thing that is his to control.

VI.

On the walk back through the dark city, I asked Holkos about the water collection.

"There is a collection basin," he said. "I observed it when we walked the boundary that afternoon. It is at the southern end of the upper slope, as he said. You noted it yourself and recorded it as agriculturally significant. I described it as being at the northern end, deliberately, to see whether he would correct me."

"You gave him a wrong fact."

"I gave him an invitation. A man who has merely acquired adjacent properties over the years has no reason to know the precise hydrology of land he does not yet own. A man who has surveyed and planned a consolidated estate for a decade knows it to the inch. He could not hear me describe it incorrectly and say nothing. The scheme was his life's work." He walked for a moment. "The correction was the confession. Not what he said about the families, not what he said about the acquisition — those were deniable. But knowing which end the water collected at, and being unable to prevent himself from saying so: that is the knowledge of a man who has planned the irrigation of a field he has not yet stolen."

I considered this. "And if he had simply said nothing? Let the error stand?"

"Then I would have found another way." He said this without concern. "But he would not say nothing. That was the thing I was most certain of, of all the uncertain things tonight."

I pressed him further, because the walk was long and I had questions that had been accumulating since the archive. "When did you know it was Lykides? Not suspect — know."

"When I saw the three Brauronian registrations arranged on the shelf. The intervals were too regular. Nine years of patience is not a response to opportunity; it is the execution of a plan. A man who files three adjacent properties over nine years at precise intervals is a man who knows what the fourth parcel will be before he has acquired any of them." He paused. "I knew the motive before I knew the name. The name was in the archive. The motive was in the stones."

"The stones told you the motive?"

"The stones told me the scheme was designed for a specific outcome, not improvised for a specific gain. A man who wants to steal a strip of land moves the boundary stones once. A man who moved them twice — first to establish a false baseline, then to frame the neighbour — needed both families to be destroyed, not merely one to be disadvantaged. That degree of thoroughness implies a greater objective. The moment I saw both sets of stones had been moved, I began looking for whoever needed both families simultaneously ruined." He glanced at me. "The archive told me who. The stones told me why."

We walked for a moment in the quiet of the cooling city.

"You said tonight was the thing you were most certain of," I said. "What were you least certain of?"

He was quiet for longer than usual. "Whether Kratinos would reach Sophranos before Lykides reached Kratinos." A pause. "And whether Demetrios would stay in the shadow of the eastern pillar."

"He did not."

"No," Holkos said. "He did not." And there was something in his voice — not quite approval and not quite amusement — that I have turned over many times since and have never been able to name precisely.

VII.

The proceedings before Sophranos the following morning occupied less time than I had anticipated. Kratinos's account, given the previous evening, had structured the evidence with the precision of a man who has decided that accuracy is now his best remaining instrument. The archive tablets — produced from Sophranos's personal custody, which caused Lykides's careful-eyed companion to go very still when they appeared — required no interpretation beyond what I provided from the wax board and Holkos confirmed from memory. Timon gave his account of the survey without evasion. The witnesses from the colonnaded court were more than sufficient.

Sophranos found for the families before the sun had cleared the eastern hills.

Lykides was assessed a penalty that fell, in my opinion, somewhat short of the full weight of what he had done. Holkos, when I said this, observed that a penalty insufficient to satisfy a scribe is often sufficient to ruin a property merchant, and that what appeared inadequate to justice frequently represented the outer limit of what the evidence would support in formal proceeding. He was not wrong. Lykides sold two of his three properties within a year. The third he retained, but it bordered nothing he had gained, and the water collection at the southern end of the upper slope was not his to manage.

The boundary stones were re-set within a fortnight. Timon the surveyor oversaw the correction himself, which seemed to me both just and slightly uncomfortable for him, though he performed the work without complaint.

In the official record — which is not this record — the case of Demetrios against Euthymios is listed as an instance of accidental marker displacement resolved by survey correction. Lykides appears in a separate proceeding, cross-referenced in a notation that requires some knowledge of the archive to locate. The connection between the two matters is not stated. It is legible, if you know what to read.

At the festival that autumn I observed Demetrios and Euthymios standing within easy speaking distance of each other. Kleitos was talking to Phila. I noted this and decided not to speculate further, as it was not within the scope of my record.

VIII.

Whether what Holkos accomplished in the case of the twice-moved stones was, in the precise sense, just — he was forced, in the end, to operate within the limits of what Sophranos's proceeding would support, and the full architecture of a decade's manoeuvring was only partially visible in the formal account — I have considered many times and cannot fully resolve.

I put it to him directly, once.

He looked at me for a moment with an expression that was not quite amusement and not quite impatience.

"The stones are where they belong," he said. "The families are not ruined. The man who built the scheme spent his evening in a court full of witnesses correcting my geography, which is a form of exposure that a legal penalty does not fully replicate." He paused. "The man who falsified the documents gave testimony that cost him more than chains would have. And whatever interest required both families destroyed has been frustrated once, at cost to itself, and knows now that the boundary records of this city are being observed." He paused again. "Whether that is justice, Philon, I cannot say. But it is an improvement on what the morning contained when I arrived."

He was already reaching for the next document on the bench.
