PUBLIUS HOLCUS

~360 BC — Carthage, Africa

Publius Holcus crossed the sea to Carthage at a time when the two great powers of the western Mediterranean were still, just barely, at peace — trading with each other, watching each other, building the mutual knowledge of strengths and weaknesses that would eventually produce, over three catastrophic wars, the destruction of one of them. He went as a merchant’s representative, stayed as a factor of some consequence, and spent fifteen years in the greatest Phoenician city in the world, the city that the Tyrian traders his ancestors had served had founded from nothing five centuries before. He moved through its harbours where rope creaked under load and timber was tested by the weight of men walking its length, listening to the sound of cargo being shifted as much as to the words spoken in negotiation.

He was a man who recognised the family connection without being able to name it — felt, in the streets of Carthage, a resonance he could not account for, a sense that the Punic merchant culture of the city was not entirely foreign to whatever he was. He learned the Punic language, navigating the phrasing of contracts written on wax tablets whose inscriptions carried both legal and commercial weight, and he learned to attend to the placement of seals and the sequence in which goods passed from one hand to another. He built a network of relationships on both sides of the Mediterranean that made him, for two decades, one of the better-informed private individuals in the ancient world. He returned to Rome before the first war made such movement impossible, bringing with him a knowledge of Carthage that certain consuls found invaluable and paid for accordingly.

His record is preserved by Diodoros of Rhodes, a Greek clerk attached to a Carthaginian merchant house, who copied accounts and correspondence for the household that employed him. Diodoros kept his own notes in Greek, describing not only transactions but the questions asked by Publius Holcus when he was brought in to examine a matter that others could not resolve. Diodoros notes that Holcus’s questions often arrived before the witnesses understood what was being examined, and that the answers, once given, contained more than the speaker intended.

The Account Of Gaius Diodoros Of Rhodes

Set down in his private journal, in the Greek tongue, in the archonship of Nikostratos at Athens, being the year that the Romans call the consulship of Gaius Marcius and Gnaeus Manlius.

The harbour of Carthage in the month of Elul smells of salted fish, of cedar resin, and of the particular kind of rot that wood acquires when it has been wet and dried a thousand times and is wet again. The oil lamps that burn on the quayside through the night use oil pressed from olives grown on the estates of the suffetes, and they burn with a thick, yellowish light that makes the faces of men look like masks of bone. I had lived in this city for six years before the morning I am about to describe, and I had grown accustomed to the smell and to the light and to the particular quality of silence that falls over the harbour district in the hours before the first merchants arrive — a silence that is not peace but rather the held breath of a city that never entirely sleeps.

It was in that silence, on the fourth day of the month, that the body of Marcus Aemilius Strabo was found.

He had been a factor for the house of Quinctius, one of perhaps a dozen Romans then resident in Carthage as commercial representatives, men who navigated the difficult water between Roman interest and Punic custom with varying degrees of competence. Strabo was neither the best of them nor the worst. He was a careful man, deliberate in his speech, who had been in the city long enough to know which gifts to give and in which order. I had seen him at the harbour many times. He was not a man who invited friendship, but he was a man who inspired a certain confidence — the confidence of regular behaviour, of predictable habits, of a man who arrived where he said he would arrive and departed when he said he would depart.

He was found at his writing table with a stylus still in his right hand and a wax tablet before him, and someone had put a knife between his ribs with considerable expertise.

The officer of the mhzm — the port magistrate's guard — who came to examine the scene was a Libyan-Phoenician named Adherbal, a man of middle height with a precisely trimmed beard and the expression of someone who has seen every category of human misfortune and filed each one in its proper place. He was competent. I wish to record that clearly, because what follows might seem to cast doubt on his competence, and it does not. He was competent within the boundaries of his office, which is a different thing from being capable of what was required.

Adherbal observed the body. He observed the knife, which had been left in the wound — an unusual detail, but not a detail he paused upon. He observed the writing table and the wax tablet, which was inscribed in Punic. He could read it perfectly well. He asked the first logical question: who had cause to harm Marcus Aemilius Strabo? He was given the first logical answer: Strabo had recently disputed a delivery contract with the house of Hanno the Younger, and the dispute had become heated. Adherbal wrote this down. He prepared to send men to find Hanno the Younger.

It was at this point that I suggested — with the extreme delicacy required when a Greek clerk suggests anything to an officer of the port magistrate — that perhaps Publius Holcus might be consulted before conclusions were reached.

Adherbal gave me the look that officials give to those who imply that official methods require supplementation. But he had dealt with Holcus before. He sent a messenger to the house near the Byrsa hill where Holcus maintained his quarters.

Publius Holcus arrived as the morning light was first reaching the lower streets — that grey, gradual light that in Carthage always seems to arrive with reluctance, as though the city discourages it. He was a lean man, not tall, with eyes the colour of weathered timber and a habit of absolute stillness that could, without warning, become absolute motion. He had not, I think, been asleep when the messenger arrived. I had known him for four years and I had never been entirely certain that he slept in any meaningful sense, or whether what he did in the intervals between activity was something more efficient than sleep and less generous than rest.

He wore a plain linen tunic and had a travelling cloak over his arm, which he did not put on despite the morning chill. He crossed the room where Strabo's body still lay and stood for a long moment in the doorway before entering, looking at everything without appearing to focus on anything.

Then he went to the body.

He examined the wound without touching it, tilting his head to one side. He examined the hand that held the stylus — looked at the angle of the fingers, the particular tension in the wrist. He straightened and went to the writing table.

"The tablet," he said to Adherbal. "You have read it."

"It is a record of payment," Adherbal said. "The dead man wrote down the name of a creditor and the amounts owed. It appears to be a financial accounting."

"It appears," said Holcus, "to be a great many things. Diodoros."

I came forward. It was the function he had assigned me, without ever formally assigning it, over the course of four years: the fixed point that his explanations required, the mind against which his reasoning was sharpened. He needed someone to be wrong in front of, so that being right could be fully performed.

"Read it aloud," he said. "Translate as you go."

I read. The tablet recorded payments — twelve of them, spanning two years — made by Strabo to a name I will not yet set down. The amounts were not large by the standards of international trade, but they were substantial for a factor of middling consequence. The final entry was different from the others. It was not a record of payment made. It was, written in a slightly different pressure of stylus — I noticed this only because Holcus's expression shifted — a statement of refusal.

I will pay no more. If you come again I will speak to the mhzm.

"He wrote his murderer's name on the tablet," I said.

"He wrote considerably more than that," said Holcus. He turned to Adherbal. "The knife."

Adherbal gestured to the weapon still in the body.

"It was left deliberately," Holcus said. "Consider what that means. A man who kills in sudden anger takes the knife. He doesn't think to take it, but he takes it — it is the object of his violence and he removes it from the scene as he removes himself. A man who plans a killing carefully leaves no weapon, because a weapon can be traced to a maker and thence to a purchaser. But this knife was left deliberately in a third configuration — left to be found. Why?"

I offered the obvious answer, because it was my function to offer the obvious answer. "To suggest the killing was the act of someone unconcerned with consequences. A warning, perhaps. The signature of someone confident in their impunity."

Holcus moved to the lamp on the writing table. He lowered himself to its level and looked at its flame.

"This lamp was lit when Strabo died," he said. "It is now nearly out. It was a fresh lamp when it was lit, or close to fresh — the reservoir is nearly empty, but the wick shows no sign of having burned down. The oil was consumed in one long burning. The merchant Strabo was alive and working at this table last night, well into the darkness."

He moved to the window. The shutter was open.

"The street below shows last night's rain in the gutter. But there is no mud on the floor inside the window, and the shutter's inner face is dry. The killer did not enter through the window."

"The door, then," said Adherbal.

"The door. Strabo admitted his visitor voluntarily, at night, after composing the final entry on the tablet — the refusal. He had written to his blackmailer to say he would pay no more and had invited him here to make that declaration in person. A brave act, or a foolish one. Perhaps he believed that naming the man in writing gave him protection."

Adherbal was looking at the tablet. "He named Mago son of Bodmilkart. I know the name. He is a shipchandler. A man of some position."

"A man who has been extracting payments from a Roman factor for two years," Holcus said, "using knowledge he acquired about Strabo through the ordinary conduct of harbour business. Strabo had made some error — a falsified delivery record, most likely, or a declared cargo that did not match the actual cargo. Common practice. Mago found it and used it." He paused. "The knife was left as a declaration of impunity. Mago believes that a Libyan-Phoenician officer will not pursue a man of position on behalf of a dead Roman. He is, in this belief, statistically well-founded."

The silence in the room was of a particular quality.

"And the tablet?" I asked.

Holcus looked at it for a moment. "Strabo was not a fool. He wrote the tablet as his final proof — more than proof, as his act of control in a situation that had taken control from him for two years. He wrote in Punic because he had learned, in fifteen years of harbour commerce, that a document in the language of the jurisdiction has more weight than a document in the language of the injured party." He straightened. "He knew, even as he prepared to confront his blackmailer, that the confrontation might end as it did. He left the tablet for whoever could read it."

He looked at me then, with the expression that I had come to understand as close as he came to the acknowledgment of something beyond calculation.

"He left it for us."

What followed was the business of the mhzm and not the business of this account, except in its broad lines.

Adherbal, to his credit, pursued the matter. He was aided by the fact that the knife's handle bore a pattern of inlaid bone work that was, when shown to the craftsmen of the harbour district, recognised as the work of a particular workshop that sold almost exclusively to three families of merchants — the house of Mago son of Bodmilkart among them. He was further aided by the testimony of Strabo's Libyan doorkeeper, who had heard voices after his master admitted a visitor and who, when shown the face of Mago son of Bodmilkart in the market two days later, identified him without hesitation or prompting.

Mago was brought before the rb mhzm, the harbour chief. He was a man of position, and he was tried with all the formality that position required, and he was convicted, and the sentence was of the kind that Carthage reserved for those who had betrayed the commerce on which the city's life depended — for what is blackmail but a corruption of the contract, the sacred instrument of trade?

The records of the rb mhzm credit the investigation to the port magistrate's office and to the diligence of officer Adherbal, which is entirely appropriate and entirely correct, in the way that official records are correct.

In my private journal I record differently.

I walked back with Holcus through the harbour district as the morning reached its full strength and the smell of the sea salt reasserted itself over the smell of oil and rot. The rope-sellers were opening their stalls. A boy was testing the tension of a mooring line by pressing his full weight against it, measuring what it would hold.

I asked Holcus whether Strabo had known, when he wrote the final entry on the tablet and invited Mago to come, that he was likely placing himself in danger.

Holcus did not answer immediately. He stopped to examine a ship's timber that had been set out for inspection — ran his hand along the grain, reading the wood the way he read everything.

"He knew," he said finally.

"Then it was courage," I said.

"It was a form of exhaustion," Holcus said. "Two years is long enough to tire even a careful man. He had calculated, I think, that the continuation of the payments was a kind of slow death, and the confrontation was a risk of fast death. He preferred the clarity of the risk." He moved away from the timber. "He was correct. The risk resolved with notable speed."

"He could not have anticipated the tablet would be read by someone capable of reading it."

"No," said Holcus. "That was fortune. Though a man who writes in Punic rather than Latin for a document he intends to be found by authorities in a Punic city is not entirely relying on fortune." He paused. "He was more sophisticated than I had given him credit for, based on his fifteen years in this city. I had categorised him as competent. He was, in the end, rather more than that."

He said nothing else. We parted at the corner where the street to the Byrsa rises from the harbour district, and I returned to my copying desk and my letters and the ordinary commerce of the house of Mago son of Bodmilkart, which continued, as commerce does, without reference to what had occurred.

I have recorded this case in my Greek journal because it will not be recorded correctly anywhere else. The official tablet of the rb mhzm will say what it will say. The house of Quinctius will receive a letter informing them of the fate of their factor and will send another man. The harbour will continue to smell of rope and resin and the particular kind of rot that comes from wood that has been wet and dried a thousand times.

But someone should know what the wax said, and who knew how to read it.

Gaius Diodoros of Rhodes, in the city of Carthage, this being the fifteenth year of my residence here, set this down for his own record, not for the reading of any magistrate.
