PUBLIUS HOLCUS

~360 BC — Carthage, Africa

The Account Of Diodoros Of Rhodes

Set down in his private journal, in the Greek tongue, in the archonship of Nikostratos at Athens, being the year that the Romans call the consulship of Gaius Marcius and Gnaeus Manlius.

The body of Marcus Aemilius Strabo was found at his writing table with a stylus still in his right hand and a knife between his ribs, and it was the knife that told Publius Holcus everything he needed to know before he had spoken a single word.

I am setting this down as it occurred, because it will not be set down correctly anywhere else.

I.

The harbour of Carthage in the month of Elul smells of salted fish, of cedar resin, and of the particular kind of rot that wood acquires when it has been wet and dried a thousand times and is wet again. The oil lamps that burn on the quayside through the night use oil pressed from the estates of the suffetes, and they burn with a thick, yellowish light that makes the faces of men look like masks of bone. I had lived in this city for nine years — arrived as a young clerk attached to the household of a Carthaginian merchant, stayed as a copying hand of some small usefulness, and grown, over those years, into something that was neither citizen nor stranger but the particular species of useful foreigner that commercial cities quietly require. I had grown accustomed to the smell and to the light and to the quality of silence that falls over the harbour district in the hours before the first merchants arrive. A silence that is not peace but the held breath of a city that never entirely sleeps.

It was in that silence, on the fourth day of the month, that the body was found.

Strabo had been a factor for the house of Quinctius — one of perhaps a dozen Romans then resident in Carthage as commercial representatives, men who navigated the difficult water between Roman interest and Punic custom with varying degrees of competence. He was neither the best of them nor the worst. A careful man, deliberate in speech, who had been in the city long enough to know which gifts to give and in which order. I had seen him at the harbour many times. He was not a man who invited friendship, but he was a man who inspired confidence — the confidence of regular behaviour, of predictable habits.

He had been found by his own Libyan doorkeeper, Hanno — a young man who slept in the anteroom and who had woken before first light to find the outer door unlatched and his master cold.

II.

The officer of the mhzm who came to examine the scene was a Libyan-Phoenician named Adherbal — a man of middle height, a precisely trimmed beard, and the expression of someone who has seen every category of human misfortune and filed each one in its proper place. He was competent within the boundaries of his office. He was also, on this morning, a man under considerable pressure, though he had not yet confided this to anyone in the room.

I learned of the pressure only when I arrived — summoned by a messenger from the household, for reasons I will explain — and found Adherbal re-reading a letter on Syrian papyrus that he then folded and placed inside his tunic with the careful deliberateness of a man concealing something he cannot afford to lose. He told me, briefly, that a letter from the Roman consul's representative had arrived the previous evening, inquiring after the welfare of Roman factors in Carthage. The timing, he said, was uncomfortable. I agreed that it was.

This, I understood, was why he had sent for me. And why he would, within the hour, send for Publius Holcus.

I should describe Holcus as he appeared that morning, since this account is also, in part, a record of the man himself. He was lean, not tall, with eyes the colour of weathered timber. His habit was absolute stillness that could, without warning, become absolute motion. He had crossed the sea from Rome to Carthage fifteen years before as a merchant's representative, had stayed as a factor of some consequence, and had built — in those fifteen years of harbour commerce — a network of knowledge on both sides of the Mediterranean that made him one of the better-informed private individuals in the ancient world. He had learned Punic in its commercial and legal registers. He had learned to attend to the placement of seals and the sequence in which goods passed from one hand to another. Certain consuls had found his knowledge of Carthage invaluable, and there were men in the Senate who knew his name while taking care that their knowledge of it did not appear in any record that might be consulted. I had known him for four years. I had never been entirely certain that he slept in any meaningful sense — whether what he did in the intervals between activity was something more efficient than sleep and less generous than rest.

He arrived as the morning light was first reaching the lower streets — that grey, gradual light that in Carthage always seems to arrive with reluctance. He wore a plain linen tunic and had a travelling cloak over his arm, which he did not put on despite the morning chill, as though discomfort were a form of information he was still collecting. He crossed the threshold of Strabo's quarters and stood for a long moment in the doorway before entering, looking at everything without appearing to focus on anything.

Then he went to the body.

III.

He examined the wound without touching it, tilting his head to one side. Then — and this is the detail I have turned over many times since — he looked not at the wound but at the knife. He did not touch it. He lowered himself until his eyes were level with the handle and he looked at it for a long time, the way a scholar looks at an inscription he is not yet willing to read aloud.

The handle was wrapped in leather at the grip, but the bolster — the swelling of metal where blade meets handle — was inlaid with a pattern in bone: a running chevron, repeated six times around the circumference, each repetition precisely spaced.

"You are recording this," Holcus said to me. It was not a question.

I wrote it down. I write down everything in Holcus's presence, because I have learned, over four years, that the things he names are the things that matter, and that the sequence in which he names them is itself a form of argument.

He examined the hand that held the stylus — looked at the angle of the fingers, the particular tension in the wrist, as a physician reads the degree of a man's struggle in the position his body assumes at its last moment. He straightened and went to the writing table.

"The tablet," he said to Adherbal. "You have read it."

"It is a record of payment," Adherbal said. "The dead man wrote down the name of a creditor and the amounts owed. It appears to be a financial accounting."

"It appears," said Holcus, "to be a great many things. Diodoros."

I came forward. This was the function he had assigned me without ever formally assigning it: the fixed point that his explanations required, the mind against which his reasoning was sharpened. He needed someone to be wrong in front of, so that being right could be fully performed.

"Read it aloud," he said. "Translate as you go."

I read. The tablet recorded payments — twelve of them, spanning two years — made by Strabo to a name I will not yet set down. The amounts were not large by the standards of international trade, but they were substantial for a factor of middling consequence.

Then I reached the final entry. And here — I must record this accurately — I was the one who paused. The final entry was inscribed at a slightly different pressure of stylus: heavier at the beginning of each stroke, lifting quickly at the end, as a man writes when he is pressing down harder than habit requires, when emotion is moving the hand.

"This last line," I said. "The pressure is different. He wrote this last entry differently from the others."

Holcus looked at me with an expression I had not often seen from him — something close to satisfaction, though not quite that. "Go on," he said.

I read the final entry aloud.

I will pay no more. If you come again I will speak to the mhzm.

The silence in the room was of a particular quality.

I looked at the tablet and at the twelve entries above that final line, and I made the error that any reasoning man in my position would have made. In my haste, I misread the Punic ligature for Baalhamon. Hence, I misinterpreted the name recorded against each payment as that of the Punic maritime broker I knew by reputation — Baalhamon of the upper district, a man who had dealings with half the factors in the harbour, including Strabo, and whose commercial interests were extensive enough that a long record of payments from a Roman factor would have attracted no attention at the harbour stalls. He was, in short, precisely the kind of man through whose hands blackmail money could pass without leaving a stain on anything that showed.

"The name on the tablet," I said to Holcus. "Baalhamon. He is a broker. His contacts with every Roman factor in the port are a matter of common knowledge. If he had discovered an irregularity in Strabo's declared cargo—"

"The knife," said Holcus, without looking up from the table.

"I beg your pardon?"

"Before you construct a theory, look at the knife."

I looked at it. I saw what I had already seen: a fine blade, a wrapped grip, the bone inlay at the bolster. I was not certain what I was meant to be seeing beyond that.

"Baalhamon," Holcus said, "is a broker. He works through instruments. He does not carry personal blades inlaid with a craftsman's distinctive pattern to the house of a man he intends to kill. A broker who engages in extortion sends men. He does not come himself, bearing an object that is in every material particular a signature." He picked up a stylus from the table and set it down again, a gesture of mild impatience I had learned to recognise. "The tablet names a recipient of payment. The knife names a killer. They are not required to be the same man. Though in this case," he added, "I believe they are."

I said nothing. He was right, and I had the professional honesty to recognise it.

"He wrote considerably more than a name," said Holcus. "He wrote a portrait."

IV.

He moved to the lamp on the writing table and raised his eyes to the ceiling above it.

"This lamp was lit when Strabo died," he said. "It burned through the night. Look at the ceiling above the flame."

I looked. The plaster above the lamp was darkened in a broad, even cone — a uniform deposit of soot that spread from directly above the wick outward, unbroken by any variation in flame height or position.

"One long, steady burning," Holcus said. "From a wick that was trimmed and fresh, and an oil reservoir that was full when lit. Strabo was alive and working at this table well into the darkness — the soot tells us the flame was constant, not guttering. He was not interrupted in the first hours."

He moved to the window. The shutter was open.

"The street below shows last night's rain in the gutter. But there is no mud on the floor inside the window, and the shutter's inner face is dry. The killer did not enter through the window."

"The door, then," said Adherbal.

"The door. Strabo admitted his visitor voluntarily, at night, after composing the final entry on the tablet. He had written to his blackmailer to say he would pay no more — and had invited him here to make that declaration in person." He paused. "A brave act, or a reckless one. He believed, I think, that naming the man in writing gave him some protection. He had miscalculated the degree to which a man like his blackmailer regards written evidence as a problem to be managed rather than a constraint to be respected."

Adherbal was looking at the tablet. "The name on the tablet is Mago son of Bodmilkart. I know the name. A shipchandler. A man of some position in the harbour district."

Holcus did not look at Adherbal. He was looking at the doorway through which the killer would have entered.

"Mago has been extracting payments from a Roman factor for two years," Holcus said, "using knowledge he acquired about Strabo through the ordinary conduct of harbour business. Strabo had made some error — a falsified delivery record, most likely, or a declared cargo that did not match the actual cargo. Common practice. Mago found it and used it. He had, I think, used this method before, though not always with this result. The fluency of the tablet entries — the regularity of spacing, the practiced ordinariness of the amounts — speaks to a man who had conducted this kind of arrangement before and found it reliable."

He turned. "Strabo's doorkeeper — where is he?"

Adherbal blinked. "The Libyan boy? In the anteroom. He found the body."

"Bring him."

The doorkeeper, Hanno, was perhaps eighteen years of age, with the precise, careful stillness of a young man who has been trained to be invisible and is now terrified that visibility is required. Holcus did not address him immediately. He looked at the boy's sandals, then at his face, then — and this was characteristic of him — at his hands, as though a man's hands in the morning hours carry the record of the previous night in ways the face has learned to conceal.

"You heard voices last night," Holcus said. It was not a question.

Hanno looked at Adherbal, then back at Holcus. "I heard my master speak," he said, in accented Punic. "And one other voice. Low. A man's voice."

"Did you see the visitor?"

A hesitation. "The lamp in the anteroom had gone out. I heard the door, and I heard the voices, but when I looked through the curtain I saw only —" He stopped.

"The back of a man," Holcus said quietly.

"Yes."

"Describe what you saw."

Hanno described: a heavyset man, broad across the shoulders, wearing a dark cloak of good quality. A merchant's cloak — not a seaman's. He had noticed the hem because the lamp from Strabo's room had thrown light across the floor, and the hem of the cloak had a particular border-work. He had noticed it because he had seen such a cloak before, at the harbour, on a man who had come to the house twice in the past months.

Holcus's eyes moved to me. I recorded every word.

"You would know the man again," Holcus said.

"Yes," said Hanno. And then, with the directness of a young man who has spent a night with a dead body and is beyond the management of social caution: "I have known him since the first time he came. I knew him by his voice last night. I did not speak because I did not know what had happened, and then in the morning I found —" He stopped. Swallowed. "I knew him. I know who he is."

The room was very quiet.

"You will be asked to say so," Holcus said, "in circumstances that will protect you. I will arrange it." He looked at Adherbal. "Mago son of Bodmilkart. The knife handle. I want to know which workshop in this city produces that chevron pattern. Before the market opens. Before word of Strabo's death reaches the stalls."

Adherbal frowned. "What of it?"

"Ask quietly," said Holcus. "Mago believes the morning is going well for him."

V.

I want to record what followed, because it is the part that is most commonly omitted in accounts of how such matters resolve, and its omission makes the resolution appear more sudden and more certain than it was. The investigation is not a thought. It is a movement through a city, and the city does not always cooperate.

Holcus did not send Adherbal's men to find the workshop. He went himself, and he took me with him, and I was grateful for it, because I have since understood that this was the part that mattered most — not the confrontation in the courtyard, which resolved as he had designed it, but the hour before it, in which the design was assembled from the raw material of the harbour district at full morning heat.

The lower harbour in the second hour smells of pitch and wet rope and the particular sharpness of metal that has been dipped in seawater. The ivory-workers and bone-carvers cluster near the shipwright's quarter, because the materials they work arrive in the same holds as the timber, and proximity to the wharves reduces what they pay the porters. Holcus moved through the district as he moved through everything — without urgency, and therefore faster than a man in a hurry, because men in a hurry announce themselves and find doors closed before they reach them.

The first carver we spoke to was a small Libyan with rings of blue ink around both wrists, who worked at a low bench outside his workshop and did not look up from his awl when we stopped before him. He knew the chevron pattern. He had not made it himself, he said, but he knew who had. His manner in saying so was the manner of a man performing a small reluctant service for which he expected and would receive nothing — one of the ten thousand minor transactions of which a harbour district's morning is composed.

He directed us to a courtyard two streets back from the quay, where a Punic craftsman named Ithobaal worked bone and shell and occasionally ivory when he could afford the import cost. Ithobaal was a precise, meticulous man of perhaps forty, with the permanently focused gaze of someone who has spent a career looking at objects smaller than his thumbnail. The chevron pattern, he confirmed, was his — a pattern he had developed and did not share with other workshops, because distinctiveness in a competitive quarter is a form of commercial currency. He had used it on eight pieces in the past three years. Seven had been commissioned directly. One — a knife bolster of the type used by harbour merchants, from good Iberian bronze with a bone inlay at the joint — had been purchased off the bench by a man who came in with no prior arrangement.

He described the purchaser without being asked. This is the quality of a craftsman who takes pride in his work: he remembers who carries it into the world.

The description was Mago son of Bodmilkart. The date of purchase was fourteen months ago. The price had been paid in Punic silver, promptly, without negotiation, which Ithobaal mentioned with the satisfied brevity of a man for whom prompt, uncontested payment is an event worth recording.

I noted it all. Holcus thanked Ithobaal with the particular brevity that in him served as the highest form of acknowledgment. We stepped back into the lane, and I looked at what I had written, and then at Holcus.

"He bought the knife fourteen months ago," I said.

"Yes."

"Mago has been collecting payments for two years. The knife was purchased fourteen months into that arrangement." I paused. "He had decided, even then, that Strabo would not pay forever. He had made his calculation fourteen months ago and had been waiting for the account to mature."

Holcus said nothing. He was looking at the end of the lane, where the street opened again onto the quayside.

"That is cold," I said.

"That," said Holcus, "is commerce." He began walking. "Now we arrange the meeting."

VI.

What was required was a trap, and the trap required three elements that did not yet exist in combination: a location that could be sealed, a pretext that would draw Mago to it voluntarily, and evidence already secured in a place that Mago could not reach. The first and third were manageable. The second required what Holcus called the language of the transaction — the particular idiom of a man who appeared to be offering Mago the same kind of arrangement that Mago had been offering others.

He sent word to Mago through a mutual acquaintance — a Phoenician merchant of no particular allegiance who was owed a small favour — that a matter concerning the late Marcus Aemilius Strabo required private discussion before it reached the ears of the rb mhzm. The phrasing was careful: it implied that Holcus possessed information that might yet be managed, if approached directly and promptly. It was, I understood, the language Mago himself used — the language of a man offering a transaction in place of a catastrophe. To be approached in one's own idiom is to feel, briefly, that one retains command of the situation. This was the feeling Holcus wished Mago to carry to the meeting. A man who believes he controls a situation does not bring enough men.

The meeting was to take place in the afternoon, in the courtyard of a small warehouse near the military harbour, a place that Holcus used occasionally for conversations that required walls. Adherbal would be waiting in an inner room with Hanno. Three of the mhzm's men would be at the single entrance. The craftsman Ithobaal's testimony was already written on a tablet in Adherbal's possession, sealed and copied. These arrangements Holcus had made with the efficiency of a man for whom planning is a kind of pleasure — not the pleasure of control, but the pleasure of a mechanism assembled correctly.

"You will translate," he said.

"Mago speaks Latin," I said. "He has dealt with Roman factors for twenty years."

"He speaks it when he chooses to," said Holcus. "He will choose not to, this afternoon."

I went with him. I will record, because accuracy requires it, that I was not easy. I had been present at the body. I had walked the harbour with Holcus through the morning. I understood, by now, what Mago son of Bodmilkart was, and the Numidian he was likely to bring with him was a figure I had imagined with increasing specificity as the afternoon approached. A Greek copying-hand's instinct for the value of correct phrasing is not the same as a capacity for physical confrontation.

Holcus appeared unconcerned. He appeared, in fact, as he always appeared in the moments immediately before something decisive — absolutely still, as though the situation had already resolved and he was merely waiting for the world to recognise the fact.

VII.

Mago arrived on time, which told me something. A man who is frightened arrives early; a man who is guilty and clever arrives late, to read the room before he enters it. Mago arrived precisely when agreed, which meant he believed he was in control of what was about to happen.

He entered the courtyard with one man beside him — a large Numidian who stationed himself against the wall with the patient immobility of a professional. Mago looked around the courtyard, identified Holcus, and crossed to him with a merchant's smile: warm at the surface, watchful underneath. He moved through the courtyard the way he moved through the harbour district — as a man moves through rooms he owns. I had always taken his ease for prosperity. I understood, that afternoon, that I had been reading the wrong quality.

"I understand," Mago said, in Punic, with a cordiality I found instantly cold, "that you have information relevant to the unfortunate business at the factor's residence."

"I do," said Holcus, in Punic. "As do you."

A pause. "I knew the man by commerce. His death is a loss to the harbour."

"He wrote your name on a wax tablet," Holcus said pleasantly. "Twelve payments across two years. The final entry is a refusal — written in a heavier hand, under some agitation, on the evening of his death."

The merchant's smile remained, but something shifted behind it. "A tablet can say many things. A name in a dead man's accounts is not —"

"The knife," Holcus said.

A silence.

"The knife that was left in Marcus Aemilius Strabo has a bone inlay at the bolster — a running chevron, six repetitions. There is one workshop in this city that produces that pattern. The craftsman Ithobaal of the lower harbour has a detailed memory. He remembers his buyers." A pause. "He remembers you."

Something moved in Mago's eyes — not fear, not yet, but the rapid recalculation of a man who has discovered that a room he believed empty contains furniture. He had built his position on the calculation that a Punic officer would not pursue a man of his consequence on behalf of a dead Roman. The calculation had been rational. It had not accounted for Holcus.

"I should also tell you," Holcus continued, in the same pleasant tone, "that Strabo's doorkeeper has a good memory for voices and a better one for faces, and that he is, at this moment, in the next room."

That was when Mago looked at the Numidian.

It was a brief look — the compression of an instruction into a glance, the kind of economy practised by men who have worked together long enough that language is an unnecessary interval between thought and action. The Numidian straightened from the wall.

I do not mind recording that I stepped back.

Holcus did not move at all. He spoke without raising his voice, without altering his position by so much as the angle of his head, and what he said was this:

"The tablet with Ithobaal's testimony and the tablet with the doorkeeper's record are not in this building. Officer Adherbal sealed them this morning and placed them with the rb mhzm's office. Your man can do what you are asking him to do. The record will remain."

The Numidian had taken three steps. He stopped.

Mago said nothing. The courtyard was very quiet. I could hear, from somewhere beyond the warehouse wall, the ordinary sounds of the harbour — the creak of timber, the distant cry of a gull, the indifferent business of a city that does not pause for individual catastrophes.

What followed in Mago's face then was not the collapse of a frightened man. It was something slower and more terrible to observe: the dismantling, piece by piece, of a calculation that had served him for years and had now, for the first time, reached its limit. He had spent his life in the harbour, in the commerce of leverage and advantage and the management of other men's vulnerabilities. He had encountered, now, a man who had taken every instrument in that commerce and turned it against him with a fluency he had not anticipated and could not, I think, entirely comprehend.

He looked at Holcus for a long moment with what was almost, I think, professional curiosity — the look of a shipchandler examining a vessel of unexpected construction.

"You are not mhzm," he said.

"No," said Holcus.

"Then what are you?"

Holcus considered the question in the way he considered questions that interested him — with his head tilted, as though listening for a resonance in the air between the words. "I am a man," he said finally, "who knew how to read the tablet."

Adherbal's men came in from the entrance.

VIII.

What followed was the business of the rb mhzm, and I will record it only in its broad lines.

Mago was tried with all the formality that a man of his position required. The tablet, the knife's provenance as established by the craftsman Ithobaal, and the doorkeeper's testimony — presented together, each substantiating the others — constituted, in the judgment of the harbour chief, sufficient weight. The sentence was of the kind that Carthage reserved for those who had betrayed the commerce on which the city's life depended, for what is blackmail but a corruption of the contract, the sacred instrument of trade?

The records of the rb mhzm credit the investigation to the port magistrate's office and to the diligence of officer Adherbal, which is entirely appropriate and entirely correct, in the way that official records are correct.

In my private journal I record differently.

IX.

I walked back with Holcus through the harbour district as the afternoon found its full heat and the smell of salt reasserted itself over the smell of oil and rope-tar. The timber merchants were setting out their stock along the quayside. A boy was testing the tension of a mooring line by pressing his full weight against it, measuring what it would hold.

Holcus stopped at a ship's timber that had been set out for inspection. He ran his hand along the grain the way he read everything — attentive to what the surface was telling him that the surface did not know it was telling him.

I asked him whether Strabo had known, when he wrote the final entry on the tablet and invited Mago to come, that he was placing himself in danger.

Holcus did not answer immediately.

"He knew," he said finally.

"Then it was courage," I said.

"It was a form of exhaustion," Holcus said. "Two years is long enough to tire even a careful man. He had calculated, I think, that the continuation of the payments was a kind of slow death, and the confrontation was a risk of fast death. He preferred the clarity of the risk." He moved away from the timber. "He was correct. The risk resolved with notable speed."

"He could not have anticipated that the tablet would be read by someone capable of reading it."

"No," said Holcus. "That was fortune. Though a man who writes in Punic rather than Latin, for a document he intends to be found by authorities in a Punic city, is not entirely relying on fortune." He paused. "He was more sophisticated than I had given him credit for. I had categorised him as competent. He was, in the end, rather more than that."

He said nothing else. We parted at the corner where the street to the Byrsa rises from the harbour district, and I returned to my copying desk and my letters and the ordinary commerce of the city, which continued, as commerce does, without reference to what had occurred.

X.

I have recorded this case in my Greek journal because it will not be recorded correctly anywhere else. The official tablet of the rb mhzm will say what it will say. The house of Quinctius will receive a letter informing them of the fate of their factor and will send another man. The harbour will continue to smell of rope and resin and the particular kind of rot that comes from wood that has been wet and dried a thousand times.

But someone should know what the wax said. And who knew how to read it. And what it cost a man named Ithobaal to remember a buyer, and what it cost a boy named Hanno to spend a night with a dead body and say, in a room full of strangers, I knew him by his voice.

And that Strabo, a careful man of middling consequence, had written his evidence in Punic in a Punic city, in a heavier hand than usual, knowing that the night might end as it did — and had left it on the table for whoever came after him. The official record will call this a matter resolved by the diligence of the port magistrate's office. I call it something that has no clean name in Greek or in Latin or in Punic, something that sits between courage and calculation and is perhaps the highest form of both.

He had, in his last hour, trusted the city that killed him to try his killer. And the city, on this occasion, did not fail him.

Diodoros of Rhodes, in the city of Carthage, this being the fifteenth year of my residence here, set this down for his own record, not for the reading of any magistrate.
