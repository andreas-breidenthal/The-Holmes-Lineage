LUCIUS HOLCUS SECUNDUS

~140 BC — Rome / Judea

Lucius Holcus Secundus inherited his father’s posting and his father’s fascination with Judea, and deepened both. He arrived in the years of the Hasmonean consolidation, when the descendants of the Maccabees were ruling a Judea that was nominally independent but practically within the Roman sphere, and he watched the internal tensions of that arrangement—the rivalry between Pharisees and Sadducees, the pressure of the Hellenising party, the persistent undercurrent of messianic expectation—with the attention of a man who understood that the most important things happening in any political situation are generally not the things that the principal actors believe they are doing.

He was a careful correspondent—his dispatches to Rome among the more lucid assessments of Judean politics in the second century—and a patient investigator of the kind of local fraud that colonial administration always generates: falsified tax records, disputed land boundaries, the systematic corruption of minor officials. He untangled these matters with a thoroughness that his superiors found valuable and the subjects of his investigations found alarming. He had his great-grandfather’s nose for the falsified account, translated into the bureaucratic medium of a provincial Roman administration.

These notes are preserved from the hand of Gaius Tertullius Varro, a junior clerk attached to Holcus Secundus. Varro kept a running log of proceedings, not as a formal scribe of state, but as one who feared the loss of detail. His account reflects what he saw, what he understood, and what he failed to grasp until after the matter was resolved.

The Account of Gaius Tertullius Varro

I have been a keeper of records for eleven years. I know the smell of a papyrus that has been scraped and rewritten—that faint ghost of old ink that the pumice cannot entirely remove—and I know the particular silence of a man who has prepared his lies in advance and is waiting with professional patience for the questions he expects to receive.

I did not know, when I first entered the service of Lucius Holcus Secundus, that I would spend the greater portion of my career watching him ask the questions no one expected, and watching men come undone entirely as a result.

This account I write for myself, and for whoever finds it after I am gone. The official record of the matter you will find nowhere, because Holcus filed it in a manner designed to ensure it would not be found for twenty or thirty years, by which time everyone involved will be dust, and the harm of the truth will have passed. He told me this with complete satisfaction, as though the delayed delivery of justice were itself a form of justice. I have concluded, after long association, that he was correct.

The city of Jerusalem in those years had a particular quality of compressed pressure, like a wine-skin filled beyond its reasonable capacity. The Hasmoneans sat on their throne—a throne that was also a high priesthood, which the Pharisees found offensive on constitutional grounds, and which the Sadducees found perfectly acceptable on grounds that were, to my observation, primarily financial. The Romans circled at a careful distance. The Parthians were a rumour on the eastern wind. And underneath all of it ran the deep current of expectation that I could never quite understand: a people who were perpetually awaiting something, though precisely what they awaited, and when, and from which direction, no two of them seemed to agree.

Holcus Secundus found this fascinating. He found everything about Judea fascinating, in the manner of a physician who is drawn to complicated cases not out of any desire to see suffering, but because suffering, properly examined, reveals the operating principles of the organism.

He was forty years old then, lean in the way of a man who forgets to eat when a problem occupies him, with hair already silver at the temples and eyes of a pale grey that I have never been able to describe to anyone's satisfaction—the best I have managed is the colour of the sky over the sea at Ostia on a winter morning, which is not a comparison that conveys warmth. He spoke Aramaic with an accuracy that alarmed the locals, who had expected barbarous mispronunciation and adjusted their conversations accordingly. He spoke Greek as though it were his mother tongue, which it nearly was—his family had been in the eastern provinces for three generations.

I was twenty-four, recently arrived from Rome, deeply proud of my Latin and my education, and entirely unprepared for the landscape, the heat, the smell of the city in summer—the dung fires and the cedar resin and beneath everything the iron-dust scent of dry stone that the hills exhaled from some reservoir deep inside themselves—or for Holcus Secundus.

He set me to copying correspondence for two weeks without explanation. On the fifteenth day he looked up from the tax register he was reading and said, without preamble: "You have been writing your copies very slowly."

I began to apologise.

"I am not criticising," he said. "I am asking why."

"I have been—" I hesitated. "I have been noting discrepancies. In the figures. There appear to be—"

"Seventeen irregularities in the Bethlehem district accounts, six in the records from the lower Kidron valley, and a systematic adjustment of the boundary assessments in a corridor of land running northeast from the city walls that cannot be accounted for by any legitimate surveying error because legitimate surveying errors do not consistently favour the same three landholders." He turned back to his register. "You have a good eye. I wondered how long it would take you to see it."

I stood with my mouth open for a moment.

"Sit down, Varro. You look like a fish."

The boundary question was not, in itself, unusual. Boundaries in Judea were contested with a fervour that I came to understand as partly theological—the land itself was considered to have moral weight, not merely economic value, and a falsified boundary stone was therefore not merely fraud but something closer to a spiritual crime. The Mosaic Law, as several people explained to me at lengths I found exhausting and Holcus found merely insufficient, had things to say about those who moved boundary markers. The word for it translated roughly as "cursed," which in Roman terms would correspond to something between damnatio memoriae and being struck by lightning, and which the Pharisees deployed with a frequency suggesting they considered it applicable to approximately a third of all land transactions currently in force.

The particular boundary that occupied us in those autumn weeks was a strip of terrain between the estate of a merchant named Ezra bar-Kohen and the holdings of a farming collective whose name I will render as the Assembly of the Sons of Shemaiah, because the full form runs to six more names and a tribal designation that I cannot reliably transcribe. The boundary had been formally adjudicated three years prior. The adjudication had been performed under the authority of Rabbi Eleazar ben-Mattitiah, one of the senior Pharisaic interpreters of the law in the city, a man of considerable reputation and—as became relevant—considerable debts of obligation to several of the more powerful families within the Hasmonean political structure.

The decision had gone against the Sons of Shemaiah. They had accepted it with the dignity of men who have been told they have no choice, which is not the same as accepting it as just.

None of this had reached us officially. What had reached us was a tax complaint. Specifically: a complaint filed by a man named Shlomo bar-Dov, a minor tax-collector of the lower city district, alleging that Ezra bar-Kohen had failed to report certain improvements to the northeastern portion of his estate and therefore owed additional assessment. The complaint was specific, documented, and—as Holcus observed when he read it for the third time on the morning it arrived—professionally constructed.

"This man bar-Dov," he said, setting the complaint on the table between us. The lamp oil was cedar-infused that morning; the smell of it had begun to mark time for me, a specific scent I associate now with the beginning of a case. "Tell me what you observe."

I had learned, by this point, not to say nothing, sir. I had learned to look.

"The handwriting is his," I said. "I've seen his other filings."

"Yes."

"The figures are—the figures are correct. I checked the assessment rolls."

"The figures are meticulously correct. What else?"

I looked at the complaint again. It was a single papyrus sheet, carefully written, without crossings-out or corrections. "He copied it," I said slowly. "This is a fair copy. There was a draft."

"He drafted a tax complaint." Holcus leaned back in his chair and looked at the ceiling, which was his posture for thinking. "A man files dozens of these each season. He does not draft them. He fills in a form. This complaint was written and rewritten until it said precisely what he intended and nothing more." He brought his gaze back down. "Which tells us?"

I thought. "He was being careful."

"He was being frightened. A man who drafts a routine tax complaint is afraid of the document he is creating. He wants every word to be right because he knows it will be examined—not by us, initially, but by someone else. Someone who gave him instructions." Holcus picked up the complaint and read from it: "the northeastern improvements, constructed without proper notification, within the zone of the disputed boundary corridor." He set it down. "He is not complaining about a tax irregularity. He is sending a message."

I looked at him. He looked at me with the faint expression of patience extended to a slow student.

"To whom?" I said.

"To the man who moved the boundary stone three years ago."

We did not go to Ezra bar-Kohen first. This was the approach I expected, and I said so, which was how Holcus came to explain to me the principle he called—in his own terminology—the Technique of the Second Story.

"The guilty party who is also a victim," he said, as we walked through the upper city in the late afternoon, through streets that smelled of bread-baking and the particular sharp green of fresh-cut hyssop from the market stalls, "is the most complicated creature in any investigation. He knows he has done wrong. He also knows he has been wronged. He will tell you nothing about either, because telling you about the second requires admitting to the first." He stopped at a corner to watch a cart navigate a narrow gate. "Bar-Dov, however, is a functionary. He has been given instructions. Instructions imply an instructor. We do not want the message—we want the channel."

Shlomo bar-Dov was a small man with the permanently harassed expression of a person who has been living above his anxieties for an extended period. He received us in his office—a single room in a building near the lower market that smelled of damp plaster and lamp-smoke—and immediately began explaining the complaint in the careful terms of a man who has rehearsed.

Holcus let him finish. Then he said, in Aramaic, in the mild conversational tone I had come to understand as his most dangerous register: "Tell me who brought you the draft."

The silence that followed lasted long enough that I counted my own heartbeats. Seven.

"I don't know what you—"

"The edges of the papyrus you filed show a consistent curve that indicates it was stored rolled for some time before you transcribed it. The draft existed before you wrote the fair copy. I am asking who brought you that draft."

"I prepared the complaint myself."

"You prepared the fair copy. You are a conscientious record-keeper—I have examined your other filings—and a conscientious record-keeper who prepares a draft keeps the draft. Your filing cabinet behind you contains tax documents going back four years. Where is the draft of this complaint?"

Bar-Dov looked at his filing cabinet as though it had betrayed him. Which, in a sense, it had—not by containing the draft, but by visibly not containing it.

Holcus waited.

"A man came to me," bar-Dov said, finally, in the tone of someone releasing a breath they have been holding for three years. "Three years ago. He showed me certain records. Concerning my own affairs." A pause. "Records I had preferred to consider private."

"And in exchange for those records remaining private, you agreed to file certain complaints when instructed, and to report certain things, and to be, in general, a reliable post for the transmission of messages."

"I am not a criminal."

"No," Holcus said, with what I recognised as genuine conviction. "You are a frightened man who made a bad agreement and has been paying for it ever since. What did the man look like?"

The description bar-Dov provided was generic in the way that frightened men's descriptions always are—middle height, middle age, dark cloak, came at evening—but it contained one specific detail: a ring on the right hand, silver, with a seal-stone carved in an unusual pattern, two interlocked keys.

Holcus said nothing when bar-Dov described it. But when we left, walking back through the market as the evening settled over the city and the lamp-lighters began their rounds, he stopped at a stall selling oil and stood for a moment without speaking.

"You know the ring," I said.

"I know the guild that uses that device." He paid for a small lamp-oil flask he had not come to buy—he did not like to stand at a stall without purchasing anything—and we walked on. "It is a merchants' association. Or presents itself as one. It has a formal structure, accounts, a meeting hall, and it is entirely legitimate in its surface operations. Beneath the surface it has a rather different function, which is the management of information. They maintain records—the kind of records that people would prefer not to have maintained." He paused. "They are very good at it. They have been operating in this city for at least fifteen years."

"You knew about them."

"I have been watching them for fourteen months. They are careful. They never commit the crime directly—they merely hold the threat of exposure, and the threat is sufficient." He was quiet for a moment. "They found a man three years ago who had made a decision he was not proud of. A man with a reputation to protect and the intelligence to understand exactly what exposure would cost him. They have been very comfortable with that arrangement."

"Rabbi Eleazar," I said.

He looked at me.

"The boundary adjudication," I said. "It benefited the same families whose names appear in the irregular assessments. He made the decision under pressure—you said that yourself, the Hasmonean political structure—and afterward, someone presented him with evidence that the decision had been—"

"Improperly made," Holcus said. "Yes. And offered him a simple arrangement: the evidence remains private, and the Rabbi makes himself available for future consultations. Small consultations. Nothing that required him to actually falsify anything—he is a man of too much principle for that—merely to advise, occasionally, in ways that benefited certain parties, and to never look too carefully at certain documents."

"For three years."

"For three years." He was quiet again. "He is, by every account, a man of genuine integrity. He spends his mornings studying, his afternoons hearing disputes, his evenings teaching. He gives extensively to the poor. He maintains positions in theological debates that make him enemies among the powerful. And every several months, a man with a silver ring comes to him and reminds him of one decision, made under political duress in a complicated moment, and the Rabbi nods and makes himself useful and hates himself for it."

The evening air was cooling now, carrying the smell of cook-fires and the dry stone of the hillside. Somewhere below us the city's gates were being closed for the night, the hinges of the Sheep Gate making their particular descending groan that I had heard every evening for six months and which I had come to associate, without intending to, with the closing of possibilities.

"What will you do?" I asked.

"I will have a conversation about taxes," Holcus said.

The meeting with Rabbi Eleazar ben-Mattitiah took place in the man's own study, a room in a house in the upper city that smelled of old parchment and the specific dry dust of scholarly work—the accumulated residue of a life spent with documents. He was perhaps sixty years old, broad-shouldered in a way that suggested a more physical youth, with a beard gone white and eyes of extraordinary intelligence that had, I thought, a quality of long-maintained weariness behind them, like a man who has been carrying something heavy for too long and has learned to carry it without showing the effort.

He received us with courtesy. He knew who Holcus Secundus was—everyone in the city's administrative circles knew—and he showed no particular alarm at our presence, which was itself, I had learned, a form of information.

Holcus began with the tax matter. He was precise and procedural. The Ezra bar-Kohen assessment, the filed complaint, the question of the northeastern improvements, the boundary corridor. He laid it out with the thoroughness of a man who had come for exactly this purpose and no other.

The Rabbi listened. He answered questions about the adjudication with careful accuracy—the legal principles applied, the witnesses consulted, the formal procedure followed. He was very good. If I had not known what I knew, I would have found him entirely credible.

"The decision followed the law," he said, in Greek, which he spoke as well as Holcus. "I examined the evidence presented to me. I considered the testimony. I applied the principles as I understood them."

"As you understood them at the time," Holcus said.

A pause. Not long. "Yes."

"And if you were to examine the same evidence now—with the benefit of three additional years of consideration—would you reach the same conclusion?"

Another pause. Longer.

I had my stylus ready and my wax tablet open—I was, ostensibly, taking notes on a tax proceeding. My hand was very still.

"That question," the Rabbi said carefully, "does not arise in the current legal framework. A decision formally rendered—"

"I am not asking about the legal framework," Holcus said, with great gentleness. "I am asking you, as one man whose profession is the accurate assessment of evidence to another whose profession is the same, whether the evidence presented to you in that adjudication was complete."

The room was very quiet. Outside, in the street, someone was calling a name—a child's name, called repeatedly with the particular note of maternal concern—and the sound faded and was replaced by silence.

"No," said Rabbi Eleazar ben-Mattitiah. "It was not complete."

He looked at Holcus with eyes that had, I thought, lost some of their weariness—or perhaps had exchanged it for something different. Not relief exactly. The particular expression of a man who has been holding his breath for three years and has just, without planning to, exhaled.

"Evidence was withheld from me," he said. "Evidence concerning the prior use of the land, which would have been material to the decision. I discovered this eight months after the adjudication. By that time I had already entered into—" He stopped. Reconsidered. "Certain parties had made me aware that my reputation was valuable to them. That this discovery, if it became known, would reflect on the integrity of the process."

"And so you kept it private."

"And so I kept it private." He looked at his hands on the table. They were very steady. "I told myself it was a small corruption. That the decision, even improperly made, had not been entirely wrong on the merits. That the harm of revelation would exceed the harm of silence." A pause. "I was, perhaps, more persuaded by those arguments than I should have been."

"Perhaps," Holcus said. He said it without judgment. He reached into the case he had brought—I had assumed it contained documents for the tax proceeding—and withdrew a small scroll. "I am going to ask you to make a statement. Not to the Hasmonean court. Not to the Roman administration. A statement for a record that I will file in our archive under a reference that will not be discoverable without specific prior knowledge of its existence. It will be found in perhaps twenty years, perhaps thirty. The men who pressured you will be, at that time, either dead or too old for the discovery to matter in the way it currently would." He set the scroll on the table. "You will tell me everything you know about the association with the silver ring. Their methods, their membership as you have observed it, their records. This is the price of the document remaining sealed."

The Rabbi looked at the scroll for a long moment.

"And the Sons of Shemaiah?" he asked. "The people who lost the land?"

"I will prepare a separate administrative assessment of the boundary corridor, based on a re-examination of the original survey records, which I will conduct myself, and which will conclude that the current boundary placement cannot be supported by the physical evidence and requires adjustment." Holcus's voice was precise and even. "This will take approximately four months. The adjustment will be administered through the normal process. It will appear entirely routine."

"You can do that?"

"I am the Roman Assessor of Accounts for this district. My office has the authority to require the production of original survey documentation and to conduct independent verification. It is an authority I have rarely used because it is cumbersome and generates administrative resentment. In this case I consider the cumbersomeness worthwhile."

Rabbi Eleazar was quiet for a long time. Long enough that I heard the gate-hinge of the house next door open and close twice.

Then he picked up the scroll.

He spoke for the better part of two hours. I wrote it all down in the abbreviated notation I had developed over my months in the office—a compressed system of my own devising, refined under Holcus’s instruction—and when the Rabbi was finished, I had seventeen full tablets of notes and a detailed account of the association with the silver ring that was more thorough than anything we had assembled in fourteen months of watching.

They had eleven functionaries in the city, placed across four distinct administrative structures. They maintained records on thirty-seven individuals, kept in a location under the market district that the Rabbi had never visited but had been shown the address of, as a reminder. They communicated through a system of ordinary commercial correspondence with particular phrases embedded in the preambles. They had been established, the Rabbi believed, approximately twenty years prior, by a man he had never seen without a veil over the lower portion of his face—a man who, based on the Rabbi's careful description of his voice, his hands, his manner of phrasing threats, Holcus identified with the practised precision of a man identifying a specific species of insect: a name I will not write down here, belonging to a merchant of the upper city who was, on the surface, a trader in Tyrian purple and whose actual commerce was of a less chromatic variety.

This man Holcus did not arrest. He did not have grounds to arrest him—the Rabbi's testimony could not be used without revealing the Rabbi, and the Rabbi's exposure would have caused exactly the harm Holcus had promised to prevent. Instead, three weeks later, I watched Holcus conduct what appeared to be a routine assessment of the purple merchant's import records, during which he made several careful notations, and during which the merchant became progressively paler, and at the conclusion of which Holcus informed the merchant that his accounts showed a significant discrepancy that would need to be resolved through a formal review process, which would take approximately two years and would require the production of documentation going back fifteen years, and that the review would, by its nature, require the examination of all his business correspondence.

The merchant chose, at that point, to relocate to Alexandria. He left within the month. The association with the silver ring did not, to our knowledge, reconstitute itself in his absence, though Holcus maintained that this was a temporary condition and that such structures had a tendency to regenerate.

"You have not destroyed it," I said, on the evening after the merchant's departure was confirmed.

"No," Holcus agreed. He was lying on the low couch in the back room of our offices, staring at the ceiling, which he did in the evenings when a case was finished and the energy that had sustained him through it had suddenly, completely, withdrawn. He looked hollowed. He always did at the end, as though the work had consumed something that would take time to replenish. On the table beside him was a bowl of the herbed salt he chewed obsessively when not engaged—he went through astonishing quantities of it—and a pile of riddle-texts in Aramaic that a local scholar had sent him in payment for some service and which he worked through in the evenings with visible pleasure. "You cannot destroy a structure of that kind. You can remove a particular brain from it, and it will drift for a while, and then another brain will find it useful, and it will coalesce again." He picked up one of the riddle-texts without looking at it. "The best one can do is leave it temporarily disoriented, rescue the people currently caught in it, and make sure someone is paying attention."

"Is someone paying attention?"

He looked at me with the pale grey eyes. "You are, Varro," he said. "That is rather the point of you."

The boundary adjustment was completed four months later, as promised, through an administrative process so thoroughly routine that no one who examined the record would find anything exceptional in it. The Sons of Shemaiah received their land without knowing why or how the decision had changed. The filing under which the Rabbi's statement was preserved was indexed in the archive under a reference notation that I will not reproduce here, except to note that it was derived from the third letter of each word in a line from Hesiod's Works and Days that Holcus had, he told me, selected because anyone competent to find it would already know what to do with it.

I asked him once, in the last months of my posting before my transfer back to Rome, whether it had been just.

He considered this for a while, with the seriousness he brought to questions that actually interested him.

"The land is restored," he said. "The extortion is ended, or at least interrupted. A man who had been slowly consumed by a decision he made under pressure has confessed it, which is a thing, I think, he needed to do." He chewed his salt. "The man who operated the mechanism will be operating some different mechanism in Alexandria within six months—I would stake the contents of this office on it. The original decision that created the vulnerability was made under political pressure that I have done nothing to change." He was quiet for a moment. "Justice, as a condition of the world, does not exist, Varro. Justice is a direction of travel. The question is whether one ends the day having moved somewhat toward it or somewhat away from it."

He picked up his riddle-text.

"I believe," he said, "that this was a day somewhat toward."

The foregoing account is complete as I am able to make it. I have omitted several names that remain in my fuller notes, held separately. The archive reference I know; I have resolved to open it in twenty-five years if I am still living, and to place a note directing whoever administers the archive after me. Holcus Secundus left Judea in the following year, recalled to assess an entirely different set of fraudulent records in the grain administration of Sicily, which he described in his farewell letter to me as "considerably less interesting, but the sailing will be easier." I never saw him again. I have kept writing things down.

Gaius Tertullius Varro, Rome, in the seventeenth year of my service.
