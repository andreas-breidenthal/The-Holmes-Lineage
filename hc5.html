IDDIYATUM

~1930 BC — Haran, Mesopotamia

The Estate of Ibbi-Adad

Transcribed by Enzu, Keeper of Records

I.

He arrived before the archive was properly open.

I heard the door before I heard the man — a sharp, deliberate knock, three times, at the hour when the shadow of the eastern wall still lay across the threshold and the lamps had not yet been trimmed. I had been in the record room since first light, cataloguing a backlog of commercial filings from the month of Nisannu, and the knock startled me sufficiently that I upset my ink-pot and spent the first moments of our acquaintance with my hands stained black.

The man in the doorway was thin, with large hands that seemed too long for his wrists — those hands were already ink-dark before he arrived, as if he had been working since before dawn, which I later learned he had — and eyes that moved across the room and across me in a single rapid pass, like a falcon quartering a field. He wore a scribe's robe, not entirely clean. One edge had been burned at some point and mended without care. He smelled of lamp oil and of something sharper: the bitumen-and-lime solution that scribes use when testing whether a clay surface has been re-smoothed. He had been testing surfaces this morning. He had been at it for some hours.

Even as he spoke, I noticed a habit that would come to unsettle me repeatedly over the course of that day: he reached, without looking, for the nearest tablet on my worktable and ran his thumbnail along its lower edge — not reading it, not examining it in any professional sense, but feeling it, the way a physician feels a pulse, as though the clay surface spoke to him in a register other men could not hear. When he noticed my attention, he set it down, with no embarrassment whatever.

"You are Enzu, the record-keeper," he said. It was not a question.

"I am," I said, looking at my hands. "And you have caught me at a disadvantage."

"Good," he said, and stepped inside. "Advantage is a variable I prefer to control myself. My name is Iddiyatum. I require access to the registration copy of the testament of Ibbi-Adad the merchant, and the commercial filings in which a woman named Amat-Ishtar appears as witness or signatory. I also require, before either of those things, a moment with my client."

I became aware then that there was a second man behind him, half-hidden in the shadow of the door-frame. He was heavyset and middle-aged, with a merchant's careful face — careful in the way of a man who had learned to keep his feelings below the surface of his expression, though that surface was not, this morning, entirely successful. He had the look of someone who had travelled quickly from a distance: the dust of the Kanesh road was still in the creases of his sandals, and there was a bruise of sleeplessness under each eye.

Iddiyatum turned and studied this man with an attention that was almost rude in its frankness. He looked at the sandals. He looked at the eyes. He looked at the left hand, which bore a merchant's signet ring worn thin on the inner edge from a habit of turning it when anxious. The man was turning it now.

"You came from Kanesh directly," Iddiyatum said to him. "You did not sleep in Haran last night — you arrived before the gates closed and went immediately to the scribal house to find me, rather than to an inn, which means the urgency is not of the practical kind but of the emotional kind. You are afraid that if you wait another day, someone else will move first." He paused. "When did you last eat?"

The merchant blinked. "Last night. Before the gates."

"Enzu." He did not look at me. "Is there bread?"

I produced bread. The merchant — who was, I now understood, Marduk-nasir, nephew of the lately-deceased Ibbi-Adad and his agent in the lapis lazuli trade out of Kanesh — sat at my table and ate it with the automatic gratitude of a man who had forgotten he was hungry.

"Now," Iddiyatum said. "Tell me what you have not told anyone else about your uncle's estate. Not the legal situation — I have read the documents. Tell me about the woman."

II.

The matter of Ibbi-Adad's estate had come to the archive, before Iddiyatum's arrival, in the form of a straightforward inheritance dispute of the kind that gives district judges their greyest hairs. There were two claimants: Marduk-nasir, who appeared as the named heir in the Sumerian original of the testament, and Amat-Ishtar, who appeared as the named heir in the Akkadian filing lodged with this archive. The difference between these two documents was, in legal terms, the difference between a man's entire commercial future and its absence.

What Marduk-nasir told Iddiyatum, in my archive room, over the course of a quarter-hour and most of the bread, was this: that Amat-Ishtar had served his uncle's household for seventeen years; that her entry into that service had followed immediately upon the death of her father, a wool merchant of modest prosperity; that her father's death had followed, by some months, a financial arrangement with Ibbi-Adad in which her father had provided silver against certain documents being kept private — documents relating to a boundary survey conducted with a false measurement; and that Ibbi-Adad had faced, eleven years ago, the dissolution of his merchant house following the loss of a caravan to raiders on the Kanesh road, and had been saved by precisely that arrangement.

"She knew," Marduk-nasir said. "She was present when my uncle made the arrangement. She watched it happen."

"And then she came to work for him," Iddiyatum said.

"There was nowhere else to go. The family was gone."

It was at this point that Marduk-nasir said something I have not forgotten. He said it with the particular flatness of a man repeating a position he has held for a long time and sees no reason to moderate:

"She is a household servant who has confused long service with entitlement. The silver my uncle left her would have kept her comfortably for three years. She should have been grateful. Instead—" he set down the bread "—she stole from him. From his memory. From me. She ought to be flogged in the square and sent into the street without the silver." He said it without heat. That was the worst of it: not that he said it, but that he said it as a man states a preference for one grain over another. There was no cruelty in his voice because there was no recognition that cruelty was required. "I want her ruined. That is what I am paying for."

Iddiyatum had said nothing during this. He had been moving, slowly, between the shelves — not aimlessly, but in the way of a man whose thinking requires physical correspondence, as though the ideas in his head require the legs to move in order to proceed. I noticed that he reached, as he passed each shelf, to run a thumbnail along the edge of whatever tablet was nearest — the compulsive tactile inventory of a man who thinks through his fingertips. He stopped now, in front of the shelf where the Ibbi-Adad filings were stored. He did not run his thumbnail along the edge. He was very still.

"Seventeen years," he said. "And in the testament — the Sumerian original — she receives?"

"A gift of silver. Appropriate to long service."

"Appropriate," Iddiyatum repeated, with a particular flatness. He pulled a tablet from the shelf. "Enzu. I will need all commercial filings in which Amat-Ishtar has countersigned. And I will need you to come with me."

I looked up from my writing. "Come with you where?"

"The temple library. The Sumerian original is held there." He was already moving toward the door. "Bring your lamp. And your seal."

I reached for my lamp. The second instruction gave me pause. "My seal?"

He looked back at me over his shoulder, and there was something in his expression that I had not seen before — the faintest trace of calculated purpose. "Imgur-Enlil is protective of his archive," he said. "He will not open the inner stacks for an examiner hired by a merchant. He may open them for a Keeper of Records conducting an official inquiry." He paused. "You are conducting an official inquiry, are you not? A discrepancy has been reported between the Sumerian original and the Akkadian filing held in your care. The resolution of that discrepancy falls within your professional authority to investigate." He waited. "Unless I am wrong about the scope of your office."

He was not wrong. I took my seal.

III.

I have been a keeper of records for eleven years, and I had not, before that morning, moved through the city at anything approaching this pace. Iddiyatum moved through it as he moved through everything — quickly, precisely, with the absolute confidence of a man who knew which streets would be emptiest at this hour and had planned the route before we left. I am not a young man, and I was breathing hard by the time we reached the temple precinct.

The archive keeper met us at the inner gate. Imgur-Enlil was an elderly priest, white-haired and deliberate in his movements, with the particular authority of a man who has spent thirty years as the sole guardian of things other people wish to touch. He regarded Iddiyatum with the serene hostility of a city wall regarding an approaching army.

"The inner archive," Imgur-Enlil said, "is not open to hired examiners."

"It is not," Iddiyatum agreed pleasantly. "Which is why I have not come as a hired examiner." He stepped aside. "I have come as the assistant of Enzu, Keeper of Records of the district archive, who is here on official business pertaining to a discrepancy between a document filed in his archive and the original held in yours. The resolution of this discrepancy is, as I am sure you are aware, a matter of inter-institutional record integrity."

Imgur-Enlil looked at me.

I produced my seal. I had held the office for eleven years. In that time I had been called upon, twice, to invoke its authority in a formal dispute. I invoked it now, in the prescribed form, in the precise language of the civic record code. My voice, I am pleased to report, did not waver.

The old priest studied the seal. He studied my face. Something shifted in his expression — not warmth, precisely, but the recognition of a boundary he was required to respect.

"The inner archive," he said, "is this way."

Iddiyatum said nothing as we followed. But as we passed through the cedar-framed doorway into the cool dark of the library passage, I felt the brief pressure of his hand on my shoulder — once, deliberate — the gesture of a man acknowledging a thing well done.

The tablet we needed was located, removed from its shelf, and placed on the reading stand. Imgur-Enlil remained. He stood against the far wall, his arms folded, watching with the vigilance of a man who intended to know exactly what happened to his tablet and would remember it precisely.

Iddiyatum took the lamp from my hand and held it at the extreme angle — almost parallel to the clay surface — that I would come to recognise as his characteristic method. He was silent for a long moment. Then:

"There," he said, and pointed.

"I see a tablet," I said.

"You see what the tablet shows you. I want you to watch what I do, and tell me if you see anything that I may have missed, because two pairs of eyes are better than one and yours are uncontaminated by my existing conclusions." He moved the lamp across the surface with a slowness that was almost reverential. "Look at line four. Not the signs. The space between the signs and the right edge of the tablet."

I looked.

"It is... tighter," I said slowly. "The signs are compressed toward the right."

"Yes. That is the hand of Ur-Namma, the temple scribe who inscribed this document. He trained on narrow tablets — his school used a smaller format — and when he has too many signs remaining for a line, he compresses them at the right edge rather than altering his layout. You can see this in lines two, four, and six. It is entirely characteristic." He moved the lamp. "Now. Look at line three."

Line three. The line where the heir's name appeared.

I looked, and I understood what I was looking for now, and I found it. The spacing in line three was different. The signs were evenly distributed. There was no compression at the right edge.

"It does not match," I said.

I said it quietly, because Imgur-Enlil was four paces behind us, and what we had just found was a violation committed within his care. I saw, from the corner of my eye, his head come up.

"What are you examining?" His voice had changed — the official frost was gone, and what replaced it was something sharper. He took a step toward the reading stand. "That examination is complete. The tablet will be returned to the shelf."

I did not think. I turned to face him and spoke in the formal register of my office — the language of the civic record code that I had used at the gate, but deployed now not as credential but as barrier, while behind me, I was acutely aware, Iddiyatum had perhaps thirty more seconds with the lamp and the clay. "Keeper Imgur-Enlil. The civic record code requires that any examination conducted under official authority be allowed to reach its natural conclusion. An interruption at this stage would itself constitute a procedural irregularity, which I would be compelled to record." I kept my eyes on his. I kept my voice level. "I do not believe you wish me to record that."

He stopped. He was not a small man, and his displeasure was not a small thing, and I was very conscious that I was a record-keeper of eleven years' standing who had, until this morning, spent approximately none of those years in direct confrontation with anyone. But I held his gaze, and he held mine, and behind us Iddiyatum worked in silence.

"Done," Iddiyatum said, quietly.

I stepped aside.

"The name in line three," Iddiyatum said to the room — to Imgur-Enlil as much as to me — setting the lamp on the stand, "is not the name Ur-Namma inscribed. The name in line three was erased — carefully, with a wet reed, while the clay was at a particular stage of partial dryness — and a different name was inscribed in its place. The replacement name is shorter. Its sign-cluster occupies less horizontal space. The forger could not compress what was not there, and so line three is generous where every other line of Ur-Namma's hand is not." He looked at me. "This is the Sumerian original. The forgery was not made in your archive. It was made here, on the tablet that was meant to be beyond alteration."

From the far wall, Imgur-Enlil made a sound. It was not a word. It was the sound of a man who has just understood something that will trouble him for a long time.

"Someone accessed this tablet," the old priest said. His voice had shed its official coldness entirely. "Someone with — with reason to be here. A witness to its making, perhaps. Someone I would not have questioned."

"Someone who understood precisely what needed to change," Iddiyatum said. He turned to the old priest, and his voice, for the first time since I had met him, was gentle. "You could not have known. The alteration required specific skill, specific knowledge, and specific patience. It was not a crude forgery. It was a considered one." A pause. "It will not be recorded that the breach occurred in your archive. You have my word. The record will show only that the original was examined and found to contain an anomaly consistent with a manufacturing variation in the clay. The matter will be resolved at the district level."

Imgur-Enlil looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded, once, and said nothing more.

We left him standing in the lamplight beside his tablet.

IV.

In the street outside the temple precinct, Iddiyatum paused.

"We require one additional stop before we return to the archive," he said. "The Ibbi-Adad household."

I looked at him. "On what pretext?"

"No pretext. The estate is currently in the possession of neither claimant pending adjudication. As Keeper of Records, you have the authority to conduct an inventory verification of assets named in a disputed testament. The warehouse on the eastern quay is named. So are the two houses in the merchant quarter. The household documents of a deceased merchant — his commercial filings, his correspondence, his private records — fall within the scope of that inventory." He was already walking. "We will take what we need."

The household steward, a worried man of fifty who had served Ibbi-Adad for twenty years and had the air of someone whose world had recently become very complicated, admitted us without resistance. Enzu's seal opened the document room. I had not known quite what Iddiyatum was looking for until he went directly to a sealed cedar box at the back of a shelf, examined the knot of its cord with that thumbnail-along-the-edge attention, and opened it.

Inside: three tablets, older than the rest, wrapped in separate linen, each bearing a different set of official seals. Commercial records from eleven years prior. A boundary survey, a silver loan arrangement, a letter that had never been sent. Iddiyatum read all three at the lamp-angle, turning each in his fingers with the unhurried patience of a man verifying what he already suspects.

He rewrapped them in the linen. He tucked them inside his robe.

"Come," he said. "Now we may return."

V.

The thing we looked at, back in the district archive, was a set of eleven commercial filings in which Amat-Ishtar had appeared as witness or signatory over the previous three years. Iddiyatum went through them in silence, at his characteristic angle to the lamp. I watched him and said nothing, which I had learned was what he required.

After perhaps twenty minutes he set the last tablet down.

"She taught herself," he said. "Not the full curriculum of the scribal school — no one outside the edubba teaches that — but enough. Enough to read a testament in both Sumerian and Akkadian. Enough to understand what had been written about her. Enough to recognise what would need to change, and where, and to practise on scrap clay until the change was clean enough to pass." He held up one of the filings. "She inscribed her own name in cuneiform in three of these documents. In two of them, she used both the Sumerian and Akkadian orthographic conventions correctly. A woman of ordinary education makes a seal impression. She wrote."

"Then she went to the temple library," I said, working through it. "When the original was filed there — she had access, as a witness to its making —"

"She was present at the dictation, as one of three witnesses. She knew the document. She knew the scribe's hand. She knew what would need to change, and she knew she had the skill to change it." He folded his hands on the table. "And then she carried the Akkadian translation to this archive herself, because Ibbi-Adad was too ill to leave his bed. She was alone with both documents, the original and the translation, from the sickroom to the temple and from the temple to here. She had prepared. The alteration is too clean for improvisation."

He stood abruptly. He went to the shelf and took down, for no reason I could immediately identify, the outermost of the eleven filings — the earliest, dating to three years prior — and held it again at the lamp angle. He set it down. He took a fresh receipt tablet from my worktable — a blank, the kind I use for daily transaction records — and a clean stylus, and placed them at the end of the table away from the Ibbi-Adad materials. He set a second lamp beside them. Then he paced to the window and back, running his thumbnail along the edge of the windowsill as he turned.

"There is one final confirmation required," he said, mostly to himself. He picked up a stylus from my worktable. Turned it in his fingers, once, twice, three times — the thumb and forefinger of a trained scribe — then shifted it, deliberately, to a different grip: the grip of someone who had taught themselves, learned not from instruction but from imitation and repetition and the evidence of what the clay retained. He held the grip for a moment. Then he looked at the blank tablet at the end of the table and set the stylus down with precision. "She will come here," he said.

I looked up. "You expect her?"

"I expect that she cannot help it. She has spent seventeen years managing uncertainty in that household — inventories, personnel, the daily arithmetic of a merchant estate. A woman of that particular temperament does not wait for news to come to her. She goes to find it." He was at the window now, looking out at the street. "She will have heard that Marduk-nasir arrived this morning. She will have heard that an examiner was retained. The question of how the examination is progressing will be, for her, intolerable not to know." He turned. "How long ago did we return from the temple?"

"Perhaps an hour."

"Then she will be here within the half-hour. Possibly sooner." He sat — for the first time that morning he actually sat, in the chair across from Marduk-nasir, and I had the unsettling impression that his stillness was as deliberate as his movement had been. "When she arrives, whatever else occurs, you will ask her to countersign a receipt. Tell her it is a standard record of her presence at an official examination. Have her use the stylus at the end of the table. The fresh one." He glanced at me. "You will know what to do."

"I will?"

He looked at me for a moment with an expression that was almost amused. "You have known what to do all morning," he said. "You simply have not always known that you knew." He settled back. "We will wait."

VI.

I heard the outer door of the archive open.

The woman who entered was not what I had constructed in my imagination. I had been picturing, I think, someone furtive — someone who moved like a secret. Amat-Ishtar moved like a person who had conducted her business in this city for seventeen years and saw no reason to behave otherwise today. She was perhaps forty, with the kind of face that had been attractive before grief had thinned it to something more severe and more interesting.

Iddiyatum was on his feet before she had fully cleared the threshold. He looked at her with the same rapid, quartering attention he had turned on Marduk-nasir that morning, and I watched his eyes move: to her sandals, which were dry and dustless, then to her hands — her right forefinger ink-stained in the precise way of someone who had taught herself to write with a stylus, not the broad-palmed stain of an edubba graduate but the mark of a person who had learned alone — and then, briefly, to her left sleeve, where a faint tide-mark of dark water was visible at the hem.

"You have come from the eastern quay," he said.

She stopped. Something shifted in her expression — not alarm, but the sharp recalibration of a person who has just learned that the ground is different from what she thought. "I have business at the quay."

"The morning delivery from the river barges. You were inspecting the warehouse inventory — the warehouse on the eastern quay that forms the principal asset of the estate you now believe to be yours." He had not moved from his position beside the table. "The tide-mark on your sleeve is from the quayside steps, which are submerged to the third stone at this hour. You checked the lower storage level." He gestured to a chair — not Marduk-nasir's chair, but mine, positioned now at the far end of the table, beside the blank receipt tablet and the fresh stylus. "Before we speak, I must ask you to countersign a record of your presence. Standard procedure for any party attending an examination under official authority." He did not wait for her agreement. He simply turned back to his documents, as though the matter were already concluded.

She looked at the chair. She looked at me. I met her eyes and said, in my record-keeper's voice — the voice I had used at the temple gate and at the document room of the Ibbi-Adad household — "If you would, please. It is a formality."

She crossed to the chair. She sat. She took the stylus.

I watched her hand.

She held it for one fraction of a second in the trained scribe's grip — thumb and forefinger balanced, the way the edubba teaches — and then shifted it, almost imperceptibly, to the other grip: the self-taught grip, the one that left the particular ink-mark at the right forefinger that I had seen in three of the eleven filings. She had not decided to do this. She had simply done it, because the hand remembers what the mind has practised ten thousand times in secret. She wrote her name. She set the stylus down.

Iddiyatum looked at the receipt. He looked at the stylus. He ran his thumbnail, once, along the edge of the tablet — and then looked directly at Amat-Ishtar.

"Thank you," he said. He set the receipt aside with the eleven filings. "Now. I want to ask you about the month of Nisannu, eleven years ago. About a caravan on the Kanesh road. About a boundary survey. About your father."

Something moved across her face. It was not guilt — I have seen guilt in this archive, on men who understood the clay had spoken against them, and this was not that. It was something more complicated. Something that had been living with her for seventeen years and that had learned, over those years, to be very quiet. And then, beneath that, briefly, something else: the cold steadiness of a person who had thought through every consequence of this moment before it arrived and had not flinch from what she found. She met his gaze and did not look away.

"You have been to the household," she said. It was not a question.

"I have." He reached into his robe and placed the three linen-wrapped tablets on the table between them. He did not unwrap them. He simply placed them there, and the silence around them was sufficient. "The cedar box at the back of the document shelf. The knot of the cord had been re-tied at some point — recently, I think, from the condition of the linen. You checked them. You knew they were there."

"I knew they were there," she said. Her voice had not changed. "I chose to leave them."

"I know." He was quiet for a moment — the stillness that I had come to understand was not absence of thought but its exact opposite. "I am not asking you to discuss your father for my benefit. I am asking because what I know about him, and about the arrangement that cost him his livelihood and not long afterward his life, determines what I write in my report." He looked at her directly, with the complete attention that was his most dangerous quality. "The Sumerian original of the testament names Marduk-nasir. The Akkadian filing names you. I have examined both documents thoroughly."

A silence.

"Then you know," she said.

"I know several things," Iddiyatum said. "I know that Ibbi-Adad's merchant house survived eleven years ago because of an arrangement that took your father's silver and his dignity and, in the end, his life. I know that you came to work in that household because you had no other place to go, and that you worked there for seventeen years. I know that the testament, as your employer dictated it when he still had his faculties, left you a gift of silver that a reasonable person might describe as modest." He set the stylus down. "I know that you are educated, in the specific and unusual way of a person who educated themselves in secret, alone, at the margin of a skill that the schools do not teach to women. And I know" — he touched the receipt tablet, lightly — "how you hold a stylus."

Marduk-nasir had been still since her entry. He was not still now. He came to his feet with a speed that surprised me, and his voice when he spoke had shed all its merchant's carefulness.

"This is the woman who has stolen everything that is mine. You stand here telling me what you know — name your finding. I will call the guards and have the matter taken directly to the district judge. Today. This hour." His ring was turning again, fast and unrhythmic. "I have the right. And I want her ruined." The flatness was back — the flatness I had heard that morning, when he had said flogged in the square in the same tone a man uses to discuss the price of barley. "Whatever it costs, whatever it requires. I want her left with nothing."

The archive felt, for a moment, very small.

Iddiyatum did not look at him. He had not looked away from Amat-Ishtar, and he did not do so now. When he finally turned to Marduk-nasir, he moved with the deliberateness of a man who has calculated every second of the pause and found the precise one at which to end it.

"You have the right," he said. "Exercise it, and I will attend that hearing. I will present my examination in full. Every document I have reviewed this morning." He reached into his robe and laid the three linen-wrapped tablets from the cedar box on the table. He unwrapped them, slowly, one by one. "These are the commercial records of the Ibbi-Adad merchant house, eleven years prior. A boundary survey in which certain measurements were, upon examination, found to be somewhat irregular. A silver loan arrangement contingent upon those documents remaining private. A letter that was never sent." He set the last tablet down and looked up. "These are not copies. These are the originals, which I have removed this morning under the authority of an estate inventory examination. They are currently in my possession." He did not raise his voice. "The Ibbi-Adad name has stood in this city for forty years. A public hearing destroys that in an afternoon. Every trader on the Kanesh road will know by the month of Abu. The lapis lazuli business — which operates, I believe, entirely on the credit of that name — will not survive the season." He waited. "The choice is entirely yours."

The ring stopped turning.

Marduk-nasir sat down.

VII.

Then Iddiyatum stood. He stood for a moment with his back to all of us — to me, to Marduk-nasir, to Amat-Ishtar — looking at the high north window of the archive room, at the rectangle of white sky above the Haran rooftops. The cedar-resin smoke from the street brazier drifted past the window in a thin thread. I could not see his face. I could see his hands, at his sides, which were very still — the stillness of a man who has made a decision and is simply waiting for the moment to speak it.

He turned around.

"I have examined the documents thoroughly," he said. "My examination reveals that the Akkadian filing is consistent with the Sumerian original, within the expected variations of translation between a ceremonial and a commercial register. The spacing variation in line three of the Sumerian original is attributable to a manufacturing characteristic of the clay batch from that archive period, which I observe in comparable tablets. There is no evidence of alteration in either document."

He rewrapped the boundary survey tablets in their linen and tucked them back inside his robe. All three of them. He did not look at Marduk-nasir as he did it.

"That is not what you found," Marduk-nasir said. His voice was quieter now, and the quietness was worse than the shouting had been. "You cannot —"

"I am telling you what my examination found." Iddiyatum looked at him with the expression of a man who is not angry, which in the circumstances was more difficult to bear than anger would have been. "I am also telling you that these documents" — he pressed his hand, briefly, against his robe where the tablets were — "are now part of the official estate inventory record. They will remain so. If you pursue this matter further — through another examiner, or through direct complaint to the judge — I will be compelled to submit the full scope of my inventory findings. I do not think that evidence would serve anyone connected to the Ibbi-Adad name." He finished adjusting the linen within his robe. "But the choice remains entirely yours."

He thanked me for the use of the archive. He asked me to record the finding as inconclusive, with no fraud detected.

He left.

VIII.

Amat-Ishtar remained seated for a moment after he was gone. Then she rose. She straightened her robe. She did not look at Marduk-nasir.

At the door, she paused. She looked back at the tablets on my table — the filing, the copies, the eleven documents Iddiyatum had examined, the receipt she had countersigned — with an expression I have thought about many times since, and which I still cannot wholly read. There was exhaustion in it, and something harder than grief, and underneath both of those, like the second layer of a palimpsest, something that I think might have been, simply, disbelief: the disbelief of someone who had spent seventeen years being certain that the world was one particular shape, and who had just discovered that she might have been wrong about the shape.

She left.

Marduk-nasir did not receive a refund.

IX.

The following is added in Enzu's hand, in a different ink, apparently composed some time after the primary account.

I have thought about that morning many times in the years since. I have thought, in particular, about two moments.

The first is the moment when Iddiyatum stood at the window — his back to us, the cedar smoke passing the rectangle of white sky, his hands very still at his sides.

The second is the moment, earlier, in the inner archive of the temple library, when Imgur-Enlil stepped toward the reading stand and I turned to face him and spoke in the formal register of my office while behind me, I was acutely aware, Iddiyatum had perhaps thirty seconds remaining. I have wondered, since, what I would say if someone asked me why I did it. The honest answer is that I did not, in that moment, decide. I simply found that I was doing it, and that it felt — not righteous, exactly, but necessary. Like the clay that holds what is pressed into it before the drying: some things are determined before you know they have been decided.

I have been a keeper of records for eleven years. I will, if I live, be one for thirty more. I have seen four thousand tablets and the disputes they contain, and I have understood, in this work, that the clay does not lie: it holds what is pressed into it and holds it until it breaks. This is the first principle of record-keeping. This is the thing we are told in the first days of our training.

What I understood, watching Iddiyatum at the window, is that the clay holds what is pressed into it — and that it matters very much who does the pressing, and with what instrument, and at what moment in the drying.

The law said: the document was altered. The heir named is not the heir chosen. The property belongs to Marduk-nasir.

What Iddiyatum weighed, I think, was this: on one side, the law. On the other, a woman who had spent seventeen years in the service of the man who had taken her father's life — not violently, but completely — who had educated herself in a craft her world did not think to offer her, alone, in secret, with scrap clay and patience, until she could read the contract that governed her own existence. Who had found, in the end, one instrument at hand: a wet reed, a dry tablet, perfect knowledge of what needed to change, and the steadiness of a person who has been preparing for a long time.

On one side, the law. On the other, a man who had used that morning's conversation to tell me, without any particular emphasis, that he wanted a woman flogged in the public square for having confused long service with entitlement.

The law would have given the property to Marduk-nasir and sent Amat-Ishtar into the street with a modest silver gift and a closed case. Iddiyatum gave her the warehouse on the eastern quay, the two houses in the merchant quarter, and what remained of her life. He also gave her the three tablets from the cedar box — or rather, he kept them, and in keeping them made them hers to lose or use as she saw fit, which amounts to the same thing.

He was paid by Marduk-nasir. The fee was not inconsiderable.

I have never decided whether what he did was just. I have decided that it was something, and that the something is worth recording, and that the official record — which reads no alteration detected, case closed — is not the whole of what happened in the archive room on that morning in the month of Dumuzi, in the heat, in the cedar-resin smoke, in the thirty-first year of the city-lord of Haran.

This is the whole of what happened. I have set it down in clay.

Let it endure.
