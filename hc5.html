IDDIYATUM

~1930 BC — Haran, Mesopotamia

He has been granted — the name a statement of gratitude for a gift that the family had possessed for generations, though they had no word for it that distinguished it from intelligence in general or from the scribal craft in particular. Iddiyatum was a merchant’s scribe in Haran, bilingual in Sumerian and Akkadian at the precise moment when the old Sumerian tongue was fading from daily use into the ceremonial register — the language of the temple and the law, no longer of the market or the household — and Akkadian was asserting itself as the lingua franca of the entire Near East.

He understood what the shift meant. A language dying is not merely a cultural event but a political and commercial one: the contracts, the legal codes, the diplomatic correspondence of three centuries were encoded in a tongue that fewer and fewer people could read with confidence, and the men who could read it fluently — who could move between the old legal formulae and the new administrative language without loss of meaning — were suddenly invaluable. Iddiyatum cultivated that fluency deliberately, treating the death of Sumerian as an opportunity rather than a loss, and built a practice as a legal translator and document interpreter that drew clients from across the upper Euphrates trading world. He read texts the way his great-great-grandfather had read grain tallies: for the discrepancy, the alteration, the carefully introduced ambiguity that changed a document’s meaning without changing its surface.

In the marketplace archives of Haran, a record keeper named Enzu was tasked with preserving copies of legal filings and commercial agreements. Enzu’s role was to record faithfully what was presented, though he did not always grasp the subtleties that lay beneath the text. His tablets, preserved among the merchant house records, capture not only the proceedings but the sequence of discovery as Iddiyatum examined the contested contract.

The Matter Of The Estate Of Ibbi-Adad

Transcribed by Enzu, Keeper of Records, from his own observations and from conversations with the principals, in the thirty-first year of the city-lord of Haran

I have been a keeper of records for eleven years, and in that time I have witnessed the filing of perhaps four thousand tablets. I have seen disputes over barley and disputes over silver, disputes over the ownership of an irrigation ditch and disputes over the paternity of a donkey. I have seen men weep and men shout and men fall silent in the way that men fall silent when they understand that the clay has spoken against them and there is nothing more to be said.

I had not, before the matter of Ibbi-Adad’s estate, seen anything like Iddiyatum.

He came to the archive building on a morning in the month of Dumuzi, when the heat of the upper Euphrates valley had already settled over Haran like a wet linen cloth pressed against the face. He came quickly — he always moved quickly when a problem had hold of him, I would later learn — and he arrived with a document folded in linen under his arm and a look about his eyes that I can only describe as a kind of controlled fever.

He was a thin man, with large, ink-darkened hands that seemed too long for his wrists, and eyes that moved across a tablet the way a falcon’s eyes move across a field: not randomly, not pleasantly, but with a specific and predatory patience. He wore a scribe’s robe, but it was not entirely clean, and one edge of it had been burned at some point and mended without particular care. He smelled of lamp oil and of something sharper — the bitumen and lime solution that scribes sometimes use when they wish to test whether a surface has been re-smoothed. He had been testing surfaces recently. He had been testing surfaces this very morning.

“You are the record-keeper,” he said. It was not a question.

I confirmed that I was Enzu, keeper of records for the district archive of Haran.

“Then you will have the registration copy of the testament of Ibbi-Adad the merchant, filed in the month of Nisannu, approximately sixty days before his death.”

I did. I went and found it.

The matter of Ibbi-Adad had come to the archive in the form of a dispute between two parties: Marduk-nasir, who was Ibbi-Adad’s nephew and his agent in the lapis lazuli trade out of Kanesh, and a woman named Amat-Ishtar, who had been Ibbi-Adad’s household manager for seventeen years and who appeared in the Akkadian version of the testament as the primary heir to the Haran property — the warehouse on the eastern quay, the two houses in the merchant quarter, and the silver reserves held against the third Kanesh caravan.

The Sumerian original of the testament, which Ibbi-Adad had dictated when he still had his full faculties, before the river sickness had taken his voice and then his strength over the following months, named Marduk-nasir as the heir to the Haran property. The Akkadian version, filed here with us, named Amat-Ishtar.

This discrepancy had produced the dispute, and the dispute had produced, after some weeks of unsuccessful local adjudication, the involvement of Iddiyatum, hired by Marduk-nasir to examine the translation.

Marduk-nasir himself had come with Iddiyatum that morning, though he stood some distance from him in the way that clients of formidable men sometimes stand — respectful, slightly afraid, not entirely certain what they have set in motion. He was a heavyset man in middle age, with a merchant’s careful eyes and a merchant’s careful hands, and he watched Iddiyatum move through the archive with an expression that mixed hope with something approaching apprehension.

“You have read the Akkadian filing,” I said to Iddiyatum, not as a question.

“I read it last night.” He had laid the linen-wrapped document on my table and was examining the registration copy that I had produced, holding it at an angle to the light from the high window — the window faced north, which was how archive buildings were designed, to avoid direct sun on the tablets. “And the Sumerian original this morning, at the temple library, where a copy is held. Sit down, Enzu. I will need you to follow this carefully.”

I sat.

“The testament of Ibbi-Adad was dictated in Sumerian and inscribed by a temple scribe named Ur-Namma — a good scribe, trained in the old style, from a school that still teaches the traditional sign forms. He was the only person present of sufficient literacy to record a testament of this complexity at the time Ibbi-Adad dictated it, since the merchant’s own household scribe had died of the river sickness two weeks previously and had not yet been replaced.”

“That is my understanding as well,” I said.

“The Sumerian text names Marduk-nasir as the heir to the Haran property, with a gift of silver to Amat-Ishtar in recognition of her service. This is consistent with everything known about Ibbi-Adad’s commercial intentions — Marduk-nasir runs the Kanesh operation, which is the commercial future of the estate, and the Haran property is the base from which the Kanesh operation is managed. To give Haran to anyone but Marduk-nasir would be to cut the root from the tree.” He set down the registration copy and turned to look at Marduk-nasir briefly. “Your uncle was not a sentimental man in commercial matters.”

“He was not,” Marduk-nasir agreed, with the slight hoarseness of a man who has been saying this for weeks without being believed.

“The Akkadian filing reverses this arrangement. Amat-Ishtar receives the Haran property; Marduk-nasir receives a share of the Kanesh profits, contingent on the caravan’s success — which means contingent on Amat-Ishtar’s continued goodwill, since she now controls the Haran warehouse that outfits the caravans.” Iddiyatum set the two documents side by side on the table and stepped back, looking at them with his arms folded. “The question before us is whether this reversal represents the genuine final wishes of Ibbi-Adad, expressed in a second Sumerian draft that was then translated, or whether it represents a fraudulent alteration of the single existing Sumerian original.”

I looked at the two tablets. To my eye, they were simply two tablets. The signs were well-formed on both. The clay was dry and hard on both.

“I cannot see any —” I began.

“No,” he said, not unkindly. “You would not, unless you knew precisely where to look. That is not a criticism. You are trained to verify that the forms are present, not to verify that the forms are authentic. Those are different skills.” He drew the lamp from its bracket on the wall — the lamp smelled of sesame oil, which was the better quality, not the fish-oil that some archives used — and held it over the Akkadian filing. “Come and look here. Not at the text. At the clay itself, in the region of the third and fourth lines.”

I came and looked. He tilted the lamp so that the light ran almost parallel to the surface.

And then I saw it.

In the region of the third line, where the name of the heir was inscribed, there was a difference in texture that was almost invisible in ordinary light but clear in the raking lamp-glow: a slight flatness, a smoothness that was not quite the same as the surrounding surface. Not the smoothness of a well-made tablet, but the smoothness of a surface that had been gently worked while the clay was still at a particular stage of dryness — not wet enough to accept deep re-inscription without visible damage, but not so dry as to crumble. There was a craft to it. There was real knowledge of clay in it.

“The original sign in that position was the cuneiform for Marduk-nasir’s name-sign cluster,” Iddiyatum said. “It was smoothed — very carefully, with a wet reed applied with extreme patience — and Amat-Ishtar’s name-sign cluster was inscribed in its place. The surrounding signs were left untouched, which is why the document reads coherently. Only this one substitution was necessary to transform the entire inheritance.”

“But —” I was still looking at the tablet. “How can you be certain the name was changed and not simply that this region of the clay was imperfect in manufacture?”

He smiled, and it was the smile of a man who had been waiting for precisely this question.

“Because,” he said, “of the line-spacing. Look at the line above and the line below. The scribe who made this filing — a man named Warad-Sin, who works from the scribal house near the west gate — has a consistent habit: he compresses his signs slightly when he approaches the right edge of a tablet and has too many signs remaining for his line. It is the mark of a scribe who learned on narrower tablets than he now uses. You can see this compression in lines two, five, and six of this filing. But in line three — the line where the name appears — there is no compression. The spacing is generous. More generous than Warad-Sin’s hand ever produces naturally.” He set the lamp back in its bracket. “Because the name that replaced the original was shorter. Amat-Ishtar’s name-sign cluster is shorter than Marduk-nasir’s. The forger did not account for the spacing. He could not, without re-inscribing the entire line, which would have been visible. So he left it, and the generosity of the spacing is itself the evidence.”

Marduk-nasir had come forward during this explanation. He was staring at the tablet with an expression I had not seen on a man’s face before: the expression of someone who has been telling the truth for weeks and has only now, in the presence of someone who can demonstrate it, begun to truly believe himself.

“Then it was Warad-Sin,” he said. “Amat-Ishtar hired Warad-Sin to alter —”

“I said the alteration was made by someone with knowledge of clay and scribal technique,” Iddiyatum said. “I did not say it was Warad-Sin. Sit down. There is more.”

He had, I should record, been in the archive for only a little over an hour at this point. He had arrived at the second meal-time of the morning; it was not yet midday. He had not eaten anything, so far as I observed. He did not appear to notice this.

He had also sent me, twenty minutes after his arrival, to fetch a particular class of document from the storage room: specifically, the filings of commercial agreements that Amat-Ishtar had witnessed or countersigned in the previous three years. There were eleven of them. He had gone through them in the way I now understood he went through all documents — at an angle to the light, quickly and then slowly, quickly for the content and slowly for the surface. He had said nothing while he read them, which I had taken as a neutral sign. It was not.

“Enzu,” he said, “what can you tell me about the scribal training of Amat-Ishtar?”

I stared at him. “She is a household manager. I would not expect her to have —”

“She countersigned three of these documents. Not in the way a woman of ordinary education makes a mark — not a seal impression alone, not a thumbprint. She inscribed her own name in cuneiform. Correctly, in both the Sumerian and Akkadian orthographic conventions, in two of the three documents.” He held up the relevant tablets. “She received scribal training. Not full scribal training — she did not go through the edubba school — but training sufficient to read a testament, to understand what had been written in it, and to understand what would need to change.” He set them down. “She did not hire Warad-Sin. She did not need to. She did it herself.”

The silence in the archive room was very complete. Outside, in the street, I could hear the water-seller calling his price and the distant complaint of a donkey being loaded at the quayside.

“She had access to the filing?” I asked, because someone had to ask it.

“She was named as a witness to the original Sumerian dictation — one of three present. Ur-Namma inscribed it; Ibbi-Adad dictated it; Amat-Ishtar and two merchants of the district were present as witnesses. When the filing was brought here for registration, it was carried by Amat-Ishtar herself, as Ibbi-Adad was by that time too ill to leave the house. She was alone with it from the moment she left the sickroom to the moment it was placed in these hands.” He gestured at the archives around us. “I do not know at what point on that journey she made the alteration. I suspect she had prepared the wet reed and the materials in advance. I suspect she had practiced on scrap clay. The alteration is too clean to have been improvised.”

Marduk-nasir stood up. His voice was controlled but only barely. “Then we bring this to the district judge. The evidence is —”

“Sit down,” Iddiyatum said again.

It was the third time he had said it, and this time there was something in his voice that was different. Not colder — it was not a cold voice, exactly, though it was precise — but quieter. Something had shifted in him. The fever-brightness had gone from his eyes and been replaced by something that I found, and still find, difficult to name.

He sat down himself, which he had not done before. He set both hands flat on the table, looked at them for a moment, and then looked at Marduk-nasir.

“Before we discuss what to do with this evidence,” he said, “you will tell me about the original testament.”

What came out of Marduk-nasir in the next quarter-hour was not given willingly. It came out the way water comes from pressed cloth: slowly, then in a rush, then slowing again as another layer of resistance was encountered.

Iddiyatum pressed him with questions, but the questions were not the questions I would have asked — not questions about the theft, not questions about Amat-Ishtar. They were questions about Ibbi-Adad himself. About the seventeen years of Amat-Ishtar’s service. About what kind of service it had been. About the nature of her position in the household. About whether there had been a period — perhaps eight years ago, perhaps ten — when Ibbi-Adad’s business had failed, when a caravan had been lost to raiders on the Kanesh road, when silver had been desperately needed.

Marduk-nasir answered carefully, and then less carefully, and then not carefully at all.

Ibbi-Adad had faced ruin eleven years ago. A caravan carrying three years of accumulated lapis lazuli profit had been taken on the Kanesh road, and he had owed silver to the trade associations that he could not pay. He had faced the complete dissolution of his merchant house.

Amat-Ishtar had saved it. Not with her own silver — she had no silver of her own — but with the silver of her family, her father’s estate in the wool trade, which she had obtained by a method that Marduk-nasir described with increasing difficulty as the interview went on. Her father had not given the silver willingly. Her father had been shown certain documents that Ibbi-Adad held — documents relating to a boundary dispute in which her father had used a surveyor’s false measurement — and her father had understood that the silver was the price of those documents remaining private. The transaction had been entirely legal, in the sense that no law had been broken in its execution, and entirely extortionate, in the sense that it had destroyed her father’s ability to do business for three years and had contributed, Marduk-nasir believed, to his death from a sickness of the liver the following year.

Amat-Ishtar had known what was done. She had been present when Ibbi-Adad made the arrangement with her father. She had come to work in Ibbi-Adad’s household six months after her father’s death, with no family remaining and no resources, because there was nowhere else to go.

She had worked in his household for seventeen years.

The room was very quiet when Marduk-nasir finished.

“And the Sumerian testament,” Iddiyatum said. “Which leaves her a gift of silver. Is the silver sufficient to establish her independently?”

Marduk-nasir looked at the table. “It is... a modest gift. Appropriate to service.”

“Appropriate to seventeen years of service performed by a woman of scribal education who managed a complex merchant household and whose father’s life and estate were taken to save the business she spent those seventeen years managing.”

Marduk-nasir said nothing.

“I see.” Iddiyatum stood up. He stood for a moment with his back to both of us, looking at the high north window of the archive room, at the rectangle of white sky above the Haran rooftops. The cedar-resin smoke drifted past him in a thin thread.

Then he turned around.

“I have examined the documents thoroughly,” he said. “My examination reveals that the Akkadian filing is consistent with the Sumerian original, within the expected variations of translation between a ceremonial and a commercial register.” He began wrapping his own documents in their linen. “The spacing variation in line three is attributable to Warad-Sin’s habit of adjusting his sign-size when a name cluster is of unusual length. The surface texture variation is a manufacturing irregularity in the clay batch, which I have observed in two other tablets from the same archive period. There is no evidence of alteration.”

Marduk-nasir was on his feet. “That is not — you cannot —”

“I am telling you what my examination found.” Iddiyatum looked at him with the expression of a man who is not angry, which in the circumstances was more difficult to bear than anger would have been. “I am also telling you that if you pursue this matter further, through another examiner or through direct complaint to the judge, I will be compelled to share with that examiner or that judge the full scope of my research — including the commercial history of the Ibbi-Adad merchant house, including the circumstances of the silver loan eleven years ago, including the documents that remain, I believe, in the household archive, now in the possession of Amat-Ishtar. Documents which may bear examination in their own right.” He finished wrapping the linen and tucked it under his arm. “I do not think that outcome would be in the interest of anyone connected to the Ibbi-Adad name. But I leave the choice entirely to you.”

He thanked me for the use of the archive. He asked me to record the finding as inconclusive, with no fraud detected. He left.

The following is added in Enzu’s hand, in a different ink, apparently composed some time after the primary account.

I have thought about that morning many times in the years since. I have thought, in particular, about the moment when Iddiyatum stood at the window. I do not know what he was thinking, but I think I know what he was weighing.

On one side: the law. The document was altered. The heir named is not the heir chosen. The property belongs, by the testament’s true meaning, to Marduk-nasir.

On the other side: a woman who spent seventeen years serving the man who destroyed her father. Who educated herself, quietly, in a craft she had no school to teach her — taught herself to read the contracts she filed, to understand the testament she witnessed, to see with her own eyes what had been written about her. Who, when the moment came, found the only instrument she had — a wet reed, a dry tablet, patience, and perfect knowledge of what needed to change — and used it.

The law would have given the property to Marduk-nasir and sent Amat-Ishtar into the street with a modest silver gift and a closed case. Iddiyatum gave her the warehouse on the eastern quay, the two houses in the merchant quarter, and what remained of her life.

He was paid, I should note, by Marduk-nasir. Marduk-nasir did not receive a refund.

I have never decided whether what Iddiyatum did was just. I have decided that it was something, and that the something is worth recording, and that the official record — which reads: no alteration detected, case closed — is not the whole of what happened in the archive room on that morning in the month of Dumuzi, in the heat, in the cedar-resin smoke, in the thirty-first year of the city-lord of Haran.

This is the whole of what happened. I have set it down in clay.

Let it endure.
