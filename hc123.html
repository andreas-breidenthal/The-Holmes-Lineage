SHERIDAN HOLMES

~1796 — Yorkshire / Oxford, England

He inherited at fourteen — which is to say he inherited the name and the house and the obligation, the money having largely preceded him into oblivion. What remained was scraped together with sufficient ingenuity to see him through Christ Church, Oxford, where he distinguished himself in the informal debating societies rather than in the schools, and where his contemporaries remembered him as a man who listened more than he spoke and, when he spoke, said something worth attending to. He was remembered also for the discipline of his habits — remarkable in an Oxford undergraduate of the period, when dissolution was the norm and academic distinction a secondary consideration — and for a quality in his manner of argument that his friends struggled to name precisely: the impression, when speaking with him, that he had already identified the weakest point in your position before you had finished stating it, and was simply waiting, with the patience of a man who had nowhere else to be, for you to reach it yourself. He was not cruel about this; he was, if anything, gentle — he had the teacher’s instinct rather than the prosecutor’s, and derived more evident satisfaction from watching a man reason his way to a correct conclusion than from demonstrating the conclusion in advance.

The account of his later years comes not from a professional chronicler, but from Reverend Thomas Elridge, curate of a parish near Kirkbymoorside, whose correspondence with Sheridan Holmes survives in fragmentary form. Elridge was not trained in the sciences of evidence, but he possessed a mind attentive to order and consequence, and his letters reveal both admiration and occasional unease at the manner in which Holmes perceived the world. It is through this correspondence that the following account is preserved.

The Matter of Mr. Phelps of Christ Church

Recovered from Letters to His Sister, Mrs. Anne Elridge Vane, Northumberland, Autumn 1817

The following is extracted and arranged from three letters dispatched between the fourteenth and twenty-eighth of October, 1817, from Kirkbymoorside, Yorkshire, to Mrs. Anne Elridge Vane of Alnwick. The originals remain in the Vane family collection. Where the Reverend's hand became agitated or his meaning compressed by haste, I have taken the liberty of clarification, but have not embellished the substance. — T.E., 1831, preface to private copy.

From the first letter, dated October 14th:

...You will forgive me, Anne, if this account proceeds by some indirection. I find I cannot tell it straight, because I did not experience it straight — I experienced it in precisely the manner that all encounters with Sheridan Holmes are experienced, which is to say: I understood what had happened only after it was finished, and understood what I had failed to observe only after he explained it to me, gently, as one explains a misheard word to a child who has been paying perfectly reasonable attention.

It begins with a smell. I set that down first because he would. The smell of the coaching yard at the Marlborough Arms, Woodstock, on a Tuesday morning when the fog from the Evenlode had come up through the fields and settled against the stone of every building in the village like a patient creditor waiting to be paid. Coal smoke, yes, and horse-dung, and the particular sourness of a yard where the drainage was laid improperly and the autumn rains had found it out — but beneath all of that, something that Holmes had remarked upon not thirty seconds after we had dismounted, touching two fingers to his nose with the air of a man confirming a calculation.

"Ink," he said. "Not the common kind. Copying-ink — the heavy sort, iron gall, used by legal clerks and men who wish their documents to appear older than they are. Someone in this establishment has been working."

I confess I smelled nothing of the sort. I smelled the yard. I told him so.

"The yard," he said, with that patience I have described to you before — the patience that is not unkind but is, perhaps, more demanding than unkindness would be — "tells you where we are. The ink tells you what has been happening here."

I ought to set down the circumstances from their origin, though I suspect by the time this letter reaches you I will still be at insufficient distance from the events to narrate them with proper composure.

You will recall that I have mentioned Sheridan Holmes in my letters before — my Christ Church contemporary, the man who came up from Yorkshire with a name worth more than his income and left Oxford with an income worth rather more than his name, having done remarkably little in the conventional sense to account for the difference. I do not mean this in any improper direction; I mean that those years between his arrival and his departure had somehow seasoned him in ways that formal study seasons very few men, and that when I encountered him again this autumn, passing through Kirkbymoorside on some business he declined to specify, he was recognisably the same figure who had listened through every meeting of the debating society with the still attention of a chess-player who has already seen six moves ahead — and yet completed, somehow, in a way that the undergraduate had not been.

He called at the rectory on the Thursday. He stayed three days, nominally as a guest, and spent the better part of his visit reading everything I possessed concerning the philosophy of evidence and the law of contracts, which is to say: Bentham's Introduction, two volumes of Blackstone I had inherited from my predecessor, and a pamphlet on mercantile acknowledgements that had arrived in a bundle from my brother-in-law and that I had not known I possessed. Holmes found it behind the others within twenty minutes of being shown the shelves, and had read it twice before dinner.

On the Sunday morning, over his second cup of tea — he drank it without milk, very strong, and complained mildly that Yorkshire tea was brewed with an enthusiasm that outran its artistry — he looked up from whatever calculation he was performing behind his eyes and said, with no particular introduction:

"Have you had any word from Phelps?"

I had not. I said so. I added that I had not, in truth, had word from George Phelps since the spring, when a letter had arrived that was somewhat breathless in its composition and concluded with a request — declined by me as beyond my means — for an immediate loan of fifteen pounds.

Holmes set down his cup. "And the loan was for the resolution of a debt he owed to a man named Carver. A legal agent. Working out of premises near Carfax."

I stared at him. "How in the world—"

"Phelps wrote to three people," he said. "I know this because I am one of them. He wrote to his cousin Meredith, who is in reduced circumstances and could not assist him. He wrote to you. And he wrote to me, on the reasonable grounds that I had some small acquaintance with the law of contracts, and also — I suspect — because he was frightened, and the frightened man does not always choose wisely when selecting his counsel." He paused. "I did not reply immediately because I wished first to understand the nature of the debt before I said anything about it. And then I became occupied with other matters. And then Phelps vanished."

From the second letter, dated October 19th:

...I should tell you what had actually happened, as I understand it now, though I understood none of it when we drove out from Oxford on that Tuesday morning with Holmes directing the coachman to Woodstock in a tone that admitted of no alternative destinations.

George Phelps had borrowed money. This was not remarkable — Phelps had always borrowed money, with the cheerful regularity of a man who regards debt as a natural feature of the landscape, like weather. What was remarkable was the source. A man named Carver had presented himself at Christ Church in the spring with a letter of introduction that satisfied the porter and, apparently, satisfied Phelps, and had extended a loan of thirty pounds at terms that Phelps had accepted, as Holmes put it, "with the attention to detail of a man reading a bill of fare in a language he does not speak." He had looked at the numbers and missed the mechanisms.

The mechanism, as Holmes explained it to me — in the coaching yard, before we had so much as entered the inn, standing in the fog while I shivered and he spoke with the focused composure of a man solving an equation — was this: the acknowledgement of debt that Phelps had signed contained a clause, buried in the lower portion of the second page and rendered in the copying-ink I have already mentioned, which permitted the creditor to revise the interest terms unilaterally upon any delay in repayment. The clause was not there when Phelps signed it. Holmes was prepared to demonstrate this.

"How?" I asked.

"The paper," he said. "The document Carver has been displaying to Phelps — and to at least one college official, which is the proximate cause of Phelps's present hiding — was manufactured in two stages. The primary text is written on paper that was produced and cut in a standard London lot. The clause — the critical clause — is written on paper from the same lot, yes, but cut with a different instrument. The edge is not identical. And the ink, while formulated to resemble the original, is applied with a freshness of penetration into the fibre that is inconsistent with a document produced in February."

I looked at him for a moment. The fog shifted against the inn-sign above our heads, and the chain of the sign moved with a low creak that seemed, in the silence that followed his explanation, inappropriately theatrical.

"You can see the difference in the paper's edge," I said carefully, "and determine from the ink's penetration that the clause was added later."

"With a magnifying lens, yes. And with the pamphlet from your bookshelf, which treats of precisely this method of fraud and was written by a solicitor in Bristol who encountered it in a shipping dispute in 1809."

I thought about the pamphlet behind Blackstone. I thought about the three days he had spent at my rectory. I began to understand that the visit had not been entirely social.

Phelps was in the second room on the upper floor. We knew this because the inn's ostler, a young man of perhaps seventeen who seemed to regard Holmes with the particular wariness that perceptive young men often develop in the presence of someone who notices too much, had offered us this information in exchange for three shillings and the quiet reassurance that nobody was going to be arrested. Holmes had provided both with equal ease, adding, as the boy turned away, a brief observation about the condition of the boot-scraper beside the service door that caused the ostler to look at it, look back at Holmes, and walk away rather more quickly than before.

I did not ask. I have learned, in Holmes's company, that some things are not explained because the explanation would consume more time than the thing itself is worth.

Phelps had been in that room for nine days. He was pale, and thinner than I remembered him, and his hands when he stood up from the chair by the window had the slight tremor of a man who has been in close company with his own thoughts for too long without relief. He looked at Holmes and then at me with an expression that was, in equal measure, relief and shame.

"You found me," he said. He said it to Holmes.

"It was not," Holmes said, settling into the other chair with the air of a man arriving at his own fireside, "a search that taxed me greatly."

From the third letter, dated October 28th:

...The college authorities were, I think, more frightened than they wished to appear. A fraud involving a document presented to them in the spring — a document they had, apparently, given some weight — was not a comfortable thing to have existed. The Dean received Holmes and Phelps and myself in his rooms on the Thursday morning, and Holmes presented the evidence with economy and without emphasis, in the manner of a man reading a mathematical proof: this fact, then this fact, then this conclusion, which follows with the inevitability of arithmetic.

When he had finished, the Dean looked at the document for a long moment and then at Holmes and said — not unkindly, but with the particular interest of a man who has just seen something he cannot fully account for: "How long had you known, Mr. Holmes?"

Holmes had been replacing the lens in his coat. He paused in this activity for just a moment — I saw it, a brief stillness — and then he said: "Long enough to be certain."

He did not say how long. He does not, as a general principle, say how long. I have come to believe that this is not evasion so much as a form of consideration for the feelings of those who had not known at all.

Carver was removed. The precise mechanism of his removal I will not here record — it is sufficient to say that the college authorities, having had the evidence placed before them, managed the matter through channels that did not require the public exposure of their own error in having received the man in the first instance. Holmes appeared entirely satisfied with this arrangement. I asked him, on the walk back across the quad, whether it troubled him that the resolution would not be made known.

He considered this for a moment with the same attention he gave to everything, and then said: "Phelps is returned. The debt is discharged. Carver will not practise this fraud again — at least not in Oxford, and not with documents. What precisely is the benefit of wider knowledge?"

I said something about justice. About the principle of the thing.

"The principle," he said, not unkindly, "is satisfied. The mechanism that enforces the principle is not always the appropriate instrument. A scalpel and a broadaxe are both capable of division. They are not equivalent tools."

I did not have an answer to this. I rarely have answers to Holmes, which is, I confess, a quality I find in him both admirable and occasionally oppressive, as one finds a very tall ceiling admirable, and cold.

Phelps wrote to me in the second week of November. He had been reinstated in good standing, was reading again for his degree, and had sent a bottle of port to Holmes at an address I did not recognise in Yorkshire. He had not, he said, thanked Holmes at the time, having been too unsettled, and hoped the port would serve as a partial expression of what he owed, since he suspected that a direct expression of gratitude would be received by Holmes with the same mild impatience as any other information he had already registered and filed away as settled business.

I thought this was, in the circumstances, an accurate assessment.

I remain, as always, your affectionate brother,

Thomas

P.S. The fee Holmes accepted from me for the journey, which I insisted upon, was the Bristol pamphlet. He said it had earned its place. I did not argue. I never argue with him about what has and has not earned its place. He is always, infuriatingly, correct about such things.
