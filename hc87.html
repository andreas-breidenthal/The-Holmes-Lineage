HOLMAR NORTHWARD

~580 AD — Rhine / Frisia

Something in this generation turned toward the sea. It may have been a marriage into a Frisian trading family, or simply the pull that the North Sea coast has always exerted on men of restless and acquisitive intelligence — the sense that the horizon there is not a boundary but an invitation. Holmar Northward, as he came to be known in the family's oral tradition, left the Rhine valley of his father and moved north and west toward the coast, toward the flat salt marshes and the grey water and the Frisian merchants who were, in the late sixth century, in the process of building the most extensive trading network in northern Europe.

He arrived with nothing except the family's talent for assessment and the Rhine German's ingrained habit of keeping accurate records, and found that both were exactly what the Frisian trading houses needed. He settled among them, learned their language — which was not difficult, closely related as it was to the Germanic dialects he already knew — and made himself useful in the way that the family had always made itself useful: by being the most reliable reader of a situation in any room he entered.

The account of his work in Frisia is preserved in the notes of Eadric, a Frisian clerk in the service of a coastal trading house at Domburg, whose responsibility it was to record contracts, disputes, and settlements. His record reflects the rhythm of maritime commerce as well as the limits of his position within it, but it preserves with care the sequence of events as they unfolded.

The Account of Holmar Northward

Set Down by Eadric, Clerk to the House of Radulf at Domburg, in the Year of Our Lord 612

The salt smell comes first, always. Before the creak of the dock-timber, before the crying of the gulls, before even the grey light finding its way through the shutter-gaps of the counting house — the salt smell, rolling in off the Frisian sea like a living thing, heavy with brine and the faint rot of low tide and the tar-smoke from the boat-sheds along the quay. I have worked twenty years in this town and I have never ceased to notice it. Holmar, when I put this observation to him once, turned from the window with that characteristic stillness of his and said he noticed it every morning also, but for a different reason.

“It tells me which way the wind is running,” he said, “and therefore which routes are practicable, and therefore which cargoes moved last night, and therefore a great deal else besides.”

He was, at that time, a man of perhaps forty years — though he was vague on the point, dismissing the question with a wave that implied the precise number was the sort of information a well-ordered mind discarded to make room for more useful material. He was lean in the manner of men who forget to eat when they are thinking, with the pale eyes common to the Rhine Germans and a quality of absolute attention that his trading partners found unnerving until they understood it, and then found simply indispensable.

He had arrived in Domburg some three years before I encountered him, during the years when the Frisian trade-network was extending itself south and east along the river routes, and the trading houses here had need of men who could negotiate at the confluence of two commercial cultures without being captured entirely by either.

He spoke Frisian without an accent. This, I learned later, had been a deliberate project; he had spent his first winter on the coast doing nothing else.

“A man who still speaks like a foreigner,” he told me once, “is still being treated like one. I could not afford that luxury.”

I record these preliminary observations because they are necessary to understanding what followed, and because I have been asked by members of the House of Radulf — who know something of what occurred — to set down a true account before the details are lost to the comfortable fog that commercial families prefer to wrap around their more embarrassing episodes.

The official record of this matter, such as it is, credits the resolution to the diligence of Guildmaster Theodric and his men. I witnessed what actually occurred. I will record it accurately.

The factor’s name was Sigebert. He was the son of a family in the Rhine valley with whom the House of Radulf had recently established commercial relations — wool and dye-stuffs moving west, amber and coastal goods moving east — and he had been sent to Domburg to spend a season learning the trade from the inside, which is how such houses built trust in those years. A young man of perhaps twenty-two or -three, with good manners and a conscientious nature and an almost touching belief that the contracts he was given to study represented the full reality of the arrangements they described.

He arrived in late spring. By midsummer he was gone.

The circumstances, as they were initially reported to Theodric, were not immediately alarming. Young men of commercial families sometimes slipped away. Gunwald, an officer of the guild, made inquiries and found nothing indicating violence. He concluded the young man had left voluntarily.

I was present at the House of Radulf when Holmar arrived to review this conclusion. He spent two hours with the contract books. I watched him read in a manner I had not previously observed — not line by line, but in some other fashion that seemed to take in the whole page and its relation to the other pages simultaneously.

“The factor had access to the full ledger,” he said.

“That is so,” I answered.

“Then he has seen something that someone wished he had not seen.”

He requested the contracts of the last two years for the Heligum route and Gunwald’s report. He read the latter standing, as if confirming a conclusion already reached.

“The young man did not purchase passage,” he said. “He was transported as cargo.”

He was absent for four days.

He returned with Sigebert beside him — thin, weathered, but alive.

The following morning, before Radulf and his partners, he explained:

“The supplementary accounts contain entries not present in the primary ledger. This is common. What is uncommon is that the Heligum route contains entries that are not merely discreet but false. Cargo valuations recorded here do not correspond to those of identical goods traded in the same season elsewhere.”

He paused.

“Sigebert is conscientious. He would have noticed. And someone would have been informed.”

He traced the route by wind, by reachable coasts, and by fishermen whose patterns he had spent years learning without immediate purpose.

“A man who stores only the facts he expects to need will always arrive at the scene of the crime to find it empty.”

He found Sigebert in a coastal storehouse used for goods whose documentation was best kept away from Domburg.

He did not name those responsible.

“They are aware that I am aware,” he said. “This is frequently more effective than any proceeding.”

The official record credited Gunwald. I wrote that record. This account I write separately.

Weeks later, I asked Holmar why he left the guilty unnamed.

He stood at the window of his small house near the east quay.

“Sigebert is home,” he said. “He will not speak. The parties involved understand the consequences of repetition.”

He paused.

“Gunwald would have brought charges. Minor men would have been fined. The true authors would have remained untouched and more careful thereafter. Tell me what justice looks like in that accounting.”

I had no answer.

This account is set down by me, Eadric, in the confidence that it will not be read by those who would object to its existence.

The factor Sigebert returned to the Rhine valley. He has not returned to Domburg.

Holmar remains. Each morning the salt comes in off the sea and he tells it which way the wind is running.
